Examining the American Recorded Music Industry

Who profits. Who doesn't. And the structures that determine which is which.

//

About This Series

The American recorded music industry generates over $17 billion in annual revenue. Three companies control roughly 65% of it. The other 35% is split among thousands of independents, each navigating a system whose rules were written by the majors and whose economics were shaped by decades of consolidation, litigation, and technological upheaval.

This series does not attempt to be neutral about that. It attempts to be accurate.

Every claim is sourced from public records: RIAA published data, SEC filings, court decisions, Congressional testimony, and established music industry journalism. Where a data point could only be verified from a single source, it is explicitly flagged. Nothing has been fabricated or estimated without disclosure.

The goal is straightforward: trace the history of how the industry arrived at its current structure, then follow the money through every channel where it flows today — streaming platforms, physical sales, touring, merchandise, and the internal economics of labels themselves. Not as a polemic, but as a map. The kind of document an artist, manager, or founder could read and come away understanding where they actually stand.

Source Methodology

Primary sources: RIAA published reports, SEC filings (UMG, Warner, Spotify, LiveNation), Copyright Royalty Board rulings, Congressional records, DOJ antitrust filings.

Secondary sources: Billboard, Music Business Worldwide, Rolling Stone, Variety — cross-referenced where possible.

Transparency: Claims verified from only a single source are flagged with [single-source]. No data has been fabricated or estimated without explicit disclosure.
