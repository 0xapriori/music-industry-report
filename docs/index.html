Axioms & Starting Assumptions

Before proceeding, the reader should understand the foundational assumptions this report operates under:

  1. Starting point: We begin at the end of World War II (~1945), the recognized inflection point where recorded music became a mass commercial enterprise in the United States. The pre-war era (Edison's phonograph in 1877, the early Columbia and Victor labels, the shellac era) is acknowledged as foundational but is not the focus.
  2. "Major" vs. "Independent": A "major" label is defined not by brand recognition or roster size, but by control of distribution. The majors own their distribution infrastructure; independents rely on third-party or major-label distribution. This is the structural fault line of the industry.
  3. Revenue lens: We primarily examine recorded music revenue (sales of physical media, downloads, and streams). Other revenue streams — live performance, publishing, merchandise, sync licensing — are discussed where they intersect with recorded music economics.
  4. Revenue figures: Unless otherwise noted, revenue figures are in nominal US dollars at estimated retail value, sourced from RIAA published data. Where inflation adjustment materially changes interpretation, it is noted.
  5. No fabricated data: Every quantitative claim is sourced. Single-source claims are marked [single-source]. Where sources disagree, ranges are presented. Where data is unavailable, the gap is stated.

The Post-War Explosion & the Rise of the Independents 1945–1965

The Vacuum

After World War II, US record sales surged from 275 million to 400 million units between 1946 and 1947 alone. The established major labels — Columbia, RCA Victor, Decca, and Capitol — had largely abandoned the "race records" market during the war. Shellac shortages forced them to economize, and they cut their least profitable lines first, which meant Black music.

This created a vacuum. An estimated 1,000 new independent labels formed in the United States between 1948 and 1954, rushing to serve the market the majors had abandoned.

Sources: University of Oregon Open Text, Britannica, History of Rock

The Independents Who Built the Foundation

The labels founded in this era did not merely fill a gap — they created the genres that would define American popular music for the next half-century.

Atlantic Records founded 1947, New York

Founded by Ahmet Ertegun, the son of a Turkish diplomat, with partner Herb Abramson. Jerry Wexler joined in 1953. Atlantic initially focused on R&B, then became the dominant soul label of the 1960s with Wilson Pickett, Otis Redding, and Aretha Franklin. Among the earliest labels to record in stereo. [single-source on "first to record in stereo"]

PBS American Masters, TeachRock, Wikipedia
Chess Records founded 1947, Chicago

Founded by Leonard and Phil Chess, Polish-Jewish immigrants. Specialized in Chicago blues — Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf — and later Chuck Berry, one of the foundational architects of rock and roll.

University of Oregon Open Text, University of Idaho
Sun Records founded 1950, Memphis

Founded by Sam Phillips. Between 1954 and 1956, Sun recorded the first commercial recordings of Elvis Presley, Carl Perkins, Jerry Lee Lewis, and Johnny Cash, pioneering the "rockabilly" sound and production techniques like slapback echo.

Tennessee Encyclopedia, University of Oregon Open Text
King Records founded 1943, Cincinnati

Founded by Syd Nathan. Uniquely recorded both "hillbilly" (country) and R&B under the same roof. By 1949, the sixth-largest label in the US. Nearly 500 singles on the R&B, country, and pop charts, 32 reaching No. 1. King's Federal subsidiary launched James Brown's career.

Wikipedia, Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Library, Billboard
Motown Records founded 1959, Detroit

Berry Gordy borrowed $800 from his family to start Tamla Records. Modeled on Detroit's assembly lines: separate specialists handled songwriting, arrangement, tracking, overdubs, vocals, and quality control. The in-house Funk Brothers and songwriting teams (Holland-Dozier-Holland, Smokey Robinson) created the crossover "Motown Sound." Became the largest Black-owned entertainment business in the country during the 1960s.

Motown Museum, Wikipedia, CBS Detroit, Detroit Historical Society
Stax Records founded 1957, Memphis

Founded by siblings Jim Stewart and Estelle Axton (Stax = Stewart + Axton). Axton mortgaged her home for $2,500 to buy an Ampex tape recorder. Converted a former movie theater into their studio. Pioneered "Southern soul" with racially integrated house bands (Booker T. & the M.G.'s) — unprecedented in the segregated South. Went bankrupt in 1975.

Stax Records official history, Wikipedia, Tennessee Encyclopedia

Why Indies Broke Black Artists When Majors Wouldn't

Of the first generation of commercially successful rock and roll singers, nearly all recorded for independents: Fats Domino for Imperial, Chuck Berry for Chess, Little Richard for Specialty, Elvis Presley for Sun. The major labels had systematically ignored the R&B market. The indie entrepreneurs were frequently outsiders themselves — immigrants, minorities, or people operating outside the mainstream establishment.

Britannica, History of Rock

The Economics of Early Indies

The indie advantage was low overhead: often one person filled the roles of A&R, producer, promoter, and distributor. But the economics were precarious:

  • Publishing exploitation: Label owners became publishers themselves, buying out writers' shares for a few dollars
  • Cash flow crises: Major hits could paradoxically bankrupt poorly capitalized labels — manufacturing and distribution costs were paid upfront, revenue arrived months later
  • Distributor leverage: Independent distributors got return rights and 90-day payment windows, squeezing label cash flow

The structural vulnerability was clear: independents could discover and develop talent, but lacked infrastructure to scale. This created the recurring pattern: indies innovate, majors acquire.

University of Oregon Open Text, History of Rock, TMorganOnline

Payola, FM Radio, and the Shifting of Power 1955–1975

Payola: The First Crisis of Gatekeeping

Payola — a portmanteau of "pay" and "Victrola" — was the practice of record labels paying radio disc jockeys cash or gifts to play specific records. The practice was not new, but radio made it enormously powerful as a gatekeeping mechanism.

The Congressional Hearings (1959–1960)

The House Subcommittee on Legislative Oversight launched hearings in 1959. In closed and open sessions, 335 disc jockeys admitted to receiving over $263,000 in "consulting fees."

Alan Freed

DJ credited with popularizing "rock and roll." Refused to sign an affidavit denying payola. Fired from WABC. Arrested May 19, 1960. Pleaded guilty to two counts. Fined $300. IRS pursued $38,000 in back taxes. Died January 20, 1965, age 43.

Dick Clark

Host of American Bandstand. Had ownership in seven labels, six publishers, three distributors, two talent agencies. Divested all interests before testifying. Committee chairman called him "a fine young man." Continued career for decades.

Multiple historians observe that racial dynamics likely played a role — Freed championed Black artists and integrated audiences, while Clark's show was more mainstream. This is an analytical interpretation, not a documented Congressional finding, but appears consistently across sources.

Legislative Outcome — And Its Limits

Congress amended the Federal Communications Act in 1960. Payola became a misdemeanor with penalties up to $10,000 and one year in prison.

Did payola end? No — it changed form. The practice evolved into the "independent promoter" system. Major labels were paying as much as $80 million per year to middlemen during the 1980s. [single-source — NPR]

Wikipedia, HISTORY.com, EBSCO, CultureSonar, NPR (2017)

The FM Radio Revolution

In 1964, the FCC prohibited FM stations from simply simulcasting AM programming (enforced January 1, 1967). FM stations suddenly needed original programming. Tom Donahue at KMPX in San Francisco pioneered freeform FM, where DJs played long sets including obscure album tracks. This shifted rock's primary vehicle from the single to the album.

Freeform radio was gradually commercialized into Album-Oriented Rock (AOR) through the 1970s. The format shift had profound economic implications: labels could charge $8–12 for an album instead of $1 for a single. The album became both the primary artistic format and the primary revenue unit — setting the stage for the CD era.

Wikipedia (AOR), Encyclopedia.com, uDiscover Music

The Corporate Era & the CD Windfall 1975–1999

The Compact Disc Revolution

The CD (co-developed by Philips and Sony) entered the US market in 1983 at 0.5% of sales. CDs overtook cassettes in 1991 and reached 95.5% market share in 2002.

The Catalog Windfall

A significant portion of CD boom revenue came from consumers re-purchasing music they already owned on vinyl. This was selling the same content twice — a one-time windfall that inflated revenue and created unsustainable expectations about the industry's "natural" size.

Revenue at the Peak

  • 1990: Cassettes ($3.47B) and CDs ($3.45B) roughly equal
  • 1999: Total US revenue peaked at $14.6B nominal (~$22.7–24B inflation-adjusted)
  • 2000: CD sales alone reached $13.2B

The Digital Disruption 1999–2015

Napster: The Earthquake (1999–2001)

Created by Shawn Fanning and Sean Parker, live June 1999. Centralized P2P: users shared MP3s via Napster's central search index. Peak: ~80 million registered users (late 2000).

A&M Records v. Napster (9th Cir., Feb 12, 2001): liable for contributory and vicarious infringement. Judge Patel ordered removal of copyrighted songs within 72 hours. Napster filed for bankruptcy June 2002.

The industry won the legal battle. It lost the war. Napster proved consumers wanted music digitally, instantly, and cheaply. The industry's response — litigation over innovation — cost it a decade of revenue.

Wikipedia, History.com, Justia

The P2P Hydra (2001–2005)

Shutting Napster down didn't stop file sharing. It decentralized it.

KazaaDecentralized (FastTrack)

4.2M simultaneous users at peak. 389M software downloads. Settled for ~$100M (2006).

LimeWireGnutella protocol

Settled for $105M (2011). RIAA sought trillions; court called it "more money than the entire music recording industry has made since Edison's invention of the phonograph."

BitTorrentProtocol, not a service

Released July 2001 by Bram Cohen. By 2004, estimated at 35% of all upstream internet traffic. Far harder to shut down — it's a protocol, not a company.

MGM v. Grokster (Supreme Court, June 27, 2005): Unanimous decision establishing the inducement theory of secondary copyright liability.

The RIAA's War on Consumers

Launched September 8, 2003. Over ~5 years, the RIAA filed suits against 30,000+ individuals. Settlements: $3,000–$12,000. The RIAA sued a 12-year-old and a 13-year-old, generating enormous public backlash. One analysis found the RIAA spent $17.6M in legal fees to recover $391K. [single-source — Techdirt]

EFF, History.com, Techdirt

iTunes: The Unbundling (2003)

Launched April 28, 2003. Individual tracks at $0.99, albums at $9.99. Apple kept 30%, rights holders got 70%. Reached ~63% of digital music downloads at peak.

The unbundling effect was arguably as destructive as piracy. Consumers could buy the single instead of the $15–18 album. Downloads peaked at $3B in 2012 (41% of revenue) — far less than the CD peak. By 2024, downloads = 2% of revenue.

Apple Newsroom, AppleInsider, CNN Money

Social Platforms

MySpace (~2005–2009): First platform where artists could upload, build followings, and bypass A&R. Arctic Monkeys, Lily Allen, Soulja Boy, Adele broke through. ~50 million tracks permanently lost in a server migration catastrophe.

SoundCloud (2008–present): Anyone could upload for free. The "SoundCloud rap" era (~2015–2018) — NYT called it "the most vital and disruptive new movement in hip-hop." Post Malone, XXXTentacion, Juice WRLD, Lil Uzi Vert, Doja Cat emerged.

YouTube

Founded 2005, acquired by Google for $1.65B (2006). Vevo launched 2009 (UMG + Sony + EMI JV). Content ID cost ~$100M to develop; generated $2B+ for rights holders by 2016. YouTube's annual music payouts: $4B (2021) → $6B (2022) → $8B (2025). Per-stream rate (~$0.00069) remains far below audio-only services — the "value gap."

Wikipedia, Music Business Worldwide, Music Week

Pro-Rata vs. User-Centric

Pro-rata (dominant): Platform pools all revenue, distributes by share of total streams. If Artist X gets 5% of all streams, they get 5% of the pool — regardless of whether any subscriber actually listened to them.

User-centric: Each subscriber's payment goes only to artists they listened to. SoundCloud adopted this (April 2021), reporting artists earned ~60% more on average. [single-source — SoundCloud]

Pro-rata favors passive, playlist-driven listening over artists with dedicated fanbases.

How Money Flows — Artist Economics Then and Now

The Traditional Major Label Deal

The Advance: Upfront, non-returnable cash. Covers recording, videos, marketing, living expenses. Recoupable — the label recoups from the artist's royalty share before the artist sees any royalty income. The artist never writes a check back, but earns nothing until the label is made whole.

Publishing: The Other Copyright

Every recorded song generates two separate copyrights: the composition (publishing — melody, lyrics) and the sound recording (master — the specific performance). These are independent rights with independent revenue streams.

The Catalog Boom

Catalog deal volume exploded from <$1B pre-2018 to >$5B in 2021. Music became a financial asset class. The Taylor Swift/Scooter Braun dispute became the most visible illustration of the master ownership problem — Braun's Ithaca Holdings acquired Big Machine Records (and Swift's first six masters) without her knowledge. Swift re-recorded four albums and ultimately reacquired the masters in 2025.

Variety, Deadline, EPGD Business Law

Themes & Second-Order Analysis

1. The Innovation Cycle

Technological disruption → Indie entrepreneurs exploit new channels → Majors observe & acquire → Consolidation → Next disruption resets the cycle

This played out with post-war indie labels, MTV-era labels, and digital distribution. The question: does streaming-era distribution (DistroKid, TuneCore) represent a structural break or the latest iteration?

2. Distribution as Power

The defining characteristic of a "major" has never been its roster — it's control of distribution. In the vinyl era: pressing plants. CD era: retail relationships. Digital era: playlist placement and algorithmic visibility.

Distribution itself has been democratized (anyone can put a song on Spotify). Discovery has not. The bottleneck moved from physical distribution to attention distribution.

3. The Artist's Paradox

Improved
  • Global distribution for $23/year
  • No A&R gatekeeping for market access
  • Direct fan relationships via social
  • 85–100% of recording revenue (indie)
Deteriorated
  • Per-unit revenue collapsed (CD → fractions of a penny)
  • 5M+ Spotify streams for minimum wage
  • 360 deals expand label claims
  • Playlist gatekeeping replaced radio gatekeeping
  • "Middle class artist" has largely disappeared

4. The Financialization of Music

Music is increasingly treated as a financial asset class: private equity owns major labels, catalogs are yield-generating investments, streaming provides predictable cash flows. The UMG IPO valued the company at €46 billion — a financial event, not a cultural one. Decisions about artists, catalogs, and deals are increasingly driven by ROI calculations.

5. Regulation Lags Reality

Every major regulatory intervention has come after the market was already reshaped:

  • Payola legislation (1960) — practice was already endemic
  • Telecom Act (1996) — consolidation already underway
  • DMCA (1998) — before implications of user-uploaded content understood
  • Music Modernization Act (2018) — streaming problems existed for a decade

This suggests future interventions (AI music, streaming payment models, platform power) will similarly arrive after the market has adapted.
