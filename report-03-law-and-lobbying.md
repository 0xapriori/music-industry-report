## Axioms and Starting Assumptions

This report inherits and extends the axioms established in [Report 1: A History of the US Recorded Music Industry](./report.md) and [Report 2: The Economics of Making Music](./report-02-economics.md). The reader should understand the additional assumptions specific to this legal and regulatory analysis:

1. **Law as architecture, not abstraction**: This report treats copyright law, consent decrees, regulatory decisions, and lobbying expenditures not as dry legal matters but as the *structural blueprints* of the music industry's power dynamics. Report 1 documented who consolidated control. Report 2 documented how money flows within the structures that emerged. This report asks the prior question: *who wrote the rules that made those structures possible, and whose interests did those rules serve?*

2. **The distinction between law and practice**: Throughout this report, we will encounter persistent gaps between what the law technically requires and what actually happens. Streaming services operated for years without proper mechanical licenses. Platforms use safe harbor protections in ways the statute's drafters never contemplated. Labels classify recordings as "work for hire" despite dubious legal footing. These gaps are not bugs — they are features of a system where enforcement is expensive and the entities with the most resources shape both the rules and their interpretation.

3. **Lobbying is a form of investment**: When the RIAA spends $6.9 million on federal lobbying in a single year, that is not a cost center — it is an investment with expected returns denominated in favorable legislation, blocked unfavorable legislation, and regulatory outcomes. This report treats lobbying expenditures as economic data, not political commentary. The numbers reveal whose interests are represented and whose are not.

4. **No fabricated data**: Every dollar figure, case citation, statutory reference, and lobbying expenditure in this report is sourced from the research files. Where a figure comes from a single source, it is marked `[single-source]`. Where data is unavailable, the gap is stated. This standard is inherited from Reports 1 and 2 and applied with particular rigor here — legal claims are especially prone to motivated framing on all sides.

5. **Forward-looking, not just diagnostic**: While much of this report documents structural failures, the final sections articulate what a better system could look like. The point is not merely to catalog dysfunction but to understand why alternatives have not emerged — and what it would take for them to.

## Part 1: The Legal Architecture

### The Scaffolding

Every industry operates within a legal framework. But most industries did not have their legal frameworks designed, in significant part, by the entities those frameworks are supposed to regulate. The American music industry did.

To understand who profits from music and who does not, you cannot start with streaming economics or label contracts or even the Big Three's market dominance. You have to start with the statutes — the copyright acts that built the scaffolding on which everything else was constructed. Each one shifted power. Each one had winners and losers. And each one was shaped by the lobbying of specific parties who understood that controlling the rules is more valuable than winning any single game.

### The Copyright Act of 1909: The Original Bargain

The modern music industry's legal architecture begins with a Supreme Court case about player pianos.

In February 1908, the Supreme Court ruled in *White-Smith Music Publishing Co. v. Apollo Co.* (209 U.S. 1) that manufacturers of player piano rolls were not required to pay royalties to composers. The reasoning: music rolls were not "copies" of musical compositions because they were not "written or printed" records. This was, on its face, absurd — the rolls existed solely to reproduce compositions — but it reflected a court struggling to apply 19th-century legal concepts to early 20th-century technology.

Congress responded with the Copyright Act of 1909, signed on March 4, 1909. The Act created the first **compulsory mechanical license**: anyone could make a mechanical reproduction of a musical composition without the copyright owner's consent, provided they paid the statutory rate. That rate was set at **2 cents per copy**.

The compulsory license was explicitly anti-monopoly in intent. The Aeolian Company had a near-monopoly on player piano rolls and had been negotiating exclusive deals with music publishers. Congress wanted to prevent any single entity from monopolizing mechanical reproduction of music. This was a genuine public interest purpose — but it also established a precedent that would prove enormously consequential: the government, not the market, would set the price for mechanical reproduction of musical compositions.

Two cents per copy. That rate remained unchanged for **69 years** — from 1909 until 1978.

For songwriters, the 1909 Act guaranteed compensation for mechanical reproductions, but at a rate they had no power to negotiate. For performers of sound recordings, the Act provided nothing at all. No federal copyright protection for sound recordings would exist until the Sound Recording Amendment of 1971. For nearly the entire first century of the recorded music industry, the people whose voices you actually heard on records had no federal copyright in those recordings.

### The Copyright Act of 1976: Modernization and Its Limits

The 1976 Copyright Act (Public Law 94-553), effective January 1, 1978, was the first comprehensive overhaul since 1909. It modernized copyright law in several ways that mattered for musicians — while embedding structural advantages that persist to this day.

The Act extended copyright protection to "original works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium of expression," which definitively included sound recordings. It established the Copyright Royalty Board's predecessor system for setting mechanical royalty rates. And it created Section 203 — the **35-year termination right** — which allowed authors to reclaim copyrights they had assigned, starting 35 years after the grant. Section 203(a)(5) made this right inalienable: "Termination of the grant may be effected notwithstanding any agreement to the contrary." Labels could not contractually prevent artists from exercising this right.

In theory.

In practice, the 1976 Act also codified the **work-for-hire doctrine** — and this is where the architecture starts to reveal its true beneficiaries. Under Section 101, a "work made for hire" vests copyright ownership in the employer (or commissioner), not the creator. If a sound recording qualifies as work for hire, the artist never owns the copyright and therefore has no termination right to exercise. The 35-year right is meaningless if the recording was "work for hire" from the start.

The critical legal question — whether sound recordings qualify as work for hire when made by artists who are technically independent contractors — has never been definitively resolved. Sound recordings are not explicitly listed among the nine statutory categories of commissioned works eligible for work-for-hire status. But record companies routinely insert work-for-hire language into recording contracts, and the legal ambiguity has never been settled by the Supreme Court. This ambiguity is not accidental. It serves the interests of labels, who benefit from a clouded legal landscape where artists are discouraged from testing their termination rights.

### The 1999 Work-for-Hire Heist

If there is a single episode that crystallizes how the legal system is manipulated by industry insiders, it is the 1999 work-for-hire amendment.

In 1999, while Congress was considering the Satellite Home Viewer Improvement Act — a bill about satellite television retransmissions, which had nothing to do with music — an RIAA lobbyist inserted language adding "sound recordings" to the list of works eligible for work-for-hire classification under Section 101 of the Copyright Act. The amendment was slipped in without public debate and without the knowledge of most congressional subcommittee members.

If this amendment had stood, it would have stripped recording artists of their 35-year termination rights permanently. Every recording made under a label contract would belong to the label in perpetuity.

The amendment was reportedly drafted by a legislative aide who subsequently accepted a position as Senior Vice President of Government Relations and Legislative Counsel at the RIAA. That aide was Mitch Glazier, who now serves as the RIAA's Chairman and CEO.

The artist response was immediate and furious. The Recording Artists Coalition, founded by Don Henley and Sheryl Crow, mobilized to fight the amendment. In 2000, Congress passed the **Work Made for Hire and Copyright Corrections Act of 2000**, repealing the amendment retroactively and directing courts to interpret the law as if neither the amendment nor its repeal had occurred.

The amendment was dead. But the episode revealed the mechanism: a lobbyist on congressional staff, an amendment buried in an unrelated bill, no public debate, and a subsequent career move to the very industry that benefited. This is how rules get written.

### The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998): Safe Harbor and Its Consequences

The DMCA was enacted in 1998 to bring copyright law into the digital era. Its most consequential provisions for the music industry were the **safe harbor protections** of Section 512, which shielded online service providers from liability for user-uploaded infringing content — provided they met certain conditions: adopting a repeat infringer policy, accommodating standard technical measures, and responding to takedown notices.

The safe harbor was designed for a specific problem: early internet service providers should not be liable for every piece of infringing content that traversed their networks. The legislative intent was reasonable. But the way the safe harbor has been used — particularly by YouTube — would have been unrecognizable to the statute's drafters.

YouTube functions as a de facto music streaming service. It is the single most-used platform for music consumption globally. Yet because it classifies itself as a user-upload platform, it operates under DMCA safe harbor rather than negotiating the kind of direct licenses that Spotify and Apple Music obtain. The result is the "value gap": YouTube pays approximately $0.00069 per stream, while Apple Music pays approximately $0.01 and TIDAL approximately $0.01284. YouTube's rate is roughly one-seventh of what other interactive streaming services pay.

The U.S. Copyright Office's own Section 512 Study, published on May 21, 2020, concluded that the safe harbor system is "unbalanced" — that "the balance has shifted almost uniformly in favor of online service providers to the detriment of rightsholders." The study identified areas where implementation diverged from Congress's original intent, including eligibility qualifications, repeat infringer policies, knowledge standards, and notice specificity.

No comprehensive Section 512 reform has passed as of March 2026. The EU addressed the equivalent problem through Article 17 of the Copyright Directive in 2019, effectively removing safe harbor for large user-upload platforms. The United States has not.

### The Digital Performance Right in Sound Recordings Act (1995)

Before 1995, there was no federal right of public performance in sound recordings — period. Radio stations paid songwriters (via PROs) but owed nothing to the artists whose voices they broadcast. The Digital Performance Right in Sound Recordings Act (Public Law 104-39) partially addressed this by creating a limited performance right for sound recordings — but only for **digital audio transmissions**. Terrestrial AM/FM radio remained exempt.

This was a deliberate carve-out. The National Association of Broadcasters successfully lobbied to exclude AM/FM radio from the new performance right, arguing that radio airplay provided "free promotion" to artists. The result: the United States joined Iran and North Korea as one of the only significant countries without a performance right for sound recordings on terrestrial radio. Every OECD member country except the U.S. pays artists for radio airplay.

The financial consequences are concrete. But understanding them requires understanding how the rest of the world handles this — because the American system is not merely different. It is an outlier, and the outlier status has a price.

Most countries operate under a system of **neighboring rights** (also called "related rights"), derived from the 1961 Rome Convention. Performers and producers of sound recordings hold rights distinct from the composition copyright — including the right to be paid when their recordings are broadcast on radio. Collecting societies like PPL in the UK and GVL in Germany administer these royalties domestically and distribute them internationally through reciprocal agreements.

The key word is **reciprocal**. The system runs on strict reciprocity: a country's artists receive neighboring rights payments from abroad only if their home country extends the same rights to foreign artists. The United States does not extend those rights. So the system excludes American artists from the money. Foreign collecting societies withhold approximately **$200 million annually** in neighboring rights payments that would otherwise flow to American performers and labels. When an American artist's recording plays on BBC Radio, PPL collects the royalty. But because the U.S. does not pay British artists when their recordings air on American radio, PPL holds that money back — redistributed domestically rather than remitted to the American rights holder.

This is not an inevitability. It is a policy choice — one that every other OECD nation has made differently. And it is a policy choice that is actively maintained. The NAB spent $11.9 million on federal lobbying in 2024, outspending the RIAA nearly 2:1, for the specific purpose of preserving terrestrial radio's exemption from paying performers. Two hundred million dollars a year leaves American artists' pockets. Eleven million dollars a year keeps it that way.

### The Music Modernization Act (2018): The Peace Treaty

The Music Modernization Act (Public Law 115-264), signed on October 11, 2018, was described by NPR as "a music industry peace treaty." It passed unanimously in both the House (415-0) and the Senate — a remarkable feat in a deeply polarized Congress. It was also the product of years of intense lobbying, negotiation, and compromise that reveals exactly how the legislative sausage gets made.

The MMA consolidated three separate bills:

**Title I** created the **Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC)**, a nonprofit entity to administer a blanket mechanical license for digital streaming. This addressed the "black box" problem documented in Report 2 — streaming services had been operating for years without proper mechanical licenses for millions of songs, filing over one million "address unknown" Notices of Intent with the Copyright Office that effectively released them from the obligation to pay. The MLC, which became operational on January 1, 2021, was supposed to fix this.

**Title II** (the CLASSICS Act) extended federal protections to pre-1972 sound recordings — recordings that had fallen through a gap between state common law and federal statute.

**Title III** (the AMP Act) established that producers, mixers, and sound engineers could receive a portion of statutory royalties directly through SoundExchange.

The MMA also changed the rate-setting standard for mechanical royalties from the policy-oriented 801(b)(1) standard — which required consideration of "minimizing disruptive impact on existing industry structures" — to a market-based "willing buyer/willing seller" standard. This was a significant victory for songwriters and publishers, who had long argued that the old standard systematically depressed mechanical rates below market levels.

But the MMA's passage required compromises that tell you who had power at the table.

**The Blackstone compromise**: SESAC and Harry Fox Agency — both owned by Blackstone — initially blocked a Senate vote. The compromise allowed the MLC to handle digital streaming mechanical licenses while private entities like Harry Fox continued handling licenses for restaurants, bars, and radio.

**The SiriusXM compromise**: Satellite radio royalty rates would remain unchanged through 2027 (rather than 2022) in exchange for SiriusXM foregoing its right to appeal a recent CRB ruling.

**The tech company buy-in**: Digital services agreed to fund the MLC in exchange for a blanket license that reduced their transaction costs and litigation risk. Critically, the MMA also constrained the ability of copyright holders to sue digital services for mechanical infringement for lawsuits filed after January 1, 2018. The platforms gained legal certainty. Whether songwriters gained proportional compensation is another question.

The MMA was real progress. But the entities at the negotiating table — RIAA, NMPA, NSAI, the recording academy, SESAC/Blackstone, SiriusXM, Spotify, Apple, Amazon, Google — were institutions with lobbying infrastructure. Individual songwriters and independent artists were represented indirectly, through organizations whose interests did not perfectly align with theirs. The MLC's board includes publisher representatives. Unmatched royalties that remain unclaimed for three years can be distributed to publishers on a market-share basis — meaning major publishers, which have the best metadata systems and therefore the lowest unmatched rates, would be the primary beneficiaries.

The peace treaty was real. But the terms of the peace reflected the relative power of the parties at the table.

## Part 2: The Courts as Battleground

### Where the Law Gets Made — and Unmade

If legislation builds the scaffolding, litigation tests its load-bearing capacity. The courtroom is where the gaps between law and practice get exposed — where artists discover what their contracts actually mean, where platforms discover the limits of safe harbor, and where the music industry's structural power dynamics play out in rulings that affect millions of creators.

What follows is not a comprehensive legal treatise. It is a selective examination of the cases that most clearly reveal the power structures at work.

### Napster and Grokster: Establishing Digital Liability

*A&M Records, Inc. v. Napster, Inc.*, 239 F.3d 1004 (9th Cir. 2001), was the music industry's first major digital copyright victory. The Ninth Circuit found that Napster users' activities constituted direct copyright infringement, that Napster had both actual and constructive knowledge of infringement and materially contributed to it (contributory infringement), and that Napster's failure to police its system combined with its financial interest in infringement established vicarious infringement. The fair use defense was rejected. Napster was effectively shut down and filed for bankruptcy in 2002.

But Napster was centralized — it maintained a searchable index. The harder question was whether decentralized peer-to-peer systems, with no central index, would face similar liability. The answer came in *MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.*, 545 U.S. 913 (2005), where the Supreme Court established the **inducement doctrine** in a unanimous 9-0 decision: anyone who distributes software with the object of promoting its use to infringe copyright is liable for the resulting infringement by third parties. Evidence of inducement included StreamCast targeting former Napster users, internal communications showing intent to attract infringers, and failure to develop filtering tools.

The music industry won both cases. But the victories were pyrrhic in a broader sense. Napster and Grokster were shut down, but the technology they represented — digital distribution of music — was unstoppable. The industry's legal strategy of suing technology companies (and, infamously, individual file sharers) bought time but did not prevent the transition to a digital economy that would ultimately compress the value of recorded music.

### Capitol Records v. Thomas-Rasset: The Individual File-Sharer Strategy

The industry's campaign of suing individual file sharers produced its most publicized case in *Capitol Records, Inc. v. Thomas-Rasset* — three jury trials, an Eighth Circuit appeal, and a denied certiorari petition, all over a Native American mother of four from Brainerd, Minnesota, who downloaded 24 songs via Kazaa.

The damage awards tell a story of their own. The first jury deliberated for five minutes before awarding $222,000 ($9,250 per song). The second awarded $1,920,000 ($80,000 per song), which the district court reduced to $54,000 as "so severe and oppressive as to be wholly disproportioned to the offense." The third jury awarded $1,500,000 ($62,500 per song). The Eighth Circuit ultimately reinstated $222,000 and held that statutory damages in copyright cases are not subject to the due process limitations applicable to punitive damages.

The case became a symbol of an industry strategy — suing its own potential customers — that the RIAA abandoned in 2008. The strategy had been wildly unpopular, expensive to pursue, and of questionable deterrent value. But it established legal precedents about statutory damages that persist.

### The Eminem/FBT Ruling: Downloads Are Licenses

*F.B.T. Productions, LLC v. Aftermath Records*, 621 F.3d 958 (9th Cir. 2010), changed digital royalty accounting for an entire generation of legacy artists — and revealed how labels had systematically underpaid them.

The case turned on a single contractual distinction: FBT's contract with Aftermath (a Universal subsidiary) provided 12-20% royalties on "Records Sold" and 50% of net revenue on "Masters Licensed." Were digital downloads through iTunes "sales" or "licenses"?

The Ninth Circuit held they were **licenses**. Aftermath did not sell physical copies to iTunes — it granted iTunes the right to use the master recordings to create and distribute digital files. That was a license, not a sale. F.B.T. and Eminem were owed the 50% licensing rate, not the 12-20% sales rate.

The ruling had massive implications. Every legacy recording contract that distinguished between sales and licensing — which was essentially all of them — was potentially affected. Labels had been paying artists the lower "sales" rate on digital downloads while the actual legal relationship between label and platform was a license. The class action that followed, brought by the Rick James Estate and Chuck D against Universal, settled for **$11.5 million** plus an approximately **10% increase** in digital download royalties going forward. The Allman Brothers' parallel lawsuit against Sony settled for **$7.95 million** plus a **3% royalty bump** for digital downloads to all Sony artists.

These settlements sound significant. They are not. They are rounding errors relative to the total revenue generated by digital downloads during the years when labels were paying the lower rate. But they established a principle: the legal classification of digital transactions matters, and labels' preferred classification does not always prevail.

### Williams v. Gaye: "Blurred Lines" and the Chill

*Williams v. Gaye*, 885 F.3d 1150 (9th Cir. 2018), the "Blurred Lines" case, sent shockwaves through the music industry — but for reasons that had nothing to do with industry power structures and everything to do with the boundaries of creative expression.

A jury found that "Blurred Lines" (2013) infringed Marvin Gaye's "Got to Give It Up" (1977). Damages included $3,188,527.50 in actual damages, nearly $2.1 million in profits, and a **continuing royalty of 50%** of future songwriter and publishing revenues from "Blurred Lines." The Ninth Circuit affirmed the infringement finding, holding that the Gaye composition was "entitled to broad protection against copyright infringement liability because musical compositions are not confined to a narrow range of expression."

Judge Nguyen's dissent warned that the majority had allowed the Gaye estate to "own a musical style" — and the music community largely agreed. The decision created a chilling effect on songwriting, leading to a reported increase in preemptive songwriting credits and settlements. If evoking the "feel" or "groove" of an earlier song could constitute infringement, the boundaries of creative influence became dangerously uncertain.

The *Skidmore v. Led Zeppelin* decision (952 F.3d 1051, 9th Cir. 2020 en banc) provided a partial counterweight, eliminating the "inverse ratio" rule in the Ninth Circuit and holding that common musical building blocks like a descending chromatic bass line are not protectable. But the tension between these two Ninth Circuit decisions remains unresolved — a legal landscape where creators cannot be confident about the line between inspiration and infringement.

### Artist-Label Contract Disputes: The Pattern

The court cases between individual artists and their labels follow a pattern so consistent it constitutes its own form of evidence. The specifics differ. The structure does not.

**Prince vs. Warner Bros. (1993-2014)**: Prince signed a reported $100 million deal in 1992 that gave Warner Bros. ownership and control of all master recordings. In 1993, he changed his name to an unpronounceable symbol, appeared publicly with "SLAVE" written on his cheek, and stated he changed his name because "his own name now belonged to the company." He fulfilled his contractual obligations, left in 1996, and released music independently. It took until 2014 — over two decades — for Prince to regain complete ownership of his Warner Bros. recordings.

**George Michael vs. Sony (1992-1995)**: Michael sued Sony in the UK, arguing his recording contract constituted an unreasonable restraint of trade. The court ruled entirely against him. He settled, paying Sony a reported $30-40 million to be released from his contract. The court found the contract was reasonable because Michael had access to expert legal advice and had terms comparable to similarly successful artists — a ruling that essentially held that if an exploitative contract is standard in the industry, it is not unconscionable.

**TLC: 65 Million Records and Bankruptcy**: TLC sold an estimated 65 million records worldwide. Under their 1991 contract with LaFace Records, each member received approximately one-third of 56 cents per album. After the label recouped recording, manufacturing, distribution, promotion, travel, hotels, food, clothing, and music video costs, there was nothing left. Each member was reportedly taking home less than $50,000 per year. The group stated they received approximately 1% of an estimated $175 million in album sales. They filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in July 1995, declaring $3.5 million in debts. At the 1996 Grammy Awards, after winning Best R&B Album, Chilli stated: "We've sold 10 million albums worldwide. We're as broke as broke can be."

**Toni Braxton: $1,972**: Despite $170 million in worldwide sales and over 40 million records sold, Braxton received a $1,972 royalty check from her first recording contract. Her lawyer told the LA Times she was receiving only 33 cents per album. She filed for bankruptcy twice — in 1998 (Chapter 7) and in 2010 (Chapter 11, citing $18.3 million in debts including $16 million owed to her labels).

**Kesha vs. Dr. Luke / Sony (2014-2023)**: Kesha sued producer Dr. Luke for sexual assault, harassment, and abuse, and sought release from her contract with Dr. Luke's label (a Sony imprint). In February 2016, a New York Supreme Court judge denied her request for a preliminary injunction — meaning she remained contractually bound to record for the label run by her alleged abuser. The case settled in June 2023. It laid bare the intersection of contractual power and personal safety in a way that no amount of industry PR could obscure.

**Megan Thee Stallion vs. 1501 Certified Entertainment (2020-2023)**: Megan challenged a contract she signed at age 20 as "not only entirely unconscionable, but ridiculously so." Roc Nation flagged the deal's terms: recording profits split 60-40 in favor of the label, versus an industry standard of 50-50. Multiple lawsuits followed — over release restrictions, album classification disputes, and alleged underpayment. The parties settled confidentially in October 2023. One week later, Megan announced her next album would be self-released.

**Lil Wayne vs. Cash Money (2015-2018)**: Wayne filed a $51 million lawsuit against Cash Money Records and Birdman, alleging financial mishandling, failure to pay owed advance money and royalties, and the withholding of his album *Tha Carter V* for four years. Wayne claimed he received only $2 million of a $10 million advance owed. Settlement: Universal paid Wayne $10 million and terminated his Cash Money contract.

The pattern: artists sign contracts when they have no leverage — often young, often without adequate legal representation, often dazzled by the apparent generosity of advances that are actually loans charged against future earnings. The contracts transfer ownership of master recordings to the label, establish recoupment structures that can consume decades of revenue, and bind the artist for multiple albums with no reciprocal obligation from the label to promote or release those albums. When artists become successful enough to understand what they signed, they discover that the legal system treats these contracts as enforceable — because the artist "had access to legal counsel" and the terms are "standard in the industry."

The recoupment trap is the structural foundation. As Courtney Love explained in her 2000 "Piracy" speech at the Digital Hollywood conference: "Record companies own copyrights forever, and the system is set up so almost nobody gets paid." She defined piracy not as Napster-style file sharing but as major label recording contracts. She was not wrong.

### Congressional Testimony: Artists on the Record

When artists have testified before Congress and state legislatures, their testimony has been remarkably consistent across decades.

**Don Henley**, testifying before the California State Senate in September 2001: "Record companies can fire us, but we can't fire them, even if they fail to perform their duties."

**Courtney Love**, at the same hearing: "I've made more for Universal than 'Titanic.'" And: "In 1987, [record companies] snuck in, and you guys got snookered" — referring to the 1987 amendment to California Labor Code Section 2855 that weakened the seven-year rule for recording artists by creating a financial penalty for exercising it.

**LeAnn Rimes**, also at that hearing, was 19 years old: "At 12, I was thrilled to sign my record contract with Curb Records, and at that age I didn't understand everything that was in my contract. I just turned 19 last month, and if I record at a rate of one album every two years, which is the industry average, I will be 35 before the contract is over."

**Don Henley again**, before the U.S. Senate Judiciary Subcommittee in June 2020, on DMCA reform: "The system is antiquated and badly broken. And the creative community is paying a very steep price." And: "For each infringing link or file taken down, a dozen more pop up in its place."

**Aloe Blacc**, testifying at a 2018 Senate Judiciary field hearing during GRAMMYs weekend, on the Music Modernization Act: the licensing reform was needed "to make sure that songwriters and music publishers are paid mechanical royalties that are currently being unpaid by the streaming companies."

The testimony spans nearly two decades. The complaints are the same. The system is structured to extract value from creators and transfer it to corporate entities. The creators know this. They say so, on the record, under oath. And the system adjusts at the margins — a reform here, a settlement there — without altering the fundamental architecture.

### The Seven-Year Rule and Its Music Industry Carve-Out

California Labor Code Section 2855, enacted in 1937, provides that personal service contracts cannot be enforced beyond seven years. This applies to "special, unique, unusual, extraordinary, or intellectual character" services — a description that plainly includes recording artists.

In 1987, the music industry lobbied for an amendment — Section 2855(b) — that created a carve-out specifically for phonorecord production. Artists can invoke the seven-year rule, but the label retains the right to recover damages for undelivered material under the contract. This effectively creates a financial penalty for exercising the right, discouraging artists from using it.

30 Seconds to Mars invoked Section 2855(a) in 2008 when Virgin/EMI sued them for $30 million for breach of contract. The band countersued, alleging "creative accounting" resulting in unpaid royalties. The parties settled and signed a new multi-album deal with Virgin/EMI — which tells you something about the relative leverage even when the law is nominally on the artist's side.

The California FAIR Act (A.B. 983 / A.B. 2926) has been introduced multiple times to restore full seven-year rule protection for recording artists by repealing the 1987 amendment. As of this writing, it has not been enacted.

## Part 3: The Lobbying Machine

### Who Lobbies and How Much They Spend

The American music industry's policy outcomes are not determined by the merits of competing arguments. They are determined by the relative resources deployed to advance those arguments. Understanding the lobbying landscape is prerequisite to understanding why the law looks the way it does.

The primary lobbying entities in the music space, ranked by spending:

**The RIAA (Recording Industry Association of America)** represents labels — not artists. Its board and funding come from its label members: Universal Music Group, Sony Music Entertainment, Warner Music Group, and hundreds of smaller labels. The RIAA's lobbying expenditures, per OpenSecrets data:

- 2018: $5,642,155
- 2024: $6,910,041
- Q1 2025 alone: more than $2,500,000 `[single-source, Digital Music News]`

In 2023, 28 out of 35 RIAA lobbyists had previously held government jobs. In 2024, 24 out of 32 had government backgrounds. The revolving door is not a metaphor — it is a staffing strategy.

**The individual major labels** also lobby independently:

- UMG: $1,920,000 (2022), $2,260,000 (2023), $2,440,000 (2024)
- Sony Corp (all divisions): $2,652,769 (2021), $3,353,894 (2024)
- Warner Music Group: $447,450 (2010), with minimal recent activity — WMG "has not reported lobbying on specific bills in 2024" per OpenSecrets

**The NMPA (National Music Publishers Association)** represents publishers — the entities that own compositions, often the publishing arms of the same major label conglomerates. NMPA lobbying: $742,500 in 2018. Its PAC raised $58,500 in the 2021-2022 cycle.

**ASCAP and BMI** lobby on behalf of their songwriter and publisher members, constrained by the consent decrees they are trying to modify:

- ASCAP: $880,000 (2024)
- BMI: $380,000 (2018), $210,000 (2024)

**SoundExchange**, the nonprofit that collects digital performance royalties: $960,000 (2015).

### The Opponents

Now look at the other side of the table.

**The National Association of Broadcasters (NAB)** — the radio industry's trade group and the music industry's primary opponent on terrestrial radio royalties — spent **$11,150,000** in 2023 and **$11,920,000** in 2024 on federal lobbying. The NAB PAC raised **$2,577,537** in the 2023-2024 cycle — approximately **40 times more** than Warner Music Group's PAC in comparable cycles.

The NAB outspends the RIAA by nearly 2:1 on lobbying. And this is on a narrow set of issues — primarily keeping AM/FM radio from having to pay artists for playing their recordings. The NAB's president, Curtis LeGeyt, has argued that a performance royalty bill "disregards the value of radio and would undermine our critical public service to line the pockets of multinational billion-dollar record labels." The framing is strategic: the NAB positions itself as defending small local stations against billion-dollar corporations, even though the broadcast industry's own consolidation (enabled by the Telecommunications Act of 1996, which allowed Clear Channel to grow from 40 to 1,240 stations) created its own set of multinational billion-dollar corporations.

**Big Tech** makes the music industry's lobbying budgets look trivial:

- Alphabet/Google: **$22,000,000** in 2018 (its record year), **$14,860,000** in 2024
- Apple Inc: **$9,360,000** in 2022, **$7,820,000** in 2024, **$10,000,000** in 2025
- Spotify: **$820,000** (2020), **$1,580,000** (2023), **$2,040,000** (2024)

Google alone outspent the RIAA by more than 2:1 in 2024. An important caveat: tech companies lobby on many issues beyond music — antitrust, privacy, tax, AI regulation. Their music-specific lobbying spend cannot be isolated from these totals. But these companies actively participate in CRB proceedings, copyright policy debates, and DMCA reform discussions. Even a fraction of their lobbying capacity, directed at music-related issues, likely matches or exceeds the entire music industry's spend.

### The Spending Asymmetry

Let this sink in. In 2024:

- **RIAA** (the entire recorded music trade group): **$6.9 million**
- **Alphabet/Google** (one company): **$14.9 million**
- **NAB** (radio broadcasters, who oppose paying artists): **$11.9 million**
- **Apple** (one company): **$7.8 million**

The RIAA's $6.9 million is the music industry's *primary* federal lobbying vehicle. It represents labels, not artists. And it is outspent by the broadcasters who refuse to pay artists for radio play, and by the tech companies that argue in CRB proceedings for lower songwriter royalty rates.

### The Artist Representation Gap

Where are the artists in this picture?

**The Artist Rights Alliance** (formerly Music Artists Coalition) was founded in 2019 — not in 1919, not in 1959, but in 2019 — by Irving Azoff and others including Dave Matthews. It was formed specifically because, as described at its launch, "artists don't really have a seat at any table."

**The Future of Music Coalition**, founded in 2000, is a nonprofit focused on ensuring artists' voices are included in policy debates. It files public comments, submits testimony, and documents. Its budget is not remotely comparable to the RIAA or NMPA.

**The Recording Academy** (the Grammy organization) has an advocacy division that lobbies on artist issues. In 2024, it organized 7 State Capitol Advocacy Days — double previous years. But it also represents the broader industry, not artists exclusively.

**The Recording Artists Coalition**, founded in 2000 by Don Henley and Sheryl Crow to counter the RIAA's lobbying, mobilized to repeal the 1999 work-for-hire amendment. Its founding was itself an indictment: if the RIAA represented artist interests, artists would not need a separate coalition to counter the RIAA.

The structural problem is stark. The RIAA represents labels. The NMPA represents publishers. ASCAP and BMI represent songwriters for performance royalties but operate under consent decree constraints. The Recording Academy lobbies on artist issues but represents the industry broadly. The only organizations that exist primarily to represent artists in policy debates are small, underfunded, and decades younger than their industry counterparts.

### The Missing Middle: Independent Labels

The standard framing of music industry power treats the landscape as a three-player game: major labels, tech platforms, individual artists. This framing is convenient. It is also wrong. It omits the independent label sector entirely — and that omission is itself a symptom of the power dynamics it claims to describe.

Independent labels, collectively, control roughly **30-40%** of global recorded music revenue, depending on how the boundaries of "independent" are drawn. These are not bedroom operations. Many have substantial rosters, international distribution deals, decades of catalog, and real payrolls. Globally, **Merlin** — which negotiates digital licensing on behalf of thousands of indie labels and distributors — aggregates this market share into something resembling collective leverage. But resembling is not the same as possessing. Merlin's negotiating power remains a fraction of what any single major commands, and in the arenas that matter most — CRB rate proceedings, DMCA reform debates, consent decree reviews — the independent perspective is present but structurally underweighted relative to the market share it represents.

The reason is positional. Independent labels occupy the worst possible location in the lobbying landscape: too small individually to command a major's leverage, too institutional to generate the sympathy that individual artist stories produce. When the RIAA lobbies, it represents its major label members first. When artist advocacy groups mobilize, they speak for creators, not label owners. The independent label — which is often the entity actually funding recording sessions, advancing marketing costs, and absorbing risk alongside the artist — falls between the cracks of both narratives. Nobody tells their story because their story complicates every other story being told.

The lobbying process itself reinforces this. It favors entities with dedicated government affairs teams and Washington offices. Most independent labels have neither. They have catalog. They have artists. They do not have lobbyists.

There is a deeper point here. The independent sector's experience undermines the comfortable framing that the system's dysfunction is a matter of "artists vs. corporations." The structural failures — opaque royalty accounting, payment delays, algorithmic gatekeeping, metadata breakdowns — affect independent *companies* as much as individual creators. The dividing line is not corporate structure. It is concentrated power, and the legal and regulatory framework that sustains it.

### SOPA/PIPA: The Lobby That Lost

The Stop Online Piracy Act (House) and PROTECT IP Act (Senate) in 2011-2012 represented the music and film industries' most ambitious legislative push in the digital era — and their most visible defeat.

SOPA/PIPA would have given copyright holders and the DOJ tools to combat online piracy by targeting foreign websites, including DNS blocking, search engine de-listing, and payment processor cutoffs. The content industry spent more than **$91 million** on lobbying for approval. 350+ businesses and organizations signed a support letter, including NBCUniversal, Pfizer, Ford, Revlon, the NBA, and Macmillan US.

The opposition was led by Google, eBay, Yahoo, Amazon, and other tech companies. Internet companies hired **246 lobbyists** on SOPA/PIPA issues, compared to **241** by the entertainment industry. Combined lobbying on both sides: **$104.6 million** in Q4 2011 alone.

But the tech companies had a weapon that traditional lobbying could not match: the ability to mobilize their users. The Wikipedia blackout on January 18, 2012, Google's homepage protest, and broader internet-coordinated action generated millions of contacts to Congress. Both bills were shelved in January 2012.

The SOPA/PIPA episode demonstrated something important about power in the digital age: traditional lobbying expenditure can be overwhelmed by platform-mediated user mobilization. The music industry spent more money. Tech won anyway — because tech companies sit between the public and the internet, and can convert user attention into political action at a scale that lobbyists cannot match.

### The Music Modernization Act: The Lobby That Won

If SOPA/PIPA showed what happens when the music industry fights tech, the MMA showed what happens when they cooperate. The MMA passed unanimously in both chambers because the key stakeholders — labels, publishers, PROs, tech companies — were all at the table and all got something they wanted.

In January 2018, RIAA, NMPA, NSAI, ASCAP, the Recording Academy, SESAC, and the Songwriters of North America united behind the Recording Academy's push. Crucially, the digital services — Spotify, Apple, Amazon, Google — also supported the final version. This was unusual. Tech companies ordinarily oppose music industry legislation. The MMA's design gave them something valuable enough to buy their support: a blanket mechanical license that reduced transaction costs and litigation risk, and a liability shield for past infringement.

The unanimous passage looks like bipartisan success. It was also the product of specific compromises that served specific interests — Blackstone's interests in SESAC/HFA, SiriusXM's interest in rate stability, tech companies' interest in legal certainty. These were not compromises made by or for individual songwriters.

### The Revolving Door

The RIAA's lobbyist workforce is, by the numbers, substantially composed of former government employees. Per OpenSecrets revolving door data: 28 out of 35 RIAA lobbyists in 2023, and 24 out of 32 in 2024, had previously held government jobs.

The Mitch Glazier story is the most dramatic example — legislative aide inserts work-for-hire language, then takes a job at the RIAA — but the broader pattern is systemic. Former U.S. Congressman Joe Crowley (D-NY) became a registered lobbyist for the musicFIRST Coalition, an RIAA-backed group. In 2022, the MPA and RIAA together deployed 60+ lobbyists, the majority of whom had prior government employment.

This is not unique to the music industry. The revolving door is a feature of Washington lobbying generally. But the concentration — over 75% of RIAA lobbyists with government backgrounds — reflects a deliberate strategy of hiring people who understand legislative process from the inside and maintain relationships with sitting legislators and staff. That institutional knowledge is worth more than any dollar figure.

## Part 4: Streaming vs. the Law

### Where Practice Diverges from Statute

The streaming era has created a set of industry practices that exist in a gray zone — not clearly illegal, not clearly consistent with the law's intent, but enormously profitable for the entities that exploit them. This section examines the most consequential divergences.

### The Mechanical Licensing Black Box

Before the MMA, streaming services were required to obtain mechanical licenses on a song-by-song basis under Section 115 of the Copyright Act. When they could not identify a song's copyright owner, they were supposed to file a Notice of Intent with the Copyright Office and pay royalties into escrow.

In practice, services filed over one million "address unknown" NOIs with the Copyright Office, which released them from the obligation to pay. Songs were streamed billions of times without compensating the people who wrote them. This was not a marginal gap — it was the system operating as designed, if "designed" means "shaped by the relative resources of the parties involved." Streaming platforms had armies of lawyers. Independent songwriters did not.

The lawsuits that followed quantified the damage. **Ferrick v. Spotify** (S.D.N.Y.) — a class action covering compositions available on Spotify between December 2012 and June 2017 — settled for **$112.55 million**, including $43.55 million in immediate cash payments. **Wixen Music Publishing v. Spotify**, filed in December 2017, sought **$1.6 billion** (maximum statutory damages of $150,000 per composition for approximately 10,784 works). It settled in 2018 for terms "well short of $1.6 billion."

These settlements, while large in absolute terms, represent a fraction of the royalties that should have been paid during the years when streaming services operated without proper licenses. And they came years after the fact, after prolonged litigation, and after millions of streams had already occurred without compensation.

The MMA's creation of the MLC was supposed to fix this. Under the new system, 20 digital music providers transferred a combined **$424.4 million** in historical unmatched royalties to the MLC (subsequently revised to approximately $397 million). By early 2024, the MLC had matched and distributed more than $25 million in historical royalties from the earliest rate periods.

But hundreds of millions in unmatched royalties remain. And the MMA's market-share distribution mechanism — where unclaimed royalties after three years go to publishers based on market share — creates a structural incentive for major publishers (who have the best metadata systems and therefore the lowest unmatched rates) to benefit from the very problem they are best positioned to solve.

The U.S. Copyright Office has recommended that the initial market-share distribution not occur for at least five years, and that the five-year period should not start until the MLC's claim portal is fully functioning. This is a recognition that rushing distribution would transfer money from unknown rightful owners to known major publishers — but it also means that hundreds of millions of dollars sit in limbo while the system catches up.

### Safe Harbor Abuse and the Value Gap

Report 2 examined YouTube's economics. Here we examine the legal framework that makes those economics possible.

YouTube's business model depends on DMCA Section 512 safe harbor. Because it classifies as a user-upload platform rather than an interactive streaming service, YouTube can use the safe harbor to negotiate lower rates than it would pay under direct licenses. The result: YouTube pays approximately $0.00069 per stream versus Apple Music's approximately $0.01 — roughly one-fourteenth the rate.

YouTube's Content ID system — its proprietary content identification technology — is voluntary. It is not required by the DMCA. Google only approves Content ID for accounts with a "substantial body of original material that's frequently uploaded by the YouTube user community" — currently approximately 9,000 accounts. Google says 98% of copyright issues are caught via Content ID. But Content ID matches do not trigger copyright strikes under YouTube's repeat infringer policy. This means that virtually all copyright-infringing material identified through Content ID is "entirely insulated" from the three-strikes termination policy required by the DMCA.

The legal framework creates a perverse incentive structure. The DMCA's notice-and-takedown system requires rights holders to police their own copyrights — to find infringing uploads, file takedown notices, and repeat the process when the same content is re-uploaded. "For each infringing link or file taken down, a dozen more pop up in its place," as Don Henley testified before the Senate Judiciary Subcommittee. The game of whack-a-mole is the point: it is expensive for rights holders and cheap for platforms, which shifts the cost of copyright enforcement from the entity profiting from the content to the entity that created it.

The *BMG Rights Management v. Cox Communications* case (4th Cir. 2018) established that ISPs can lose safe harbor protection if they fail to reasonably implement their repeat infringer policies. Internal Cox emails showed employees determined they would terminate infringing accounts but did not do so because of the revenue those accounts generated. Cox had "blacklisted" a rights enforcement company in 2011, auto-deleting millions of infringement complaints. The court held that "at a minimum, an ISP has not 'reasonably implemented' a repeat infringer policy if the ISP fails to enforce the terms of its policy in any meaningful fashion."

The subsequent *Sony Music v. Cox* case (4th Cir. 2024) vacated an unprecedented $1 billion judgment against Cox, reversing the vicarious liability verdict while preserving the contributory infringement framework. The legal boundaries of safe harbor continue to be litigated, case by case, at enormous expense.

### Discovery Mode: Modern Payola?

Spotify's Discovery Mode, introduced in 2020, allows artists or their labels to flag tracks for algorithmic priority in exchange for accepting a **30% royalty reduction** on streams generated by the promotion. Report 2 examined Discovery Mode's economics. Here we examine its legal dimensions.

In 2021, the House Judiciary Committee requested information on Discovery Mode over its similarities to **payola** — the practice of secretly paying for radio airplay, prohibited under 47 U.S.C. Section 508 since 1960.

The parallels are structural, not merely superficial. Under payola law, broadcasters must disclose payments received for airing specific content. Discovery Mode involves an undisclosed financial arrangement — an artist accepts lower compensation in exchange for algorithmic promotion — but presents recommendations to users as if they are algorithmically neutral, organic suggestions. The listener does not know that the track they are hearing was promoted because the artist accepted reduced compensation.

In November 2025, subscriber Genevieve Capolongo filed a class action lawsuit against Spotify (S.D.N.Y.), alleging Discovery Mode constitutes "modern payola" — selling visibility through undisclosed promotional deals while presenting recommendations as algorithmically neutral. The suit seeks restitution, punitive damages, and a court order requiring disclosure of the financial arrangements behind Discovery Mode recommendations. Spotify called the lawsuit "nonsense."

Under the FTC's Endorsement Guides, accepting a royalty discount in exchange for promotion arguably constitutes a material connection that must be disclosed to consumers. Failure to disclose could transform Discovery Mode from a personalization feature into a deceptive advertisement. No FTC enforcement action has been initiated.

The legal question is unresolved. But the functional reality is clear: Spotify has created a system where artists pay for algorithmic visibility by accepting lower royalties — and listeners are not told which recommendations are organic and which are paid.

### Spotify's Audiobook Bundling and the Rate Arbitrage

In March 2024, Spotify reclassified its Premium subscription tiers as "bundles" after adding 15 hours of monthly audiobook access. The reclassification was not primarily about audiobooks. It was about mechanical royalty rates.

Under the Phonorecords IV settlement, bundled multimedia services may pay lower mechanical royalty rates than standalone music subscriptions. By adding audiobooks and calling the result a "bundle," Spotify reduced the revenue base on which it owes mechanical royalties to songwriters. Spotify values music at 52% of the bundle — approximately $5.70 per subscriber. The mechanical royalty rate effectively dropped from the headline rate to approximately 24.5% of Total Content Cost for the relevant accounting period `[single-source on specific 24.5% figure]`.

The NMPA projected a loss of approximately **$150 million** in mechanical royalties in the first year, with cumulative losses projected at over **$3.1 billion** through 2032.

The MLC filed suit challenging the bundling classification. In January 2025, Judge Analisa Torres (S.D.N.Y.) ruled that Spotify could legally classify its Premium service as a bundle, finding that audiobook streaming constitutes a distinct product with more than "token value." The case was dismissed with prejudice.

This is legal. It is also exactly the kind of move that reveals whose interests the legal framework serves. The CRB sets mechanical royalty rates through a process that is "costly and long," as the Recording Academy has described it — years of adversarial proceedings in which songwriters are pitted against "some of the biggest companies in world history." Songwriters win a 44% rate increase after years of litigation (Phonorecords III). And then a platform finds a mechanism to reduce its effective rate by reclassifying its product. The letter of the law is satisfied. The intent — fairly compensating songwriters — is circumvented.

### The CRB Rate Fight: Labels Against Their Own Publishers

The Phonorecords III proceedings produced one of the music industry's most revealing contradictions.

In 2018, the CRB ruled to increase mechanical streaming royalty rates from 10.5% to 15.1% over five years — a nearly 44% increase, hailed as a "monumental victory" for songwriters. In 2019, **Spotify, Amazon, Google, and Pandora** appealed the rate increase. Apple Music notably did not join the appeal.

Here is where it gets structurally interesting. The three major label groups — Universal, Sony, and Warner — also operate the world's three largest music publishing operations. They benefit enormously from streaming's growth on the recording side. But the streaming services they partner with on recordings — Spotify, Amazon, Google — were simultaneously fighting to suppress the mechanical (publishing) rates that the labels' own publishing subsidiaries would have received.

The major labels did not join the appeal. But they also did not publicly oppose it — even though a lower mechanical rate would directly reduce revenue to their own publishing divisions. The labels' primary relationship with streaming services is on the recording side, where they negotiate freely and receive approximately 50-52% of total streaming revenue. The publishing side — subject to CRB rate-setting — generates much less per stream. The labels' silence on the appeal was a calculation: the value of maintaining good relationships with streaming platforms (for lucrative recording deals) outweighed the value of defending higher publishing rates.

The CRB reaffirmed the 15.1% headline rate in July 2022 after remand. The final determination was not published in the Federal Register until August 10, 2023 (88 FR 54336). Songwriters waited years for a rate increase they had already won, while their own publishers' parent companies sat on their hands.

### Pro-Rata vs. User-Centric: A Legal Non-Issue That Should Be

As documented in Report 2, all major streaming services use the pro-rata ("big pool") payment model, where each track's share of total streams determines its share of royalties. A subscriber's individual payment does not go to the artists they listen to.

There is no legal requirement to use pro-rata. There is no statute or regulation that mandates one payment model over another. The choice is a business decision by the platforms, and it happens to benefit the most-streamed artists (primarily major-label roster artists) at the expense of mid-tier and niche creators.

Research on alternatives has been conducted. A French study by the Centre National de la Musique found that user-centric payment would increase classical music revenues by an estimated 24% while reducing rap/hip-hop revenues by 21%. Academic research from a study using 150,000+ users and 890 million streams on a French platform found mixed results — suggesting that pro-rata may actually outperform user-centric in efficiency under certain conditions. The UK has discussed an "Artist Growth Model" that would redistribute a share of top earners' revenue to mid-tier artists.

Deezer has adopted a modified user-centric system ("artist-centric"). The results will be watched. But no regulator has mandated a change, no statute addresses the question, and the platforms that benefit from the status quo — and the labels that benefit from their artists' dominant stream share — have no incentive to change.

### AI Streaming Fraud: The New Frontier

*United States v. Michael Smith* (S.D.N.Y., 2024) was the first criminal prosecution of AI-generated streaming fraud in the U.S. Smith, 52, of Cornelius, North Carolina, used AI to generate thousands of fake songs — titles like "Zygotic Washstands" by fake artists like "Callous Post" — and bots to stream them millions of times. The scheme ran from 2017 to 2024 and generated over **$10 million** in fraudulent royalties. Charges include wire fraud conspiracy, wire fraud, and money laundering conspiracy, each carrying up to 20 years.

The case illustrates a vulnerability inherent in the pro-rata streaming model: if your payout is determined by your share of total streams, and if the system cannot distinguish between legitimate and fraudulent streams, then fraud directly dilutes the revenue available to legitimate artists. Every fraudulent stream reduces the per-stream rate for everyone else.

In April 2024, Spotify introduced a minimum threshold of 1,000 streams in the previous 12 months for tracks to be eligible for royalty payments. Streams from sub-threshold tracks are redirected to the general payout pool. Spotify projected this would move $40 million from sub-threshold tracks to qualifying tracks. The policy is explicitly anti-fraud — but it also disproportionately affects small independent artists with legitimate but modest audiences.

## Part 5: The PRO Problem and the Case for a Marketplace

### How PROs Actually Work

The performing rights organization system is one of the music industry's oldest institutional structures — and one of its most poorly understood, even by the songwriters it ostensibly serves. Understanding how PROs work, and why they work the way they do, is essential to understanding the broader dysfunction in music rights.

**ASCAP** (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers) was founded in 1914 and operates as a not-for-profit membership association. It is governed by a board of 12 writer members and 12 publisher members, elected by the membership. ASCAP surpassed 1 million members in 2024 and 1.1 million in 2025. Its total revenue reached $1.945 billion in 2025, with $1.759 billion available for royalty distribution and a 10-year compound annual growth rate of 6.7%.

**BMI** (Broadcast Music, Inc.) was founded in 1939, initially as a competitor to ASCAP that championed genres ASCAP underrepresented — country, R&B, jazz. In a development that would have surprised its founders, BMI was acquired by **New Mountain Capital** in February 2024, making it the **only for-profit PRO** in the US with an open-door membership policy. A Google/Alphabet fund acquired a minority stake in the transaction. BMI affiliates received a one-time $100 million payout in connection with the sale — but the for-profit model has consequences: BMI raised its retained margin from approximately 10% to 15% of collections. BMI's last reported revenue was $1.573 billion in FY2022, but the company has ceased publishing detailed revenue figures since going for-profit.

**SESAC** was founded in 1930 and acquired by **Blackstone** in 2017 for approximately $1.125 billion. Unlike ASCAP and BMI, SESAC is a private, invitation-only organization. It operates outside the consent decrees — meaning it negotiates rates in a free market.

**Global Music Rights (GMR)** was founded in 2013 by Irving Azoff. Like SESAC, it is a private, for-profit company that operates outside the consent decrees. GMR represents a small but high-profile roster and does not publicly disclose its revenue or rate terms.

**SoundExchange** is a nonprofit that collects and distributes digital performance royalties for sound recordings — from satellite radio, internet radio, cable TV music channels. It distributed $1.052 billion in 2024.

**The Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC)**, created by the MMA, has distributed more than $3.3 billion cumulatively since its operations began in 2021.

### The Consent Decree Constraint

ASCAP and BMI have operated under DOJ antitrust consent decrees since **1941**. The decrees were originally entered because the DOJ determined that blanket licensing — conditioning the license for one song on the purchase of a blanket covering millions — constituted illegal tying and price-fixing under the Sherman Antitrust Act.

The decrees impose fundamental constraints:

1. ASCAP and BMI may obtain only **nonexclusive** rights (songwriters retain the right to license individually)
2. Must grant a license to **any applicant** on request
3. Must accept as a member any songwriter who meets minimum requirements
4. If they cannot agree on a rate with a licensee, either party may petition a federal **"rate court"** judge to set a reasonable rate

The rate courts are presided over by two SDNY judges — Judge Denise Cote for ASCAP and Judge Louis Stanton for BMI — who have wielded enormous influence over music licensing rates. Their interpretations have not always aligned: Judge Stanton ruled that the BMI consent decree neither bars fractional licensing nor requires full-works licensing, while Judge Cote's rulings on ASCAP have at times created different standards.

The DOJ has reviewed the consent decrees three times — in 2014-2016, and again in 2019-2021 — and each time declined to modify or terminate them. ASCAP and BMI have argued the decrees are "outdated relics from the 1940s" that hamper fair compensation in the digital age. The NMPA called the DOJ's inaction "a massive missed opportunity." BMI has publicly stated that "a free market with less government regulation is the best way to ensure music creators can be fairly rewarded."

The consent decrees create a structural reality that is the single most important fact about music rights pricing in the United States: **record labels negotiate freely with streaming platforms for master recording licenses, while composition rates are constrained by government-set rates and consent decree oversight.** The same streaming service pays whatever the market will bear for recordings and whatever the CRB or rate court dictates for compositions. This asymmetry is why songwriters earn a fraction of what their recordings generate — and why the companies that own both recordings and compositions (Universal, Sony, Warner) have a structural incentive to let the composition side be undervalued while maximizing the recording side.

### The Negotiation Asymmetry in Detail

This is worth dwelling on because it is the foundational distortion.

When Spotify negotiates with Universal Music Group for the right to stream UMG's recordings, the negotiation is between two private parties in a free market. There is no consent decree, no rate court, no CRB proceeding. UMG can refuse to license. It can demand whatever terms it can negotiate. The resulting deals are confidential — covered by NDAs — but labels collectively receive approximately 50-52% of Spotify's total revenue. This is market-rate pricing, determined by supply and demand and relative bargaining power.

When the same songs' *compositions* are licensed, the process is entirely different. Performance royalties flow through PROs (ASCAP, BMI) that are constrained by consent decrees. Mechanical royalties are set by the CRB in Phonorecords proceedings — multi-year, adversarial, expensive legal battles. The headline mechanical rate for interactive streaming has been set at 15.1-15.35% of service revenue (Phonorecords III and IV).

The composition side receives roughly 15-20% of total streaming revenue. The recording side receives 50-52%. The same creative work — a song written and recorded by the same person — generates fundamentally different compensation depending on which right is being monetized, because the legal frameworks governing those rights are fundamentally different.

Publishers attempted to address this. In the early 2010s, some large publishers withdrew their catalogs' digital rights from ASCAP and BMI to negotiate directly with streaming services, believing they could get better rates in the free market. The Second Circuit determined that the consent decrees prohibit selective (partial) withdrawal — publishers must either use the PRO for all mediums or withdraw entirely. The NMPA petitioned the DOJ to amend the consent decrees to allow partial withdrawal. The DOJ declined in both the 2016 and 2021 reviews.

The result: publishers are locked into an all-or-nothing choice. They cannot negotiate digital rates freely while keeping their PRO affiliation for other uses. The constraint is structural, and it benefits the licensees — streaming platforms, broadcasters, venues — who get predictable, government-constrained rates on composition rights while paying market rates on recording rights.

### The $2.5 Billion Black Box

The "black box" — unmatched royalties that cannot be attributed to specific rights holders — has been estimated at **$2.5 billion globally** `[single-source, Digital Music News, 2017]`. The causes are systemic: poor or missing metadata, incomplete databases, fragmented rights ownership, failure to file proper Notices of Intent.

The MLC has partially addressed the domestic portion: 20 DSPs transferred $424.4 million in historical unmatched royalties, and the MLC has matched and distributed over $225 million (57%+) of this amount. But the black box is not merely a historical problem. It is a structural feature of a system that requires accurate metadata to function and has no reliable mechanism to ensure that metadata is accurate.

Modern songs frequently have **10+ co-writers**, each potentially represented by different publishers affiliated with different PROs. A single song can have ownership shares split across ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, and GMR simultaneously. Each share must be independently tracked, licensed, and paid. Even small mismatches in metadata — "Jon Smith" vs. "Jonathan D. Smith" — can block royalty payouts indefinitely. One study found **46 million examples** of "author unknown" songs being streamed `[single-source]`. A 2025 PRO report highlighted that up to **15% of indie music submissions** contained metadata mismatches that delayed or blocked royalty disbursement `[single-source]`.

The structural unfairness is this: when black box royalties are eventually distributed — historically based on market share — major publishers receive the largest share. But major publishers are the entities with the best metadata systems and the lowest unmatched rates. The unmatched royalties disproportionately belong to independent songwriters and smaller publishers who lack the infrastructure to ensure their songs are properly registered and matched. The system takes money from those who can least afford to lose it and gives it to those who least need it.

### Payment Delays

The contrast with other financial systems is striking.

- Stock trades settle in T+1 (one business day)
- Credit card transactions settle in 1-3 business days
- Streaming mechanical royalties: **2-3 months** after streams occur
- Performance royalties through PROs: paid **quarterly or semi-annually** (3-6 months or longer)
- International royalties: **12-18 months** due to reciprocal agreement processing times

Each intermediary in the royalty chain — DSP, distributor, PRO, sub-publisher — has its own reporting and reconciliation cycle. The delays cascade. A songwriter who writes a hit that streams millions of times in January may not see performance royalty payments until July or later. International royalties from that same song may not arrive for over a year.

These are not technology limitations. The technology to process and settle financial transactions in real time has existed for years. The delays persist because the system was designed by and for intermediaries whose business models depend on holding money during the settlement period. Float is free capital. The question is whose capital — and the answer is the creators'.

### The Case for a Liquid Marketplace

Everything documented above — the consent decree constraints, the negotiation asymmetry, the black box, the payment delays, the metadata failures, the information asymmetry — points toward a single structural diagnosis: music rights lack a functioning market.

Consider what a market requires: transparent pricing, willing buyers and sellers with access to the same information, efficient settlement, standardized units of trade, and mechanisms for price discovery. The current system has none of these.

**Sync licensing** is the exception that proves the rule. Sync — licensing music for use in film, TV, advertising, video games — operates closer to a true marketplace than any other music rights transaction. There is no compulsory license for sync rights. Rights holders can refuse or set any price. Music supervisors function like brokers, matching buyers (productions) and sellers (rights holders). Prices are freely negotiated based on supply and demand. The range — $500 for a small indie film to $500,000+ for a major feature film or global advertising campaign — reflects genuine market value. US average commercial sync license: $15,000-$50,000 (2024) `[single-source, Billboard]`.

Sync works because it lacks the regulatory constraints that govern performance and mechanical rights. No consent decree. No CRB. No rate court. Just willing counterparties discovering a price. And it generates rates that dwarf what the same songs earn through constrained licensing — because in a free market, music is worth more than the regulated rates suggest.

### What a Marketplace Could Look Like

A liquid marketplace for music rights would have several core features:

**Direct counterparty discovery**: Rights holders and licensees find each other without mandatory intermediaries. A songwriter should be able to list rights for licensing or sale as easily as listing a stock. A production company seeking sync rights should be able to browse available music, see pricing, and execute a transaction — without routing through layers of publishers, PROs, and administrators.

**Transparent pricing**: Rates visible to all participants, enabling genuine price discovery. The current system's opacity — where every deal is covered by NDAs — benefits sophisticated parties with superior information. Transparency would democratize access to pricing data.

**Real-time settlement**: Royalties flow from platform to rights holder in days, not months. Smart contracts — self-executing agreements on blockchain infrastructure — could enable near-instant settlement upon verified play events. The technology exists. What does not exist is the incumbent willingness to adopt it.

**Standardized rights units**: Fractional ownership stakes in compositions and recordings, tradeable like securities. A songwriter who owns 25% of a composition should be able to sell or license that stake as a standardized unit with clear terms and transparent pricing.

**Automated matching**: Play data matched to rights holders in real time, using verified ownership records. This eliminates the black box — if ownership is recorded on a transparent, auditable ledger, the "we don't know who to pay" problem disappears.

**Global scope**: A single marketplace serving all territories, eliminating cross-border collection losses. The current system of reciprocal agreements between national collecting societies involves multiple intermediary deductions, opaque processing, and royalties that "never find their way home."

### Existing Experiments

This is not purely hypothetical. Several experiments have tested elements of a marketplace model.

**Royalty Exchange** is an online auction platform for buying and selling royalty assets. It has completed over **2,000 transactions** totaling more than **$170 million** in volume. Most transactions are under $1 million each, with 30,000 registered investors. It demonstrates that price discovery is possible for music rights at scale — though liquidity remains limited.

**Royal.io**, founded by 3LAU and backed by Founders Fund, allowed artists to tokenize royalty streams and sell fractional ownership to fans. Nas sold royalty tokens. But Royal.io **scaled back investment offerings in 2024** amid regulatory uncertainty about whether tokenized rights constitute securities under US law. It is sunsetting its marketplace, continuing token trades through OpenSea. The lesson: regulatory classification of tokenized music rights remains unresolved, and the SEC's framework is not designed for this asset class.

**Audius**, a decentralized music streaming platform founded in 2018, claims 6+ million monthly users and 100,000+ artists, with artists retaining 90% of revenue. But it has not achieved mainstream adoption — limited mainstream catalog, crypto price volatility, user experience issues, and governance challenges inherent to decentralized decision-making have constrained growth.

**ANote Music**, a European platform, has operated a secondary market for music royalties since 2020 with the goal of becoming "the stock exchange for music royalties."

### The Hipgnosis Episode: Validating the Asset Class

Hipgnosis Songs Fund, founded by Merck Mercuriadis and publicly listed on the London Stock Exchange in 2018, tested the thesis that song catalogs are undervalued assets with predictable, growing cash flows — comparable to bonds or infrastructure assets. Hipgnosis aggressively acquired catalogs, spending approximately **$2.206 billion** across its portfolio.

What happened next was messy. Shareholder revolt over business plans and a planned catalog sell-off. A third-party review that accused management of overstating revenue (disputed by the company). And ultimately, **Blackstone acquired Hipgnosis Songs Fund for $1.6 billion** in July 2024. An independent valuation firm subsequently valued the portfolio at **$2.36 billion** — approximately $150 million more than both the acquisition price and the total purchase cost. Blackstone, in other words, bought the portfolio for less than it was worth — a transaction enabled by the governance failures and limited liquidity that characterize the current system.

The Hipgnosis experience validated the thesis — music rights are investable assets with quantifiable value — while simultaneously demonstrating the risk of operating in a market without adequate liquidity and transparency. A functioning marketplace with competitive bidding might have produced a very different outcome.

### The Private Equity Flood

Total capital deployed into music rights since 2018 exceeds **$30 billion** `[single-source, McKinsey]`. The major players:

- **Blackstone**: Acquired SESAC ($1.125 billion, 2017), acquired Hipgnosis Songs Fund ($1.6 billion, 2024)
- **New Mountain Capital**: Acquired BMI majority stake (2024)
- **KKR**: Partnered with BMG for catalog acquisitions; acquired Kobalt's KMR Music Royalties II portfolio ($1.1 billion, 62,000+ copyrights including The Weeknd, Lorde, Paul McCartney); $500 million debt financing to HarbourView (2024)
- **Primary Wave**: Acquired Britney Spears' entire catalog (~$200 million, December 2025)
- **Round Hill Music**: 100+ catalog deals worth $1 billion+; $200 million+ across 40 acquisitions in 2022 alone

The PE flood validates music rights as an asset class. But it also concentrates ownership further. These firms are sophisticated buyers operating in an opaque market where information asymmetry favors well-resourced participants. A transparent marketplace would democratize access — both for smaller investors and for the creators selling their rights — and potentially improve pricing by introducing competitive bidding where currently there are private negotiations.

### Why Incumbents Resist Transparency

The entities that benefit most from the current system have the least incentive to change it.

**Information asymmetry is a competitive advantage.** Major labels and publishers possess superior data on catalog valuations, streaming trends, and rights ownership. This informational edge allows them to acquire catalogs at favorable prices from less-informed sellers. A transparent marketplace with public pricing would erode this advantage.

**Intermediary fees depend on intermediation.** Major publishers serve as essential administrators of rights — a role that a direct marketplace would partially displace. PROs' blanket licensing model provides a reliable revenue base. Labels' negotiating leverage with streaming services depends partly on the opacity of deal terms.

**Black box distributions by market share benefit majors.** When unmatched royalties are distributed proportionally to known market share, the major publishers — who already have the most identified works — receive the largest share of money that likely belongs to independents.

The resistance is not irrational. From the incumbents' perspective, the current system works well — for them. The question is whether a system designed to serve intermediaries can be reformed to serve creators, or whether the creators need an alternative system entirely.

### What a Marketplace Would Solve

**The black box**: Real-time matching of plays to rights holders using verified ownership records eliminates the "we don't know who to pay" problem. Transparent, auditable chains from play event to payment. No market-share fallback that disadvantages independents.

**Payment delays**: Smart contracts enabling near-instant settlement upon verified play. Eliminate cascading delays through multiple intermediaries. Target: payment within days, not months.

**Price discovery**: Open bidding for rights reveals true market value — comparable to how stock exchanges replaced opaque over-the-counter markets. The fundamental question — what are specific music rights actually worth when freely traded? — has never been answered because the infrastructure to answer it has never existed.

**International collection**: A single global platform eliminates the need for reciprocal agreements between national collecting societies. No fee erosion from multiple intermediary deductions. Consistent rules and transparent processes across territories.

### What Could Go Wrong

A marketplace is not a panacea, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the risks.

**Fragmentation**: A marketplace with thousands of individual rights holders could make blanket licensing more difficult for small businesses — the restaurant or bar that currently obtains a single ASCAP license might face more complex licensing requirements.

**Power imbalances**: Sophisticated institutional investors (the KKRs and Blackstones of the world) could exploit information advantages in a marketplace just as they do in private transactions — unless the marketplace is designed with transparency mechanisms that genuinely level the playing field.

**Regulatory uncertainty**: Royal.io's experience demonstrates that tokenized music rights may be classified as securities, triggering costly compliance requirements. The regulatory framework is not ready.

**Loss of collective bargaining**: Individual creators negotiating alone may get worse terms than they would through collective representation. There is a reason unions exist — collective action addresses power asymmetry. A marketplace that atomizes creators could make them more vulnerable, not less.

**Technology barriers**: Not all rights holders have the technical sophistication to participate in a digital marketplace. Any system that requires blockchain literacy or crypto wallets risks excluding the very creators it is supposed to empower.

**Transition costs**: Migrating from the current system to a marketplace would be enormously complex and disruptive. Incumbents have legitimate operational concerns about transition, even if they also have self-interested reasons for resisting change.

The path forward likely involves hybrid models — marketplaces that operate alongside reformed versions of existing institutions, rather than replacing them overnight. Sync licensing works as a market. Royalty Exchange demonstrates that secondary trading of royalty assets can function. The technology for real-time settlement exists. What is missing is the political will to assemble these pieces into a coherent alternative — and the political will is missing because the entities with the most lobbying power are the ones that benefit most from the status quo.

## Part 6: What Changed and What Didn't

### Recent Developments in Context

The legal and regulatory landscape is not static. Several significant developments since 2023 warrant examination — not because they fundamentally alter the structures documented in this report, but because they illustrate the ongoing negotiation between the interests that shape those structures.

### AI Copyright: The Next Frontier

AI has created the first genuinely new copyright question since the rise of digital distribution. The core issues:

**Can AI-generated works be copyrighted?** The U.S. Copyright Office's Part 2 report on copyrightability (January 29, 2025) affirmed that copyright does not extend to purely AI-generated material. Prompts alone provide insufficient human "control" for authorship. Three types of qualifying human contribution were identified: human-provided prompts (insufficient alone), human-created expressive inputs perceivable in the output, and human modifications or arrangements of AI output. AI-generated work can be registered "when and if it embodies meaningful human authorship." The U.S. Court of Appeals reinforced this in a March 21, 2025 ruling denying copyright protection for purely AI-generated works.

**Can AI be trained on copyrighted music?** Sony, UMG, and WMG filed lawsuits against AI music generators Suno and Udio in 2024 for alleged unauthorized use of copyrighted recordings in training data. The **TRAIN Act** (introduced 2024) would require transparency from AI companies about whether copyrighted works were used in training data. These cases will define whether the "fair use" defense extends to AI training — a question with billions of dollars at stake.

**Can AI replicate an artist's voice or likeness?** This is the most immediately personal of the AI questions. The Copyright Office's Part 1 report (July 31, 2024) addressed AI digital replicas of performers. The legislative response has been swift — primarily at the state level.

### The ELVIS Act and State AI Laws

Tennessee became the first state to modernize right-of-publicity laws for AI when Governor Bill Lee signed the **Ensuring Likeness Voice and Image Security (ELVIS) Act** on March 21, 2024, effective July 1, 2024. The Act adds "voice" to existing publicity-rights protections and prohibits AI voice cloning of artists without consent. It passed unanimously — 93-0 in the House, 30-0 in the Senate. The music industry, centered in Nashville, was the key driver.

Other states followed:

| State | Law | Key Provisions |
|-------|-----|----------------|
| California | AB 2602 | Voids contract clauses granting broad "digital replica" rights without detailed terms, legal counsel, or union oversight |
| California | AB 1836 | Extends publicity rights to deceased personalities; damages up to $10,000/violation |
| California | SB 942 (AI Transparency Act) | Requires AI systems with 1M+ monthly visitors to disclose AI-generated content |
| New York | Digital Replica Law | Invalidates exploitative contracts for replicas replacing personal services; new civil remedies (January 2025) |
| Illinois | HB 4762 | Unenforces vague digital replica clauses in performer agreements (August 2024) |
| Arkansas | HB 1071 | Amends Publicity Rights Protection Act to include AI-generated images and voice |
| Michigan | HB 4047 / HB 4048 | Criminalizes nonconsensual intimate AI deepfakes (August 2025) |

The state-level response has been faster than federal action — a pattern consistent with the broader observation that concentrated industry interests (Nashville's music community, Hollywood's entertainment industry) can move state legislatures more quickly than the federal lobbying machinery can move Congress.

### The NO FAKES Act

The federal answer — the **Nurture Originals, Foster Art and Keep Entertainment Safe (NO FAKES) Act** — was reintroduced in April 2025 (H.R. 2794, 119th Congress). It grants individuals the exclusive right to authorize digital replicas, creates civil liability for unauthorized use, and includes safe harbors for platforms acting in good faith. Over 400 artists signed a letter of support, including 21 Savage, Missy Elliott, Deadmau5, and Scarlett Johansson. The Recording Academy, RIAA, and all three major labels support it.

Opposition comes from the EFF, Center for Democracy and Technology, ALA, and CCIA, arguing the bill's DMCA-style notice-and-takedown provisions risk incentivizing removal of lawful speech.

The NO FAKES Act has not been enacted as of March 2026. Whether it passes will depend on the same factors that determine all music policy outcomes: the relative lobbying resources deployed by supporters and opponents, and whether a sufficient constituency of affected creators can generate political pressure beyond what their institutional advocates can produce.

### #BrokenRecord and the Unrecouped Balance Clearing

The most tangible artist-advocacy victory of the past five years was not legislative. It was the **#BrokenRecord campaign**, founded in April 2020 by Tom Gray of the band Gomez and PRS board member.

The campaign's goals — UK government regulation of the music industry, improved creator rights, and a shift from pro-rata to user-centric payment — led to the most thorough governmental examination of streaming economics ever conducted. The UK Digital, Culture, Media and Sport Committee received evidence from 300+ artists, heard oral testimony from Nile Rodgers, Ed O'Brien, and Guy Garvey, and concluded that the streaming market needed a "complete reset."

The campaign's pressure — amplified by an open letter to Boris Johnson co-signed by Paul McCartney, Kate Bush, The Rolling Stones, Chris Martin, and 200+ others — produced a concrete result: all three major labels announced programs to clear legacy unrecouped balances.

- **Sony Music** (June 2021): Cleared historic unrecouped debts for legacy artists, effective January 1, 2021.
- **Warner Music Group** (February 2022): Launched a program for artists signed before 2000 who did not receive an advance after 2000, effective July 1, 2022. Warner reported 4,500 artists benefiting.
- **Universal Music Group** (April 2022): Cleared unrecouped balances for eligible creators who received no payments since January 1, 2000, effective January 1, 2022.

These were meaningful changes. Artists who had been trapped in permanent recoupment — never earning a royalty check despite generating revenue for decades — could finally receive streaming income. The details of eligibility varied by label, and the programs were unilateral corporate decisions, not legal requirements. They could theoretically be reversed.

But the mechanism matters as much as the outcome. It was not a lawsuit, a statute, or a regulatory action that cleared unrecouped balances. It was a UK parliamentary inquiry triggered by a grassroots campaign, combined with public pressure from some of the most famous names in music. The labels responded because the political and reputational cost of not responding became higher than the financial cost of clearing the balances. This is a different kind of power — not lobbying power, not legal power, but the power of public attention.

### UMAW and Justice at Spotify

The **Union of Musicians and Allied Workers (UMAW)** launched its "Justice at Spotify" campaign in October 2020, demanding one cent per stream, a more transparent business model, user-centric streaming payments, and appropriate crediting of all contributors. Over 30,000 musicians, workers, and fans signed the petition. Protests occurred at Spotify offices in 31 cities in March 2021.

The outcomes were partial. Spotify launched its "Loud and Clear" transparency initiative. Apple Music publicly claimed to pay one cent per stream, matching UMAW's primary demand (at least nominally). But UMAW itself acknowledged that "the exploitation of musicians and allied workers unfortunately remains unchanged."

### Taylor Swift vs. Apple Music (2015)

One artist, with sufficient fame and leverage, accomplished in one Tumblr post what years of advocacy could not.

On June 21, 2015, Taylor Swift posted an open letter titled "To Apple, Love Taylor," refusing to allow *1989* on Apple Music because of Apple's plan not to pay royalties during Apple Music's three-month free trial. "Three months is a long time to go unpaid, and it is unfair to ask anyone to work for nothing... We don't ask you for free iPhones. Please don't ask us to provide you with our music for no compensation."

Within hours, Apple executive Eddy Cue reversed the policy.

The episode is frequently cited as evidence that artists can win. It is more accurately evidence that *one of the most commercially powerful artists in history* can win. Swift's leverage was unique — she was arguably the only artist in the world who could credibly threaten to withhold a #1 album from a major platform and have the threat matter commercially. For the vast majority of artists, whose individual absence from a platform would go unnoticed, the Swift precedent offers inspiration but not a replicable strategy.

Swift's subsequent dispute with Scooter Braun and Big Machine Records — Braun's $330 million purchase of her masters, Swift's re-recording campaign (four albums topping the Billboard 200 as "Taylor's Version"), and her eventual acquisition of her masters from Shamrock Capital for a reported sum close to $360 million in 2025 — demonstrated both the financial value of master ownership and the extraordinary resources required to fight for it.

### SAG-AFTRA: Collective Bargaining Works

The SAG-AFTRA Sound Recordings Agreement ratified in 2024 offers a counterpoint to the atomized world of individual artist contracts:

- **Wage increases**: 14.5% upon ratification, plus 3.75% (Jan 2025), 3.25% (Jan 2026), and 3% (Dec 2026) — compounding to **26.3% total** over the contract.
- **Health/Retirement contributions**: Streaming contribution increases from 50% to 75% (Jan 2025) and 75% to 100% (Dec 2026).
- **AI Protections**: Clear consent required with minimum compensation and specific details of intended use before releasing any sound recording using a digital replication of an artist's voice.

The SAG-AFTRA agreement covers session singers, royalty artists, announcers, actors, comedians, narrators, and sound effects artists. It demonstrates that collective bargaining can achieve outcomes — particularly on AI protections — that individual artists cannot negotiate alone.

### What the Listener Never Sees

Three reports. Hundreds of pages. And nearly all of it has been about the supply side — who creates music, who owns the rights, who collects the royalties, who writes the rules. The listener has been almost entirely absent from the analysis. This is not an oversight. The listener is absent from the power structure too. But the power structure shapes what the listener hears, and that consequence deserves examination.

Start with the pro-rata model. The majority of streaming subscription revenue flows to the most-streamed tracks. The most-streamed tracks are overwhelmingly major-label releases — releases backed by playlist placement budgets, algorithmic promotion deals like Discovery Mode, and marketing spend that independent artists cannot match. The algorithm rewards engagement. Labels optimize for engagement. The result is a feedback loop: the music most likely to be surfaced is the music most likely to conform to patterns that have already proven successful. Not because the algorithm is malicious. Because that is what the economic architecture rewards.

Discovery Mode makes this visible. Labels trade a 30% royalty reduction for algorithmic visibility — and the listener is not told. The fan believes they are discovering music through a personalized recommendation engine. In practice, they are being served music whose rights holders accepted a pay cut to be placed in front of them. This is the consumer-facing consequence of the payola problem documented throughout this series. The harm is not only to the artist who accepted worse terms. It is to the listener who does not know that the recommendation was purchased.

Then there is the music that never gets made. Report 2 documented that a solo independent artist needs roughly 4.7 million annual streams to reach a $15,080 income. The artists who persist under those economics are disproportionately those with access to independent wealth, external income, or the backing of a label willing to absorb years of losses. The music that disappears is the music that cannot be subsidized — the experimental, the niche, the culturally significant but commercially marginal. The listener never hears the album that was never recorded because the artist could not afford to record it.

None of this is explicit. The legal and regulatory framework documented in this report does not dictate taste. It does something more durable: it creates the economic conditions under which certain music thrives and other music does not. Concentrated ownership, opaque royalty flows, platform-mediated gatekeeping — these constrain the diversity of what reaches the listener's ears through forces the listener cannot see and did not choose. The power structures are not just revealed in who gets paid. They are revealed in what gets played.

### Where Things Stand

The legal and regulatory landscape of the American music industry in March 2026 looks like this:

**The consent decrees remain in place.** The DOJ has declined to modify them in three separate reviews. ASCAP and BMI continue to operate under constraints that do not apply to their competitors (SESAC, GMR) or to record labels negotiating master recording licenses.

**The CRB rate fights continue.** Mechanical royalty rates for interactive streaming are set at 15.1-15.35% of service revenue. The bundling loophole allows platforms to reduce their effective rate. The 44% increase won by songwriters in Phonorecords III took years of litigation and years more of appeals before implementation.

**Safe harbor remains unreformed.** The Copyright Office concluded Section 512 is "unbalanced." No legislation has passed to address this. YouTube continues to operate under safe harbor while functioning as one of the world's most-used music platforms, paying a fraction of what other streaming services pay.

**Artists still do not have a seat at the table.** The Artist Rights Alliance was founded in 2019. The Future of Music Coalition operates on a nonprofit budget. Neither has lobbying expenditures remotely comparable to the RIAA, let alone to Google or the NAB.

**AI is the new battleground.** State laws are moving fast. Federal legislation is stalled. The majors have filed lawsuits against AI music generators. The Copyright Office has established that purely AI-generated works cannot be copyrighted. But the harder questions — training data rights, voice cloning at scale, the economic impact of AI-generated music on human creators — remain unresolved.

**The marketplace alternative does not yet exist at scale.** Royalty Exchange has proven the concept. Royal.io has demonstrated the regulatory obstacles. The PE flood has validated music rights as an asset class. But no platform has assembled the pieces — transparent pricing, real-time settlement, standardized rights units, global scope — into a functioning marketplace that could genuinely compete with the incumbent system.

The rules of the game were written by the entities with the most resources to write them. The rules can be changed — the #BrokenRecord campaign proved that. But changing them requires sustained political pressure from constituencies that are structurally underrepresented in the lobbying process. The history documented in this report suggests that change comes not from the system working as designed, but from moments when the system's dysfunction becomes too visible to ignore — when TLC goes bankrupt after selling 65 million records, when a congressional aide is caught inserting industry-friendly language into unrelated legislation, when a parliamentary inquiry calls for a "complete reset" that forces the majors' hand.

The architecture remains. The question is whether the architects will be challenged by new builders — or whether they will continue to redesign the building from the inside, making adjustments at the margins while the foundations stay the same.
