## Axioms and Starting Assumptions

This is the capstone report. Everything documented in the first three reports -- the consolidation of the industry into three dominant groups ([Report 1](./report.md)), the economics that flow through those structures ([Report 2](./report-02-economics.md)), the laws and lobbying that maintain them ([Report 3](./report-03-law-and-lobbying.md)) -- converges here. The reader should understand the assumptions that frame this analysis:

1. **The sample clearance system is a microcosm of the industry's power structure: major labels and publishers as gatekeepers, independent artists bearing disproportionate costs, legal ambiguity serving incumbents.** This is not a closing observation. It is the thesis of the entire series, and this report is where we prove it. Every time an independent artist samples a recording owned by a major, they experience the full architecture at once -- the consolidation (who owns the catalog), the economics (what it costs), and the legal framework (what happens if they do not pay). Sample clearance is not a niche corner of copyright law. It is the mechanism in action.

2. **Two copyrights, two gatekeepers**: A single recorded song embodies two distinct copyrights -- the musical composition (owned by songwriters and publishers) and the sound recording (owned by artists and labels). Sampling requires clearance from both, independently. This dual-copyright structure doubles the cost, doubles the negotiation, and doubles the leverage held by rights holders. Understanding this is prerequisite to understanding everything that follows.

3. **The absence of a compulsory license for sampling is a policy choice, not an inevitability**: Cover songs have a compulsory mechanical license at a statutory rate. Sampling does not. There is no legal requirement that sampling be left to unregulated private negotiation where the sampled party holds all leverage. The system was designed this way, and it was designed this way because the entities that benefit from it had the power to ensure no alternative emerged.

4. **No fabricated data**: Every dollar figure, case citation, and royalty percentage in this report is sourced from the research files or verified public reporting. Where a figure comes from a single source, it is marked `[single-source]`. Where data is unavailable -- and there are significant gaps, particularly in aggregate clearance statistics -- the gap is stated explicitly.

5. **This report closes the arc**: Reports 1 through 3 documented who has power, how money flows, and what rules maintain the structure. This report demonstrates all three operating simultaneously in a single economic transaction -- the sample clearance -- and then looks at what happens when technology threatens to make that transaction obsolete.

## Part 1: Anatomy of a Sample

### The Two-Copyright Problem

Before we can talk about the economics of sampling, we need to understand what a sample actually touches.

Under U.S. copyright law, a single recorded song embodies two separate, independently owned copyrights:

| | **Musical Composition** | **Sound Recording** |
|---|---|---|
| **What it covers** | Melody, harmony, lyrics, arrangement | The specific recorded performance |
| **Typical owner** | Songwriter / music publisher | Artist / record label |
| **Copyright symbol** | (c) | (p) |
| **Sampling requires clearance?** | Yes (both sampling and interpolation) | Yes (sampling only; not interpolation) |
| **De minimis defense available?** | Yes (all circuits) | Yes (9th Cir.) / No (6th Cir.) |
| **Compulsory license?** | Yes, for cover recordings (Section 115) | No |

When you sample an existing recording -- when you take a piece of the actual audio, the actual waveform, and place it into a new work -- you are using both copyrights simultaneously. You need permission from both owners. Each negotiation is independent. Either side can refuse. Either side can demand whatever terms they want.

This dual-clearance requirement is the foundational mechanism of the sample economy. It doubles every cost, every negotiation, every point of potential failure. And it creates a structural dynamic where the entities most likely to own both copyrights -- the major labels, which operate the world's three largest publishing companies through their subsidiaries -- hold leverage on both sides of the transaction.

### What "Clearing a Sample" Actually Means

The process is more opaque than it needs to be. But the mechanics are straightforward enough to lay out step by step.

**Step 1: Identify all rights holders.** This is often the hardest part. Use ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC databases to find publishers and songwriters. Use label copy to find master owners. For older recordings -- the kind most commonly sampled -- rights may have changed hands multiple times through acquisitions, bankruptcies, and catalog sales. The chain of title for a 1970s soul recording can pass through half a dozen corporate entities before landing at its current owner. Finding the right person to even ask is itself a research project.

**Step 2: Submit written clearance requests to both the master owner and the publisher(s).** The request must specify the original work, the new work, how the sample is used, how long the sample runs, and the intended release format and territory. A separate request goes to each rights holder.

**Step 3: Review and negotiation.** Rights holders review the request and decide -- at their sole discretion -- whether to grant permission. If willing, they propose terms: upfront fees, royalty splits, ownership percentages. Each side negotiates independently. There is no coordination requirement. There is no arbitration mechanism. There is no obligation to offer reasonable terms. There is, in fact, no obligation to respond at all.

**Step 4: Execute license agreements.** Separate written agreements for the master use license and the publishing/composition license. Terms specify territory, format, duration, financial arrangements. Clearance must be completed before release.

That is the process. Two to six months is typical. Major publishers may take months to respond. Finding rights holders alone can consume weeks. And throughout, the requesting artist has no leverage and no recourse. If either rights holder says no, the sample cannot be used. There is no compulsory license for sampling -- no statutory mechanism that forces a rights holder to license at a set rate, as exists for cover songs under Section 115. The sampled party has absolute discretion.

This asymmetry is not a bug. It is the system.

### The Role of Specialists

The opacity of the clearance process has created its own professional ecosystem. Several companies specialize in navigating it:

**DMG Clearances**, founded in 1996 by Deborah Mannis-Gardner -- known in the industry as the "Queen of Sample Clearance" -- handles clearances for artists including Kendrick Lamar, Beyonce, and Rihanna. DMG charges flat rates starting at $150/hour minimum for the first hour, with Stage One clearances (submission and negotiation) at $325 each for publishing and master rights. Mannis-Gardner has been clearing samples since 1990 and also handles all music clearance for Rockstar Games, including every Grand Theft Auto since *San Andreas* (2004).

**Tracklib** represents a newer model: a marketplace offering pre-cleared samples organized into three licensing tiers -- Category A ($1,500), Category B ($500), Category C ($50), with over 90% of the catalog in Category C. In 2024, Tracklib added unlimited clearances to subscription tiers ($8.99-$19.99/month).

The existence of these specialists tells you something. In a functioning market, you do not need a dedicated professional class to help you buy something. The fact that sample clearance requires its own cottage industry of brokers, lawyers, and intermediaries is a symptom of a process that is opaque by design -- opaque in ways that benefit incumbents and disadvantage newcomers.

Consider the sheer number of entities that must independently say "yes" before a two-second audio snippet can legally appear in a new song. The master recording owner (typically a label or its corporate successor). The publisher or publishers of the underlying composition -- and if the sampled song has multiple co-writers, each co-writer's publisher must clear independently. The PRO that administers the performance rights. The sampling artist's own label, which must approve the financial terms. The sampling artist's own publisher, who must agree to the reduced share. Attorneys on both sides. Clearance specialists brokering the negotiation. A single two-second sample can require the affirmative consent of six to ten separate entities, any one of which can refuse, demand renegotiation, or simply fail to respond. The system does not have a bottleneck. It *is* a bottleneck.

### The Economics of Clearance

The cost structure of sample clearance involves two components: an upfront advance or fee, and ongoing royalties.

**Publishing (composition) clearance** typically involves an advance of $1,500 to $5,000, plus an ownership percentage of the new song's publishing -- typically 15% to 50%, depending on how prominently the sample features. For a small vocal snippet, DMG Clearances targets around 5% of publishing `[single-source]`. If the sample forms the hook of the new song, publishers may demand 50% to 100%.

**Master (sound recording) clearance** typically involves an advance of $2,000 to $10,000, plus a royalty of 1% to 5% of retail price per unit sold, or an equivalent penny rate (approximately $0.03 per unit). Some labels prefer a flat-fee buyout -- a higher one-time payment in exchange for no ongoing royalty.

**Combined**, the minimum cost for both clearances is roughly $4,000 to $15,000 in advances alone, plus ongoing royalties. And that is per sample. A single sample. Albums that use multiple samples multiply these costs accordingly.

To put this in streaming-era context: with per-stream revenue at approximately $0.008 before splits among rights holders, a $5,000 advance requires roughly 625,000 streams just to generate that amount in gross revenue -- before the label, distributor, and other parties take their shares. An independent artist releasing a song on their own, keeping a larger share, still needs hundreds of thousands of streams to cover a single sample clearance.

DJ Shadow put it plainly in 2017: "Music has never been worth less as a commodity, and yet sampling has never been more risky."

### What Happens When Clearance Is Denied

Rights holders have absolute discretion to deny clearance. There is no appeal. There is no compulsory mechanism. The artist must remove the sample, replace it, or abandon the track.

Some artists maintain informal "Do Not Sample" lists -- artists who categorically refuse to grant sample clearances. Tracy Chapman is a documented example; DMG Clearances confirmed she was on an unofficial "do not sample or interpolate" list. When Nicki Minaj wanted to sample Chapman's "Baby Can I Hold You" for her album *Queen*, Chapman refused. Minaj delayed her album, but the track leaked to radio. Chapman sued. Minaj settled for $450,000.

The "Do Not Sample" list is the logical endpoint of a system where clearance is entirely discretionary. An artist or estate can permanently remove their work from the raw materials available to future creators -- not because of any legal principle, but because the system grants them unilateral veto power. For cover songs, the compulsory license ensures that any artist can record any previously released song by paying the statutory rate. No such mechanism exists for sampling. The asymmetry is a policy choice.

### What Happens When a Sample Is Used Without Clearance

Using an uncleared sample constitutes copyright infringement. The consequences are severe and asymmetric:

- **Statutory damages**: Up to $150,000 per sample, per copy distributed
- **Injunctions**: Courts can order the song removed from sale and streaming
- **Retroactive licensing demands**: The sampled party negotiates from maximum leverage, often demanding far more than pre-release clearance would have cost
- **Songwriting credit reassignment**: Original writers added to credits, diluting the sampling artist's ownership
- **Settlement payments**: Often confidential, but documented cases range from hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars

Post-release, the power dynamic shifts even further toward the sampled party. The sampling artist has already invested in production, marketing, and release. Walking away means losing that investment. The sampled party knows this and prices accordingly.

### The Independent Artist Problem

The economics of sample clearance create a structural disadvantage for independent artists that goes beyond mere cost.

**Financial barriers**: An independent artist may have a total recording budget of $2,000 to $5,000 -- less than the cost of clearing a single sample. Major label artists have clearance costs covered by the label (recoupable against the artist's royalties, but not an out-of-pocket expense). The independent artist pays upfront, out of pocket, before knowing whether the song will generate any revenue.

**Leverage disparity**: A request from Universal Music for a Warner Music sample carries institutional weight. A request from an unknown independent artist does not. Major labels benefit from pre-existing relationships and can offer reciprocal arrangements -- "we will clear your sample if you clear ours" -- that independents cannot. Rights holders are more likely to grant clearance when the new song has commercial backing and marketing support, creating a circular dynamic where only artists who already have institutional support can afford to sample.

**Legal risk**: Statutory damages of up to $150,000 per infringement make uncleared sampling an existential financial risk for independent artists. Lord Finesse sued Mac Miller for $10 million over an uncleared sample on a free mixtape. The Notorious B.I.G.'s estate faced a jury award of $733,878 in damages plus $3.5 million in punitive damages over five seconds of uncleared horns.

**The creative consequence**: The economics of sample clearance have measurably changed what music sounds like. The Bomb Squad -- Public Enemy's production team, pioneers of a dense, layered approach using dozens of samples per track on albums like *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* (1988) and *Fear of a Black Planet* (1990) -- could not exist under the current system. Law professors Peter DiCola and Kembrew McLeod estimated that clearing the samples on *Fear of a Black Planet* at modern rates would result in a loss of at least $5 per copy -- a loss of $5 million on a platinum record. The production style that defined a generation of hip-hop was made economically extinct by the legal framework that emerged after 1991.

This is not an incidental effect. An entire aesthetic -- the multi-sample collage that gave hip-hop its most innovative sonic architecture -- was killed by a legal system that treats every two-second snippet as a separately licensable commodity. The art form adapted. It survived. But what replaced the Bomb Squad's 20-sample-per-track approach was not creative evolution. It was creative contraction, driven by legal and economic constraints that fall hardest on independent artists and reward those with the deepest catalogs and the most corporate infrastructure.

### The Bomb Squad Extinction

The economics deserve a closer look, because they quantify the creative loss.

Public Enemy's *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* (1988) and *Fear of a Black Planet* (1990) were produced by the Bomb Squad -- Hank Shocklee, Keith Shocklee, and Eric "Vietnam" Sadler -- using a dense layering technique that stacked 10 to 20 or more samples per track. The result was a sonic collage unlike anything in popular music: abrasive, disorienting, political, and unmistakably original despite being built entirely from other people's recordings.

That production style is dead. Not because producers lost interest in it, but because the math killed it.

DiCola and McLeod's academic analysis estimated that clearing the samples on *Fear of a Black Planet* at modern rates would cost at least $5 per copy. On a platinum record (one million copies), that is $5 million in clearance costs alone -- before recording, mixing, mastering, marketing, distribution, or the label's recoupment. And that estimate assumes every sample holder agrees to license. If even one refuses -- and they have absolute discretion to refuse -- the entire track must be reworked or abandoned.

Hank Shocklee himself has spoken about this directly. In an interview with Little Village, he and Chuck D described how the post-Grand Upright landscape made their signature production approach impossible to replicate. The legal framework did not merely raise the cost of sampling. It eliminated an entire mode of musical expression -- one that was disproportionately associated with Black American artists working in hip-hop, the genre most dependent on sampling as a creative tool.

The racial dimension of this suppression deserves explicit quantification. Hip-hop and R&B have consistently represented approximately 25% to 30% of total U.S. music consumption in recent years -- the largest or second-largest genre by market share in any given year. Yet artists in these genres bear a wildly disproportionate share of sample clearance costs, mechanical licensing friction, and copyright litigation, simply because the foundational production norms of the genre depend on sampling in ways that rock, pop, and country do not. The post-1991 legal framework did not target hip-hop by name. It did not need to. By eliminating the de minimis defense for sound recordings and establishing that any unauthorized sample constitutes infringement regardless of duration or recognizability, the law created a regime whose costs fall heaviest on the genre most dependent on the practice it criminalized. The neutrality is formal. The impact is not.

The replacement approaches -- fewer samples per track, interpolation, original composition using synthesizers and drum machines -- are not inferior as artistic choices. Dr. Dre's interpolation-heavy approach on *The Chronic* (1992) pioneered a different aesthetic. But they emerged under duress, as adaptations to a legal environment that priced out the alternative. When an artistic movement dies not because audiences reject it but because the legal system makes it unaffordable, the word for that is not "evolution." It is suppression.

### The Interpolation Escape Route

The rise of interpolation as a production technique deserves specific attention, because it is the music industry's most successful adaptation to the clearance economy -- and it illustrates both the ingenuity of producers and the limitations of workarounds.

**Sampling** takes a portion of the actual sound recording and incorporates it into a new work. It requires two clearances: master (from the label) and publishing (from the publisher/songwriter).

**Interpolation** re-records or re-performs a recognizable musical element -- a melody, a chord progression, a lyric, an arrangement -- using new musicians and new equipment. The original recording is not used. It requires clearance only from the composition copyright holder (the publisher/songwriter), not the sound recording owner (the label).

The economics favor interpolation. One negotiation instead of two. No master clearance fee. No dealing with labels that may demand co-ownership of the new recording. Dr. Dre pioneered this approach, frequently hiring musicians to replay recognizable elements for his productions. Rather than sampling James Brown's drum break directly, a session musician plays a similar pattern. Rather than lifting a horn riff from the original recording, a horn player performs the same riff in the studio. The sonic flavor is captured. Only the publishing royalty is owed.

But interpolation is not a safe harbor. As the *Juice WRLD / Sting* and *Williams v. Gaye* cases demonstrate, composition rights holders can demand enormous percentages (85% in Juice WRLD's case), and courts can find infringement based on the "feel" of a composition without any direct copying. The compulsory license that exists for cover songs (Section 115, at the statutory rate of 12 cents per copy for songs over 5 minutes or 9.1 cents for songs 5 minutes or under) does not apply to interpolations that substantially transform the original composition. An interpolation that changes the composition enough to avoid the compulsory license falls outside its protection; one that reproduces the composition faithfully falls within it. The gray zone in between is where most interpolation disputes live.

## Part 2: The Case Law That Built the Rules

### Before the Rules: The Wild West (1979-1991)

From the late 1970s through 1991, hip-hop producers sampled freely with little concern for copyright clearance. Copyright law existed and applied in theory, but no court had directly addressed digital sampling in a published opinion. The practice was not just common -- it was foundational. Early sampling used turntables, then drum machines (E-mu SP-1200, Akai MPC60), and early digital samplers. Producers built entire tracks from collages of existing recordings.

The albums of this era -- Public Enemy's *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* (1988), De La Soul's *3 Feet High and Rising* (1989), the Beastie Boys' *Paul's Boutique* (1989) -- represent the pinnacle of unregulated sampling. *Paul's Boutique* contains approximately 105 identifiable samples. The Beastie Boys reportedly spent over $250,000 on clearances -- extraordinary for the late 1980s -- but many samples were cleared "easily and affordably" because the market for sample licensing was not yet established. Rights holders had not yet recognized the value of their catalogs in this context.

Could *Paul's Boutique* be made today? The consensus among music attorneys is no -- or at least not at any reasonable cost. Estimates for clearing all samples under modern conditions range from $300,000 to $500,000 or more, and many rights holders would likely refuse to license at any price. The album's dense, collage-like approach is economically infeasible under the legal regime that emerged after 1991.

Two events in 1991 ended the era.

### Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc. (1991): "Thou Shalt Not Steal"

**Case**: 780 F. Supp. 182 (S.D.N.Y. 1991)
**Judge**: Kevin Thomas Duffy
**Decided**: December 17, 1991

Rapper Biz Markie sampled a portion of Gilbert O'Sullivan's 1972 hit "Alone Again (Naturally)" for his track "Alone Again" on his third album, *I Need a Haircut*. Warner Bros. had contacted O'Sullivan's representatives to obtain a license. They were denied. They released the album anyway.

Judge Duffy's opinion opened with the biblical admonition: **"Thou shalt not steal."** It was the only legal authority he cited beyond the Seventh Commandment. He granted a preliminary injunction halting distribution of the album and took the extraordinary step of referring the matter to the U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of New York for potential criminal prosecution under 17 U.S.C. Section 506(a) and 18 U.S.C. Section 2319. No criminal charges were ultimately filed. But the threat of criminal prosecution for sampling -- not just civil liability, but potential prison -- sent shockwaves through the industry.

The opinion has been widely criticized by legal scholars for its brevity, lack of legal analysis, and failure to apply established copyright doctrines such as fair use or de minimis use. It is, by any standard of legal reasoning, a poor opinion. But its impact was enormous. It was the first federal court ruling to directly establish that unauthorized digital sampling constitutes copyright infringement, and it did so in language so blunt -- so morally charged, so devoid of nuance -- that it created a chilling effect that persists three decades later.

The same year, former Turtles members Howard Kaylan and Mark Volman sued De La Soul over an unauthorized 12-second sample of the Turtles' 1969 song "You Showed Me," which appeared in an interstitial skit on *3 Feet High and Rising*. A critical detail: many of the album's major, recognizable samples -- from Hall & Oates and Steely Dan -- had been cleared. The smaller samples used in interstitial "skits" had not. The Turtles sample was a 12-second snippet in a skit, not a featured element. The case settled for reportedly up to $1.7 million from an initial claim of $2.5 million. Volman's public statement: "Sampling is just a longer term for theft."

Grand Upright and the Turtles settlement, arriving in the same year, established the new reality: all sampling requires permission. The combination of these two events is widely regarded as the end of hip-hop's uncleared sampling era.

The WNYC Studios *On the Media* series documented the consequences: "free sampling was banned, and all samples now had to be cleared by the labels of the music getting sampled, and in many cases royalties had to be paid." The primary upshot was that "the practice's creative potential" was constrained. The shift was immediate and measurable. Producers adapted in several ways: reduced sampling (fewer, more carefully chosen samples that could be cleared at reasonable cost), original production (synthesizers, drum machines, live instrumentation -- the rise of West Coast G-funk and Southern hip-hop styles), interpolation (re-recording recognizable elements to avoid master clearance), and heavy manipulation (pitch-shifting, time-stretching, reversing, layering to make samples unrecognizable).

### Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc. (1994): Fair Use Lives

**Case**: 510 U.S. 569 (1994)
**Court**: Supreme Court of the United States
**Opinion**: Justice David Souter (unanimous on core holding)
**Decided**: March 7, 1994

Three years after Grand Upright shut the door, the Supreme Court opened a window.

2 Live Crew's 1989 song "Pretty Woman" was a parody of Roy Orbison's 1964 classic "Oh, Pretty Woman." 2 Live Crew's manager had requested a license from Acuff-Rose Music. Acuff-Rose refused. 2 Live Crew released the song anyway, crediting Orbison and William Dees as songwriters. The Sixth Circuit held that the commercial nature of the parody created a presumption against fair use.

The Supreme Court reversed, unanimously. Commercial parody can qualify as fair use under 17 U.S.C. Section 107. The commercial nature of a work is only one factor in the four-factor analysis, not a presumption against fair use. And the Court established the concept that would become central to all subsequent fair use analysis: **transformative use**. "The more transformative the new work, the less will be the significance of other factors, like commercialism." Parody, the Court held, has "an obvious claim to transformative value" because it can provide social benefit by commenting on the original.

Campbell v. Acuff-Rose was not a sampling case in the strict sense -- it involved parody, not the use of audio snippets in a new composition. But it established the transformative use framework that has been the primary tool available to sampling defendants ever since. It was also, critically, the last time the Supreme Court said anything definitive about fair use in the music context. Thirty-two years and counting.

### Newton v. Diamond (2004): The Two-Copyright Distinction

**Case**: 388 F.3d 1189 (9th Cir. 2004)

The Beastie Boys sampled the opening six seconds of James Newton's "Choir" (1978) -- three notes (C, D-flat, C) with a held C note underneath -- and looped it over 40 times in their track "Pass the Mic." They obtained a license from the sound recording owner (ECM Records) but not from Newton for the underlying composition.

The Ninth Circuit held that the three-note sequence was **de minimis** -- too trivial to constitute infringement of the composition copyright. The case drew a critical line between the sound recording copyright and the musical composition copyright, establishing that clearing one does not automatically clear the other. And it applied the de minimis doctrine to musical compositions in the sampling context -- a doctrine that would be rejected for sound recordings (but not compositions) the following year.

### Bridgeport Music, Inc. v. Dimension Films (2005): "Get a License or Do Not Sample"

**Case**: 410 F.3d 792 (6th Cir. 2005)
**Opinion**: Circuit Judge Ralph B. Guy Jr.

If Grand Upright was the moral verdict, Bridgeport was the bright-line rule.

N.W.A.'s 1990 track "100 Miles and Runnin'" sampled a two-second guitar chord from Funkadelic's 1975 song "Get Off Your Ass and Jam." The sample was lowered in pitch and looped five times. The district court had granted summary judgment to the defendants, finding the sample was de minimis.

The Sixth Circuit reversed and created the strictest rule in American sampling law: **the de minimis defense does not apply to sound recordings.** The court's instruction was unambiguous: **"Get a license or do not sample."**

The statutory basis was 17 U.S.C. Section 114(b), which provides that the copyright in a sound recording "is not infringed by the duplication of another sound recording that consists entirely of an independent fixation of other sounds." The Sixth Circuit interpreted this to mean that Congress intended to give sound recording owners exclusive rights over any unauthorized copying -- no matter how small the portion taken. Even a fraction of a second. Even if the snippet was unrecognizable to any listener.

The court's reasoning offered four justifications: sound recording owners have exclusive sampling rights, even short samples take something of value, the market for licensing is well-established, and a bright-line rule provides clarity. The opinion was widely criticized by legal scholars, musicians, and First Amendment advocates for being overly rigid, discouraging creativity, and misinterpreting the Copyright Act.

But criticism does not change precedent. In the Sixth Circuit -- covering Michigan, Ohio, Kentucky, and Tennessee, including Nashville -- any unauthorized use of even a fraction of a second of a copyrighted sound recording constitutes infringement. No exceptions. No analysis of recognizability or substantiality. Get a license or do not sample.

### VMG Salsoul, LLC v. Ciccone (2016): The Ninth Circuit Disagrees

**Case**: 824 F.3d 871 (9th Cir. 2016)
**Decided**: June 2, 2016

VMG Salsoul alleged that producer Shep Pettibone copied a **0.23-second** segment of horns from the Salsoul Orchestra's 1983 track "Ooh I Love It (Love Break)" and used a modified version in Madonna's 1990 hit "Vogue."

The Ninth Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the defendants: even if the sample was copied, the use was de minimis. A reasonable listener would not recognize the brief snippet in "Vogue" as originating from "Love Break."

Then the court did something the music industry had been waiting -- or dreading -- for over a decade. It **expressly declined to follow Bridgeport**. The majority held that the de minimis exception applies to sound recordings just as it applies to all other copyright infringement actions. Congress did not eliminate it when it enacted Section 114(b). Bridgeport's reasoning was "not persuasive" and its interpretation of the statute was incorrect.

This created a **circuit split** -- two federal appeals courts applying contradictory rules to the same legal question.

### The Circuit Split: Two Americas of Sampling Law

The United States currently operates under two contradictory legal standards for digital sampling of sound recordings:

| | **Sixth Circuit (Bridgeport, 2005)** | **Ninth Circuit (VMG Salsoul, 2016)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Rule** | No de minimis defense. Any unauthorized sampling constitutes infringement. | De minimis defense applies. |
| **Standard** | "Get a license or do not sample." | Would a reasonable audience recognize the appropriation? |
| **Geographic coverage** | Michigan, Ohio, Kentucky, Tennessee (Nashville) | California, Oregon, Washington, Arizona, Nevada (Los Angeles) |
| **Practical effect** | Absolute liability for any unauthorized sample | Liability only if the sample is recognizable |

The Supreme Court has not resolved this split. No certiorari petition appears to have been filed in *VMG Salsoul* itself `[single-source]`. As of March 2026, the law of sampling depends on where you are standing when you press record.

The split creates incentives for strategic forum shopping. A plaintiff seeking strict liability files in Nashville. A defendant wants Los Angeles. The music industry has two centers of gravity -- Nashville and LA -- and they operate under opposite rules for the same creative practice. This is not a stable legal framework. It is a jurisdictional lottery.

Why has the Supreme Court not acted? Several possible reasons: the split is relatively narrow, affecting a specific subset of copyright cases rather than a broad doctrinal question. The industry has largely adapted by clearing samples or avoiding sampling in strict jurisdictions. The Court may be waiting for a case with a more compelling factual record or one where the circuit split directly determined the outcome. The issue has not generated the volume of litigation that typically draws Supreme Court attention, in part because the chilling effect of Bridgeport has discouraged uncleared sampling in the Sixth Circuit -- fewer cases means fewer opportunities for the Court to grant certiorari.

But there is a deeper reason that goes unspoken: the ambiguity serves incumbents. Major labels and publishers, which control the catalogs most likely to be sampled, benefit from a legal landscape where the safest course is always to pay for clearance. Uncertainty does not hurt the party selling the license. It hurts the party who needs to buy one.

### Williams v. Gaye (2015/2018): The Chill

**Case**: 895 F.3d 1106 (9th Cir. 2018)
**Original Verdict**: March 10, 2015

This was not a sampling case. No audio was taken from Marvin Gaye's recording. But it belongs in this analysis because it expanded the scope of what courts might consider protectable musical expression in ways that sent a chill across the entire creative landscape.

The Gaye estate alleged that Pharrell Williams and Robin Thicke's "Blurred Lines" (2013) infringed the copyright in Marvin Gaye's "Got to Give It Up" (1977). The claim was based on the musical composition copyright -- the "groove," the "feel," the keyboard and bass patterns. The jury found infringement and awarded $7.4 million, later reduced to approximately $4.98 million plus a running royalty of 50% of future songwriting royalties.

The Ninth Circuit affirmed. Judge Nguyen's dissent warned that the majority had allowed the Gaye estate to "own a musical style." The music community largely agreed. The decision created a chilling effect on songwriting that extends far beyond traditional sampling -- if evoking the "feel" of an earlier song can constitute infringement, the boundaries of creative influence become dangerously uncertain. The result has been a reported increase in preemptive songwriting credits and settlements. Artists now add co-writing credits defensively, not because they sampled or copied, but because the legal risk of not doing so exceeds the cost of sharing credit.

For the sample economy, *Blurred Lines* matters because it demonstrates that even interpolation -- re-performing rather than directly sampling -- does not provide a safe harbor if the resulting composition is substantially similar to the original. The traditional escape route from sample clearance costs (just replay it yourself, and you only need publishing clearance) became less reliable after a jury decided that capturing a "groove" was enough to constitute infringement.

### Recent Cases: The System in 2024

The sample clearance system is not a relic of the 1990s. It remains active and consequential.

**Sony Music v. Trefuego (2024)**: A Texas federal court ordered rapper Trefuego to pay approximately $803,000 in damages after his viral hit "90mh" used an unauthorized sample of Toshifumi Hinata's "Reflections," a recording controlled by Sony Music. The case illustrates the persistent risk: a viral hit built on an uncleared sample generates not revenue for the artist but liability. The $803,000 judgment likely exceeds whatever revenue "90mh" generated for Trefuego.

**Kanye West / Vultures sampling disputes (2024-2025)**: As detailed in the case studies section below, the Vultures albums generated multiple lawsuits. German singer-songwriter Alice Merton sued over an unauthorized sample of "Blindside" in "Gun To My Head," alleging she learned of the use only after West previewed the song publicly. West had allegedly attempted to obtain clearance in February 2024 -- after the song had already circulated. The pattern -- release first, clear later -- inverts the system's intended sequence and demonstrates that for artists with sufficient resources and risk tolerance, the clearance process is treated as an afterthought rather than a prerequisite.

**Travis Scott sampling suit (2024)**: Travis Scott, Metro Boomin, and James Blake were sued for allegedly sampling a copyrighted work without permission `[single-source]`.

These cases confirm that the legal framework established by Grand Upright and Bridgeport remains fully operative. The penalties are severe. The leverage belongs to the rights holders. And the system's costs continue to fall disproportionately on artists -- particularly independent artists who lack the resources to absorb litigation.

### International: Kraftwerk v. Pelham (Germany/EU, 1999-ongoing)

The world's longest-running sampling lawsuit spans over two decades and involves multiple national and supranational courts.

Ralf Hutterand Florian Schneider-Esleben of Kraftwerk sued producers Moses Pelham and Martin Haas for sampling a two-second rhythmic sequence from Kraftwerk's 1977 track "Metall auf Metall" and looping it as the beat for rapper Sabrina Setlur's 1997 song "Nur Mir."

The litigation timeline: suit filed in 1999, German Federal Court of Justice ruled for Kraftwerk in 2008, German Federal Constitutional Court remanded in 2012, questions referred to the CJEU in 2017, CJEU ruled in July 2019, BGH ruled in April 2020, case remanded again to examine the "pastiche" exception, further questions referred to the CJEU. As of March 2026, the case has produced approximately ten separate judicial opinions over 27 years and is not yet finally resolved `[single-source]`.

The CJEU established principles that mirror the Ninth Circuit's approach: sampling that produces an unrecognizable result does not infringe the reproduction right; sampling that produces a recognizable copy without authorization does. But the case also demonstrates the costs of legal uncertainty. Twenty-seven years of litigation over two seconds of audio. The transaction costs alone -- the lawyers' fees, the court time, the uncertainty -- dwarf any conceivable licensing fee. A system that generates 27 years of litigation over a two-second sample is not functioning as a mechanism for resolving disputes. It is functioning as a mechanism for generating them.

## Part 3: Case Studies -- Who Pays, Who Profits, Who Gets Erased

The legal framework is abstract until you see what it does to actual people. These case studies are not selected for their novelty. They are selected because each one demonstrates a different dimension of the power structure that sample clearance reveals.

### Isaac Hayes: The Artist Who Lost Everything Before He Died

Isaac Hayes was a foundational figure at Stax Records in Memphis, co-writing (with David Porter) iconic songs including "Soul Man" and "Hold On, I'm Coming" for Sam & Dave. His 1971 score for *Shaft* won the Academy Award for Best Original Score. He was one of the most important songwriters and performers in American music.

In 1976, Hayes filed for bankruptcy with approximately $6 million in debt. He lost his home, his personal property, and the rights to all future royalties from his pre-bankruptcy catalog. He sued Stax for $5.3 million. Stax itself had gone bankrupt in 1975.

Follow the chain of title for the masters: Stax's catalog was purchased by Fantasy Records in 1977. Fantasy was acquired by Concord Records in 2004. Concord reactivated the Stax label in 2006. The Hayes estate does not control the original Stax-era masters. They belong to Concord.

Follow the chain for the publishing: In October 2023, Primary Wave Music announced a publishing administration and marketing deal with the Hayes estate. But the specific composition rights for co-written Stax-era songs are controlled by Universal Music Publishing Group and Warner Chappell Music Publishing. "Hold On, I'm Coming," one of the most sampled and licensed songs in soul music history, has its publishing split between UMPG and Warner Chappell -- not the Hayes estate.

In August 2024, the Hayes estate sued Donald Trump's presidential campaign for unauthorized use of "Hold On, I'm Comin'" at over 133 campaign events. The Trump campaign's defense was revealing: the Hayes estate does not own the copyright to the song. The rights belong to the publishers. The estate sought $3 million in damages. A federal judge denied Trump's motion to dismiss, allowing the case to proceed, and the lawsuit was mutually resolved in February 2026. But the Trump campaign's argument -- technically correct -- exposed the structural reality: Hayes's own family cannot fully control the use of songs he co-wrote because the copyrights were lost through bankruptcy and corporate acquisition chains that stretch back half a century.

Isaac Hayes died on August 10, 2008. He was 65. His family controls publishing income through the Primary Wave deal and his name, image, and likeness. They do not control the original recordings. They share control of the compositions with major publishers who acquired those rights through chains of corporate transactions that began when Hayes was bankrupt. If you want to sample an Isaac Hayes Stax recording, you negotiate with Concord (masters) and UMPG/Warner Chappell (publishing). The Hayes family's involvement depends on the specific song and the specific right. The artist who created the music is the least powerful party in the transaction.

This is what the power structure looks like from the bottom.

### Biz Markie: The Man Grand Upright Destroyed

The *Grand Upright* ruling is typically discussed as a legal precedent. Less discussed is what it did to the human being on the other side of the case citation.

Biz Markie's album *I Need a Haircut* was recalled from stores. The track "Alone Again" was pulled. His next album, pointedly titled ***All Samples Cleared!*** (1993), reflected the new legal reality in its title. It did not achieve commercial success comparable to his earlier work. Biz Markie's recording career effectively stalled after *Grand Upright*.

He pivoted. He became a DJ, a live performer, a personality. He appeared on children's television. He became known as "the Clown Prince of Hip-Hop" -- a moniker that was affectionate but also marked the distance between what he had been (a recording artist whose work pushed the boundaries of sample-based production) and what the legal system left him as (an entertainer whose recording career was defined by a court case that referenced the Ten Commandments).

Biz Markie died on July 16, 2021, at age 57. The legal case that bears his name established the precedent that all samples must be cleared before release. The cost fell not on Warner Bros. Records -- a corporate entity with resources to absorb the loss -- but on Marcel Hall, the artist, whose career trajectory was permanently altered by a ruling that was legally thin, morally absolutist, and structurally consequential.

The pattern is consistent throughout the history of sampling law: the legal precedents are set in cases involving individual artists, but the structural beneficiaries are the corporate entities that own catalogs and control clearance. Biz Markie bore the cost of establishing the rule. Every major label's catalog became more valuable because of it.

There is a racial dimension to this pattern that should not be elided. Hip-hop -- the genre most dependent on sampling, the genre most affected by the post-Grand Upright legal regime -- is a predominantly Black American art form. The Bomb Squad's dense sampling technique, the crate-digging culture that sourced material from Black musical traditions (funk, soul, R&B, jazz), and the creative practice of building new works from existing recordings were all rooted in Black musical traditions that predate copyright law's attempt to regulate them. The legal framework that emerged after 1991 did not target these traditions explicitly. But it targeted them structurally -- by making the creative practices most associated with Black American music production the most legally and economically constrained. The production styles that survived -- interpolation, original composition, minimal sampling -- required either studio resources (session musicians to replay samples) or creative pivots away from sampling altogether. Both favored artists with institutional backing over those working independently. The clearance system is race-neutral in its text and racially disproportionate in its impact.

### Nicki Minaj / Tracy Chapman: The "Do Not Sample" List in Action

In 2018, Nicki Minaj wanted to include the song "Sorry" on her album *Queen*. "Sorry" sampled Tracy Chapman's 1988 hit "Baby Can I Hold You." Minaj's team contacted Chapman's representatives to request clearance. Chapman refused. She was, according to DMG Clearances, on an unofficial "do not sample or interpolate" list -- an artist who categorically denies all sample requests.

The story should have ended there. Under the sample clearance system, a refusal is final. There is no appeal, no compulsory mechanism, no override. The artist must remove the sample and move on.

Minaj delayed her album. But "Sorry" leaked to radio -- specifically, to DJ Funkmaster Flex, who played it on New York's Hot 97. The song was broadcast publicly despite the clearance refusal. Chapman sued.

In January 2021, Minaj settled for $450,000.

The case is instructive on several levels. First, it demonstrates the absolute nature of the clearance veto: Chapman said no, and that should have been the end. The fact that the song reached the public anyway -- through a radio DJ, not through Minaj's official release -- illustrates how the clearance system's bright-line rules interact with the messy reality of music distribution. A song can leak. A DJ can play an uncleared track. And the artist who wanted to use the sample bears the financial consequences regardless of how the leak occurred.

Second, the case raises the question of whether a categorical "do not sample" policy represents creative autonomy or cultural suppression. The American University Business Law Review examined this question in the context of Chapman's position, asking whether a blanket refusal to allow sampling constitutes "creative security or creative suppression." The answer depends on whose perspective you take. For Chapman, the refusal is an exercise of her property rights -- she owns the composition and has the right to control its use. For Minaj and artists like her, Chapman's refusal removes "Baby Can I Hold You" from the available palette of sounds that can be legally built upon. Neither perspective is wrong. But the system provides no mechanism for balancing them. The property right is absolute. The creative interest has no legal weight.

Third, the $450,000 settlement was the cost of a leak, not the cost of a release. Minaj never officially released "Sorry." The settlement covered damages from the unauthorized radio broadcast. Had the song been officially released and become a hit, the damages would have been orders of magnitude higher. The sample clearance system's penalties scale with commercial success -- which means the artists most likely to face catastrophic consequences are the ones whose music is most popular. An unknown artist who leaks a song with an uncleared Tracy Chapman sample might face no consequences at all, because no one hears it. A major artist faces a $450,000 settlement for a radio leak.

The asymmetry is structural, not incidental. The clearance system penalizes success.

### Clipping: How Clearance Difficulty Becomes Creative Constraint

Clipping (stylized as clipping.) -- rapper Daveed Diggs and producers William Hutson and Jonathan Snipes -- represents the other end of the spectrum: artists who have internalized the clearance system's constraints and built their entire aesthetic around avoiding them.

Clipping primarily builds beats from found sounds and field recordings rather than traditional music samples: beer bottles, alarm clocks, bricks breaking, thermoses. The group has stated explicitly that they avoid traditional music samples in part because they are difficult to clear. This creative constraint shaped their sonic identity -- an approach that aligns with hip-hop's foundational ethos of "digging up sounds" but substitutes crate-digging for environmental sound capture.

The case of "Say the Name," from their 2020 album *Visions of Bodies Being Burned*, illustrates the friction even when artists try to work within the system. The album's title quotes a line from Geto Boys' "Mind Playing Tricks on Me." For the single "Say the Name," the chorus repeats this quote. Rather than sampling the original recording, the band recorded Daveed Diggs's voice and pitch-shifted it down to approximate the original's character. Even this approach required clearance negotiations. Obtaining permission proved difficult -- the track was originally intended for the predecessor album *There Existed an Addiction to Blood* but was held back due to clearance delays. The song was described as already "old to them" by the time it appeared, indicating a clearance timeline of potentially over a year `[single-source]`.

Clipping is what happens when creative artists accept the clearance system as a given and route around it. The result is interesting art. But the fact that an experimental hip-hop group must avoid referencing the genre's own history because the legal costs of engaging with that history are prohibitive tells you something about who the system serves. It does not serve artists who want to build on what came before. It serves the entities that own what came before.

### Kanye West: The Economics of Sampling at Scale

Kanye West is one of the most prolific samplers in modern music, and his career illustrates every dimension of the sample economy -- the costs, the leverage, the legal exposure, and the structural advantages that major-label backing provides.

**My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy** (2010) contains approximately 29 distinct samples across 13 tracks. Sample clearance was handled by Eric Weissman of Music Licensing, Inc. One documented clearance cost: $62,500 was paid to clear the sample of Eddie Bo's "Hook & Sling" -- and even that led to a lawsuit from TufAmerica, the rights owner, which alleged that Roc-a-Fella/Universal used the song in ways beyond the written license agreement.

**The Life of Pablo** (2016) contains approximately 39 samples. The album's evolving nature -- West updated tracks after initial release -- complicated clearance timelines.

**The Vultures albums** (2024) represent something unprecedented: a major artist releasing commercial recordings with widespread uncleared samples, apparently by design.

Donna Summer's estate publicly stated West asked to use "I Feel Love," was denied, but used it anyway. The estate sued; the track "Good (Don't Die)" was removed from streaming. Black Sabbath's "Iron Man" sample on "CARNIVAL" was pulled after threats from the Osbournes. James Brown's estate told Billboard that "Funky President" was not cleared. Portishead co-founder Geoff Barrow publicly stated their song "Machine Gun" was sampled on "Field Trip" without proper clearance. Brand Nubian's publisher Reservoir Media was still negotiating clearance for the "Slow Down" sample in "KEYS TO MY LIFE" after release.

Rell Lafague, president/COO of Reservoir Media, stated: "On the level of Kanye in 2024 to put out an entire album with samples that haven't even been requested to be cleared, I don't think I've ever really seen that today."

According to Pusha T, sample holders sometimes request "97 percent of the record" when clearing samples for Kanye, exploiting his fame and perceived willingness to pay. Kanye's response has been to frequently have samples replayed by session musicians, requiring only a publishing license rather than a master recording license. He also benefits from an in-house advantage: many songs he samples are owned by Universal, the label he was signed to, allowing internal deals at reduced rates.

The Kanye case study reveals the system's full dynamic range. An artist with major-label backing, a dedicated clearance team, and a history of prolific sampling can absorb costs that would bankrupt an independent artist. He can even, as the Vultures albums demonstrate, release uncleared samples and absorb the resulting lawsuits as a cost of doing business. For Kanye, sample clearance is an annoyance to be managed. For an independent artist, it is a barrier to entry.

The system does not treat every artist the same. It treats them according to their position in the power structure. The same two-second sample that costs an indie artist $5,000 and three months of negotiation gets "replayed" by Kanye's session musicians under an in-house publishing deal. The same uncleared sample that would result in career-ending litigation for an unknown artist generates manageable lawsuits for one of the best-selling musicians in history. The rules are the same. The game is different.

### The Verve / "Bitter Sweet Symphony": Twenty-Two Years in the Machine

The Verve's "Bitter Sweet Symphony" (1997) is the most complete illustration of how the sample clearance system can consume an artist.

The Verve sampled a 1966 orchestral recording of the Rolling Stones' "The Last Time" by the Andrew Oldham Orchestra. The composition rights were controlled by ABKCO Records, headed by Allen Klein -- the Stones' former business manager. The initial deal: Decca Records (master holder) licensed a five-note segment. The agreed royalty split: 50% to the Jagger/Richards composition rights holders.

Then the song became a hit.

Allen Klein claimed the Verve used more of the sample than agreed -- a larger portion than the licensed five-note segment. He demanded the Verve either relinquish 100% of songwriting royalties or pull the single from sale. The Verve settled: all songwriting credits were changed to Jagger-Richards, and 100% of publishing royalties went to ABKCO. Richard Ashcroft reportedly received a total of $1,000 from what became one of the decade's biggest songs. Estimated lost revenue: $5 million in publishing revenue by Billboard's estimate, and potentially $7-12 million over 22 years `[single-source on the $7-12M figure]`.

It took 22 years for the situation to resolve. Allen Klein died in 2009. In early 2019, Ashcroft's managers approached Klein's son Jody Klein, who connected them to the Rolling Stones' manager. In April 2019, ABKCO, Jagger, and Richards agreed to return the songwriting credits and royalties to Ashcroft. The resolution was voluntary -- not court-ordered. It required the death of the gatekeeper and the goodwill of the original songwriters. If Klein had lived, or if Jagger and Richards had been less amenable, Ashcroft would still be receiving nothing from a song he wrote.

The Bitter Sweet Symphony case demonstrates every element of the power structure. The sample's composition rights were controlled by a corporate intermediary (ABKCO) whose interests were purely financial. The initial deal was renegotiated under duress after the song's commercial success gave the rights holder maximum leverage. The artist's only options were total capitulation or withdrawal of the song. And resolution came not through the legal system -- which had produced the unjust outcome in the first place -- but through personal relationships and individual goodwill, two decades later.

### Juice WRLD / "Lucid Dreams": Interpolation Is Not a Safe Harbor

"Lucid Dreams" (2018) was not a traditional sample. It was an **interpolation** -- a re-performance of the melody from Sting and Dominic Miller's 1993 song "Shape of My Heart." Producer Nick Mira built the beat around the interpolated guitar melody. No audio from Sting's recording was used. Only a publishing license was required.

Sting received 85% of the royalties.

That number bears repeating: eighty-five percent. From a song that became one of 2018's biggest hits, the artist who created the new work and the producer who built the beat together received fifteen percent. The remaining eighty-five went to the composers of the original melody, through negotiation rather than litigation. Sting denied suing Juice WRLD; a spokesperson told Billboard the claims of legal action were "simply untrue." The royalty arrangement was a negotiated license.

Sting described the song as a "beautiful interpretation that is faithful to the original song's form" and joked that the royalties would "put my grandkids through college."

But there is a reason interpolation remains the preferred workaround despite these draconian publishing splits: the master recording royalties. Because no audio from Sting's recording was used, Juice WRLD and his label retained 100% of the master recording royalties. In the streaming era, master royalties generally pay out significantly higher than publishing royalties -- roughly three to four times higher per stream. Sting got 85% of the smaller pie; Juice WRLD kept all of the bigger one. The total economics of "Lucid Dreams" were punishing but survivable. Had it been a direct sample rather than an interpolation, Juice WRLD would have faced the same 85% publishing demand *plus* a separate master clearance negotiation with Sting's label -- potentially losing majority stakes on both sides of the revenue split.

The Lucid Dreams case demonstrates that interpolation -- the standard workaround for avoiding master clearance costs -- is not a loophole so much as a calculated trade-off. You surrender most of the publishing in exchange for keeping all of the masters. For a streaming-era hit generating tens of millions of plays, that trade-off can be worth tens of millions of dollars. But if the interpolation is recognizable, the publishing rights holder still has all the leverage that the sample clearance system provides -- and the fundamental power asymmetry remains intact.

### De La Soul: Thirty-Four Years Locked Out

De La Soul's classic albums -- including *3 Feet High and Rising* (1989) -- were unavailable on streaming platforms for 34 years until March 3, 2023. The reason was a combination of sample clearance complexity and exploitative contract terms.

The group's dispute with Tommy Boy Records, their original label, centered on who would pay for uncleared samples and on what terms. In early 2019, Tommy Boy announced plans to bring the catalog to streaming, but De La Soul criticized the terms as "unbalanced, unfair" -- the split would be roughly 90% Tommy Boy, 10% De La Soul. The group launched a "Tommy Boycott" campaign and received public support from artists like Nas and Questlove.

After seven months of stalled negotiations, De La Soul cut ties with Tommy Boy in August 2019. By 2021, they had acquired ownership of their masters. After Tommy Boy was acquired by Reservoir Media, DMG Clearances restarted sample negotiations in January 2022. It took approximately one year to license most samples. Samples that could not be cleared were interpolated or replayed for the streaming versions. Some tracks were altered -- "Cool Breeze on the Rocks" became "Cool Breeze on the Rocks (The Melted Version)."

On March 3, 2023, De La Soul's catalog finally debuted on streaming. Thirty-four years after release. Posdnuos (Kelvin Mercer), one-third of the group, called it "a long time coming." Dave (David Jude Jolicoeur), another member, died on February 12, 2023 -- less than three weeks before the catalog reached streaming. He did not live to see his music become legally accessible to the streaming generation.

The De La Soul case sits at the intersection of every structural failure this series has documented. The original contract (90/10 split) reflects the exploitative economics documented in Report 2. The label's control of the masters reflects the consolidation documented in Report 1. The sample clearance complexity -- requiring re-negotiation of dozens of licenses before the catalog could reach streaming -- reflects the legal architecture documented in Report 3. And the 34-year delay represents the human cost: an entire generation of listeners who could not legally access one of hip-hop's foundational albums because the economics of sample clearance made it unprofitable for the label to bother.

### DJ Shadow / Endtroducing: The Album That Cannot Be Repeated

Released in 1996 on Mo' Wax Records, *Endtroducing.....* entered the Guinness Book of Records as the first album composed entirely from sampled material. DJ Shadow used approximately 500 samples, sourced primarily from obscure vinyl records found through crate-digging.

How was this possible after Grand Upright and Bridgeport? Several factors: many samples were drawn from extremely obscure sources where rights holders were difficult to locate or had minimal commercial leverage. The album was released on Mo' Wax, a UK-based independent label that may have faced different legal and economic dynamics. And the exact clearance strategy -- which samples were cleared, which were deemed too obscure to pursue -- has not been fully documented publicly.

DJ Shadow himself has articulated the fundamental contradiction: "If you're clearing one sample out of 18, and they want 50% and everybody down the line wants 50%, then it's kind of like, 'Hang on guys, this isn't going to work!' It literally makes the art form illegal and dangerous."

*Endtroducing* could not be repeated today. Not because the creative impulse has disappeared, but because the legal and economic infrastructure makes it impossible. An album built from 500 samples, each requiring separate master and publishing clearances, each subject to percentage demands that can exceed 50% -- the math does not work. The album exists as a monument to a brief window when the creative possibilities of sampling outran the legal system's ability to monetize them. That window closed.

### J Dilla: Estate Under Siege

*Donuts* (2006) is an instrumental hip-hop album containing 31 tracks built from approximately 34 primary samples drawn from rock, jazz, and soul. Released on February 7, 2006 -- J Dilla's 32nd birthday -- on Stones Throw Records, just three days before his death.

The album is widely regarded as one of the most influential hip-hop albums ever made. It is also a case study in how sample-based music remains legally vulnerable long after release.

In September 2020, Music Sales Corporation -- which had purchased 10cc's intellectual property in 2019 -- sued the Dilla estate over samples. This has been characterized by commentators as potential "sample trolling": acquiring catalogs specifically to pursue infringement claims against existing works that used those catalogs' material `[single-source for the characterization]`.

The pattern is worth noting. An investment entity acquires a catalog. It reviews where those catalog's recordings have been sampled. It files infringement claims. The original creative relationship between the sampled work and the sampling artist is irrelevant; what matters is the chain of title and the statutory damages available. The sample economy creates an ongoing liability for any work that contains samples -- a liability that can be weaponized by anyone who acquires the underlying rights, at any time, for any reason.

### Girl Talk: The Fair Use Argument Nobody Wants Tested

Gregg Gillis, performing as Girl Talk, creates densely layered mashup albums using hundreds of unlicensed samples per album. None have been formally cleared. No rights holder has ever sued him.

Why? Multiple theories. The most compelling: rights holders fear establishing favorable fair use precedent. A court ruling in Gillis's favor could establish broad fair use protections for sampling that would undermine the entire clearance economy. The potential precedent is more dangerous to rights holders than the infringement itself.

This theory -- which Gillis himself has not confirmed but which is widely discussed in music law circles -- reveals something fundamental about the sample economy: it operates in a zone of deliberate legal ambiguity. The Bridgeport rule says "get a license or do not sample." Girl Talk does not get a license and does sample. And the rights holders who would enforce Bridgeport choose not to, because the risk of losing a case and narrowing their rights outweighs the benefit of winning one.

The system's power depends partly on uncertainty. If the law were clear -- if fair use for sampling were either definitively established or definitively rejected -- the leverage dynamics would shift. Rights holders benefit from a legal landscape where the outcome of any given case is unpredictable, because unpredictability makes licensing the safer option. Girl Talk's continued existence as an unlicensed sampler is, paradoxically, evidence that the system is working as intended -- not as a mechanism for resolving disputes, but as a mechanism for maintaining the leverage that makes licensing the rational choice.

### Madlib / MF DOOM -- Madvillainy: Crate-Digging as Resistance

*Madvillainy* (2004), the collaboration between producer Madlib and rapper MF DOOM on Stones Throw Records, represents a different response to the clearance economy: strategic obscurity.

Madlib produced the album using minimal equipment -- a Boss SP-303 sampler, a turntable, and a tape deck. His samples spanned an extraordinary range: a 1942 film (*The Ghost of Frankenstein*), 1960s-70s instrumentals from around the world, Bollywood film soundtracks. The sourcing was deliberately obscure. Madlib traveled to Brazil, purchased multiple crates of vinyl records, and recorded hundreds of beats in his hotel room. He reportedly lost two of the crates he acquired `[single-source]`.

When asked to change certain instrumentals to address clearance concerns, Madlib reportedly lied about forgetting the samples in order to keep them on the album `[single-source]`. This anecdote -- if true -- reflects the tension between artistic vision and clearance requirements on independent labels with limited budgets. Stones Throw Records, as an independent label, operated with far smaller clearance budgets than major labels. The clearance strategy likely relied on a combination of formal clearance for identifiable samples and calculated risk-taking on obscure sources where rights holders were unlikely to discover or pursue the use.

*Madvillainy* is widely regarded as one of the greatest hip-hop albums ever made. Its existence depends partly on the fact that its source material was obscure enough to escape the clearance economy's full weight. If Madlib had built the album from well-known funk and soul recordings owned by major labels, the production approach would have been economically impossible on an independent label budget. The obscurity of his sources was not just an aesthetic choice -- it was a survival strategy.

This points to a structural distortion in the sample economy: the system does not equally affect all sampling. It most severely constrains sampling of commercially successful recordings -- exactly the recordings most likely to be culturally significant, most likely to be owned by major labels, and most likely to resonate with audiences. Sampling obscure Brazilian vinyl from flea markets is economically viable. Sampling James Brown is not. The clearance system does not prohibit building on the past. It prohibits building on the parts of the past that matter most.

### Vanilla Ice / "Ice Ice Baby": The $4 Million Bassline

Vanilla Ice's "Ice Ice Baby" (1990) sampled the bassline of Queen and David Bowie's "Under Pressure" without credit or payment. Queen and Bowie threatened litigation. The resolution: Vanilla Ice reportedly paid $4 million to acquire the publishing rights to "Under Pressure," resolving the dispute by buying the asset rather than licensing it.

This is the inverse of the typical sample clearance dynamic. Instead of paying for a license, Vanilla Ice acquired the underlying copyright. The $4 million price reflected both the value of the sample and the leverage that the rights holders -- two of the most commercially powerful artists in music history -- could bring to bear. For the vast majority of artists facing sampling disputes, buying the copyright is not an option. The Vanilla Ice case is instructive precisely because it illustrates a resolution available only to artists with extraordinary resources.

### Chance the Rapper: The Mixtape-Era Clearance Trap

Chance the Rapper's *Acid Rap* (2013) was released as a free mixtape in the tradition of hip-hop promotional culture -- no commercial sale, no label involvement, just music distributed for free to build an audience. The strategy worked. *Acid Rap* made Chance a star.

But when the mixtape era gave way to streaming, the uncleared samples became liabilities. "Juice," one of the mixtape's standout tracks, sampled Donny Hathaway's "Jealous Guy" -- written by John Lennon. The Lennon estate has rarely approved sample clearances since 1980. The track could not be released on streaming platforms. Chance was also sued by jazz musician Abdul Wali Muhammad over "Windows" from his earlier *10 Day* mixtape, which sampled Lonnie Liston Smith's "Bridge Through Time."

Chance's experience illustrates a temporal trap in the sample economy. The mixtape era (roughly 2005-2015) operated under an informal understanding: free mixtapes existed in a zone of tolerated infringement. Labels and publishers looked the other way because mixtapes were promotional, not commercial. But streaming eliminated the distinction between "free" and "commercial." Every track on a streaming platform generates revenue through advertising or subscription splits. The samples that were tolerated in a free-download context became actionable once the same music appeared on Spotify or Apple Music.

Artists who built their careers during the mixtape era now have catalogs that are partially inaccessible -- tracks that cannot be streamed because samples cannot be cleared, either because rights holders refuse (the Lennon estate), because the cost is prohibitive, or because the rights holder cannot be located. The informal tolerance of the mixtape era created a generation of music that exists in legal limbo -- too commercially valuable to ignore, too legally entangled to distribute.

### The Sample Chain Problem: Three 6 Mafia and Downstream Liability

Three 6 Mafia's "Sippin' on Some Syrup" (2000, featuring UGK and Project Pat) samples the instrumental from Marvin Gaye's 1978 "Is That Enough." The track itself has been extensively re-sampled by subsequent artists, creating a downstream sample chain where the Three 6 Mafia track becomes source material for the next generation.

This illustrates a structural complexity in the clearance system that gets little attention: sample chains. When Artist A samples Recording B, and later Artist C samples Artist A's track (which contains the sample of Recording B), Artist C technically needs clearance from both Artist A and the owner of Recording B. The deeper the chain, the more rights holders are involved. In practice, many downstream samplers do not realize they are sampling a sample -- they treat the intermediary recording as the source, unaware that it contains prior copyrighted material.

Three 6 Mafia and Juicy J drew heavily from soul, funk, and horror film soundtracks, with Willie Hutch being a frequently sampled source. Their production approach -- looping soul/funk samples over drum machine patterns -- became foundational to Memphis and Southern hip-hop production. Every subsequent artist who samples a Three 6 Mafia track is potentially sampling Willie Hutch, or Marvin Gaye, or Isaac Hayes, through the intermediary layer. The clearance obligation cascades backward through the chain of title, potentially involving rights holders who have no relationship to the sampling artist and no awareness that their work is being used.

The sample chain problem is a direct consequence of the two-copyright structure and the absence of a registry that tracks derivative works. There is no public database that documents which recordings contain which samples. WhoSampled.com, a crowdsourced platform, provides the most comprehensive resource, but it is not legally authoritative and does not cover all works. An artist sampling a track from 2000 has no reliable way to determine whether that track itself contains samples from 1978 that require separate clearance.

### Mac Miller / Lord Finesse: The Mixtape Myth

Mac Miller used the beat from Lord Finesse's 1995 track "Hip 2 Da Game" for "Kool Aid and Frozen Pizza," which appeared on his 2010 mixtape *K.I.D.S.* -- released as a free download on DatPiff.com.

Lord Finesse filed a $10 million lawsuit. At the time, *K.I.D.S.* had been downloaded more than 500,000 times, streamed over 450,000 times, and the video had nearly 24 million YouTube views.

The case highlighted a legal gray area that many hip-hop artists had relied on: the assumption that free mixtapes existed in a zone of tolerated infringement, where uncleared samples were accepted as part of the promotional economy. Miller's mixtape was free. He was not selling it. But it had generated enormous commercial value through YouTube ad revenue and career momentum -- the kind of exposure that translates directly into concert tickets, merchandise, and subsequent label deals.

The case settled confidentially in December 2012. The principle it established was clear: the "free mixtape" defense does not immunize artists from sample infringement claims, particularly when the work generates significant commercial attention and revenue through indirect channels. In the streaming era, where any uploaded song can accumulate millions of plays and ad impressions, the line between "free" and "commercial" has effectively disappeared.

## Part 4: The Publishing Power Structure and Masters Ownership

### The Big Three Publishers

Report 1 documented the consolidation of recorded music into three dominant groups. The publishing side mirrors this structure almost exactly. The three major music publishers control approximately 60.6% of global publishing revenue:

| Publisher | 2024 Global Market Share |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Sony Music Publishing | 25.2% |
| Universal Music Publishing Group (UMPG) | 23.2% |
| Warner Chappell Music (WCM) | 12.2% |
| **Major total** | **~60.6%** |
| Independent publishers (combined) | 39.4% |

These are the same corporate parents that control the major record labels. Universal Music Group owns both the world's largest recorded music company and the second-largest music publisher. Sony Music Entertainment and Sony Music Publishing share a corporate parent. Warner Music Group and Warner Chappell are the same entity. The consolidation documented in Report 1 applies to publishing with equal force.

Sony Music Publishing's share increased from 24.9% (2023) to 25.2% (2024). UMPG declined slightly from 23.3% to 23.2%. Warner Chappell dipped from 12.4% to 12.2% -- its first share decline since 2020. Independent publishers' collective share held steady at 39.4% in 2024 after four years of contraction. The trend is consolidation at the top, stability in the middle, and slow erosion of the independent share over time.

The structural consequence for sample clearance is direct. When 60.6% of global publishing is controlled by three corporate groups -- the same groups that control roughly 70% of recorded music -- the probability that any given sample requires negotiation with a major publisher approaches certainty for commercially significant recordings. The independent artist sampling a well-known track is almost guaranteed to face a major publisher on the composition side, a major label on the master side, or both.

### Publishers vs. Publishing Administrators: A Structural Distinction

Two fundamentally different models exist in publishing, and the distinction matters for the sample economy.

A **traditional publisher** (Sony Music Publishing, UMPG, Warner Chappell) takes ownership of a percentage of compositions -- often 50% in a co-publishing deal. In a typical co-pub arrangement, the writer receives 75% total: their 50% writer's share plus half of the publisher's 50%. The publisher actively pitches songs for sync placements, provides creative services, and retains ownership often beyond the contract term, potentially forever.

A **publishing administrator** (Songtrust, TuneCore Publishing, Kobalt) does not take ownership. They register songs with PROs and collection societies globally, collect royalties, and take a smaller commission -- typically up to 25% of collected royalties. Rights revert fully to the songwriter when the administration term ends.

For sample clearance purposes, the distinction matters because traditional publishers have permanent ownership stakes in compositions, meaning their approval is required for sample clearances in perpetuity. An administrator's involvement is temporary. But the market reality is that the songs most likely to be sampled -- commercially successful, culturally significant recordings from the 1960s through the 1990s -- are overwhelmingly controlled by traditional publishers, not administrators. The administration model is newer and predominantly serves contemporary independent songwriters.

### Sync Licensing: The Revenue Stream That Reinforces Catalog Value

Sync licensing -- the placement of music in TV, film, advertising, and video games -- has become one of the fastest-growing revenue categories. In 2024, sync licensing generated approximately $650 million in trade revenue, up 7.4% year-over-year according to IFPI data. The global sync market is projected to grow from approximately $5.9-6.8 billion (total market) to $12.1-12.9 billion by 2033.

Sync licensing matters for the sample economy because it increases the economic value of catalog ownership. Every additional revenue stream -- streaming, radio, sync, sample clearance -- makes catalogs more valuable, which justifies higher acquisition multiples, which attracts more institutional capital, which concentrates ownership further. The same recording that generates sync revenue from a Toyota commercial and streaming revenue from Spotify playlists also generates sample clearance revenue when a hip-hop producer wants to use two bars of it. Each revenue stream reinforces the others. Each makes the catalog worth more. And each makes the catalog owner's leverage in any clearance negotiation stronger, because the opportunity cost of licensing a sample at a low rate is higher when the recording is already generating income from multiple sources.

### How Publishing Compounds the Sample Clearance Problem

The dual-ownership structure means that when an independent artist wants to sample a recording from a major label's catalog, they often face the same corporate parent on both sides of the transaction. The master is owned by (say) Universal Music Group. The composition is controlled by Universal Music Publishing Group. Two negotiations, both with entities that ultimately report to the same corporate parent.

This creates a structural advantage for major-label artists sampling from within the same corporate family. If a Universal artist wants to sample a Universal-owned recording, the clearance can be handled internally -- an intra-company transaction where both the master and publishing sides have aligned incentives. If an independent artist wants the same sample, they face two separate external negotiations with entities that have no incentive to offer favorable terms.

The cost structure compounds accordingly. Publishing clearance fees of $1,500 to $2,500 (plus ongoing royalty percentage) stack on top of master clearance fees of $1,500 to $10,000 (plus potential rollover payments). Total per sample: $500 to $25,000 or more. Statutory damages for infringement: up to $150,000 per sample, per copy distributed. When a song has multiple publishers -- common with co-writes -- each must independently clear the sample. Missing one publisher can result in infringement claims even when all others have agreed.

### Masters Ownership: The Central Economic Lever

Under standard major-label recording contracts, the label finances recording costs via recoupable advances and in return owns the master recordings outright. The artist receives a royalty -- historically 12-20% of revenue -- but only after the advance recoups. The label retains ownership typically in perpetuity, or for the duration of copyright (life of the author plus 70 years, or 95 years for works made for hire).

The economics are stark: under a typical label deal, an artist receives 15-20% of revenue after recoupment. Under ownership, they receive 80-100% minus distribution fees of 10-30%. Catalog music -- older recordings -- generates roughly 50% of a major label's revenue but approximately 70% of its profits, because catalog requires minimal incremental promotion. Masters ownership is the central economic lever in recorded music. Whoever owns the master controls the asset.

This matters for the sample economy because it determines who sits on the other side of the negotiation table when an artist wants to sample a classic recording. In almost every case, it is not the original artist. It is the label that signed them -- or the corporate successor that acquired the label that signed them, or the investment fund that acquired the corporate successor. The chain of title stretches from the recording studio to the boardroom, and by the time it arrives, the original artist's interests are usually represented, at best, by a royalty statement.

### The Artists Who Fought Back

A handful of high-profile artists have challenged or circumvented the masters ownership structure:

**Taylor Swift** signed with Big Machine Label Group as a teenager. When Scooter Braun's Ithaca Holdings acquired Big Machine for over $300 million in June 2019 -- including Swift's masters -- she called it "my worst case scenario." Braun sold the masters to Shamrock Holdings for a reported $405 million. Swift began re-recording her catalog, releasing four "Taylor's Version" re-recordings that strategically devalued the originals. In May 2025, Swift purchased all her original masters back from Shamrock for approximately $360 million.

**Prince** signed to Warner Bros. Records in 1977. In 1993, he changed his name to an unpronounceable symbol, appeared publicly with "SLAVE" written on his face, and told industry figures: "Imagine yourself sitting in a room with the biggest of the big in the recording industry, and you have 'slave' written on your face. That changes the entire conversation." In 2014, Warner Bros. returned Prince's masters -- one of the first major instances of a label returning masters to an artist. Prince died in April 2016 without a will.

**Frank Ocean** released the visual album *Endless* in August 2016 to fulfill his contractual obligation to Def Jam (a UMG subsidiary), then immediately released *Blonde* the next day through his own label. He reportedly paid back a $2 million advance to retain ownership. By self-releasing, he increased his ownership from approximately 17% to 70% of royalties and secured a reported $20 million exclusivity deal from Apple Music `[single-source on the $20M figure]`.

**Jay-Z** co-founded Roc-A-Fella Records in 1995, retaining more control than typical artists. In 2024, his label operations were consolidated into Roc Nation Distribution, which operates on a model where independent artists retain ownership of their masters while receiving label services.

These cases are held up as victories. They are. But they are also exceptional -- available only to artists with extraordinary commercial leverage, legal resources, and public visibility. Taylor Swift spent $360 million to buy back her own recordings. Prince fought for two decades. Frank Ocean had to fulfill an entire album's contractual obligation to free himself. These are not models that generalize. They are escape stories from a system that most artists never escape.

### The Catalog Acquisition Boom

Report 1 documented the consolidation of the industry into three major groups. The catalog acquisition boom of 2018-2023 represents a second wave of consolidation -- this time driven not by labels but by private equity and institutional capital.

**Hipgnosis Song Fund**, founded by Merck Mercuriadis, launched in 2018 as a publicly listed vehicle for acquiring music catalogs. Mercuriadis argued that songs were "as valuable an asset as oil or gold." Hipgnosis spent more than $2.2 billion acquiring 105 catalog deals, including rights to works by Red Hot Chili Peppers, Shakira, Justin Bieber, Neil Young, and Blondie.

The financial reality was less inspiring. A due-diligence report revealed that Hipgnosis Song Management materially overstated the fund's revenue and EBITDA and supported acquisitions with analysis that "failed to meet music industry standards." 67 of 105 acquisition deals were worth less than their purchase price. 75% of the fund's catalogs missed growth forecasts by an average of 23% annually. Overall annual royalties fell from $134.2 million to $121.6 million. Dividends were suspended.

In April 2024, Blackstone agreed to acquire Hipgnosis for $1.6 billion ($1.31/share) -- a significant discount to the $2.2 billion Hipgnosis had spent assembling the portfolio. The fund was taken private in July 2024. In March 2025, the combined business was rebranded as Recognition Music Group.

Other institutional buyers include KKR (which backed BMG and purchased Kobalt Capital's catalog of approximately 65,000 songs for $1.1 billion), Primary Wave (active acquirer of legacy artist catalogs including a 50% stake in Prince's estate assets), and Round Hill Music (acquired by Concord for $468.8 million).

Current catalog valuation multiples: 16.1x Net Publisher Share (NPS) for publishing, 13.0x Net Label Share (NLS) for masters, with iconic/evergreen catalogs commanding 19-20x NPS or higher.

### What Catalog Acquisitions Mean for the Sample Economy

The wave of catalog acquisitions changes the sample clearance landscape in a specific way: it replaces human decision-makers with financial ones.

When a songwriter's estate controls their catalog, clearance decisions are made by people with some connection to the music -- family members, managers, creative associates. They may refuse clearance for artistic reasons or grant it on favorable terms because they appreciate what the sampling artist is doing with the material.

When a private equity fund controls the catalog, clearance decisions are made by portfolio managers whose fiduciary duty is to maximize return on investment. Every sample clearance request is evaluated as a revenue opportunity. The question is not "does this serve the music?" but "does this maximize the value of the asset?" The result, as widely discussed in the industry though not empirically documented, is more aggressive monetization: higher clearance fees, more active enforcement against uncleared samples, and less willingness to grant favorable terms to emerging artists.

The Hipgnosis experience suggests that overpaying for catalogs creates pressure to extract more revenue -- but in practice, many catalogs underperformed growth projections. The tension between acquisition multiples and actual revenue generation may moderate clearance fees in some cases. But the structural dynamic is clear: institutional capital treats music as a financial asset, and financial assets are managed to maximize yield.

### Section 203: The Industry's Most Underutilized Tool

Section 203 of the U.S. Copyright Act provides authors (or their heirs) with an inalienable right to terminate and reclaim any grant of copyright made on or after January 1, 1978. The right activates 35 years after the original grant. Written notice must be served between 2 and 10 years before the termination effective date. The right cannot be waived or contracted away, even if the original agreement says otherwise.

In theory, this means that any artist who signed a deal in 1978 or later can reclaim their masters 35 years later. In practice, the right is rarely exercised.

**Why it is underutilized:**

1. **Lack of awareness**: Many artists and their estates do not know the right exists.
2. **Complex procedural requirements**: Wrong dates, wrong parties, or improper service can invalidate the termination.
3. **Label opposition**: Labels routinely contest termination notices, requiring expensive litigation.
4. **Work-for-hire ambiguity**: If recordings qualify as work for hire, the termination right does not apply. The legal question is unresolved. In November 1999, an RIAA lobbyist inserted language into an unrelated bill adding "sound recordings" to the work-for-hire categories. The amendment was repealed in 2000 after furious artist opposition, but the underlying legal ambiguity persists. Labels continue to insert work-for-hire language into contracts.
5. **Relationship concerns**: Filing termination notices can damage ongoing business relationships.
6. **Time horizon**: 35 years is a long time. Many artists who signed deals in the late 1970s and 1980s are now elderly, deceased, or disengaged from the industry.

Recent cases suggest the landscape may be shifting. A jury sided with 2 Live Crew in a termination dispute with Lil' Joe Records, affirming their rights. Salt-N-Pepa filed suit alleging UMG refused to transfer rights after proper termination notice; the case is ongoing. But these are exceptions, not the norm. Section 203 remains the music industry's most powerful and least used tool for artist empowerment.

### Estate Case Studies: What Happens After Death

The fate of music estates reveals the power structure in its starkest form. When an artist dies, their catalog becomes an asset -- managed by executors, administrators, family members, and corporate partners whose interests may diverge from each other and from the artist's legacy.

**Prince**: Died in April 2016 without a will. Six siblings/half-siblings were ultimately recognized as heirs. Comerica Bank & Trust was appointed administrator. The estate was valued at $82.3 million by the administrator and $163.2 million by the IRS -- a gap of $81 million that took until January 2022 to resolve at approximately $156.4 million. Half went to Prince Legacy LLC (representing three siblings plus advisors). The other half was acquired by Primary Wave. Six years of proceedings. Tens of millions in legal and administrative costs. An estate valued at over $400 million by mid-2025 `[single-source]`. All because Prince did not leave a will.

**Michael Jackson**: Purchased ATV Music Publishing (including the Beatles catalog) for $47.5 million in 1985. Formed a joint venture with Sony in 1995. Sony acquired the estate's 50% stake in 2016 for $750 million. In 2024, the estate sold a 50% stake in Jackson's own publishing and masters to Sony for approximately $600 million (total catalog valued at approximately $1.2 billion). Total estimated earnings since his death in June 2009: $3.5 billion -- the highest posthumous earnings of any celebrity. An estate lawyer quoted by Forbes: "When it comes to estate earnings... It's MJ, then an enormous canyon, then everybody else."

**Nipsey Hussle**: The counterexample. After leaving Epic Records, Hussle founded All Money In No Money Out Records, an independent label that gave him full control. His brother Samiel Asghedom serves as estate administrator. The family intentionally waited years before releasing new music. An upcoming project, *Prolific*, was reportedly sequenced and curated by Nipsey himself before his death -- not a label-assembled posthumous compilation. Independent ownership meant the estate retained full control of masters and brand. But it also meant no label infrastructure for ongoing promotion and distribution. The burden falls entirely on family members.

**Juice WRLD**: Died December 8, 2019, at age 21. Signed to Grade A Productions in a joint venture with Interscope Records (a UMG subsidiary). He reportedly left behind over 3,000 unreleased tracks. Three posthumous albums have been released: *Legends Never Die* (2020), *Fighting Demons* (2021), *The Party Never Ends* (2024). Decisions rest primarily with his mother, Carmela Wallace, in consultation with Grade A Productions. The sheer volume of unreleased material creates both opportunity and ethical tension -- questions of curation, quality control, and whether the artist would have approved specific releases. The estate has stated Juice WRLD wanted his music heard. Critics have questioned whether three posthumous albums (and counting) honor the artist's vision or represent label-driven monetization.

The Juice WRLD case connects directly to the sample economy. "Lucid Dreams" -- the song that established him as a major artist -- gives 85% of its publishing to Sting. The estate's posthumous earnings are built partly on a catalog where a significant portion of revenue from one of the biggest songs flows to the original rights holder. The sample clearance system does not stop operating when the sampling artist dies. The royalty obligations persist. The power dynamics persist. The structural disadvantage persists.

The estate cases illustrate the endgame of the power structure. Artists who achieved independence in life -- Nipsey, Prince (eventually) -- leave their families in stronger positions. Artists whose copyrights were lost through bankruptcy (Isaac Hayes), exploitative contracts (countless others), or the simple passage of corporate time leave their families fighting to control music they no longer own. And artists whose biggest songs were built on samples or interpolations -- Juice WRLD, the Verve -- leave estates where a significant share of ongoing revenue flows not to their families but to the rights holders who controlled the source material.

## Part 5: The AI Horizon

### Three Tiers of Analysis

What follows uses a three-tier framing to distinguish between what we know, what we can reasonably infer, and what we are guessing.

- **FACT**: Documented events, filed lawsuits, enacted legislation, published rulings.
- **ANALYSIS**: Reasonable inferences from established facts, informed by the structural patterns documented in Reports 1-3.
- **SPECULATION**: Forward-looking projections that may or may not materialize.

### FACT: The Legal Landscape

The intersection of AI and music copyright is being contested across multiple fronts simultaneously.

**RIAA v. Suno and RIAA v. Udio (filed June 24, 2024)**: The major labels -- UMG, Sony, and Warner -- sued both AI music generation platforms for mass copyright infringement, alleging the companies trained their models on copyrighted sound recordings without authorization.

The settlement trajectory reveals the emerging strategy. Warner Music Group and UMG settled with Suno in late 2025, with Suno committing to launch a new model trained only on licensed material. Current models will be phased out. UMG settled with Udio on October 29, 2025, announcing plans for a joint "music creation, consumption and streaming" service launching in 2026. The deal structure is revealing: artists who opt in will be compensated both for training data use and when subscribers create new works using their music. Warner Music Group settled with Udio on November 19, 2025, with an opt-in licensing model.

Sony has not settled with either company. Both cases remain ongoing.

**Concord Music / ABKCO / UMG v. Anthropic**: Publishers filed suit in 2023 alleging Anthropic's Claude AI was trained on song lyrics from at least 500 songs without authorization. In January 2025, Anthropic agreed to prevent Claude from providing copyrighted lyrics. In March 2025, a federal judge denied the publishers' preliminary injunction, ruling they had not proven the licensing market was being undercut. In January 2026, publishers filed expanded claims covering more than 20,000 songs, with potential statutory damages exceeding $3 billion. The filing alleges Anthropic built a permanent internal library sourced from "pirate repositories" via BitTorrent.

**Thaler v. Perlmutter**: The foundational ruling on AI authorship. The D.C. Circuit unanimously affirmed in March 2025 that the Copyright Act requires human authorship -- AI cannot be listed as the author of a copyrighted work. The Supreme Court denied certiorari on March 2, 2026. The "human-only rule" for copyright authorship is now settled law.

**Zarya of the Dawn (2023)**: The Copyright Office granted limited registration for an AI-assisted comic book, establishing that human selection and arrangement of AI outputs can be copyrightable, but the AI-generated elements themselves are not. This principle -- that the human creative contribution is protectable, but the machine's output is not -- has direct implications for AI-generated music. A producer who uses AI to generate a sample and then arranges, edits, and contextualizes it within a larger composition may hold copyright in the arrangement. But the AI-generated sample itself, standing alone, has no copyright protection. It cannot be registered. It cannot be enforced. This creates an asymmetry: traditionally sampled music is copyrighted and must be cleared; AI-generated music is uncopyrighted and can be freely used by anyone. The implications for the sample economy are significant -- AI-generated sounds exist outside the copyright framework that makes sample clearance necessary.

**USCO Report Series**: The U.S. Copyright Office published two key reports. Part 2 (January 2025) affirmed that human authorship remains a "bedrock" requirement, that prompt selection alone is insufficient to yield copyrightable work, and that mixed human-AI works protect only the human contributions. Part 3 (May 2025) -- a 108-page analysis of fair use in AI training -- concluded that AI training is **not categorically fair use**. The Office stated: "Making commercial use of vast tropes of copyrighted works to produce expressive content that competes with them in existing markets... goes beyond established fair use boundaries." Wholesale taking of entire works "ordinarily weighs against fair use." The market effects analysis noted that AI may result in lost sales, market dilution, and lost licensing opportunities -- precisely the kind of market harm that weighs against fair use in the traditional four-factor analysis.

**Legislative responses**:

- **ELVIS Act (Tennessee, enacted March 2024)**: First U.S. legislation specifically protecting musicians from unauthorized AI voice cloning. Adds "voice" to right-of-publicity protections. Passed unanimously (93-0 in the House, 30-0 in the Senate). Effective July 1, 2024.
- **NO FAKES Act (reintroduced April 2025)**: Would establish a federal right of publicity for digital replicas. Bipartisan support. Supported by SAG-AFTRA, UMG, Warner, RIAA, Disney, Amazon, Adobe, Google, YouTube, and OpenAI. Opposed by EFF, Center for Democracy & Technology, and others on free expression grounds. Not yet passed.
- **TRAIN Act (introduced January 2026)**: Would require AI companies to provide copyright holders access to training records.
- **EU AI Act**: Copyright provisions in force as of August 2, 2025. Requires use of licensed or public-domain training materials. Transparency obligations for AI providers to publish "sufficiently detailed" summaries of training content. Opt-out rights for rightsholders. Penalties up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover. The European Parliament itself considers the summary requirement "completely inadequate."

**UK precedent (Getty Images v. Stability AI, November 2025)**: The English High Court's first major AI copyright ruling held that AI model weights are not a "copy" of images in the sense required by UK copyright law. Getty abandoned its primary copyright claims but prevailed on limited trademark claims. The ruling's implication for music: if model weights do not constitute copies, the question of whether AI music models "contain" the copyrighted recordings they were trained on becomes more complex.

### ANALYSIS: What the Settlements Reveal

The UMG/Suno and UMG/Udio settlement structures are the most important data points for understanding where the sample economy is headed.

The deal model: artists who opt in are compensated on two dimensions -- for the use of their recordings as training data, and on a per-creation basis when subscribers generate new works using those models. This is, structurally, a licensing framework that mirrors the existing sample clearance system -- but with the AI platform as an intermediary layer between the sampled catalog and the sampling artist.

Read that again. The major labels are positioning themselves to control the AI licensing layer using the same playbook they used for streaming: negotiate deals that give them a percentage of the platform's economics, create opt-in structures that flow through the labels' existing catalog infrastructure, and ensure that the labels -- not the original artists -- sit at the negotiating table.

The parallels to the streaming transition documented in Report 2 are structural, not superficial. When streaming emerged, the major labels negotiated equity stakes and advance payments from Spotify, Apple Music, and others -- deals that benefited the labels as corporate entities but did not necessarily flow through to individual artists in proportional ways. The AI licensing deals follow the same template: the labels negotiate on behalf of their catalogs, the artists receive whatever terms the labels pass through, and the fundamental power dynamic remains unchanged.

Sony's refusal to settle is also revealing. Sony is the only major that has not reached agreements with Suno or Udio. Whether this reflects a different strategic calculus (litigation as leverage for better terms) or a principled objection to AI training on copyrighted works, the practical effect is that Sony's catalog remains outside the licensing framework -- creating uncertainty for AI platforms and potentially giving Sony leverage to negotiate more favorable terms later.

The USCO's Part 3 report -- "not categorically fair use" -- provides the legal backdrop. If AI training is ultimately found to constitute fair use, the licensing model collapses because there is no legal obligation to license. If it is not fair use, the licensing model becomes mandatory and the question is simply who negotiates the terms (the labels, as they are currently doing) and at what price.

The structural analysis is straightforward: if the sample clearance economy is a system where major labels and publishers act as gatekeepers between artists and the raw materials of creation, the AI licensing economy is poised to be the same system with different raw materials. Instead of clearing a sample of a specific recording, artists will pay to use a model trained on that recording. The gatekeeper changes form but not function.

### SPECULATION: What Comes Next

AI-generated samples as the new crate-digging. If an artist can generate a sound that evokes the character of a vintage soul horn section without sampling any specific recording, the traditional clearance economy loses its economic rationale. The AI platform becomes the sample library. The subscription fee becomes the clearance cost. And the entities that control training data -- the entities that own the catalogs -- become the entities that determine what sounds are available.

Synthetic interpolation. If AI can analyze a melody and generate a new melody that captures its "feel" without reproducing its specific notes, the distinction between inspiration and infringement -- already blurred by *Williams v. Gaye* -- becomes even harder to draw. "Style transfer" may be the next legal frontier: can you copyright a style? Current law says no. But current law also said you could not copyright a "groove" until a jury in *Blurred Lines* said otherwise.

On-chain provenance. Several initiatives are developing blockchain-based systems where every AI generation triggers micro-payments distributed to artists who opted into the training dataset, with payout splits embedded in smart contract metadata. The rationale is that blockchain's immutability removes intermediaries from the payment chain. The limitation is that on-chain provenance solves the tracking problem but does not resolve the underlying legal question of whether AI training requires consent. And the history of music industry technology adoption -- as documented in Reports 1 through 3 -- suggests that the entities with the most to gain from intermediary positions will resist systems that disintermediate them.

### ANALYSIS: The Clearance Economy Collapse Scenario

Consider the scenario in detail. An independent hip-hop producer in 2027 wants to build a track around a sound that evokes the character of a 1970s soul horn section -- the kind of sound that, in the sample-based tradition, would have been sourced by crate-digging through vinyl collections and finding the perfect two-bar phrase from an obscure Stax or Motown session.

Under the current system, the producer would need to identify the specific recording, trace its ownership through potentially decades of corporate transactions, negotiate master and publishing clearances separately, pay $4,000 to $15,000 in advances (if the rights holders agree at all), and accept ongoing royalty obligations. The process takes months. The outcome is uncertain.

Under an AI-mediated system, the producer opens an AI music generation tool, types a description of the sound they want, and receives an original audio clip in seconds. The clip sounds like a 1970s soul horn section because the model was trained on thousands of such recordings. But it does not reproduce any specific recording. There is no sample to clear because there is no sample. There is only a statistical echo of a genre's sonic characteristics, rendered as new audio.

Who loses in this scenario? The clearance intermediaries -- lawyers, brokers, specialists like DMG Clearances. The legacy catalog owners -- major labels and publishers who derive revenue from licensing their back catalogs for sampling. The session musicians and producers whose original recordings generated residual income from clearance fees. The entire ecosystem that the sample clearance system sustained.

Who gains? The independent producer, who can now create sonically complex productions without the $15,000 barrier to entry. The AI platform operator, who becomes the new intermediary -- the entity that trained the model effectively replaces the sample library. And, potentially, artists who opt into licensed AI training, receiving compensation through the UMG/Suno and UMG/Udio deal structures.

But look more closely at those deal structures. The UMG/Udio settlement creates a joint "music creation, consumption and streaming" service. Artists who opt in receive dual compensation -- for training data and for per-creation usage. The opt-in flows through UMG. The terms are set by UMG. The independent artist whose recording was included in UMG's training data catalog receives whatever pass-through terms UMG negotiates on their behalf. The structural relationship is identical to the one documented in Report 2 for streaming: the label sits between the artist and the platform, negotiating terms that serve the label's interests and passing through a fraction to the artist.

This is the recurring pattern of the music industry's relationship with technology. When a new distribution mechanism emerges -- radio, television, CDs, digital downloads, streaming, AI -- the first wave of disruption creates a moment of vulnerability for incumbents. Then the incumbents reposition themselves as gatekeepers of the new mechanism. The technology changes. The power structure adapts.

**Synthetic interpolation.** If AI can analyze a melody and generate a new melody that captures its "feel" without reproducing its specific notes, the distinction between inspiration and infringement -- already blurred by *Williams v. Gaye* -- becomes even harder to draw. "Style transfer" may be the next legal frontier: can you copyright a style? Current law says no. Musical style is not copyrightable expression. But current law also said you could not copyright a "groove" until a jury in *Blurred Lines* said otherwise. The Williams v. Gaye precedent, combined with AI's ability to generate music that is sonically similar to specific artists without reproducing specific recordings, creates a legal gray zone that will likely be tested in court within the next five years.

**On-chain provenance.** Several initiatives are developing blockchain-based systems where every AI generation triggers micro-payments distributed to artists who opted into the training dataset, with payout splits embedded in smart contract metadata. The rationale is that blockchain's immutability removes intermediaries from the payment chain, enabling direct artist-to-artist compensation without label or publisher mediation. The limitation is that on-chain provenance solves the tracking problem but does not resolve the underlying legal question of whether AI training requires consent. And the history of music industry technology adoption -- as documented in Reports 1 through 3 -- suggests that the entities with the most to gain from intermediary positions will resist systems that disintermediate them. The major labels have no incentive to support a provenance system that allows artists to license directly to AI platforms, bypassing the label's aggregation function.

**The $2.5 billion in unclaimed royalties.** An estimated $2.5 billion in music royalties already goes unclaimed due to inadequate rights tracking. AI-generated music further complicates provenance and attribution. If a model trained on 100,000 recordings generates a new piece of audio, what is the attribution? Which of the 100,000 source recordings contributed to the output? How should royalties be divided? The existing infrastructure for tracking music rights -- PROs, the MLC, SoundExchange -- was built for a world where each song has identifiable authors and a clear chain of ownership. AI-generated music may not fit these structures at all.

**The most likely outcome**, based on the structural patterns this series has documented, is that AI does not destroy the sample economy. It reorganizes it. The gatekeepers remain the same -- the entities that own the catalogs that train the models. The costs shift from per-sample clearance fees to per-platform licensing fees. And the fundamental asymmetry persists: major labels, which own the largest catalogs, will command the most licensing revenue from AI platforms, while independent artists will face a choice between opting into major-label-controlled AI licensing frameworks or generating AI sounds from models trained on smaller, less commercially valuable datasets.

The technology changes. The power structure adapts. It always has.

## Part 6: What the Sample Economy Reveals

### The Proof Case

This series has documented, across three prior reports, a power structure built on consolidation, sustained by economics, and maintained by law. This fourth report has attempted to prove all three operating simultaneously in a single transaction.

When an independent artist wants to sample a recording owned by a major label, they experience the entire structure at once.

**The consolidation** (Report 1): The recording they want to sample is owned by one of three corporate groups that control approximately 70% of recorded music revenue globally. The composition is owned by one of three publishing companies -- often a subsidiary of the same corporate group -- that control approximately 60.6% of global publishing. The chain of title for the recording may pass through multiple corporate acquisitions, bankruptcies, and catalog sales before reaching its current owner. The artist who created the original music may be dead, or bankrupt, or wholly disconnected from the corporate entity that controls their work.

**The economics** (Report 2): The clearance costs range from $4,000 to $15,000 in advances alone, plus ongoing royalties -- per sample. An independent artist with a $5,000 recording budget cannot afford a single sample. A major-label artist has clearance costs absorbed by the label. The streaming economy generates approximately $0.008 per stream before splits, making it nearly impossible for a sample-heavy track to recoup clearance costs without hundreds of thousands of plays. The production styles that defined hip-hop's creative peak -- multi-sample collages by the Bomb Squad, De La Soul, the Beastie Boys -- are economically extinct.

**The legal framework** (Report 3): The law provides no compulsory license for sampling, unlike cover songs. Two federal circuits apply contradictory rules for the same creative practice. Statutory damages of up to $150,000 per infringement make uncleared sampling an existential risk. The Supreme Court has not resolved the circuit split. The legal ambiguity serves the entities selling licenses, not the entities buying them. And the case law that established these rules -- *Grand Upright*, *Bridgeport* -- was set in cases where individual artists bore the costs while corporate catalog owners reaped the structural benefits.

Every element of the power structure converges on this single transaction. The sample clearance system is not a peripheral feature of the music industry. It is the mechanism through which the industry's fundamental dynamics -- who has power, how money flows, what rules apply -- are enacted in real time, on individual human beings, every time someone wants to build something new from something that came before.

### What Would Need to Change

If the goal is a system where sampling is accessible, fair, and transparent -- where the creative practice that helped build hip-hop, electronic music, and countless other genres is not restricted to artists who can afford five-figure clearance fees -- several structural changes would be necessary.

**A compulsory license for sampling**. Cover songs have a compulsory license under Section 115: anyone can record any previously released song by paying the statutory rate. No permission required. The original songwriter receives guaranteed compensation. The market for cover recordings functions because neither side has a veto. A similar mechanism for sampling -- a statutory rate for the use of short segments of existing recordings, with guaranteed compensation to the original rights holders but no ability to block the use -- would fundamentally alter the power dynamics documented in this report. It would remove the gatekeeper function that makes sample clearance a mechanism of structural control.

**Resolution of the circuit split**. The Supreme Court could resolve the Bridgeport/VMG Salsoul split by granting certiorari in a case that presents the issue cleanly. Whether the Court adopts the Sixth Circuit's bright-line rule or the Ninth Circuit's de minimis approach, a uniform national standard would eliminate forum shopping and reduce the legal uncertainty that currently benefits incumbents.

**Transparency in clearance costs and terms**. The clearance process is opaque by design. No public data exists on aggregate denial rates, typical clearance timelines, or the distribution of clearance costs across different types of requesting artists. The asymmetry of information -- where major labels have dedicated clearance departments with institutional knowledge and independent artists have nothing -- could be partially addressed by requiring disclosure of clearance terms in the same way that public companies disclose material contracts.

**Section 203 awareness and enforcement**. If more artists and their estates exercised the 35-year termination right, the ownership landscape would shift over time. The catalogs that major labels and publishers currently control would gradually return to artists and their families. This would not eliminate the sample clearance system, but it would change who sits on the other side of the negotiation.

**AI licensing frameworks that center artists, not labels**. As the UMG/Suno and UMG/Udio settlements demonstrate, the AI licensing layer is being built now. The structural question is whether these frameworks will replicate the power dynamics of the existing system -- with labels negotiating on behalf of their catalogs and passing through whatever terms they choose -- or whether they will create direct relationships between the creators of source material and the artists who use AI to build on it.

**Rethinking the "two-copyright" structure**. The dual-copyright system -- composition and sound recording as separately owned, separately licensed assets -- is the mechanical core of the clearance problem. For cover songs, the compulsory license collapses this complexity: pay the statutory rate and you can record the composition. The master is not implicated because you are making a new recording. For sampling, both copyrights are implicated, and neither has a compulsory mechanism. A statutory framework that treated sampling as a single transaction -- requiring one payment to a collective body that distributes to both master and composition owners -- would reduce transaction costs, eliminate the problem of asymmetric clearance (where one side approves and the other refuses), and bring sampling closer to the economic logic that makes cover songs workable.

**International harmonization**. The Kraftwerk v. Pelham saga -- 27 years and counting over two seconds of audio -- demonstrates the cost of jurisdictional fragmentation. The Berne Convention guarantees that foreign works receive the same protection as domestic works, but the scope of that protection varies by jurisdiction. The CJEU's approach (recognizable samples infringe, unrecognizable ones do not) resembles the Ninth Circuit's de minimis standard. A global framework that aligned the U.S., EU, and UK approaches would reduce forum shopping and provide artists with consistent rules regardless of where their music is distributed.

None of these changes are politically probable. Each would face opposition from the entities that benefit from the current system. The RIAA, NMPA, and their member companies have the lobbying infrastructure, the legal resources, and the institutional relationships to block reforms that reduce their gatekeeping power -- as documented in Report 3, the same entities that benefit from the rules are the entities that write them. Report 3 documented the RIAA's $6.9 million in lobbying expenditures in 2024. It documented the revolving door between congressional staff and industry trade groups. It documented the Mitch Glazier episode -- a legislative aide who inserted the work-for-hire amendment into an unrelated bill and subsequently became the RIAA's Chairman and CEO. The infrastructure that maintains the current system is robust, well-funded, and deeply embedded in the legislative process.

But probable and necessary are different words. And the gap between them is where the work happens.

### What the Sample Economy Tells Us About the Industry

The sample clearance system is not broken. It is functioning exactly as designed. It concentrates power in the hands of catalog owners, extracts maximum value from artists who want to build on existing works, maintains structural advantages for major-label artists over independent ones, and uses legal ambiguity to ensure that licensing -- and the fees that come with it -- remains the rational default.

The artists documented in this report -- Isaac Hayes, whose family cannot control the songs he wrote; Biz Markie, whose career was ended by a ruling that cited the Ten Commandments; De La Soul, whose music was locked away for 34 years; Richard Ashcroft, who received $1,000 from one of the decade's biggest songs -- are not victims of a system that malfunctioned. They are participants in a system that worked. It worked for the catalog owners. It worked for the corporate intermediaries. It worked for the entities that wrote the rules and staffed the lobbying operations and negotiated the deals.

It did not work for the artists.

This is the throughline of the entire series. Report 1 showed that three corporate groups consolidated control of the American recorded music industry through a century of acquisitions, mergers, and market power. Report 2 showed that money flows through these structures in ways that systematically favor the entities at the top -- labels, publishers, platforms -- over the artists who create the music. Report 3 showed that the legal and regulatory framework was built by and for these entities, through lobbying, litigation, and the revolving door between industry and government.

Report 4 has shown all three forces converging on a single transaction. The sample clearance system is the proof case. It is where consolidation meets economics meets law, and the result is a system where an independent artist who wants to use two seconds of a forty-year-old recording must negotiate with a corporate entity that acquired the rights through a chain of transactions the original artist never benefited from, at prices the independent artist cannot afford, under legal rules that were written to protect the interests of catalog owners, in a jurisdiction where the legal standard depends on which side of a circuit split you happen to be standing on.

The sample clearance system did not create the music industry's power structure. But it is the mechanism that makes that power structure legible. Every cleared sample is a transaction where you can see the consolidation, count the money, and read the law. And every uncleared sample -- every track that was never made because the clearance was too expensive, every artist who gave up because the rights holder would not respond, every creative vision that was modified or abandoned because the economics did not work -- is a ghost in the machine. You cannot count what was never created. But you can understand the system that prevented its creation.

That understanding is the purpose of this series.

### The Final Accounting

Four reports. One structure.

Report 1 traced a century of consolidation that reduced a diverse ecosystem of labels, distributors, and publishers into three corporate groups that control roughly 70% of recorded music revenue and 60.6% of global publishing. Report 2 followed the money through those structures and found recoupment traps, opaque accounting, platform economics that favor scale over quality, and a streaming economy where per-stream rates compress the value of recorded music while catalog assets inflate in value. Report 3 documented the laws that built and maintain these structures -- copyright acts shaped by industry lobbying, consent decrees that constrain songwriter rates while label rates float freely, a revolving door between government and industry, and a lobbying asymmetry where the entities that benefit most from the status quo spend the most to preserve it.

Report 4 showed all of it converging on a single transaction. The sample clearance system is small enough to understand in detail and large enough to encompass every structural dynamic this series has documented. It is the music industry's power structure in miniature -- readable, traceable, and unjust in ways that are not abstract but specific. Specific dollar amounts demanded from specific artists by specific corporate entities under specific legal rules that were written by specific lobbyists to serve specific interests.

Isaac Hayes lost his copyrights in a 1976 bankruptcy. Forty-eight years later, his family still cannot fully control the use of songs he co-wrote. The rights passed through Stax to Fantasy to Concord, and through publishers to UMPG and Warner Chappell. The chain of title is a map of the power structure.

Biz Markie was told "thou shalt not steal" by a judge who cited the Ten Commandments instead of copyright doctrine. His career never recovered. The labels' catalogs became more valuable.

De La Soul waited 34 years for their music to reach streaming. The delay was caused by sample clearance complexity compounded by an exploitative contract. When the music finally arrived, some tracks had to be altered because samples could not be cleared at any price.

Richard Ashcroft received $1,000 from "Bitter Sweet Symphony." Allen Klein's estate received the rest for 22 years. Resolution came not through the legal system but through the personal goodwill of Mick Jagger and Keith Richards, after Klein had died.

These are not outliers. They are the system working as designed. The sample clearance system concentrates power in catalog owners, extracts value from artists who build on existing works, maintains structural advantages for major-label artists over independents, and uses legal ambiguity to ensure that licensing remains the rational default. It was built by the entities it serves. It is maintained by the laws they lobbied into existence. And it will be adapted to whatever technology comes next -- streaming, AI, blockchain, whatever follows -- unless the fundamental dynamics are addressed.

The power structure is revealed. What happens next is not this series' question to answer. It is yours.

### Data Gaps

The following data does not exist in publicly available sources:

- Aggregate statistics on how many sample clearance requests are denied vs. approved
- Total industry spending on sample clearance fees
- Systematic comparison of clearance costs for the same sample when requested by major-label vs. independent artists
- Detailed financial terms of most clearance deals (typically confidential)
- Complete clearance strategy for DJ Shadow's *Endtroducing*
- Exact terms of most sampling settlements (typically confidential)

These gaps are themselves a feature of the system's opacity.
