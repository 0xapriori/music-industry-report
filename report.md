# Power Structures Revealed: A History of the US Recorded Music Industry

*From the Post-War Boom to the Big Three and Beyond*

---

## Axioms and Starting Assumptions

Before proceeding, the reader should understand the foundational assumptions this report operates under:

1. **Starting point**: We begin at the end of World War II (~1945), the recognized inflection point where recorded music became a mass commercial enterprise in the United States. The pre-war era (Edison's phonograph in 1877, the early Columbia and Victor labels, the shellac era) is acknowledged as foundational but is not the focus. Where pre-war context is essential, it is briefly noted.

2. **"Major" vs. "Independent"**: Throughout this report, a "major" label is defined not by brand recognition or roster size, but by **control of distribution**. The majors own their distribution infrastructure; independents rely on third-party or major-label distribution to get their music to market. This distinction is the structural fault line of the industry.

3. **Revenue lens**: We primarily examine **recorded music revenue** (sales of physical media, downloads, and streams) as the economic backbone of the industry. Other revenue streams — live performance, publishing, merchandise, sync licensing — are discussed where they intersect with recorded music economics, particularly in the context of 360 deals and the streaming era.

4. **Revenue figures**: Unless otherwise noted, revenue figures are presented in **nominal US dollars** at estimated retail value, sourced from RIAA published data. Where inflation adjustment materially changes the interpretation, it is noted. The RIAA shifted to wholesale reporting in 2025, which is flagged where relevant.

5. **No fabricated data**: Every quantitative claim in this report is sourced. Where a figure could only be verified from a single source, it is marked `[single-source]`. Where sources disagree on a figure, the range is presented. Where data is unavailable, the gap is stated rather than filled with estimates.

## Part I: The Post-War Explosion and the Rise of the Independents (1945–1965)

### The Vacuum

After World War II, US record sales surged from 275 million to 400 million units between 1946 and 1947 alone. The established major labels of the era — Columbia, RCA Victor, Decca, and Capitol — had largely abandoned the "race records" market during the war. Shellac shortages forced them to economize, and they cut their least profitable lines first, which meant Black music.

This created a vacuum. **An estimated 1,000 new independent labels formed in the United States between 1948 and 1954**, rushing to serve the market the majors had abandoned.

*Sources: University of Oregon Open Text (Pay for Play), Britannica (Independent Record Labels), History of Rock. The 1,000-label figure appears in both the Oregon textbook and History of Rock.*

### The Independents Who Built the Foundation

The labels founded in this era did not merely fill a gap — they created the genres that would define American popular music for the next half-century. Nearly every foundational rock and roll, R&B, and soul artist recorded first for an independent label.

**Atlantic Records (founded 1947, New York)** — Founded by Ahmet Ertegun, the son of a Turkish diplomat, with partner Herb Abramson. Jerry Wexler joined in 1953 and became a defining creative force. Atlantic initially focused on rhythm and blues, signing artists like Ray Charles, then became the dominant soul label of the 1960s with Wilson Pickett, Otis Redding, and Aretha Franklin. Atlantic was also a technical pioneer — among the earliest labels to record in stereo. `[single-source on "first to record in stereo" — University of Oregon/History of Rock; Atlantic was certainly an early adopter]`

*Sources: PBS American Masters (Ahmet Ertegun), TeachRock, Wikipedia (Atlantic Records)*

**Chess Records (founded 1947, Chicago)** — Founded by Leonard and Phil Chess, Polish-Jewish immigrants who had previously operated nightclubs on Chicago's South Side. Chess specialized in Chicago blues, recording Muddy Waters and Howlin' Wolf, and later Chuck Berry — one of the foundational architects of rock and roll.

*Sources: University of Oregon Open Text, University of Idaho*

**Sun Records (founded 1950, Memphis)** — Founded by Sam Phillips, who ran the Memphis Recording Service before launching the label. Between 1954 and 1956, Sun recorded the first commercial recordings of Elvis Presley, Carl Perkins, Jerry Lee Lewis, and Johnny Cash, pioneering the "rockabilly" sound. Phillips also introduced production techniques like slapback echo that became genre-defining.

*Sources: Tennessee Encyclopedia, University of Oregon Open Text*

**King Records (founded 1943, Cincinnati)** — Founded by Syd Nathan. Uniquely, King recorded both "hillbilly" (country) and R&B artists under the same roof — an extraordinary decision for the segregated 1940s. By 1949, King was the sixth-largest label in the US and one of the largest independents. The label accumulated nearly 500 singles on the R&B, country, and pop charts, with 32 reaching No. 1. King's Federal Records subsidiary launched James Brown's career. Nathan was posthumously inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1997.

*Sources: Wikipedia (King Records), Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Library, Billboard*

**Motown Records (founded 1959, Detroit)** — Berry Gordy borrowed $800 from his family to start Tamla Records on January 12, 1959; it was reincorporated as Motown Record Corporation on April 14, 1960. Gordy modeled the operation on Detroit's automobile assembly lines: separate specialists handled songwriting, arrangement, tracking, overdubs, vocals, and final quality-control approval. The in-house band (the Funk Brothers) and songwriting teams (Holland-Dozier-Holland, Smokey Robinson) created a consistent, crossover-friendly "Motown Sound." The first million-selling hit was "Shop Around" by the Miracles in 1960. Motown became the largest and most successful Black-owned entertainment business in the country during the 1960s.

*Sources: Motown Museum, Wikipedia (Berry Gordy), CBS Detroit, Detroit Historical Society*

**Stax Records (founded 1957, Memphis, as Satellite Records)** — Founded by siblings Jim Stewart and Estelle Axton (Stax = Stewart + Axton). Axton mortgaged her home to invest $2,500 in an Ampex tape recorder. They converted a former movie theater on East McLemore Street into their studio. Stax pioneered "Southern soul" and the "Memphis Sound," featuring racially integrated house bands (Booker T. & the M.G.'s) — unprecedented in the segregated South. Stax went bankrupt in 1975, but its artistic legacy is immeasurable.

*Sources: Stax Records official history, Wikipedia (Stax Records), Tennessee Encyclopedia*

### Why Indies Broke Black Artists When Majors Wouldn't

Of the first generation of commercially successful rock and roll singers, nearly all recorded for independent labels that initially served the R&B market: Fats Domino for Imperial, Chuck Berry for Chess, Little Richard for Specialty, Elvis Presley and Carl Perkins for Sun. The major labels had systematically ignored this market. Independent labels were often the only platforms available for Black musicians to release and distribute their work.

The indie entrepreneurs who founded these labels were frequently outsiders themselves — immigrants (the Chess brothers), minorities, or people operating outside the mainstream music establishment. They recognized the commercial and artistic value of music the majors dismissed.

*Sources: Britannica (Independent Record Labels), History of Rock*

### The Economics of Early Indies

The independent label advantage was **low overhead**: often one person (the owner) filled the roles of A&R, producer, promoter, and distributor. Because most lacked nationwide distribution networks, they concentrated on signing artists popular in their specific region and relied on independent distributors to reach other markets.

However, the economics were precarious:

- **Publishing exploitation**: When label owners discovered that song publishers were legally entitled to two cents per title per record sold, many became publishers themselves. Some bought out writers' shares for a few dollars, taking all proceeds. This was standard practice, not the exception.
- **Cash flow crises**: Major hits could paradoxically put poorly capitalized labels out of business. Manufacturing and distribution costs had to be paid upfront, and revenue from a hit single could take months to arrive — if it arrived at all.
- **Distributor leverage**: Independent distributors were given attractive terms including the right to return unsold records and 90-day payment windows, which squeezed label cash flow further.

The structural vulnerability was clear: independent labels could discover and develop talent, but they lacked the infrastructure to scale. This created the recurring pattern that would define the industry for decades — **indies innovate, majors acquire**.

*Sources: University of Oregon Open Text, History of Rock, TMorganOnline*

## Part II: Payola, FM Radio, and the Shifting of Power (1955–1975)

### Payola: The First Crisis of Gatekeeping

Payola — a portmanteau of "pay" and "Victrola" — was the practice of record labels or their promoters paying radio disc jockeys cash or gifts to play specific records. The practice was not new; music publishers had paid performers to plug songs since the vaudeville era. But radio made it enormously powerful as a gatekeeping mechanism. If a DJ wouldn't play your record, it didn't exist.

### The Congressional Hearings (1959–1960)

The House Subcommittee on Legislative Oversight launched hearings in 1959. In closed and open sessions, **335 disc jockeys from around the country admitted to receiving over $263,000 in "consulting fees."**

The hearings produced two dramatically different outcomes for the two most prominent DJs of the era:

**Alan Freed**, the DJ widely credited with popularizing the term "rock and roll," refused "on principle" to sign an affidavit stating he had never accepted payola. WABC fired him. On May 19, 1960, Freed and seven other DJs were arrested and charged with commercial bribery. In December 1962, he pleaded guilty to two counts and was fined $300 with a suspended sentence. The IRS also pursued him for $38,000 in unpaid taxes. Freed died on January 20, 1965, in Palm Springs, California, of uremia and cirrhosis. He was 43.

**Dick Clark**, host of American Bandstand, had far more extensive financial entanglements: part ownership in seven indie labels, six publishers, three record distributors, and two talent agencies. However, Clark **divested himself of all these interests before testifying**. Committee chairman Oren Harris called him "a fine young man." Clark received effectively no punishment and continued his career for decades.

Multiple historians have observed that racial dynamics likely played a role in the disparity — Freed championed Black artists and integrated audiences, while Clark's show was more mainstream. This is an analytical interpretation, not a documented finding of Congress, but it appears consistently across academic and journalistic sources.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Payola), HISTORY.com, EBSCO Research Starters, CultureSonar, In These Times, Performing Songwriter, Washington Examiner*

### Legislative Outcome — And Its Limits

In 1960, Congress amended the Federal Communications Act to outlaw under-the-table payments and require broadcasters to disclose if airplay for a song had been purchased. Payola was made a **misdemeanor** with penalties of up to $10,000 in fines and one year in prison.

**Did payola end? No — it changed form.** The practice evolved into the "independent promoter" system in the 1980s–1990s. Major labels hired independent promoters as middlemen to liaise with radio stations, creating legal distance from direct payments. Major labels were paying as much as **$80 million per year** to these middlemen during the 1980s. `[single-source on the $80M figure — NPR. The practice itself is widely documented.]`

The consolidation of radio in the 1990s (discussed below) further corporatized payola, integrating it into larger business structures rather than the one-DJ-one-envelope model of the 1950s.

*Sources: NPR (2017), Wikipedia (Payola), The Regulatory Review (2024)*

### The FM Radio Revolution

In July 1964, the FCC adopted a non-duplication rule prohibiting FM stations from simply simulcasting their AM counterparts' programming. AM/FM station owners fought the regulation, delaying enactment until January 1, 1967. This was the pivotal moment: FM stations suddenly needed original programming, and they filled the void cheaply by turning to **freeform formats**.

**Tom Donahue** at KMPX in San Francisco pioneered the freeform FM format, where DJs had freedom to play long sets spanning genres, including obscure album tracks, not just singles. This new format helped shift rock's primary artistic vehicle from the single to the album — a transformation that reshaped the entire economics of the music business.

Throughout the 1970s, freeform radio was gradually commercialized into **Album-Oriented Rock (AOR)** — a more structured format that still played album tracks but with tighter playlists. Radio consultants Kent Burkhart and Lee Abrams were particularly influential in this evolution. By the early 1980s, AOR stations were commercially dominant.

The format shift from singles to albums had profound economic implications: labels could charge $8–12 for an album instead of $1 for a single. The album became both the primary artistic format and the primary revenue unit. This set the stage for the CD era's extraordinary profitability.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Album-Oriented Rock), Encyclopedia.com, uDiscover Music*

## Part III: The Corporate Era and the CD Windfall (1975–1999)

### The Compact Disc Revolution

The compact disc, co-developed by Philips and Sony, entered the US market in 1983 at just 0.5% of recorded music sales. CDs overtook cassettes in 1991 and reached a peak market share of 95.5% in 2002.

The CD was the most profitable format the music industry had ever seen:

| Metric | Vinyl/Cassette Era | CD Era |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Manufacturing cost per unit | Higher (vinyl pressing) | ~$4.50 including packaging `[single-source — Spokesman-Review, 1995]` |
| Wholesale price | Lower | $10.72–$11.20 |
| Retail list price | $8–10 | ~$16.98 |
| Profit margin for labels | Lower | Substantially higher |

A 1995 breakdown of a typical $16.98 CD retail price `[single-source — Spokesman-Review]`:
- **35%** to the retail store
- **27%** to the record company
- **16%** to the artist
- **13%** to the manufacturer
- **9%** to the distributor

### The Catalog Windfall

A significant portion of the CD boom's revenue came from consumers **re-purchasing music they already owned on vinyl**. The major labels aggressively marketed CDs as a replacement format, encouraging consumers to rebuild their record collections. This was selling the same content twice — a one-time windfall that inflated revenue figures and created unsustainable expectations about the industry's "natural" size.

### Revenue at the Peak

- In 1990, cassette album sales ($3.47 billion) and CD album sales ($3.45 billion) were roughly equal.
- By 1999, total US recorded music revenue peaked at **$14.6 billion** in nominal dollars.
- Adjusted for inflation, the 1999 peak was approximately **$22.7–$24 billion** (the range reflects different inflation base years).
- CD sales alone reached **$13.2 billion** in 2000.

*Sources: RIAA Revenue Database, Digital Music News, AEI, Visual Capitalist, Music Business Research. The inflation-adjusted range ($22.7B–$24B) reflects different base years across sources.*

### Radio Consolidation and the Telecommunications Act of 1996

The Telecommunications Act of 1996 eliminated the national cap on radio station ownership (previously 40 stations) and raised local ownership limits. It was the first major overhaul of telecommunications law since 1934.

The results were immediate and dramatic:

- **2,045 radio stations** were sold in the year following the Act for a net value of $13.6 billion.
- Within five years, radio station ownership dropped from **5,100 owners to 3,800**.
- **Clear Channel** (now iHeartMedia) expanded from approximately **40 stations in 1996 to over 1,200 by 2001**, spending roughly $30 billion.
- By 2001, Clear Channel and Viacom together claimed **42% of the commercial radio listening audience**.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Radio Homogenization), Wikipedia (iHeartMedia), Vanderbilt University study, VICE, 35000 Watts*

### What Radio Consolidation Meant for Music

Before consolidation, radio programming was a **regional art** — local DJs and program directors curated playlists and could break unknown artists. After consolidation:

- **National playlists** were devised centrally for all stations within a format.
- Market researchers and consultants replaced local DJ autonomy in song selection.
- Cookie-cutter playlists emerged, compiled to be "as widely appealing as possible rather than basing song choices on merit."
- New independent and local talent lost their primary avenue to radio exposure.

Radio consolidation and label consolidation were **mutually reinforcing**. As fewer radio companies controlled airplay, only labels with the resources to work the consolidated system could get their artists heard. This favored the majors and further squeezed independents.

*Sources: Claymore Sound, NPR (2019), LitHub*

### Genre Winners and Losers

**Pop and mainstream rock** benefited most from consolidation — these were the formats most amenable to standardized national playlists.

**Jazz, blues, black gospel, classic country, and regional genres** were underserved. Studies submitted to a Senate hearing on media ownership showed that formats including pop and jazz actually saw a **decrease in the number of unique songs played** in the post-consolidation era.

**Hip-hop's trajectory** is the most dramatic indie-to-mainstream story of this era:

- **1979**: Sugar Hill Records released "Rapper's Delight" by the Sugarhill Gang — the first rap single to sell a million copies.
- **1984**: Def Jam Recordings, founded by Rick Rubin and Russell Simmons, became the first independent rap label to sign a successful distribution deal with a major label, launching LL Cool J, Public Enemy, and the Beastie Boys.
- **1990s**: Hip-hop rose to mainstream chart prominence with multi-platinum releases becoming routine.
- **December 1999**: Billboard renamed its charts to "Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums" and "Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Singles & Tracks," acknowledging the genre's commercial dominance.
- **February 2005**: Billboard created the Pop 100 chart specifically to address criticism that the Hot 100 was "too dominated by hip-hop and R&B."

*Sources: Senate Hearing on Media Ownership (CHRG-108), Wikipedia (Radio Homogenization), VICE, Billboard, HipHopDX, Wikipedia (Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs)*

### The Transformation of A&R

The A&R (Artists and Repertoire) function — the process by which labels discover, sign, and develop artists — changed fundamentally as the industry corporatized:

- **Traditional model**: A&R executives were often composers, arrangers, and producers with deep musical knowledge who scouted talent at clubs and local venues.
- **Corporate model**: As composer Richard Niles observed, A&R staff increasingly became "businessmen looking at music from the standpoint of marketing, not from the standpoint of music and talent."

This shift had consequences. The corporate A&R model favored artists who fit proven commercial formulas over those who expanded the boundaries of their genres. Innovation increasingly came from outside the major label system.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Artists and Repertoire), Berklee College of Music*

## Part IV: The Consolidation — From Big Six to Big Three

### The Big Six (Late 1980s)

By the late 1980s, the global recorded music industry was dominated by six major label groups:

| # | Major Label Group | Parent Company | Country |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Warner Music Group (WEA) | Time Warner | USA |
| 2 | Sony Music (CBS Records) | Sony Corporation | Japan |
| 3 | EMI | Thorn EMI / EMI Group | UK |
| 4 | PolyGram | Philips | Netherlands |
| 5 | BMG (Bertelsmann Music Group) | Bertelsmann | Germany |
| 6 | MCA Music Entertainment | MCA Inc. / Seagram | USA/Canada |

What follows is the full merger and acquisition history that reduced these six to three. For visual lineage diagrams, see [label-lineage.md](label-lineage.md).

### The Universal Music Group Lineage

**Origins**: American Decca Records was established in 1934 as a US arm of British Decca. In 1962, MCA (Music Corporation of America), a talent agency, acquired American Decca — which also owned Universal Pictures. MCA was forced to exit the talent agency business to complete the deal. By 1971, MCA had consolidated Decca, Uni, and Kapp Records under the new **MCA Records** label.

**The Seagram play**: In 1995, the Seagram Company (Canadian spirits conglomerate, controlled by the Bronfman family) purchased 80% of MCA from Matsushita for $5.7 billion. On December 9, 1996, MCA Music Entertainment was renamed **Universal Music Group**, headed by Doug Morris.

**Absorbing PolyGram**: In 1998, Seagram acquired **PolyGram** for $10.4 billion and merged it into UMG, creating the world's largest music company. PolyGram brought with it a massive portfolio of acquired indie labels (see below). This deal reduced the **Big Six to the Big Five**.

**The Vivendi merger**: On December 11, 2000, Seagram merged with French conglomerate **Vivendi** and Canal+ to form Vivendi Universal. Edgar Bronfman Jr. and Jean-Marie Messier drove this deal. Vivendi retained UMG through subsequent corporate restructurings.

**Absorbing EMI**: In September 2012, UMG completed the acquisition of **EMI's recorded music division** for ~$1.9 billion (£1.2 billion), absorbing Capitol Records, Virgin Records, Blue Note, and other EMI imprints. EU regulators required significant divestitures (roughly one-third of the combined portfolio), which Warner Music acquired.

**Going public**: On September 21, 2021, UMG went public on the Amsterdam Euronext at a valuation of ~€46 billion.

**Current ownership (2024–2025)**: Bolloré family ~28% (18% direct + 10% via Vivendi), Tencent ~20%, Pershing Square ~10%, remainder public float.

*Sources: Wikipedia (MCA Records, UMG), Britannica Money (UMG), Deseret News (Seagram/PolyGram), CNN (Vivendi/Seagram), Music Business Worldwide (UMG ownership)*

### What PolyGram Brought to Universal

PolyGram's own history traces back to 1962, when Philips and Siemens formed a joint venture combining Phonogram Records and Deutsche Grammophon/Polydor. Renamed PolyGram in 1972, it became one of the most acquisitive labels in the industry:

- **1989**: Acquired **A&M Records** (founded 1962 by Herb Alpert and Jerry Moss) and **Island Records** (founded 1959 by Chris Blackwell)
- **1994**: Purchased Sony's 50% stake in **Def Jam Recordings**

When Seagram absorbed PolyGram into UMG in 1998, all of these labels came with it. The deal also brought Mercury Records, Verve Records, Polydor, and Deutsche Grammophon under the Universal umbrella.

*Sources: Wikipedia (PolyGram), FundingUniverse (PolyGram history)*

### The Sony Music Lineage

**Origins**: In 1938, CBS (Columbia Broadcasting System) acquired the American Record Corporation and renamed it Columbia Recording Corporation. Reorganized as **CBS Records** in 1966, it became one of the most powerful labels in the world.

**Sony's acquisition**: On November 17, 1987, Sony Corporation agreed to acquire CBS Records Group for **$2 billion** — at the time the largest-ever foreign acquisition by a Japanese company. The deal closed January 5, 1988. Renamed **Sony Music Entertainment** in 1991.

**The BMG merger**: In August 2004, Sony and Bertelsmann formed **Sony BMG**, a 50-50 joint venture merging Sony Music and BMG. This reduced the **Big Five to the Big Four**. In October 2008, Sony acquired Bertelsmann's 50% stake for **$1.5 billion**, reverting to the Sony Music Entertainment name.

**BMG's history**: Bertelsmann had entered the record business in 1958 with Ariola Records, acquired Arista Records (founded by Clive Davis in 1974) in 1979, and bought GE's RCA Records division in 1986. In 1987, Bertelsmann formally established BMG, unifying Ariola, Arista, and RCA Records.

**Absorbing EMI Publishing**: In 2012, a Sony-led consortium acquired **EMI Music Publishing** for $2.2 billion. In November 2018, Sony completed full acquisition of EMI Music Publishing for $2.3 billion (acquiring the remaining ~60% stake), making Sony the world's largest music publisher.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Sony Music, Sony BMG), UPI (Sony/CBS completion), Music Business Worldwide (Sony/EMI)*

### The Warner Music Group Lineage

**Origins**: **Warner Bros. Records** was founded in 1958 as a division of the Warner Bros. film studio. In 1963, it acquired **Reprise Records** (founded by Frank Sinatra in 1960). Mo Ostin came over as executive and built Warner Bros. Records into a creative powerhouse.

**Building the empire**:
- **1967**: **Atlantic Records** became a wholly owned subsidiary of Warner Bros.-Seven Arts.
- **1970**: Kinney National Company acquired **Elektra Records** and **Nonesuch Records** for $10 million.
- **1971**: Formed **WEA (Warner-Elektra-Atlantic)** as in-house distribution.
- **1972**: Kinney renamed to Warner Communications.
- **1990**: Warner Communications merged with Time Inc. to form **Time Warner**.

**Ownership changes**:
- **2004**: Time Warner sold WMG for **$2.6 billion** to a private investor group led by **Edgar Bronfman Jr.** — the same Bronfman whose family had previously sold Seagram/Universal to Vivendi.
- **2011**: WMG sold to **Access Industries** (controlled by Len Blavatnik) for **$3.3 billion**.
- **2013**: WMG acquired **Parlophone, Chrysalis Records**, and other EMI labels for **$765 million** from divestitures required by EU regulators after UMG's EMI acquisition.

**Current ownership (2024–2025)**: Access Industries remains the largest shareholder, owning ~72% equity and controlling ~98% of voting power.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Warner Music Group, Atlantic Records), Variety (Blavatnik buys WMG), BSNPubs (Warner story)*

### The Death of EMI

EMI's story is the most dramatic of the consolidation era — a once-great label destroyed by private equity leverage.

**Origins**: Electric and Musical Industries Ltd (EMI) was formed in 1931 by the merger of the Columbia Graphophone Company and the Gramophone Company in the UK. EMI opened Abbey Road Studios the same year. In 1955, EMI acquired **Capitol Records**, gaining a major American foothold.

**The leveraged buyout**: In August 2007, **Terra Firma Capital Partners** (led by Guy Hands) acquired EMI in a leveraged buyout for **£4.2 billion (~$4.7 billion)**, with Citigroup providing the debt. This was one of the last large European buyouts before the 2008 financial crisis.

**The collapse**: EMI could not service its debt. On February 1, 2011, Citigroup seized control, wiping out Terra Firma's equity.

**The breakup**: In November 2011, Citigroup sold EMI in two pieces:
- **Recorded music** to Universal Music Group for ~$1.9 billion
- **Music publishing** to a Sony-led consortium for $2.2 billion

This completed the reduction from **Big Four to Big Three**.

WMG acquired Parlophone and other divested EMI labels in February 2013 for $765 million, completing the final redistribution of EMI's assets.

*Sources: Wikipedia (EMI), NPR (EMI felled in 2012), Jones Day, Wikipedia (Terra Firma Capital Partners)*

## Part V: The Digital Disruption (1999–2015)

### Napster: The Earthquake (1999–2001)

**Napster** was created by **Shawn Fanning** and **Sean Parker** and went live in **June 1999**. It was a centralized peer-to-peer file-sharing service: users installed client software that indexed their MP3 files and made them searchable via Napster's central servers. Files transferred directly between users, but the search index was centralized — a critical legal distinction.

At its peak in late 2000/early 2001, Napster had approximately **80 million registered users** (some sources cite 60 million active users — the discrepancy likely reflects registered vs. active counts).

**A&M Records, Inc. v. Napster, Inc.** (239 F.3d 1004, 9th Cir. 2001): The Ninth Circuit affirmed the district court on **February 12, 2001**, finding Napster liable for both **contributory infringement and vicarious infringement**. On March 5, 2001, Judge Marilyn Patel ordered Napster to remove copyrighted songs within 72 hours. The service was effectively crippled. Napster filed for bankruptcy in June 2002.

The industry had won the legal battle. It had lost the war. Napster had demonstrated that consumers wanted music digitally, instantly, and cheaply. The industry's response — litigation rather than innovation — would cost it a decade of revenue.

*Sources: Wikipedia (Napster, A&M Records v. Napster), History.com, Justia*

### The P2P Hydra (2001–2005)

Shutting down Napster did not stop file sharing. It merely **decentralized** it.

**Kazaa** — Developed by Niklas Zennstrom and Janus Friis (who later created Skype), Kazaa used the decentralized FastTrack protocol. At its peak, it had **4.2 million simultaneous users** and claimed over **389 million downloads** of its software. In July 2006, Sharman Networks settled with the four major labels for approximately **$100 million** (some sources report $115 million — the variance may reflect different settlement components).

**LimeWire** — A Java-based Gnutella client popular through much of the 2000s. In May 2010, Judge Kimba Wood found LimeWire liable for inducing copyright infringement. In May 2011, LimeWire settled for **$105 million**. The RIAA had initially sought damages potentially in the trillions, which the court called "an absurd result" — "more money than the entire music recording industry has made since Edison's invention of the phonograph in 1877."

**BitTorrent** — Protocol designed by Bram Cohen, released July 2001. Unlike Napster and Kazaa, BitTorrent is a protocol, not a service — making it far harder to shut down. By 2004, BitTorrent traffic was estimated at **35% of all upstream internet traffic**.

**MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.** (545 U.S. 913, 2005): Decided unanimously by the Supreme Court on June 27, 2005. Justice Souter established the **inducement theory** of secondary copyright liability: "One who distributes a device with the object of promoting its use to infringe copyright, as shown by clear expression or other affirmative steps taken to foster infringement, is liable for the resulting acts of infringement by third parties."

*Sources: Wikipedia (Kazaa, LimeWire settlement), CBS News, Wikipedia (MGM v. Grokster), EFF*

### The RIAA's War on Consumers

On **September 8, 2003**, the RIAA launched a litigation campaign against individual file sharers. Over approximately five years, the RIAA filed suits against more than **30,000 individuals** (EFF estimate).

- Typical settlements ranged from **$3,000 to $12,000**
- The RIAA sued a **12-year-old** (Brianna LaHara, NYC) and a **13-year-old** (Brittany Chan, Michigan), generating significant public outrage
- According to one analysis, the RIAA spent **$17.6 million** in legal fees to recover only **$391,000** in settlements `[single-source — Techdirt]`
- The campaign was wound down around **2008**, shifting to working with ISPs on "graduated response" systems

The campaign achieved its deterrence goal at the cost of enormous public relations damage. An entire generation of music consumers came to view the recording industry as adversarial.

*Sources: EFF (RIAA v. The People), History.com, Techdirt*

### iTunes: The Unbundling (2003–2010s)

The iTunes Music Store launched on **April 28, 2003** (Mac only; Windows added October 16, 2003). Steve Jobs negotiated directly with the five major labels, pitching: legal, convenient, affordable.

**The model**: Individual tracks at **$0.99**, albums at **$9.99**. Labels agreed on the condition that music be protected by DRM (FairPlay). Apple retained **30%** of revenue, with **70%** going to rights holders.

At launch, the store offered approximately 200,000 tracks. It sold its 25 millionth download by December 2003 and 100 millionth by July 2004. iTunes commanded approximately **63% of digital music downloads** at its peak (2013).

**The unbundling effect** was arguably as destructive to industry revenue as piracy itself. By selling individual tracks instead of requiring $15–18 album purchases, iTunes enabled consumers to buy only the songs they wanted. Consumers were "buying 10% of what they used to" because they only needed the single, not the album. Digital downloads peaked at **$3 billion in 2012**, representing **41% of US music revenue** — but this was far less than the album-driven CD peak.

By 2024, downloads accounted for just **2% of US recorded music revenue**.

*Sources: Apple Newsroom, Wikipedia (iTunes Store), AppleInsider, CNN Money*

### The Revenue Crater

The numbers tell the story:

| Year | US Revenue | Change from Peak |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1999 | ~$14.6B | Peak |
| 2001 | ~$13.7B | -6% |
| 2003 | ~$11.9B | -18% |
| 2007 | ~$10.4B | -29% |
| 2010 | ~$7.0B | -52% |
| 2014 | ~$6.97B | **-52% (trough)** |

The industry lost more than half its revenue in 15 years. See [revenue-data.md](revenue-data.md) for the full year-by-year breakdown.

*Sources: RIAA year-end reports (2018, 2020, 2022, 2024), Music Business Research, Digital Music News. Some mid-2000s individual year figures are single-source.*

### MySpace: The First Platform for Artists (~2005–2009)

MySpace (launched 2003, acquired by News Corp for $580M in 2005) became the first major social platform where musicians could upload tracks, build a following, and connect directly with fans — bypassing traditional label A&R gatekeepers.

Key artists who broke through on MySpace:
- **Arctic Monkeys** — Fans uploaded demos without the band's involvement; the buzz attracted Domino Recording Company
- **Lily Allen** — Posted demos that attracted thousands of listeners; debut album largely previewed on MySpace
- **Soulja Boy** — At age 15, promoted "Crank That" on MySpace; it hit #1 on the Hot 100 in 2007
- **Sean Kingston**, **Calvin Harris**, **Adele** — all cited as MySpace discovery artists

MySpace's music legacy was marred by a catastrophic data loss during a server migration (~2015–2019): an estimated **50 million tracks** uploaded between 2003–2015 were permanently lost.

*Sources: The National, NME, Official Charts, DJBooth*

### SoundCloud: Democratization Completed (~2008–Present)

SoundCloud was founded in 2007 in Stockholm and launched in 2008. Its key differentiator: **anyone could upload audio for free** with no intermediary, no label deal, and no subscription required.

The **"SoundCloud rap" era (~2015–2018)** was described by the New York Times as "the most vital and disruptive new movement in hip-hop." Key artists who emerged: **Post Malone, XXXTentacion, Juice WRLD, Lil Uzi Vert, Playboi Carti, Lil Pump, Lil Peep, Doja Cat, Denzel Curry**.

In 2017, rap/hip-hop became the most popular music genre in the US by consumption, and SoundCloud was a significant driver. The platform nearly went bankrupt in 2017 before securing emergency funding.

*Sources: Wikipedia (SoundCloud), ArtistPush, The Ringer, Afterglow ATX*

### YouTube: The Largest Music Platform in the World

YouTube was founded in February 2005 and acquired by Google in October 2006 for $1.65 billion. Music quickly became its most-viewed content category.

**Vevo** launched December 8, 2009 as a joint venture between UMG, Sony Music, and EMI, becoming the most-visited music destination in the US. Vevo shut down its standalone apps in 2018 and now exists purely as YouTube channels.

**Content ID** — YouTube's automated copyright detection system, tested from June 2007:
- Uses digital fingerprinting to match uploads against rights holder reference files
- Rights holders can block, monetize, or track matched content
- Development cost: approximately **$100 million** invested by Google
- Revenue generated: over **$2 billion** to rights holders by 2016

**YouTube's annual payouts to the music industry:**
- 12 months to June 2021: **$4 billion**
- 12 months to June 2022: **$6 billion**
- 12 months to June 2025: **$8 billion**

The **"value gap" controversy**: YouTube's per-stream rate (~$0.00069) is far below audio-only services. The industry argues YouTube leverages DMCA safe harbor protections to negotiate lower rates than fully licensed services like Spotify.

*Sources: Wikipedia (History of YouTube, Vevo, Content ID), Music Business Worldwide (YouTube $6B, $8B), Music Week*

### How Streaming Counts

The RIAA established in February 2016 that **1,500 on-demand audio/video streams = 1 album sale** for Gold & Platinum certifications. Billboard later differentiated: **1,250 premium streams** or **3,750 ad-supported streams** = 1 album-equivalent unit, reflecting the different revenue generated by each tier.

### The Revenue Recovery

Streaming reversed the industry's 15-year revenue decline:

| Year | US Revenue | Key Milestone |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| 2014 | ~$6.97B | Trough |
| 2016 | ~$7.7B | Streaming-driven growth begins (11.4% increase) |

By 2024, streaming alone generated **$14.9 billion** — more than the entire industry's total revenue at its 1999 peak. The format breakdown:
- **Streaming: 84%** ($14.9B)
- **Physical: 11%** ($2.0B, led by vinyl resurgence)
- **Downloads: 2%**

The industry has now posted **nine consecutive years of growth** (2016–2024).

*Sources: RIAA year-end reports (2018, 2020, 2022, 2024), Billboard, Variety*

### Per-Stream Economics

Platforms do not publish fixed per-stream rates; payouts depend on pool-based calculations. These are widely reported estimates:

| Platform | Est. Per-Stream Rate | Streams for $1,000 |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Tidal | ~$0.01284 | ~78,000 |
| Apple Music | ~$0.007–0.01 | ~100,000–143,000 |
| Deezer | ~$0.0064–0.007 | ~143,000–178,000 |
| Spotify | ~$0.003–0.005 | ~200,000–333,000 |
| Pandora | ~$0.0013–0.0027 | ~370,000–770,000 |
| YouTube Music | ~$0.00069 | ~1,449,000 |

Apple Music pays roughly **double** Spotify's rate, primarily because Apple Music has no free tier — every stream comes from a paying subscriber.

*Sources: Rebel Music, Ditto Music, Digital Music News, 9to5Mac, Streaming Calculator*

### Pro-Rata vs. User-Centric Payment

**Pro-rata (current dominant model)**: The streaming service pools all monthly revenue, keeps ~30%, and distributes ~70% to rights holders based on their share of **total streams** that month. If Artist X gets 5% of all streams, they get 5% of the payout pool — regardless of whether any individual subscriber actually listened to Artist X.

**User-centric**: Each subscriber's payment is divided among only the artists **that specific subscriber** listened to.

Nearly all major platforms use pro-rata. **SoundCloud** is the notable exception, adopting "fan-powered royalties" on April 1, 2021. SoundCloud reported that artists on their platform earned on average **60% more** under user-centric `[single-source — SoundCloud's own reporting]`.

The pro-rata model is widely criticized for favoring artists with passive, playlist-driven listening (background music, mood playlists) over artists with dedicated fanbases who listen intentionally.

*Sources: Music Business Research, Curve Royalty Systems, Duke Fuqua*

## Part VII: How Money Flows — Artist Economics Then and Now

### The Traditional Major Label Deal

**The Advance**: An upfront, non-returnable payment from the label to the artist. It covers recording costs, music videos, marketing, and living expenses. The advance is **recoupable** — the label recoups it from the artist's royalty share before the artist sees any royalty income. The artist never has to write a check back, but they earn nothing until the label is made whole.

**Recoupable expenses** typically include: the advance itself, recording costs, video budgets, tour support, and some marketing costs. Until the label has recouped all of these from the artist's royalty percentage, the artist receives **zero royalties**.

*Sources: Julia Holt Law, Promise Legal, ASCAP*

**Royalty rates**:
- New artists on major labels: approximately **15–20%** of revenue
- Indie label artists: approximately **50%**
- Higher-service indie distributors (e.g., AWAL): **65–85%**

These percentages are applied to various bases (retail price, wholesale price, or net receipts) depending on the contract, which complicates direct comparison.

*Sources: Rexius Records, AWAL, Curve Royalty Systems*

**Master ownership**: Under most traditional exclusive recording contracts, the artist **assigns copyright in the sound recordings to the label** for the full life of the copyright. Even after the label recoups all costs, it still owns the masters. Some newer licensing deals grant the label an exclusive license for a fixed term, after which masters revert to the artist — these are more common with indie labels.

*Sources: Sound On Sound, Rexius Records*

### Historical Deductions That Reduced Artist Earnings

- **Packaging deductions**: 20–25% off the retail price for CDs, ostensibly to cover packaging costs (though actual CD packaging costs were negligible in volume)
- **Breakage deductions**: 10% off for vinyl records (originally from the shellac era when records literally broke in transit — a deduction that persisted decades after it ceased to reflect reality)
- **Cross-collateralization**: Allowed labels to recoup losses from one album using earnings from another. If Album 1 fails to recoup its advance, the label can use Album 2's royalties to cover the balance before the artist sees any money from Album 2.

These deductions could reduce an artist's effective royalty rate well below the headline number in their contract.

*Sources: Sound On Sound, HowStuffWorks, Songstuff, Reprtoir, Songtrust*

### 360 Deals: The Label's Response to Declining Record Sales

360 deals (also called "multiple rights deals") emerged in the early-to-mid 2000s as record label revenue from physical sales declined sharply. The premise: if record sales alone can't sustain the label's investment in artist development, the label should participate in **all** of an artist's revenue streams.

**Pioneering deals**:
- **Robbie Williams / EMI (2002)**: Widely cited as one of the first major 360 deals — an £80 million contract extending beyond recordings to merchandising and other revenue
- **Madonna / Live Nation (2007)**: A landmark $120 million deal. Live Nation received 30% of her merchandising. Notably, this was with a concert promoter, not a traditional label
- **Jay-Z / Live Nation (2008)**: $150 million, following the Madonna model

**Revenue streams covered**: Recording revenue, touring/live performance, merchandise, publishing, endorsements, and media/branding.

**Typical label take on non-recording streams**: **10–35%**, with **10–20%** from touring and live performances commonly reported. Some deals use threshold-based percentages where the label only takes a cut above a certain revenue level.

*Sources: Wikipedia (360 deal), Lee Marshall (2013 academic paper), Deseret News, Cordero Law, Stone & Sallus Law*

**The case for 360 deals (label perspective)**: Labels invest significant capital in artist development with no guarantee of return. If record sales can't cover that investment, participating in other revenue streams makes the business model viable. Labels argue they help generate touring and merch revenue through promotion and brand-building.

**The case against (artist perspective)**: Labels take a percentage of revenue streams they did nothing to build — an artist's live show may have been developed through years of independent touring. Cross-collateralization across all streams can make recoupment even harder.

### Independent Artist Economics in 2025

The rise of digital distribution services has created a genuine alternative to the major label system:

| Distributor | Pricing Model | Artist Revenue Share |
|------------|---------------|---------------------|
| DistroKid | $22.99/year (1 artist, unlimited uploads) | 100% (20% from YouTube Content ID) |
| TuneCore | $9.99/year per single, $29.99/year per album | 100% (20% from social/YouTube Content ID) |
| CD Baby | One-time fee per release | 91% (9% commission) |
| AWAL | No upfront fee (invite-only) | 85% (15% commission) |
| UnitedMasters | $19.99–$59.99/year | 100% of streaming royalties |
| Stem | Open acceptance | 95% (5% commission) |

*Sources: Aristake, Chartlex, UnitedMasters, AWAL*

**The math**: A major label artist at a 15–20% royalty rate keeps roughly **$1.05–$1.40** per $10 subscription attributable to their streams (after the platform's 30% cut and the label's 80–85% share). An indie artist using DistroKid keeps the full **$7.00** share attributable to their streams (minus the flat annual fee).

**The trade-off**: Major labels provide marketing budgets, radio promotion, playlist placement leverage, and infrastructure that most indie artists cannot replicate. The indie market is growing — indie labels and self-distributed artists held approximately **32%** of global recorded music revenue — but for every indie success story, hundreds of artists barely sustain themselves.

### How Many Streams to Earn Minimum Wage?

An independent artist needs approximately **5 million Spotify streams annually** to earn the US federal minimum wage (~$15,080/year). On higher-paying platforms like Tidal, the threshold is significantly lower.

For a major label artist at a 20% royalty rate, the required stream count is roughly **5x higher** — approximately 25 million streams on Spotify — because the label takes the majority of the revenue.

*Sources: EDM.com, MusicTech, Digital Music News (Duetti report, 2025)*

### Publishing: The Other Copyright

Every recorded song generates **two separate copyrights**:

1. **The composition (publishing rights)**: The melody, lyrics, and musical structure. Owned by the songwriter(s) and/or their publisher.
2. **The sound recording (master rights)**: The specific recorded performance. Typically owned by whoever financed the recording.

These rights are independent. A cover version earns master royalties for the new recording but publishing royalties still go to the original songwriter.

**Types of publishing royalties**:
- **Mechanical royalties**: Generated when a composition is reproduced. The US statutory rate is **$0.127 per unit** as of January 1, 2025 (for songs under 5 minutes). For streaming, mechanicals are calculated as a percentage of service revenue.
- **Performance royalties**: Generated when a composition is publicly performed or broadcast. Collected by PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR). PROs split collections **50/50** between the songwriter share and the publisher share.
- **Sync royalties**: One-time licensing fees for pairing a composition with visual media. Negotiated case by case.

*Sources: Soundcharts, The MLC, Hypebot, Musicians Institute*

**The PROs**:

| PRO | Type | Members/Catalog |
|-----|------|----------------|
| ASCAP | Non-profit | ~650,000+ members, ~12M songs |
| BMI | Non-profit | ~800,000+ members, ~13M titles |
| SESAC | For-profit | Invitation-only, selective |
| GMR | For-profit | ~150–200 songwriters `[single-source]`; founded 2013 by Irving Azoff |

*Sources: Soundcharts, Songpreneurs, Global Music Rights*

### The Catalog Boom

The music catalog investment market exploded from under $1 billion in annual deal volume before 2018 to over **$5 billion in 2021** (some estimates say $7 billion). Drivers:

- Low interest rates making catalogs attractive as income-generating assets
- Growing streaming revenue increasing catalog values
- COVID-19 eliminating touring income, pushing artists to monetize catalogs
- Private equity interest in music as an asset class

**Hipgnosis Songs Fund** (founded by Merck Mercuriadis) spent nearly **$700 million** between March 2019 and March 2020 acquiring 42 catalogs `[single-source on exact figure]`.

**Taylor Swift / Scooter Braun dispute**: In June 2019, Scooter Braun's Ithaca Holdings acquired Big Machine Records — and with it, the masters to Swift's first six albums — without Swift's knowledge or consent. Shamrock Holdings later acquired the masters from Braun. Swift re-recorded four of the six albums (2021–2023) and ultimately acquired the masters from Shamrock in 2025. This dispute became the most visible public illustration of the master ownership problem in modern music.

*Sources: Variety, Wikipedia (Taylor Swift masters dispute), Deadline, EPGD Business Law*

### Key Legislation

**Copyright Act of 1976**: Extended copyright protection to sound recordings, introduced "fair use," eliminated the requirement for formal registration, and established the framework for mechanical licensing.

**Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998)**: Created the **safe harbor** provision shielding online platforms from liability if they promptly remove infringing material upon notification. This is what allows YouTube to host user-uploaded content. The music industry has long argued safe harbor is too broad, creating a "value gap."

**Music Modernization Act (2018)**: The biggest update to music legislation in 40 years:
- **Title I**: Created the **Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC)** to administer blanket mechanical licenses for streaming
- **Title II (CLASSICS Act)**: Extended copyright protection to pre-1972 sound recordings
- **Title III (AMP Act)**: Established direct royalty payments for producers, mixers, and engineers

**ASCAP and BMI Consent Decrees**: Both PROs have operated under DOJ consent decrees since 1941, requiring them to offer blanket licenses to any user who requests one and submit to judicial rate-setting. The DOJ's most recent review closed in January 2021 without changes.

**FTC CD Price-Fixing Settlement (2000)**: The FTC settled with the five largest music distributors for illegally modifying cooperative advertising programs to inflate CD prices.

*Sources: University of Oregon Open Text, Wikipedia (DMCA), Congress.gov, Recording Academy, K&L Gates, Variety (consent decrees), FTC*

## Part VIII: Themes and Second-Order Analysis

### Theme 1: The Innovation Cycle

The history of the US music industry reveals a recurring pattern:

1. **Technological disruption** creates new distribution channels
2. **Independent entrepreneurs** exploit those channels to serve underserved markets
3. **Major corporations** observe what works and **acquire** the successful independents
4. **Consolidation** follows, concentrating power in fewer hands
5. **The next disruption** resets the cycle

This played out with post-war indie labels (acquired by majors in the 1960s–70s), with MTV-era labels (absorbed in the 1980s–90s), and with digital-era distribution (where major labels partnered with or acquired digital platforms). The question for the current moment is whether streaming-era distribution (DistroKid, TuneCore, etc.) represents a structural break in this cycle or merely the latest iteration.

### Theme 2: Distribution as the Source of Structural Power

The defining characteristic of a "major" label has never been its talent roster — it has been its **control of distribution**. In the vinyl era, this meant pressing plants and physical distribution networks. In the CD era, it meant retail relationships and manufacturing scale. In the digital era, it means playlist placement, marketing budgets, and algorithmic visibility.

The majors' current power rests on two pillars:
1. **Catalog ownership**: They own the masters to decades of recorded music, generating revenue in perpetuity
2. **Marketing and promotion infrastructure**: They can get an artist heard at scale in ways most indies cannot

Distribution itself has been democratized (anyone can put a song on Spotify), but **discovery** has not. The bottleneck has simply moved from physical distribution to attention distribution.

### Theme 3: The Artist's Shrinking and Expanding Options

The artist's economic position has both improved and deteriorated simultaneously:

**Improved**:
- Artists can now distribute music globally for $23/year
- No longer dependent on label A&R for access to market
- Can build direct fan relationships via social media
- Keep 85–100% of recording revenue through indie distribution
- Publishing awareness has increased; more artists retain or co-own their publishing

**Deteriorated**:
- Per-unit revenue has collapsed (from ~$10+ per CD to fractions of a penny per stream)
- The volume required to sustain a career is enormous (5M+ Spotify streams for minimum wage)
- 360 deals have expanded the label's claim on non-recording revenue
- Playlist gatekeeping has replaced radio gatekeeping
- The "middle class artist" who could sustain a career selling 50,000–100,000 albums has largely disappeared

### Theme 4: The Financialization of Music

Music has increasingly been treated as a **financial asset class** rather than a cultural product:
- Private equity firms now own major labels (Access Industries/Warner, Bolloré/UMG)
- Catalog acquisitions are structured as yield-generating investments
- Streaming revenue provides the predictable cash flows that financial markets prize
- The UMG IPO valued the company at €46 billion — a financial event, not a cultural one

This financialization has consequences: decisions about which artists to sign, which catalogs to acquire, and how to structure deals are increasingly driven by return-on-investment calculations rather than artistic judgment.

### Theme 5: Regulation Has Consistently Lagged Reality

Every major regulatory intervention in the music industry has come **after** the market had already been reshaped by the forces the regulation sought to address:

- Payola legislation (1960) came after the practice was endemic
- The Telecommunications Act (1996) enabled radio consolidation that was already underway
- The DMCA (1998) created safe harbor rules before the full implications of user-uploaded content were understood
- The Music Modernization Act (2018) addressed streaming-era licensing problems that had existed for a decade

This pattern suggests that any future regulatory intervention — whether addressing AI-generated music, streaming payment models, or platform market power — will similarly arrive after the market has already adapted.
