# AI and Music Copyright: The Future of Sampling, Masters, and Publishing

*Research compiled: March 9, 2026*
*All claims sourced from public filings, court documents, government records, and verified reporting. Items marked [single-source] have not been independently corroborated.*

## 1. Active Lawsuits Involving AI and Music/Publishing IP

### RIAA v. Suno (D. Mass., filed June 24, 2024)

- **Plaintiffs**: Major record labels (UMG, Sony, Warner) via RIAA
- **Claims**: Mass copyright infringement — alleging Suno trained its AI music generation models on copyrighted sound recordings without authorization
- **Suno's defense**: Fair use (asserted in August 1, 2024 answer)
- **Settlement developments**:
  - Warner Music Group and Suno announced a settlement in late 2025, with Suno committing to launch a new model in 2026 trained only on licensed material. Current models will be phased out.
  - UMG also reached a settlement with Suno.
  - **Sony has not settled** — case remains ongoing as of early 2026.
- **Related action**: Country music artist Anthony Justice filed a separate complaint against Suno on behalf of a group of musicians (June 2025). Motions to dismiss pending in early 2026.

Sources: [RIAA announcement](https://www.riaa.com/record-companies-bring-landmark-cases-for-responsible-ai-againstsuno-and-udio-in-boston-and-new-york-federal-courts-respectively/), [Copyright Alliance 2025 review](https://copyrightalliance.org/ai-copyright-lawsuit-developments-2025/), [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/lists/biggest-ai-music-stories-2025-suno-udio-charts-more/)

### RIAA v. Udio (S.D.N.Y., filed June 24, 2024)

- **Plaintiffs**: Same major label coalition via RIAA
- **Claims**: Same copyright infringement theories as the Suno case
- **Settlement developments**:
  - **UMG settled with Udio (October 29, 2025)** and announced plans for a joint "music creation, consumption and streaming" service launching in 2026. Artists who opt in will be compensated both for training data use and when subscribers create new works using their music.
  - **Warner Music Group settled (November 19, 2025)** with an opt-in licensing deal for WMG artists.
  - **Sony has not settled** — case remains ongoing.

Sources: [Music Ally](https://musically.com/2025/10/30/umg-settles-udio-lawsuit-companies-plan-new-ai-music-service-together/), [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/pro/ai-music-udio-settles-lawsuit-universal-music-group/), [McKool Smith tracker](https://www.mckoolsmith.com/newsroom-ailitigation-46)

### Concord Music / ABKCO / UMG v. Anthropic

- **Original lawsuit (2023)**: Filed in Tennessee federal court, alleging Anthropic's Claude AI was trained on song lyrics from at least 500 songs (artists including Katy Perry, Rolling Stones, Beyonce) without authorization.
- **Guardrails agreement (January 2025)**: Anthropic agreed that Claude will no longer provide copyrighted lyrics or generate new lyrics based on copyrighted material.
- **Preliminary injunction denied (March 2025)**: U.S. District Judge Eumi Lee ruled that publishers did not prove the existing licensing market was being undercut, denying the request to prevent Anthropic from training future models on lyrics.
- **Expanded lawsuit (January 2026)**: Publishers filed new claims covering infringement of more than 20,000 songs, with potential statutory damages exceeding **$3 billion**. The filing alleges Anthropic built a permanent internal library sourced from "pirate repositories" via BitTorrent, compounding the alleged infringement through redistribution.
- **Case remains ongoing.**

Sources: [Hollywood Reporter](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/business/business-news/anthropic-wins-first-round-lawsuit-music-publishers-song-lyrics-1236173148/), [Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/anthropic-must-face-music-publishers-copyright-claims-after-judge-denies-dismissal-motion/), [Variety](https://variety.com/2023/music/news/universal-concord-abkco-sue-ai-company-anthropic-copyright-violation-1235761250/)

## 2. AI and Training Data — Broader Context Cases

### Getty Images v. Stability AI

- **UK proceeding**: On November 4, 2025, the English High Court issued its judgment — the first major UK ruling on AI and copyright.
  - Getty **abandoned its primary copyright infringement claims** shortly before closing submissions, accepting there was no evidence that training and development of Stable Diffusion took place in the UK.
  - The Court held that **AI model weights are not a "copy"** of images in the sense required by UK copyright law (CDPA), rejecting secondary copyright claims.
  - Getty prevailed on **limited trademark claims** (watermark reproduction) under Sections 10(1) and 10(2) of the Trade Marks Act 1994.
- **US proceeding** (D. Del., Case No. 1:23-cv-00135): Ongoing.
- **Significance for music**: The UK ruling that model weights do not constitute copies has implications for whether AI music models "contain" the copyrighted recordings they were trained on.

Sources: [Latham & Watkins](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/getty-images-v-stability-ai-english-high-court-rejects-secondary-copyright-claim), [Mayer Brown](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2025/11/getty-images-v-stability-ai-what-the-high-courts-decision-means-for-rights-holders-and-ai-developers), [Ropes & Gray](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/viewpoints/102lvxe/getty-image-loses-copyright-infringement-claim-against-stability-ai-in-uks-first)

### New York Times v. OpenAI & Microsoft (S.D.N.Y., filed December 2023)

- Alleges OpenAI used millions of NYT articles to train its models without consent.
- **Key discovery ruling (January 2026)**: U.S. District Judge Sidney Stein affirmed an order compelling OpenAI to produce **20 million de-identified ChatGPT conversation logs**, rejecting OpenAI's attempt to submit only cherry-picked conversations.
- No summary judgment decision on fair use expected until **summer 2026 at the earliest**.

Sources: [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/openai-loses-privacy-gambit-20-million-chatgpt-logs-likely-headed-copyright), [Lawyer Monthly](https://www.lawyer-monthly.com/2026/01/openai-sdny-discovery-20m-chat-logs-legal-impact/)

### Authors Guild v. OpenAI (S.D.N.Y., filed 2023)

- **October 28, 2025**: U.S. District Judge Sidney Stein denied OpenAI's motion to dismiss authors' claims that ChatGPT outputs infringe their copyrights, ruling that authors may be able to prove ChatGPT's text is "similar enough" to their works to violate book copyrights.
- Case proceeds to discovery and potential summary judgment.

Sources: [Publishers Weekly](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/digital/copyright/article/98961-authors-class-action-lawsuit-against-openai-moves-forward.html), [McKool Smith](https://www.mckoolsmith.com/newsroom-ailitigation-42)

### How These Cases Inform the Music Industry Debate

These cases collectively address whether:
1. Training AI on copyrighted works constitutes fair use (no definitive ruling yet)
2. AI model weights themselves are infringing "copies" (UK court says no; US courts have not ruled)
3. AI outputs that resemble training data constitute infringement (courts are allowing these claims to proceed)
4. The scale of commercial use and market displacement weighs against fair use (a recurring theme)

## 3. US Copyright Office Guidance

### Thaler v. Perlmutter — Human Authorship Requirement Affirmed

- **D.C. Circuit (March 18, 2025)**: Unanimously affirmed that the Copyright Act requires human authorship. Dr. Stephen Thaler's AI system "Creativity Machine" cannot be listed as the author of a copyrighted work.
- **Supreme Court (March 2, 2026)**: Denied certiorari. The "human-only rule" for copyright authorship is now settled law with no further avenue of appeal.

Sources: [Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/the-final-word-supreme-court-refuses-to-hear-case-on-ai-authorship), [Carlton Fields](https://www.carltonfields.com/insights/publications/2025/no-copyright-protection-for-ai-assisted-creations-thaler-v-perlmutter), [SCOTUSblog](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/thaler-v-perlmutter/)

### Zarya of the Dawn (2023)

- The Copyright Office granted **limited registration** for AI-assisted comic book *Zarya of the Dawn* by Kris Kashtanova.
- Copyright was granted for the text and overall arrangement (human-authored elements) but **not** for individual images generated by Midjourney.
- Established the principle that human selection and arrangement of AI outputs can be copyrightable, but the AI-generated elements themselves are not.

Source: [Creative Commons analysis](https://creativecommons.org/2023/02/27/zarya-of-the-dawn-us-copyright-office-affirms-limits-on-copyright-of-ai-outputs/)

### USCO Report Series on AI and Copyright

**Part 2 — Copyrightability (January 29, 2025)**:
- Human authorship remains a "bedrock" requirement for copyright.
- Prompt selection alone is insufficient to yield copyrightable work (though this determination could evolve with technology).
- Mixed human-AI works: only the human contributions are potentially copyrightable.
- Use of AI as a tool (e.g., for editing) does not render the entire work uncopyrightable.

**Part 3 — Generative AI Training (May 9, 2025)**:
- 108-page report analyzing fair use in the context of AI training.
- **Key conclusion**: AI training is **not categorically fair use** — it is context-dependent.
- "Making commercial use of vast troves of copyrighted works to produce expressive content that competes with them in existing markets... goes beyond established fair use boundaries."
- Wholesale taking of entire works "ordinarily weighs against fair use."
- Market effects analysis: AI may result in lost sales, market dilution, and lost licensing opportunities.

Sources: [Skadden](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/02/copyright-office-publishes-report), [Crowell & Moring](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/us-copyright-office-releases-third-report-on-ai-and-copyright-addressing-training-ai-models-with-copyrighted-materials), [Wiley](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Generative-AI-in-Focus-Copyright-Offices-Latest-Report), [Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/news/us-copyright-office-ai-report-part-3-what-authors-should-know/)

### Current Registration Requirements for AI-Assisted Works

- Applicants **must disclose** the inclusion of AI-generated content in registration applications.
- Must provide a brief explanation of the human author's contributions.
- Use the Standard Application form, identifying human authors and describing what they created in the "Author Created" field.
- AI-generated content must be disclaimed in the "Limitation of Claim" field.

Source: [USCO AI Policy Guidance](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/ai_policy_guidance.pdf), [Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/03/16/2023-05321/copyright-registration-guidance-works-containing-material-generated-by-artificial-intelligence)

## 4. Legislative Responses

### ELVIS Act (Tennessee) — Enacted

- **Signed**: March 21, 2024 by Governor Bill Lee
- **Effective**: July 1, 2024
- First US legislation specifically protecting musicians from unauthorized AI voice cloning.
- Adds "voice" to existing right-of-publicity protections (name, image, likeness).
- Prohibits AI voice cloning without consent; enforceable as a **Class A misdemeanor**.
- Protects both living and deceased individuals.
- Passed unanimously: 93-0 in the House, 30-0 in the Senate.

Sources: [Tennessee Governor's Office](https://www.tn.gov/governor/news/2024/1/10/tennessee-first-in-the-nation-to-address-ai-impact-on-music-industry.html), [Vanderbilt Law School](https://law.vanderbilt.edu/why-tennessees-elvis-act-is-the-king-of-artificial-intelligence-protections/)

### NO FAKES Act — Reintroduced (April 2025)

- **Full name**: Nurture Originals, Foster Art, and Keep Entertainment Safe Act
- Would establish a **federal right of publicity** for digital replicas of individuals' likenesses.
- Bipartisan support in both chambers:
  - Senate: Senators Blackburn (R-TN), Coons (D-DE), Tillis (R-NC), Klobuchar (D-MN)
  - House: Reps. Salazar (R-FL), Dean (D-PA), Moran (R-TX), Balint (D-VT), Morelle (D-NY)
- Supported by SAG-AFTRA, UMG, Warner, RIAA, Disney, Amazon, Adobe, Google, YouTube, OpenAI, IBM.
- Opposed by EFF, Center for Democracy & Technology, ALA, CCIA (free expression concerns).
- **Status**: Introduced in both chambers; not yet passed as of March 2026.

Sources: [Congress.gov S.1367](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1367), [Recording Academy](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/no-fakes-act-introduced-in-the-senate)

### TRAIN Act (Transparency and Responsibility for AI Networks)

- Introduced by Rep. Madeleine Dean (D-PA) and Rep. Nathaniel Moran (R-TX) in January 2026.
- Would require AI companies to provide copyright holders access to training records, enabling creators to determine if their copyrighted work was used without permission.

Source: [Rep. Dean press release](https://dean.house.gov/2026/1/ean-moran-introduce-bipartisan-bill-to-protect-creators-from-unauthorized-ai-training) [single-source]

### TRUMP AMERICA AI Act Framework [single-source]

- Senator Blackburn (R-TN) announced intent to introduce legislation in 2026 codifying President Trump's executive order on unified federal AI policy.
- Sections 18-21 address copyright, digital replicas, and derivative works.
- Would create a federal right to sue over unauthorized use of data (including copyrighted works) for AI training.
- Would establish a "limited, conditional safe harbor permitting copyright owners to collectively license works for specified AI uses."
- **Status**: Framework announced December 2025; formal bill text not yet introduced as of March 2026.

Source: [Skadden](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/12/white-house-launches-national-framework)

### Content Origin Protection and Integrity from Edited and Deepfaked Media Act (S.1396)

- Introduced in the 119th Congress (2025-2026).
- Addresses provenance and authenticity of media content.

Source: [Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1396/text)

### EU AI Act — Music Implications

- **Copyright provisions in force**: August 2, 2025 (enforcement powers kick in one year later).
- **Training data requirements**: Must use licensed or public-domain materials; no unauthorized scraping.
- **Transparency obligations**: General-purpose AI providers must publish a "sufficiently detailed summary" of content used for training.
- **Opt-out rights**: Article 4 allows text/data mining for commercial use unless rightsholders expressly reserve their rights.
- **Criticism**: European Parliament considers the summary requirement "completely inadequate" — does not give creators sufficient information to determine if their works were used. The Act is not retroactive, meaning previously scraped data is unaddressed.
- **Penalties**: Up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.
- **New companies**: Must comply by 2026; existing operators by 2027.

Sources: [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/next/2025/08/02/eus-ai-act-doesnt-do-enough-to-protect-artists-copyright-creative-groups-say), [European Parliament briefing](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2025/782585/EPRS_BRI(2025)782585_EN.pdf), [Global Policy Watch](https://www.globalpolicywatch.com/2026/02/european-parliament-proposes-changes-to-copyright-protection-in-the-age-of-generative-ai/)

## 5. AI Music Generation Tools

### Suno

- AI music generation platform producing full songs (vocals, instrumentation, lyrics) from text prompts.
- **Training data**: Not publicly disclosed. Lawsuits allege training on copyrighted sound recordings.
- **Post-settlement trajectory**: Raised $250 million in November 2025 at a $2.45 billion valuation. Committed to releasing a new model in 2026 trained only on licensed material; current models to be retired.
- Settlement deals with UMG and WMG include compensation for training data use and per-creation payments when subscribers use licensed music to generate new works.

### Udio

- AI music generation platform launched late 2023 by former Google DeepMind researchers.
- **Training data**: Has confirmed use of a combination of licensed content and original compositions. [single-source — exact training data composition unverified]
- **Post-settlement trajectory**: Partnering with UMG to launch a licensed "music creation, consumption and streaming" platform in 2026. WMG artists can opt in to license works for the new service.

### Stable Audio (Stability AI)

- Developed by Stability AI (same company behind Stable Diffusion for images).
- **Training data**: Most transparent of the three — has published details about licensed training datasets.
- Uses attention-enhanced U-Net architecture; conditioning mechanism allows control through multiple input parameters.
- Employs curriculum learning strategies (gradually increasing task complexity).

### Major Label Responses

- **UMG**: Shifted from litigation to strategic partnerships. Settled with both Udio and Suno; licensing catalog for AI training with artist opt-in and dual compensation (training + per-creation). "Very actively engaged" with nearly a dozen AI companies.
- **Warner Music Group**: Settled with both Suno and Udio. Opt-in licensing model for artists.
- **Sony Music**: Has **not settled** with either Suno or Udio. Continues litigation.
- **Spotify**: Announced deal with UMG, Sony, and Warner to produce "responsible AI products."

### "Style Transfer" vs. Direct Copying — Legal Distinctions

- **Style transfer**: AI analyzes waveform structures and sonic aesthetics to produce new music that evokes an artist's style without reproducing copyrighted material. Generally considered legally permissible under current law (musical style is not copyrightable).
- **Direct copying**: AI outputs that reproduce specific melodies, harmonic progressions, or recordings that are "substantially similar" to copyrighted works. Subject to takedowns and infringement claims.
- **Key legal principle**: Copyright protects specific expression, not style or genre. An AI model that learns to produce "music that sounds like Drake" without reproducing Drake's actual recordings may not infringe — but the line is legally untested in AI-specific litigation.
- **Practical risk**: Platforms like Spotify and YouTube may issue takedowns based on substantial similarity, regardless of whether the similarity arose from AI training or human composition.

## 6. The Sample Economy Implications

### If Artists Use AI to Generate Custom Samples

The traditional sample clearance economy relies on licensing fees paid to original recording owners (masters) and songwriters (publishing). AI-generated samples could disrupt this in several ways:

**Who loses leverage:**
- **Sample clearance intermediaries**: Lawyers, clearance houses, and brokers who facilitate licensing deals face reduced demand if producers generate original sounds via AI instead of sampling existing recordings.
- **Legacy catalog owners**: Major labels and publishers derive significant revenue from sample clearance fees. If producers can AI-generate sounds that evoke a classic recording without actually sampling it, the economic incentive to clear samples diminishes.
- **Session musicians and producers** whose original recordings are sampled: They lose the residual income stream from clearance fees.

**Who gains leverage:**
- **Independent producers**: AI lowers the barrier to creating sonically complex productions without clearance costs (which can range from thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars).
- **AI platform operators**: Become new gatekeepers — the entity that trained the model effectively replaces the sample library.
- **Artists who opt in to licensed AI training**: Under the UMG/Udio and UMG/Suno deals, artists receive compensation when their music is used for training AND when subscribers generate new works using those models. This creates a new, potentially more passive revenue stream.

**Structural concerns:**
- An estimated **$2.5 billion in unclaimed music royalties** already exists due to inadequate rights tracking. AI-generated music further complicates provenance and attribution.
- AI-generated music is diluting royalty pools and revealing structural flaws in existing compensation frameworks.

### On-Chain Provenance and Attribution Systems

Several initiatives are developing blockchain-based solutions:

- **Blockchain ledger tracking**: Platforms are building systems where every AI generation triggers micro-payments distributed to artists who opted into the training dataset, with payout splits embedded in immutable smart contract metadata.
- **JGGL and on-chain attribution**: Projects like JGGL are pushing for blockchain-based AI music attribution systems, though questions remain about whether blockchain can solve the fundamental training data consent problem. [single-source — via CCN reporting]
- **Rationale**: Blockchain's immutability removes the need for fee-capturing intermediaries to track rights and manage payments. Provenance anchored to a widely trusted settlement layer reduces fragmentation and platform risk.
- **Limitations**: On-chain provenance solves the tracking problem but does not resolve the underlying legal question of whether AI training on copyrighted works requires consent in the first place.

Sources: [CCN](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/jggl-dzmitry-saksonau-suno-warner-ai-music-blockchain-cant-fix-training-data/), [Unite.AI](https://www.unite.ai/ai-music-is-exposing-a-hidden-infrastructure-gap-in-the-creator-economy/)

## Summary: Key Unresolved Questions

1. **Is AI training on copyrighted music fair use?** No US court has ruled on the merits. The USCO says it is not categorically fair use. Summary judgment decisions are not expected before summer 2026.

2. **Do AI model weights constitute "copies" of training data?** The UK High Court says no (Getty v. Stability AI). US courts have not addressed this question.

3. **Can AI-generated music be copyrighted?** Only the human-authored elements. Purely AI-generated output cannot be registered (Thaler v. Perlmutter, affirmed through SCOTUS cert denial March 2, 2026).

4. **Will licensing deals become the industry norm?** The UMG and WMG settlements with Suno/Udio suggest a "license rather than litigate" model is emerging for major labels. Sony's continued litigation means this is not yet universal.

5. **What happens to the sample clearance economy?** If AI can generate bespoke sounds that evoke but do not copy existing recordings, the economic rationale for traditional sample clearance weakens — but the legal and ethical frameworks for this transition are still being established.
