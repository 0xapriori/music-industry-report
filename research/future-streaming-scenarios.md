# Future Scenarios for the Music Industry: Streaming Paradigm Disruption or Entrenchment

*Research compiled March 2026. Three-tier framing used throughout:*
- **FACT** — Verified data from identified sources
- **ANALYSIS** — Reasoned inference from established facts
- **SPECULATION** — Forward-looking projections, clearly flagged

---

## 1. Current Streaming Paradigm — State of Play (March 2026)

### Global Streaming Revenue

**FACT:** Global recorded music streaming revenues exceeded $20 billion for the first time in 2024, reaching $20.4 billion and representing 69% of total recorded music revenues. Total global recorded music revenues grew 4.8% YoY in 2024 to $29.6 billion, marking the tenth consecutive year of growth, though the pace has slowed compared to prior years.
— *Source: [IFPI Global Music Report 2025](https://www.ifpi.org/ifpi-amidst-highly-competitive-market-global-recorded-music-revenues-grew-4-8-in-2024/)*

**FACT:** Paid subscription streaming revenue increased 9.5% in 2024. Ad-supported streaming grew only 1.2%, reaching $5.1 billion.
— *Source: IFPI Global Music Report 2025*

**FACT:** The total number of paid music streaming subscription accounts grew 10.6% YoY to 752 million globally in 2024. U.S. music streaming subscribers hit 130 million in 2025, a record high.
— *Sources: [IFPI GMR 2025](https://www.ifpi.org/ifpi-amidst-highly-competitive-market-global-recorded-music-revenues-grew-4-8-in-2024/); [Digital Music News](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2026/03/05/music-streaming-subscribers-us-2025/)*

### Subscriber Counts by Platform

**FACT:** As of Q1 2025, Spotify reported 675 million monthly active users (up 22% YoY) with 263 million premium subscribers (up 15% YoY).
— *Source: Spotify earnings; [Demand Sage](https://www.demandsage.com/apple-music-statistics/)*

**FACT:** Apple Music has approximately 110 million subscribers worldwide, built entirely on paid subscriptions (no free tier).
— *Source: [Demand Sage](https://www.demandsage.com/apple-music-statistics/)* [single-source — Apple does not regularly disclose exact subscriber counts]

### Market Share by Platform

**FACT:** Spotify holds approximately 37% global market share. Apple Music holds approximately 20% globally. In the U.S., Spotify holds 36% and Apple Music 30.7%, making it effectively a two-player market.
— *Source: [Piano Dreamers](https://www.pianodreamers.com/spotify-market-share/); [AMW Group](https://amworldgroup.com/statistics/music-streaming-statistics)*

### Price Increases

**FACT:** Spotify raised U.S. Premium Individual pricing to $12.99/month in Q1 2026 ($1 increase), with Duo at $19, Family at $22, Student at $7. This makes Spotify the most expensive major music streaming service — $2 more than Apple Music ($10.99), Tidal ($10.99), and Amazon Music ($10.99).
— *Sources: [Headphonesty](https://www.headphonesty.com/2025/11/spotify-become-most-expensive-music-streaming-service/); [Digital Music News](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2026/02/16/apple-music-prices-february-2026/)*

**FACT:** Amazon Music Unlimited also raised prices: Individual to $13/month ($12 for Prime members), Family to $22/month.
— *Source: [World Today News](https://www.world-today-news.com/streaming-services-every-price-hike-in-2025-2026-netflix-spotify-more/)*

**ANALYSIS:** Price increases have not caused meaningful subscriber churn so far. U.S. subscribers hit 130 million in 2025 despite multiple rounds of increases. This suggests music streaming has crossed a stickiness threshold — consumers treat it as a utility, not a discretionary purchase. However, the widening price gap between Spotify and Apple Music creates a competitive vulnerability for Spotify, which Apple has begun to exploit in marketing.

### Is Revenue Growth Slowing?

**FACT:** Global recorded music revenue growth was 4.8% in 2024, down from higher rates in prior years. Streaming's share of total revenues declined slightly for the first time, from 61.5% to 61.3%, with streaming growing marginally slower than the overall market.
— *Source: [MIDiA Research](https://www.midiaresearch.com/blog/recorded-music-market-2024-362-billion-up-65)*

**ANALYSIS:** Revenue growth is decelerating but not plateauing. The shift from explosive growth (10%+ years) to mature growth (4-6%) reflects market maturation in developed economies. Future growth will depend on emerging markets, price increases, and new monetization models (superfans, bundling) rather than raw subscriber acquisition in saturated markets.

## 2. Scenario 1: Entrenchment (Streaming Status Quo Persists and Deepens)

### What Happens If Pro-Rata Stays and Majors Maintain Dominance

**FACT:** The three major labels — UMG (31.7% market share), Sony Music Group (21.7%), and Warner Music Group (15.3%) — collectively control approximately 68.7% of global recorded music market share (2024 data). The independent music community holds approximately 20.17%.
— *Sources: [Music & Copyright via MBW](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/); [Mordor Intelligence](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/independent-artists)*

**ANALYSIS:** Under the pro-rata model, the mathematical reality is stark: roughly 1% of tracks capture the majority of streaming revenue. This concentration benefits major labels and superstar artists. If this structure persists, the economics tilt further toward catalog owners and hit-makers while the "long tail" of artists sees diminishing per-stream returns as the total track count grows.

### Catalog vs. Frontline Economics

**FACT:** In 2024, catalog music (tracks older than 3 years) accounted for 66% of UMG's recorded music digital and physical revenue, up from 62% in 2023. Streams grow 177% from an album's first 18 months to months 19-36, while sales decline 33% in the same comparison.
— *Source: [MBW/UMG Annual Report](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/5-million-songs-and-288m-spend-on-catalog-8-things-we-learned-from-universal-music-groups-2024-annual-report/); [Chartmetric](https://hmc.chartmetric.com/oldies-but-goodies-streaming-makes-catalogue-records-new-again/)*

**ANALYSIS:** Streaming has fundamentally inverted the traditional economics of recorded music. Under physical/download models, frontline releases drove revenue and catalog depreciated. Under streaming, catalog appreciates because listeners repeatedly return to familiar music, and the marginal cost of keeping it available is near zero. This explains the catalog acquisition spree (Hipgnosis, major label investments) and creates a structural advantage for rights holders with deep back catalogs. For new artists without catalog, streaming economics are a treadmill — you must constantly release to maintain visibility and income.

### The "Middle Class Musician" Problem

**FACT:** To earn a modest $3,000/month solely from Spotify streams in 2026, an artist needs approximately 750,000-850,000 monthly streams.
— *Source: [Artistrack](https://artistrack.com/streaming-royalties-explained-2026/)*

**ANALYSIS:** In an entrenchment scenario, the middle-class musician problem gets worse, not better. As the total track count on platforms grows (see Section 4 on AI), the denominator in the pro-rata pool expands while the numerator (total subscription revenue) grows more slowly. Mid-tier artists are increasingly forced to treat streaming as a discovery/marketing tool rather than a revenue source, shifting to live performance, merchandise, sync licensing, and direct-to-fan models for actual income. The industry mantra for 2026: "streaming is your business card, not your salary."

**FACT:** Merchandise and physical releases are expanding at 8.45% CAGR for independent artists, indicating that fans will pay premium prices for tangible goods.
— *Source: [Mordor Intelligence](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/independent-artists)*

### Subscription Fatigue

**ANALYSIS:** Music streaming has proven more resilient to subscription fatigue than video streaming. At $10.99-$12.99/month for access to essentially all recorded music, the value proposition remains strong compared to multiple $15-20 video subscriptions each offering only a fraction of available content. However, Spotify's positioning as the most expensive option creates room for competitors — particularly Apple, which bundles music into the broader Apple One ecosystem.

## 3. Scenario 2: Platform Disruption (New Models Emerge)

### User-Centric / Artist-Centric Payment Models

**FACT:** Deezer launched its Artist-Centric Payment System (ACPS), which doubles per-stream payments for artists with at least 1,000 streams from 500 different subscribers monthly. Songs actively searched for or found in non-algorithmic playlists also receive the double boost. Deezer removed 26 million tracks (~13% of its catalog) — noise, functional sounds, and tracks with no listens in 12 months.
— *Source: [Deezer Support](https://support.deezer.com/hc/en-gb/articles/360002471277-Artist-Centric-Payment-Model-ACPS); [MBW](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/indie-artists-shift-to-artist-centric-payments-on-deezer-as-streaming-service-signs-new-deal-with-merlin/)*

**FACT:** Deezer made EUR 134 million in Q1 2025 with 9.4 million subscribers (1.1% YoY revenue growth). All three majors (UMG, WMG, and Merlin for indies) have signed onto the ACPS in France.
— *Source: [MusicRadar](https://www.musicradar.com/music-industry/streaming-sharing/artist-centric-music-streamer-deezer-just-made-eur134-million-in-the-first-quarter-of-2025)*

**FACT:** In January 2025, Deezer partnered with SACEM to apply ACPS to publishing rights — the first update to the publishing remuneration model since streaming began.
— *Source: [Deezer Newsroom](https://newsroom-deezer.com/2025/01/deezer-and-sacem-partner-for-fairer-remuneration-of-publishing-rights-with-the-artist-centric-streaming-model/)*

**ANALYSIS:** Deezer's experiment provides early evidence that artist-centric models can work without destroying platform economics. However, Deezer's small market share (~2% globally) limits the conclusions that can be drawn. The 1,000 streams / 500 listeners threshold inherently creates a two-tier system — artists above the threshold benefit; those below it see reduced rates. Whether Spotify or Apple Music adopt similar models remains the critical question. Spotify has shown no public interest in abandoning pro-rata; Apple has not disclosed its internal payout methodology in detail.

**SPECULATION:** If Deezer demonstrates sustained improvement in artist retention and satisfaction, competitive pressure may push larger platforms to adopt modified artist-centric elements by 2028-2030. However, major labels — who benefit from pro-rata because their catalogs dominate total streams — have limited incentive to push for systemic change.

### Social Streaming: TikTok as Music Platform

**FACT:** UMG and TikTok resolved their licensing dispute in May 2024, with a new agreement including "improved remuneration" for artists and songwriters, plus AI-related protections. TikTok's 2025-2026 licensing framework is a hybrid blanket/modular system. In July 2025, TikTok implemented new Music Terms of Service defining three categories: creator use, brand/commercial use, and live-streaming.
— *Sources: [Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/umg-and-tiktok-end-battle-with-new-agreement-1235013809/); [Spring Sound](https://springsoundmusic.com/tiktok-2025-music-licensing-changes-explained/)*

**ANALYSIS:** TikTok remains primarily a promotional vehicle rather than a direct revenue generator for artists. Its economic contribution to the music industry is in driving discovery and streaming activity on traditional platforms. The 2024 UMG standoff demonstrated that the music industry has leverage over social platforms — but also that TikTok's promotional value is difficult for labels to abandon. TikTok is unlikely to become a primary music consumption platform; it lacks the lean-back listening experience that drives subscription value.

### Spatial Audio / Immersive Experiences

**FACT:** More than 90% of Apple Music listeners have tried Spatial Audio, and it accounts for nearly one-third of all plays on Apple Music. Apple introduced Apple Spatial Audio Format (ASAF) at WWDC 2025, going beyond Dolby Atmos with head-tracking and environment-adaptive sound. Google and Samsung introduced Eclipsa Audio in 2025 as an open-source alternative to Dolby Atmos.
— *Sources: [Apple Music for Artists](https://artists.apple.com/support/5477-elevate-sound-spatial-audio-dolby-atmos); [FlatpanelsHD](https://www.flatpanelshd.com/news.php?subaction=showfull&id=1750327296)*

**ANALYSIS:** Spatial audio is becoming a meaningful differentiator but not a revenue driver in itself. It does not change the underlying economics of streaming. Its primary impact is on platform competition (Apple leveraging hardware integration) and on production costs for artists (mixing in Atmos is more expensive than stereo). It could, however, support premium pricing tiers — a "hi-fi immersive" tier at a higher price point, which would increase ARPU if consumers adopt it.

### Web3 / Decentralized Platforms

**FACT:** Audius remains operational, integrating with Feature.fm in August 2025 and upgrading its validator nodes in January 2026. It runs a grants program. However, no major streaming share data or subscriber numbers are publicly available, suggesting it remains a niche platform.
— *Source: [Audius Blog](https://blog.audius.co/); [CoinMarketCap](https://coinmarketcap.com/cmc-ai/audius/latest-updates/)*

**ANALYSIS:** Web3 music platforms have failed to achieve mainstream adoption as of March 2026. Audius, the most prominent, operates as a niche community rather than a competitive streaming service. The fundamental challenge remains unchanged: consumers choose platforms based on catalog breadth and user experience, not payment philosophy. Without major label catalogs, Web3 platforms cannot compete for mainstream listeners. DAOs and token-based models remain experimental with no proven path to scale.

### Bundling Trends

**FACT:** Spotify has bundled up to 15 hours of audiobook listening per month into Premium, covering 500,000+ titles. This bundling strategy reduced the mechanical royalty rate owed to songwriters, triggering a lawsuit from the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC). The lawsuit was initially dismissed in February 2025 but was reopened later that month.
— *Sources: [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/business/streaming/spotify-songwriters-less-mechanical-royalties-audiobooks-bundle-1235673829/); [Variety](https://variety.com/2025/music/news/spotify-wins-lawsuit-bundling-royalties-1236289823/)*

**FACT:** Apple bundles Apple Music into Apple One (alongside TV+, Arcade, News+, iCloud+, and Fitness+), creating a value proposition that makes Music effectively cheaper for multi-service subscribers.

**ANALYSIS:** Bundling is Spotify's strategy to justify price increases and diversify revenue, but it creates a structural conflict with music rights holders who see their share of subscription revenue diluted. The MLC lawsuit outcome will set precedent for whether bundling constitutes a legitimate basis for reducing per-stream royalty calculations. If Spotify prevails, expect more aggressive bundling across the industry. If the MLC wins, it could force a renegotiation of how subscription revenue is allocated between content types.

### Could a New Platform Break the Duopoly?

**SPECULATION:** Breaking the Spotify/Apple duopoly would require either: (a) a platform with a fundamentally different value proposition (not just a slightly better version of the same thing), or (b) a tech giant with an existing massive user base (e.g., YouTube Music, Amazon Music) making a major strategic push. YouTube Music is the most plausible challenger given YouTube's 2+ billion monthly users, but it has yet to reach parity in paid subscribers. The barrier is not technology — it is catalog licensing, which the majors control, and user inertia. No credible new entrant is visible as of March 2026.

## 4. Scenario 3: AI Disruption Reshapes the Economics

### The "Infinite Content" Problem

**FACT:** Deezer receives approximately 60,000 AI-generated tracks daily, representing 39% of all uploads to the platform. Over 2 million AI-created tracks were streamed globally in Q1 2025 alone. Music streaming platforms now host over 250 million tracks total.
— *Sources: [WIPO](https://www.wipo.int/en/web/wipo-magazine/articles/how-ai-generated-songs-are-fueling-the-rise-of-streaming-farms-74310); [MBW](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/quarter-of-a-billion-tracks-now-sit-on-music-streaming-services-where-does-it-end/)*

**FACT:** Apple Music identified and demonetized approximately 2 billion fraudulent streams in 2025, translating to approximately $17 million in royalties that would have been improperly distributed. Deezer reported that up to 85% of streams generated by fully AI-produced music were flagged as fraudulent.
— *Source: [Revolution 935](https://www.revolution935.com/2026/02/03/apple-music-flags-2-billion-fraudulent-streams-amid-ai-audio-surge/)*

**FACT:** An estimated minimum of $1 billion annually is being siphoned from the royalty pool through fraudulent and AI-generated streaming activity.
— *Source: [Revolution 935](https://www.revolution935.com/2026/02/03/apple-music-flags-2-billion-fraudulent-streams-amid-ai-audio-surge/)* [single-source — this estimate should be treated with caution]

**ANALYSIS:** If AI-generated music continues to flood platforms at the current rate, the mathematical consequence under pro-rata is clear: per-stream rates decline as the total stream count is diluted by low-value content. This is the "infinite content" problem — when the cost of creating music approaches zero, the supply of tracks on platforms becomes effectively unlimited, while the revenue pool (total subscriptions) grows slowly. The artists most harmed are those in the middle — not famous enough to command dedicated listening, but creating genuinely human music that must compete for attention with an ocean of AI-generated material.

### AI as Production Tool vs. AI as Competitor

**ANALYSIS:** The distinction is critical:
- **AI as tool:** Lowers production costs for human artists. Democratizes music creation. Potentially raises quality floor. Benefits: emerging artists who couldn't afford studio time. This is broadly positive.
- **AI as competitor:** Generates complete tracks that displace human music in playlists, background listening, and mood-based consumption. Dilutes the royalty pool. This is broadly negative for human artists.

The battleground is functional music — playlists for focus, sleep, workout, ambient background. This category is most vulnerable to AI replacement because listeners care about mood/function, not artist identity. Deezer's removal of "noise and functional sounds" from royalty calculations is a direct response to this dynamic.

### Licensed AI Models

**FACT:** Warner Music Group struck a licensing deal with Suno in November 2025, settling their copyright lawsuit. Under the deal, Suno must train on licensed works and users must pay to download tracks. UMG settled with Udio in October 2025, creating a "walled garden" where AI-generated content cannot leave the platform. UMG has not settled with Suno; UMG also partnered with NVIDIA on AI tools.
— *Sources: [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/pro/what-suno-udio-licensing-deals-mean-future-ai-music/); [MBW](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/warner-music-group-settles-with-suno-strikes-first-of-its-kind-deal-with-ai-song-generator/)*

**ANALYSIS:** Two competing models are emerging:
1. **Open licensed model (WMG/Suno):** AI-generated music enters the market but is trained on licensed content. Revenue flows to original rights holders through licensing fees. This legitimizes AI music creation while maintaining some economic return to catalog owners.
2. **Walled garden model (UMG/Udio):** AI creations stay contained. Rights holders maintain control, AI is treated as an engagement/entertainment tool rather than a music production pipeline.

The WMG/Suno model is more likely to become standard because it is more commercially viable — Suno's value proposition depends on users being able to use what they create. However, neither model addresses the fundamental dilution problem: more licensed AI music still means more tracks competing for the same royalty pool.

### Impact by Artist Segment

**ANALYSIS:**
- **Superstars:** Least affected. Their value is identity, brand, live performance, cultural significance — none of which AI replicates. Streaming income is a fraction of their total revenue.
- **Mid-tier artists (100K-1M monthly listeners):** Most vulnerable. They rely more heavily on streaming income and lack the brand power to differentiate from AI alternatives. Per-stream rate decline hits them hardest.
- **Emerging artists:** Mixed impact. AI tools lower the barrier to creating professional-sounding music (positive). But AI-generated competition makes discovery harder (negative). Net effect depends on whether they can build audience identity beyond just the music.

**SPECULATION:** AI likely lowers the floor (easier to create passable music, harder to earn from it) and may raise the ceiling for artists who use AI tools creatively while maintaining human identity and connection. The middle gets squeezed hardest.

## 5. Scenario 4: Regulatory Intervention

### Consent Decree Modification (ASCAP/BMI)

**FACT:** The ASCAP and BMI consent decrees have been in place since 1941 (most recently modified in 2001 and 1994, respectively). The DOJ opened a review in 2019 but ended it in 2021 without proposing changes. No modifications have been enacted as of March 2026.
— *Source: [Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11463)*

**ANALYSIS:** If consent decrees were modified or terminated, ASCAP and BMI would gain the ability to negotiate rates freely rather than accepting Copyright Royalty Board determinations. This would likely increase songwriter compensation from streaming services. However, it would also increase costs for licensees (streaming platforms, venues, broadcasters), potentially leading to higher subscription prices or reduced profitability for platforms. The political environment for modification is uncertain — broadcasters and tech companies lobby heavily against changes.

### Terrestrial Radio Performance Royalty

**FACT:** The American Music Fairness Act was reintroduced in January 2025 (H.R.861 / S.326) with bipartisan sponsorship. It would establish a performance right for sound recordings broadcast by AM/FM radio. Small broadcasters (under $1.5M revenue) would pay flat fees ($500/year commercial, $10/year noncommercial). A congressional hearing was held in December 2025.
— *Sources: [Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/861); [ITIF](https://itif.org/publications/2025/12/19/fixing-music-royalties-through-the-american-music-fairness-act/)*

**FACT:** The U.S. is one of the only countries in the world that does not require terrestrial radio to pay performance royalties to artists for sound recordings.
— *Source: [Recording Academy](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/issues-policy/american-music-fairness-act)*

**ANALYSIS:** Passage would create a new revenue stream for recording artists and labels but would be modest in absolute terms — terrestrial radio revenue is declining, and the small-broadcaster exemptions limit the total pool. Its symbolic significance outweighs its economic impact: it would align the U.S. with international norms and could unlock reciprocal royalty payments from other countries for U.S. artists' music played on foreign radio. However, the NAB (National Association of Broadcasters) remains a powerful opposing lobby, and the bill has failed to pass multiple times despite bipartisan support.

### AI Training and Consent

**FACT:** The EU AI Act requires GPAI model providers to publish summaries of training data content (Article 53(1)(d)). The GPAI Code of Practice was published by the European Commission's AI Office in July 2025. New AI companies must be compliant by 2026, existing ones by 2027. In January 2026, the European Parliament voted on a report proposing "a more robust licensing and remuneration regime for training data," with a full Parliament vote scheduled for March 2026.
— *Sources: [Taylor Wessing](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/interface/2025/predictions-2026/copyright-in-2026); [Global Policy Watch](https://www.globalpolicywatch.com/2026/02/european-parliament-proposes-changes-to-copyright-protection-in-the-age-of-generative-ai/)*

**FACT:** Creative groups have stated that the EU AI Act "doesn't do enough to protect artists' copyright."
— *Source: [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/next/2025/08/02/eus-ai-act-doesnt-do-enough-to-protect-artists-copyright-creative-groups-say)*

**ANALYSIS:** The EU is ahead of the U.S. on AI/copyright regulation but implementation remains uncertain. Transparency requirements (disclosing training data) are a necessary precondition for enforcement but insufficient on their own. The critical question is whether "opt-in" consent for AI training becomes the global standard. If it does, AI music companies would need licensing deals with every rights holder whose work they train on — dramatically increasing costs and potentially limiting AI music quality. The WMG/Suno and UMG/Udio deals may represent early versions of this licensing framework.

**SPECULATION:** A transatlantic regulatory divergence is likely: the EU moving toward mandatory licensing/consent, the U.S. relying more on market-based solutions (voluntary licensing deals, platform policies). This creates compliance complexity for global AI music companies and potential safe-harbor arbitrage.

### Safe Harbor Reform

**FACT:** The European Commission is required to assess the DSM (Digital Single Market) Directive no sooner than June 2026, which includes provisions that modified safe harbor for user-upload platforms (Article 17).
— *Source: [Taylor Wessing](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/interface/2025/predictions-2026/copyright-in-2026)*

**ANALYSIS:** Safe harbor reform (limiting platforms' protection from liability for user-uploaded infringing content) would shift the economic burden of policing copyright from rights holders to platforms. This could increase licensing payments from platforms like YouTube but could also lead to more aggressive content takedowns and filtering, potentially limiting emerging artists' ability to gain exposure through user-generated content.

## 6. Scenario 5: Artist-Led Structural Change

### Collective Bargaining Expansion

**FACT:** UMAW (United Musicians and Allied Workers) has organized tens of thousands of music workers around streaming royalties, record contracts, venue safety, merch cuts, and SXSW payment practices. UMAW advocates for the Living Wage for Musicians Act, which would create a new streaming royalty to supplement existing rates.
— *Source: [UMAW](https://weareumaw.org/make-streaming-pay)*

**ANALYSIS:** The music industry lacks the unionization infrastructure that has benefited screen actors (SAG-AFTRA) and writers (WGA). Key differences: musicians are predominantly classified as independent contractors, not employees; there is no single employer to bargain with (unlike studios); and the industry is fragmented across labels, distributors, platforms, and venues. UMAW represents a growing consciousness but has not yet achieved the kind of collective action that forced change in Hollywood.

**SPECULATION:** The SAG-AFTRA strikes of 2023 demonstrated that collective action can extract meaningful concessions from technology companies on AI and compensation issues. If a critical mass of mid-tier artists organized around specific, actionable demands (transparent royalty accounting, minimum per-stream rates, AI consent requirements), it could create pressure — particularly if combined with coordinated catalog withdrawal threats. However, the collective action problem is acute: individual artists who withdraw from platforms sacrifice their own discovery and income, even if collective withdrawal would give leverage.

### Transparent / Real-Time Royalty Systems

**ANALYSIS:** Blockchain-based royalty tracking has been discussed for a decade but has not been adopted at scale. The obstacles are not technological — they are political. Transparent, real-time royalty systems would expose the opaque accounting practices that major labels and publishers use, including cross-collateralization, breakage, and delayed payments. Incumbents have limited incentive to adopt transparency. Independent distributors (DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby) already provide relatively transparent, near-real-time reporting — but they lack negotiating power to increase the underlying rates.

### The Taylor Swift Effect — Does It Scale?

**FACT:** Taylor Swift's album "The Life of a Showgirl" debuted at 4 million equivalent album units in its first week (October 2025). Her label Republic finished 2025 at 16.04% current market share, heavily driven by Swift's releases.
— *Source: [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/pro/record-label-market-share-2025-republic-taylor-swift/)*

**ANALYSIS:** Taylor Swift demonstrated that a superstar can renegotiate the terms of the music industry — re-recording masters, leveraging platform exclusives, demanding higher royalty rates. However, this model does not scale. Swift's leverage comes from her irreplaceability: she is one of a handful of artists whose catalog withdrawal would materially impact a platform's subscriber numbers. The vast majority of artists — even successful ones — do not have this leverage. The "Taylor Swift effect" is more accurately described as the "superstar exception" — it proves that power exists, but only for those who already have it.

### New Organizational Forms

**ANALYSIS:** Artist cooperatives (e.g., AWAL before Sony acquisition, Amuse before acquisition) have repeatedly been absorbed by major-label structures because the economics of music distribution create strong incentives for consolidation. DAOs have not demonstrated viability for music at scale. The most promising alternative structures are direct-to-fan platforms (Bandcamp, Patreon, Substack for musicians) that allow artists to monetize their most engaged fans at higher per-fan rates than streaming. However, these serve as complements to streaming, not replacements.

## 7. Revenue Projections and Market Data

### Global Recorded Music Revenue Trajectory

**FACT (published forecast):** Goldman Sachs projects global recorded music trade revenues to reach $53.2 billion by 2030, with paid streaming generating $27.5 billion for labels and artists by 2030. Goldman projects 1.15 billion paid streaming subscribers globally by 2030.
— *Source: [Goldman Sachs "Music in the Air"](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/global-music-revenues-are-forecast-to-double-to-200-million-in-2035); [MBW](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/goldman-sachs-says-1-15bn-people-will-pay-for-music-streaming-by-2030-as-it-ups-forecast/)*

**FACT:** Goldman Sachs projects total global music revenues (including live, publishing, sync) to reach $163.7 billion by 2030 and nearly $200 billion by 2035, up from $105 billion in 2024.
— *Source: [Goldman Sachs](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/global-music-revenues-are-forecast-to-double-to-200-million-in-2035)*

### Publishing Revenue Trajectory

**FACT (published forecast):** Global music publishing market valued at approximately $7.7 billion in 2025. Goldman Sachs projects publishing to reach $12.5 billion by 2030 (from $8.3 billion in 2023).
— *Source: [Bolero Music](https://www.boleromusic.com/blog/music-in-the-air-2025)* [single-source for specific projection]

### Live Music Revenue Trajectory

**FACT (published forecast):** Global live music generated $34.6 billion in 2024. Goldman Sachs projects approximately 7% CAGR to 2035, reaching $67.1 billion.
— *Source: [Goldman Sachs "Music in the Air"](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/global-music-revenues-are-forecast-to-double-to-200-million-in-2035)*

### Sync Licensing Growth

**FACT:** Sync licensing generated an estimated $600-650 million globally in 2025 per IFPI. Revenue from sync licensing grew 18% in 2025. Goldman Sachs projects sync to grow from $1.6 billion in 2023 to $3.4 billion by 2040.
— *Sources: [IFPI](https://www.ifpi.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/GMR2025_SOTI.pdf); [Goldman Sachs](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/global-music-revenues-are-forecast-to-double-to-200-million-in-2035)*

Note: The IFPI figure ($600-650M) and the Goldman figure ($1.6B in 2023) likely use different definitions of sync revenue scope. The discrepancy may reflect Goldman including broader soundtrack/production music markets. These should not be directly compared without understanding the methodological differences.

### How Music Compares to Other Entertainment Sectors

**ANALYSIS:** At $105 billion total (2024), the global music economy is smaller than gaming (~$184 billion), film/TV (~$315 billion including theatrical, home entertainment, and streaming), but larger than podcasting (~$4 billion) or audiobooks (~$7 billion). However, music's revenue per hour of consumption is among the lowest of any entertainment form — a reflection of the all-you-can-eat subscription model at relatively low price points. Goldman's $200 billion 2035 projection would bring music closer to gaming but still well below film/TV.

## Summary: Likelihood Assessment of Scenarios

| Scenario | Likelihood by 2030 | Key Signal to Watch |
|---|---|---|
| **Entrenchment** (status quo deepens) | HIGH — This is the default trajectory | Subscriber growth rates in emerging markets; price increase tolerance |
| **Platform disruption** (new models) | LOW-MODERATE — Incremental changes more likely than paradigm shift | Whether any major platform adopts artist-centric payments; YouTube Music subscriber growth |
| **AI disruption** (economics reshaped) | HIGH — Already underway | Per-stream rate trends; platform policies on AI-generated content; track count growth rate |
| **Regulatory intervention** | MODERATE — EU likely, U.S. uncertain | EU AI Act enforcement outcomes; American Music Fairness Act vote; DSM Directive review |
| **Artist-led structural change** | LOW — Collective action problems remain unsolved | UMAW membership growth; any coordinated platform withdrawal; legislative wins |

**ANALYSIS:** These scenarios are not mutually exclusive. The most probable near-term future (2026-2030) combines elements of entrenchment (streaming remains dominant, pro-rata persists, majors maintain share) with AI disruption (accelerating content flood, platform policy responses, licensing frameworks for AI tools). Regulatory intervention will be incremental and geographically uneven. Artist-led structural change remains aspirational but lacks the organizational infrastructure to force systemic shifts in the near term.
