## 1. LANDMARK COPYRIGHT LEGISLATION

### 1.1 Copyright Act of 1909 (Public Law 60-349)

- **Signed:** March 4, 1909
- **Effective:** July 1, 1909
- **Context:** In February 1908, the Supreme Court ruled in *White-Smith Music Publishing Co. v. Apollo Co.* (209 U.S. 1) that manufacturers of pianola (player piano) music rolls were not required to pay royalties to composers, holding that music rolls were not "copies" of musical compositions because they were not "written or printed" records.
- **Key provision — Compulsory Mechanical License:** The Act created the first compulsory license allowing anyone to make a mechanical reproduction of a musical composition without the copyright owner's consent, provided they adhered to the license provisions. This was a direct response to the *White-Smith* ruling.
- **Statutory Rate:** The initial mechanical royalty was set at **2 cents per copy**. This rate remained unchanged from 1909 until 1978 — a 69-year freeze.
- **Anti-monopoly intent:** Congress created the compulsory license specifically to prevent any single entity from monopolizing recordings. At the time, the Aeolian Company had a near-monopoly on player piano rolls and had been negotiating exclusive deals with music publishers.
- **Impact on musicians:** Songwriters received guaranteed (if modest) compensation for mechanical reproductions. Performers of sound recordings received no federal copyright protection — a gap that persisted for decades.
- **Source:** [Copyright Act of 1909 full text (copyright.gov)](https://www.copyright.gov/history/1909act.pdf); [U.S. Copyright Office history](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/creation.html)

### 1.2 Copyright Act of 1976 (Public Law 94-553)

- **Signed:** October 19, 1976, by President Gerald Ford
- **Effective:** January 1, 1978 (most provisions)
- **Significance:** First major revision of copyright law since 1909.

**Key provisions relevant to the music industry:**

1. **Copyright Duration:** Extended protection to "life of the author plus 50 years" (Section 302). Works made for hire and anonymous/pseudonymous works received a static 75-year term from publication. The prior law allowed only 28 years plus a 28-year renewal (56 years maximum).

2. **Sound Recordings as Protected Subject Matter:** Included sound recordings among categories of copyrightable works (codifying the Sound Recording Amendment of 1971, Public Law 92-140). However, protection for sound recordings remained more limited than for other works — notably, no public performance right for sound recordings (terrestrial broadcast).

3. **Work-for-Hire Doctrine (Section 101, Section 201(b)):** Defined "work made for hire" as either: (a) work by an employee within scope of employment, or (b) work specially ordered/commissioned falling within nine enumerated categories, with a signed written agreement. Sound recordings were NOT included in the nine enumerated categories — a significant omission. Record labels nonetheless frequently asserted that featured artists were "employees" creating recordings in the course of employment.
   - **1999 amendment controversy:** In November 1999, a provision was inserted into the Satellite Home Viewer Improvement Act (without public hearings) adding "sound recordings" to the enumerated categories. After heavy artist backlash, Congress repealed this addition in 2000, stating the repeal was without prejudice to any legal position.

4. **Termination Rights (Section 203):** Authors (or their heirs) may terminate grants of copyright 35 years after execution. The termination window runs from year 35 to year 40, with notice required 2-10 years in advance. Does NOT apply to works made for hire.
   - **Impact on music:** Termination rights began taking effect in 2013. Notable examples include Victor Willis (Village People) successfully terminating rights to compositions, and the Eagles filing termination notices for albums including "The Long Run."

5. **Pre-1972 Sound Recordings:** Congress left pre-1972 sound recordings under state law protection, providing that states could continue protecting them until the end of 2047.

- **Source:** [Public Law 94-553 (copyright.gov)](https://www.copyright.gov/history/pl94-553.pdf); [Copyright Act of 1976 (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright_Act_of_1976); [U.S. Copyright Office timeline](https://www.copyright.gov/timeline/timeline_1950-1997.html)

### 1.3 Audio Home Recording Act of 1992 (Public Law 102-563)

- **Signed:** October 28, 1992
- **Context:** Congress brokered a compromise between the music industry and consumer electronics industry over digital audio recording technology, specifically Digital Audio Tape (DAT).

**Key provisions:**

1. **Royalty levies on devices and blank media:**
   - Digital audio recording **devices**: 2% of transfer price (minimum $1, maximum $8 per device; $12 max for physically integrated multi-device units)
   - Digital audio recording **media** (e.g., blank DAT tapes, blank CDs): 3% of transfer price

2. **DART Fund distribution:**
   - **Sound Recordings Fund:** receives 2/3 of collected royalties. Within this fund, 40% goes to featured artists, 2.5% to non-featured vocalists, 1.5% to non-featured instrumentalists, and the remainder to "interested copyright parties" (labels).
   - **Musical Works Fund:** receives 1/3 of collected royalties, split evenly between songwriters and publishers.

3. **Serial Copy Management System (SCMS):** Required all consumer digital audio recording devices to incorporate SCMS, which allows unlimited first-generation digital copies from an original source but prevents making digital copies of copies.

4. **Consumer protection:** Legalized private, noncommercial audio recording by consumers. Provided immunity from copyright infringement suits for consumers making personal copies.

5. **Financial performance:** DART Fund revenues peaked at approximately $5.2 million in 2000 and declined steadily thereafter (approximately $2.4 million by 2005), as the technologies covered by the Act were overtaken by computers and digital formats not covered.

- **Impact:** The AHRA enabled DAT to be sold to consumers without infringement liability fears. However, DAT never achieved mass consumer adoption. The Act's narrow definition of "digital audio recording device" was later held not to cover MP3 players (RIAA v. Diamond Multimedia, 180 F.3d 1072, 9th Cir. 1999), limiting the Act's relevance in the digital age.

- **Source:** [Public Law 102-563 (govinfo.gov)](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/statute/106/4237); [GovTrack bill summary](https://www.govtrack.us/congress/bills/102/s1623); [Copyright Office DART page](https://www.copyright.gov/licensing/sec_1003.html)

### 1.4 Digital Performance Right in Sound Recordings Act of 1995 (DPRA) (Public Law 104-39)

- **Signed:** November 1, 1995
- **Significance:** Created, for the first time, an exclusive right for copyright owners of sound recordings to perform publicly their works by means of certain digital audio transmissions.

**Key provisions:**

1. **Three-tier system based on impact on record sales:**
   - **Exempt transmissions:** Non-subscription broadcast transmissions (traditional AM/FM radio simulcasts) — no license fees required.
   - **Statutory license:** Nonexempt, noninteractive digital subscription transmissions — subject to compulsory licensing at rates set by the Copyright Royalty Board (then the Copyright Arbitration Royalty Panel).
   - **Negotiated license:** Interactive services (e.g., on-demand streaming) — must negotiate directly with copyright owners.

2. **Effective dates:** Most provisions took effect 3 months after enactment, except Sections 114(e) and 114(f) (rate-setting provisions), which took effect immediately.

- **Impact:** Established the legal framework for digital music services to compensate sound recording copyright holders. Laid groundwork for SoundExchange and digital royalty collection. Did NOT create a performance right for terrestrial (AM/FM) radio — a gap that persists to this day.

- **1998 Modifications (DMCA):** The DMCA expanded the statutory license to include eligible nonsubscription transmissions (e.g., webcasting) and preexisting satellite digital audio radio services.

- **Source:** [Public Law 104-39 (copyright.gov)](https://www.copyright.gov/legislation/pl104-39.html); [DPRA (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Performance_Right_in_Sound_Recordings_Act); [17 U.S.C. Section 114 (LII)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/114)

### 1.5 Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) (Public Law 105-304)

- **Signed:** October 28, 1998
- **Purpose:** Implemented the WIPO Copyright Treaty and WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty; ushered copyright law into the digital era.

**Key provisions relevant to music:**

1. **Title I — Anti-Circumvention (Section 1201):**
   - Made it illegal to circumvent technological measures that control access to copyrighted works.
   - Made it illegal to manufacture, distribute, or traffic in devices designed to circumvent such measures.
   - **Impact on music:** Provided legal backing for DRM on digital music files. Later became relevant in the Sony BMG rootkit litigation.

2. **Title II — Safe Harbor Provisions (Section 512 — Online Copyright Infringement Liability Limitation Act / OCILLA):**
   - Created four safe harbors for online service providers (OSPs):
     - (a) Transient digital network communications (mere conduit)
     - (b) System caching
     - (c) Information residing on systems at direction of users (user-uploaded content)
     - (d) Information location tools (search engines, directories)
   - **Notice-and-takedown:** OSPs must promptly remove or disable access to allegedly infringing material upon receiving proper notification from copyright holders.
   - **Requirements for safe harbor:** OSPs must designate an agent to receive takedown notices, implement a repeat infringer policy, and accommodate standard technical measures.
   - **Impact on music:** The safe harbor provisions became central to the music industry's relationship with platforms like YouTube. Music industry groups have consistently argued that Section 512's safe harbors create a "value gap" — platforms profit from music while shifting the burden of enforcement to rights holders through the takedown process. This remains one of the most contested aspects of music copyright law.

3. **Title IV — Expanded DPRA provisions:**
   - Extended the digital performance right statutory license to cover webcasting (eligible nonsubscription transmissions) and ephemeral recordings made in connection with licensed transmissions.

- **Source:** [DMCA full text (copyright.gov)](https://www.copyright.gov/legislation/dmca.pdf); [CRS report on safe harbors (congress.gov)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11478); [EFF DMCA page](https://www.eff.org/issues/dmca)

### 1.6 Music Modernization Act (MMA) (Public Law 115-264)

- **Full title:** Orrin G. Hatch-Bob Goodlatte Music Modernization Act
- **Signed:** October 11, 2018, by President Donald Trump
- **Passed:** Unanimously by the Senate (October 2018) after passing the House 415-0 (April 2018)
- **Structure:** Consolidation of three separate bills:

#### Title I — Musical Works Modernization Act

1. **Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC):**
   - Created a nonprofit entity to administer a compulsory blanket mechanical license for digital phonorecord deliveries (interactive streaming and downloads).
   - The MLC receives usage reports from digital music providers, collects and distributes royalties, and identifies musical works and their copyright owners.
   - Must establish and maintain a publicly accessible database of musical works, ownership, and associated sound recordings.
   - For unmatched works: the MLC may distribute unclaimed royalties to identified copyright owners based on relative market shares.
   - The MLC became operational on January 1, 2021.

2. **Rate-setting standard change:**
   - Replaced the policy-oriented 801(b)(1) rate-setting standard with a market-based "willing buyer/willing seller" standard for mechanical royalties on interactive streaming.
   - This was the same standard already used for sound recording digital performance royalties.

3. **Impact:** Addressed the "black box" problem of unmatched mechanical royalties in streaming. Prior to the MMA, digital services had to send individual notices of intent to every copyright owner — often impossible given fragmented ownership data.

#### Title II — CLASSICS Act (Compensating Legacy Artists for their Songs, Service, and Important Contributions to Society Act)

1. **Pre-1972 sound recordings:** Extended federal protections to pre-February 15, 1972 sound recordings for purposes of digital audio transmission.
2. **Protection timeline:**
   - Recordings published before 1923: protection until December 31, 2021
   - Recordings published 1923-1946: protection for 100 years from publication
   - Recordings published 1947-1956: protection for 110 years from publication
   - Recordings published after 1956 but before February 15, 1972: protection until February 15, 2067
3. **Royalty obligations:** Digital music services must track, provide notice of, and pay royalties for use of pre-1972 sound recordings equivalent to royalties paid for post-1972 recordings.

#### Title III — AMP Act (Allocation for Music Producers)

1. **Producer/engineer royalties:** Established that the designated collective (SoundExchange) shall distribute a portion of statutory royalties directly to producers, mixers, and sound engineers who were part of the creative process.
2. **Mechanism:** Distribution pursuant to a "letter of direction" from the featured artist.
3. **Retroactive application:** The AMP Act included provisions for royalties on recordings fixed before November 1, 1995.

- **Source:** [Public Law 115-264 (congress.gov)](https://www.congress.gov/115/plaws/publ264/PLAW-115publ264.pdf); [Copyright Office MMA page](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/); [Copyright Office MMA FAQ](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/faq.html)

## 2. LANDMARK COURT CASES

### 2.1 A&M Records, Inc. v. Napster, Inc.

- **Citation:** 239 F.3d 1004 (9th Cir. 2001)
- **Case Numbers:** Nos. 00-16401, 00-16403
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit
- **Decision date:** February 12, 2001
- **Parties:** A&M Records and other major labels (plaintiffs) vs. Napster, Inc. (defendant), a peer-to-peer file-sharing service
- **Key holdings:**
  - Napster users' activities constituted direct copyright infringement (violation of reproduction and distribution rights).
  - Napster had "knowledge, both actual and constructive, of direct infringement" and materially contributed to it — liable for **contributory infringement**.
  - Napster's failure to police its system combined with its financial interest in infringement established **vicarious infringement**.
  - Fair use defense rejected: the court found that Napster use was commercial in nature, involved creative works, copied entire works, and had a demonstrable adverse impact on the market for copyrighted recordings.
- **Outcome:** The Ninth Circuit affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded the district court's preliminary injunction. Napster was effectively shut down and filed for bankruptcy in 2002.
- **Impact:** Established that facilitating peer-to-peer copyright infringement creates liability. Did not resolve whether decentralized P2P systems (without a central index) would face similar liability — that question went to *Grokster*.
- **Source:** [Justia case page](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/239/1004/636120/); [Copyright Office fair use summary](https://www.copyright.gov/fair-use/summaries/a&mrecords-napster-9thcir2001.pdf)

### 2.2 MGM Studios, Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd.

- **Citation:** 545 U.S. 913 (2005)
- **Docket Number:** No. 04-480
- **Court:** Supreme Court of the United States
- **Decision date:** June 27, 2005
- **Parties:** Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Studios and other media companies (petitioners) vs. Grokster, Ltd. and StreamCast Networks (respondents)
- **Key holding — Inducement doctrine:**
  - **Unanimous decision (9-0):** Anyone who distributes a device (or software) with the object of promoting its use to infringe copyright, as shown by clear expression or other affirmative steps taken to foster infringement, is liable for the resulting acts of infringement by third parties.
  - Evidence of inducement included: StreamCast targeting former Napster users with advertising; internal communications showing intent to attract infringers; revenue models dependent on high-volume use (including infringing use); and failure to develop filtering tools.
- **Relationship to Sony Betamax precedent:** The Court explicitly stated this ruling did not disturb the *Sony Corp. v. Universal City Studios* (464 U.S. 417, 1984) holding that distributing a product capable of substantial non-infringing use does not, standing alone, create liability. The inducement theory requires additional evidence of intent to promote infringement.
- **Impact:** Established inducement liability as a distinct theory in copyright law. Grokster ceased operations and agreed to a $50 million settlement. The decision provided content industries with a powerful tool against technology companies that actively promote piracy.
- **Source:** [Justia Supreme Court page](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/545/913/); [Oyez case page](https://www.oyez.org/cases/2004/04-480); [LII text](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/04-480)

### 2.3 Capitol Records, Inc. v. Thomas-Rasset

- **Case Number:** No. 06-1497 (D. Minn.); Nos. 11-2820, 11-2858 (8th Cir.)
- **Citation (appellate):** 692 F.3d 899 (8th Cir. 2012)
- **Parties:** Capitol Records and other major labels vs. Jammie Thomas-Rasset, a Native American mother of four from Brainerd, Minnesota
- **Allegations:** Unauthorized downloading and sharing of 24 copyrighted sound recordings via Kazaa file-sharing network.
- **Procedural history — three trials:**
  1. **First trial (October 4, 2007):** Jury found willful infringement, awarded $9,250 per song x 24 songs = **$222,000**. (The jury deliberated only 5 minutes.) Later vacated by the district court on the grounds that "making available" does not constitute distribution.
  2. **Second trial (June 2009):** Jury found willful infringement, awarded $80,000 per song = **$1,920,000** total. The district court reduced this to **$54,000** ($2,250 per song) as "so severe and oppressive as to be wholly disproportioned to the offense."
  3. **Third trial (November 2010):** Jury awarded **$1,500,000** ($62,500 per song). The district court again reduced to **$54,000**.
- **Eighth Circuit decision (September 11, 2012):** Reinstated the original first-trial verdict of **$222,000**. The court held that statutory damages in copyright cases are not subject to the Due Process limitations applicable to punitive damages, and that $9,250 per song was within the statutory range and not unconstitutionally excessive.
- **Supreme Court:** Certiorari denied (No. 12-715).
- **Impact:** Established that large statutory damage awards against individual file sharers can withstand constitutional scrutiny. The case became a symbol of the music industry's controversial strategy of suing individual consumers, which the RIAA ultimately abandoned in 2008.
- **Source:** [Justia appellate decision](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca8/11-2820/11-2820-2012-09-11.html); [EFF case page](https://www.eff.org/cases/capitol-v-thomas)

### 2.4 Sony BMG Rootkit Scandal and Resulting Litigation (2005-2007)

- **Discovery:** October 31, 2005 — security researcher Mark Russinovich of Winternals (later Microsoft) published a blog post revealing that Sony BMG music CDs installed hidden software (a rootkit) on consumers' computers.
- **The technology:** Approximately 22 million CDs distributed by Sony BMG contained one of two copy protection software packages:
  - **Extended Copy Protection (XCP):** Developed by British company First 4 Internet. Installed a rootkit that hid itself from the operating system and "phoned home" with listening data, even if users declined the EULA.
  - **MediaMax CD-3:** A second DRM program not disclosed in the EULA.
  - Both programs contained code from copylefted open-source software, raising additional infringement concerns.
- **Litigation:**
  - **Class-action lawsuits:** Three separate class-action suits were filed in November 2005 and consolidated. Settlement terms: $7.50 per purchased XCP CD plus a free album download, or three additional album downloads. Claims deadline: June 30, 2007.
  - **State attorneys general:** Texas AG Greg Abbott filed the first state suit on November 21, 2005 (first suit under Texas's 2005 spyware law). A multistate settlement with New York and 39 other states required Sony BMG to pay **$4.25 million** total to settling states. California and Texas consumers could receive up to $175 for computer repair costs. Sony agreed to detailed disclosure requirements for any future DRM.
  - **FTC settlement (January 30, 2007):** The FTC charged Sony BMG with unfair and deceptive business practices. Settlement terms required Sony to compensate consumers.
- **Corporate response:** Sony recalled affected products, issued (and re-issued) rootkit removal tools.
- **Impact:** The scandal severely damaged the music industry's credibility on DRM. It demonstrated the risks of aggressive copy protection and contributed to the industry's eventual move away from DRM on digital music files (Apple's iTunes Store went DRM-free in 2009).
- **Source:** [EFF Sony BMG litigation page](https://www.eff.org/cases/sony-bmg-litigation-info); [NY AG settlement announcement](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2006/new-york-joins-nationwide-settlement-sony-bmg-over-hidden-files)

### 2.5 F.B.T. Productions, LLC v. Aftermath Records (The Eminem Case)

- **Citation:** 621 F.3d 958 (9th Cir. 2010)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit
- **Parties:** F.B.T. Productions, LLC (Eminem's original production company) and Marshall Bruce Mathers III (Eminem) vs. Aftermath Records (a subsidiary of Universal Music Group)
- **Background:** In 1995, FBT signed Eminem to a recording contract. In 1998, FBT transferred its rights to Eminem's recordings to Aftermath. The contract distinguished between "records sold" (lower royalty rate) and "masters licensed" (50% of net receipts).
- **Central legal question:** Are permanent digital downloads (via iTunes etc.) and mastertones/ringtones "sales" of records or "licenses" of masters?
- **Key ruling:** The Ninth Circuit held that Aftermath's agreements with third-party digital distributors constituted **licenses, not sales**. Because Aftermath did not "sell" physical copies to distributors but rather granted them the right to use the master recordings to create and distribute digital files, the "Masters Licensed" provision applied.
- **Royalty impact:** The court concluded that FBT/Eminem were owed a royalty rate of **50%** for downloads and ringtones under the licensing provision, rather than the approximately 12-20% typical of "records sold" provisions.
- **Appellate outcome:** The district court's judgment favoring Aftermath was reversed, the attorneys' fees award to Aftermath was vacated, and the case was remanded.
- **Industry impact:** Potentially affected all legacy recording contracts that distinguished between sales and licensing. Major labels faced potential liability for underpaying artists on digital downloads. The case prompted widespread renegotiation of recording contracts. The practical financial impact was enormous — one leaked audit suggested the stakes could exceed $25 million for Eminem's catalog alone [single-source: Hollywood Reporter report].
- **Source:** [Ninth Circuit opinion (pdf)](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2010/09/03/09-55817.pdf); [Quimbee case brief](https://www.quimbee.com/cases/f-b-t-productions-llc-v-aftermath-records)

### 2.6 Williams v. Gaye (The "Blurred Lines" Case)

- **Citation:** 885 F.3d 1150 (9th Cir. 2018)
- **Case Number:** No. 15-56880
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit
- **Decision date:** March 21, 2018
- **Parties:** Pharrell Williams, Robin Thicke, and Clifford Harris Jr. (T.I.) vs. the Estate of Marvin Gaye (Frankie Christian Gaye, Nona Marvisa Gaye, Marvin Gaye III)
- **Claim:** The 2013 hit "Blurred Lines" infringed on Marvin Gaye's 1977 composition "Got to Give It Up."
- **District court verdict (March 2015):** Jury found infringement.
- **Damages awarded:**
  - $3,188,527.50 in actual damages
  - $1,768,191.88 in infringer's profits against Thicke
  - $357,630.96 in infringer's profits against Williams
  - **Continuing royalty of 50%** of future songwriter and publishing revenues from "Blurred Lines"
- **Ninth Circuit ruling (2018):** Affirmed in part, reversed in part. Upheld the finding of infringement but reversed the award against Harris (T.I.), finding insufficient evidence of his contribution to the infringing elements.
- **Key legal finding:** "Got to Give It Up" was "entitled to broad protection against copyright infringement liability because musical compositions are not confined to a narrow range of expression."
- **Dissent (Judge Nguyen):** Warned that the majority's decision allowed the Gaye estate to "own a musical style" and would chill artistic expression.
- **Impact:** Sent shockwaves through the music industry. Songwriters and legal commentators warned it could stifle creativity by making artists liable for evoking the "feel" or "groove" of an earlier song, as opposed to copying specific melodic or lyrical elements. Led to a reported increase in preemptive songwriting credits and settlements.
- **Source:** [Justia case page](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/15-56880/15-56880-2018-03-21.html); [WIPO Magazine analysis](https://www.wipo.int/en/web/wipo-magazine/articles/blurred-lines-the-difference-between-inspiration-and-appropriation-39329)

### 2.7 Skidmore v. Led Zeppelin (The "Stairway to Heaven" Case)

- **Citation:** 952 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2020) (en banc)
- **Case Number:** No. 16-56057
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit (en banc, 11 judges)
- **Decision date:** March 9, 2020
- **Parties:** Michael Skidmore (as trustee of the Randy Craig Wolfe Trust — Spirit frontman Randy Wolfe) vs. Led Zeppelin (Jimmy Page, Robert Plant, John Paul Jones, and Warner Music)
- **Claim:** Led Zeppelin's "Stairway to Heaven" (1971) infringed Spirit's instrumental "Taurus" (1968), specifically a descending chromatic bass line.
- **Procedural history:**
  - Lawsuit filed in 2014
  - **2016 jury trial:** Verdict for Led Zeppelin — jury found no infringement
  - **2018 three-judge panel:** Reversed in favor of the Wolfe estate, finding jury instruction errors
  - **2020 en banc decision:** Reversed the panel and upheld the original jury verdict for Led Zeppelin
- **Key legal holdings:**
  - The en banc court **overruled** the Ninth Circuit's prior rule requiring "inverse ratio" analysis (the doctrine that a lower standard of proof of substantial similarity applies when access to the prior work is established). The court held the inverse ratio rule was "not part of the copyright statute" and created "uncertainty for the courts and parties."
  - For pre-1978 works, the copyright comparison must be based on the **deposit copy** (sheet music) filed with the Copyright Office, not a particular sound recording of the work.
  - Common musical building blocks (like a descending chromatic bass line) are not protectable.
- **Supreme Court:** Certiorari denied (October 2020).
- **Impact:** Provided a counterweight to the *Blurred Lines* decision. By eliminating the inverse ratio rule in the Ninth Circuit and limiting copyright comparison to deposit copies for older works, the decision made it harder to bring music plagiarism claims based on general similarity or "feel."
- **Source:** [Justia en banc decision](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/16-56057/16-56057-2020-03-09.html); [NPR coverage](https://www.npr.org/2020/03/09/813763864/led-zeppelin-wins-copyright-dispute-over-stairway-to-heaven)

### 2.8 Notable Artist Contract Disputes

#### Prince vs. Warner Bros. Records (1993-2014)
- In 1992, Prince signed a reported $100 million contract with Warner Bros., but the relationship deteriorated over creative control and master ownership.
- Prince publicly appeared with "SLAVE" written across his face and changed his name to an unpronounceable symbol ("The Artist Formerly Known as Prince").
- Core dispute: Prince wanted the right to release music on his own schedule and own his master recordings.
- He left Warner Bros. in 1996 and released music independently and online.
- In 2014, Prince reportedly regained control of his master recordings from Warner Bros.
- **Impact:** Became the most visible symbol of artist-label power imbalances. Inspired subsequent generations of artists to negotiate for master ownership.

#### George Michael vs. Sony Music (1992-1995)
- George Michael sued Sony Music in 1992, claiming his contract was an unreasonable restraint of trade and that Sony had deliberately undermined promotion of his work.
- The case was heard in the UK High Court (Panayiotou v. Sony Music Entertainment, 1994).
- **Ruling:** The court ruled against George Michael, finding the contract was not an unreasonable restraint of trade.
- Michael eventually settled with Sony and moved to DreamWorks Records (Virgin/EMI in the UK).

#### Kesha v. Dr. Luke / Kemosabe Records (2014-ongoing)
- In October 2014, Kesha filed a civil lawsuit against songwriter/producer Dr. Luke (Lukasz Gottwald), alleging physical, verbal, sexual, and emotional abuse.
- Kesha sought a preliminary injunction to be released from her recording contract with Dr. Luke's Kemosabe Records (a Sony Music imprint).
- **February 2016 ruling:** New York Supreme Court Justice Shirley Kornreich denied Kesha's request for a preliminary injunction, finding that the contract was not an unconscionable restraint on her career since Sony had offered to let her record without Dr. Luke's involvement.
- Kesha ultimately fulfilled all obligations under her contract and released subsequent music on her own label.
- **Impact:** Sparked a broader industry conversation about power dynamics, particularly regarding producer-artist relationships and the enforceability of long-term recording contracts.

- **Source:** [CBS News — Musicians v. Record Labels](https://www.cbsnews.com/pictures/musicians-v-record-labels-famous-feuds/); [Billboard — Music Industry Legal Cases](https://www.billboard.com/music/music-news/biggest-music-industry-legal-cases-2020-8547683/)

## 3. CONSENT DECREES AND ANTITRUST

### 3.1 ASCAP and BMI Consent Decrees

**Original consent decrees (1941):**
- In the 1930s, the DOJ investigated ASCAP and BMI for anticompetitive business practices.
- DOJ alleged that the practice of offering only blanket licenses constituted illegal "tying" (conditioning sale of one good on purchase of another) and price-fixing in violation of Section 1 of the Sherman Antitrust Act.
- In 1941, both ASCAP and BMI entered into consent decrees with the DOJ.

**Key requirements of the consent decrees:**
1. ASCAP and BMI may obtain only **nonexclusive** rights to license musical performances (songwriters retain the right to license individually).
2. Must grant a license to **any applicant** on terms equal to similarly situated licensees.
3. Must accept as a member any songwriter who meets minimum requirements.
4. If ASCAP/BMI and a licensee cannot agree on a reasonable rate, either party may apply to a federal court ("rate court") for determination of reasonable terms.

**Amendment history:**
- **1950 amendment (ASCAP):** Following litigation throughout the 1940s, the consent decree was amended to establish rate court judicial proceedings.
- **1994 amendment (BMI):** Last amendment to the BMI consent decree, approved by the rate court.
- **2001 amendment (ASCAP):** Last amendment to the ASCAP consent decree, approved by the rate court.

**2014-2016 DOJ Review:**
- June 2014: DOJ announced a review of both consent decrees after solicitation by ASCAP and BMI.
- August 4, 2016: DOJ completed review and **declined** to seek termination or modification.
- Notable controversy: DOJ proposed that the decrees required "100% licensing" (meaning either PRO could license an entire work even if it only represented a fractional interest). This interpretation was disputed and a federal court later ruled against it (BMI v. DMX, Inc., subsequently reversed on other grounds).

**2019-2021 DOJ Review:**
- June 5, 2019: DOJ opened a second review, receiving nearly 1,000 public comments.
- January 15, 2021: DOJ concluded the review, **proposing no modifications** at that time.
- ASCAP and BMI publicly expressed disappointment, arguing the decrees are outdated and impose asymmetric burdens not faced by competitors (SESAC, GMR).

- **Source:** [DOJ ASCAP/BMI Consent Decree Review 2019](https://www.justice.gov/atr/antitrust-consent-decree-review-ascap-and-bmi-2019); [CRS report (congress.gov)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11463); [DOJ 2021 closing statement](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-completes-review-ascap-and-bmi-consent-decrees-proposing-no-modifications)

### 3.2 CD Price-Fixing Antitrust Actions (1995-2003)

**FTC Action (2000):**
- The FTC charged the five largest recorded music distributors — Universal Music, Sony Corp. of America, Time-Warner, EMI Music Distribution, and Bertelsmann Music Group (BMG) — with restraining competition through "Minimum Advertised Price" (MAP) policies.
- These MAP policies penalized retailers (such as Best Buy, Circuit City, and Target) who advertised CDs below set minimum prices by withholding cooperative advertising funds.
- The five companies controlled approximately **85%** of all CDs sold in the United States.
- **Settlement (May 2000):** All five companies agreed to discontinue MAP programs for seven years.
- **FTC estimate:** U.S. consumers overpaid approximately **$480 million** for CDs over the prior three years due to these policies.

**Multi-State Settlement (2002):**
- Led by New York AG Eliot Spitzer and Florida AG, joined by 41 other states and commonwealths.
- Charged that between 1995 and 2000, record companies and distributors conspired to inflate CD prices.
- **Settlement: $143 million** in total consumer benefits.
  - $67.38 million in cash distributed to settling states for consumer compensation, administration, and attorneys' fees.
  - Individual consumers who filed claims received refunds of **$13.86** each.

- **Source:** [FTC press release (2000)](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2000/05/record-companies-settle-ftc-charges-restraining-competition-cd-music-market); [California AG announcement](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-lockyer-announces-143-million-consumer-benefits-multi-state); [NY AG announcement](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2002/new-york-settles-price-fixing-case-against-major-distributors-compact-discs)

### 3.3 Payola Investigations

#### 1950s Payola Scandal
- Record companies bribed radio disc jockeys for airplay of specific songs, a practice known as "payola."
- In November 1959, 335 disc jockeys nationwide admitted to receiving over **$263,000** in "consulting fees."
- **Alan Freed** — pioneering rock-and-roll DJ — was fired from WABC radio on November 20, 1959, and later charged with commercial bribery. He pleaded guilty in 1962, was fined $300, and received a suspended sentence.
- **Dick Clark** — host of American Bandstand — testified before Congress but was largely exonerated, partly because he had divested his music business interests before the hearings.
- **Legislative response (1960):** Congress amended the Federal Communications Act (adding Section 508) to make payola a misdemeanor, requiring broadcasters to disclose any payments received for airing specific content. Violations punishable by up to $10,000 fine and one year imprisonment.

#### 2005 Spitzer Investigation
- Beginning in 2002, the office of New York AG Eliot Spitzer investigated major label payola practices involving commercial radio chains.
- **Settlements:**
  - **Sony BMG (July 2005):** $10 million settlement. Funds earmarked for music education programs in New York State.
  - **Warner Music Group (November 2005):** $5 million settlement.
  - **Universal Music Group (May 2006):** $12 million settlement.
- **Total settlements: approximately $27 million.**
- **Practices exposed:** Outright bribes to radio programmers (vacation packages, electronics); contest giveaways for station audiences; payments to cover station operational expenses; use of independent promoters as conduits for illegal payments; and payments for "spin programs" (airplay disguised as advertising).
- **Impact:** Led to internal compliance reforms at major labels and greater scrutiny of independent promotion practices.

- **Source:** [NY AG Sony settlement announcement](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2005/sony-settles-payola-investigation); [NY AG Warner settlement announcement](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2005/warner-second-settle-payola-investigation); [NPR payola coverage](https://www.npr.org/2005/08/09/4785403/payola-the-beat-goes-on)

## 4. COPYRIGHT ROYALTY BOARD (CRB) PROCEEDINGS

### 4.1 How the CRB Works

- **Established:** Copyright Royalty and Distribution Reform Act of 2004 (Public Law 108-419), effective May 31, 2005. Replaced the Copyright Arbitration Royalty Panel (CARP) system.
- **Structure:** Three permanent Copyright Royalty Judges, appointed by the Librarian of Congress.
- **Qualifications:** Each judge must have a law degree and at least 7 years' legal experience. One must have significant copyright law knowledge, one must have significant economics knowledge, and one must have at least 5 years' judicial/quasi-judicial experience.
- **Jurisdiction:** Determines rates and terms for statutory copyright licenses, including mechanical royalties (Section 115), digital performance royalties for sound recordings (Section 114), and ephemeral recording licenses.
- **Process:** Trial-type proceedings with written direct statements, discovery (60-day period), witnesses, exhibits, oral arguments, and written findings. Alternatively, participants may reach voluntary settlement agreements at any time, which the CRB may adopt.
- **Participants:** Copyright owners (publishers, labels, songwriters via their representatives such as NMPA, NSAI, RIAA), digital service providers (Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon, Pandora, represented by DiMA), SoundExchange, broadcasters (NAB), and other stakeholders.
- **Appeal:** CRB determinations may be appealed to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit.

### 4.2 Phonorecords Proceedings (Mechanical Royalty Rates)

#### Phonorecords I (Rate period: 2008-2012)
- Set mechanical royalty rates for the first rate period under the CRB system.
- Physical/download rate: Maintained at **9.1 cents per track** (or 1.75 cents per minute), the rate that had been in effect since 2006.
- Initial streaming rates established for interactive services.

#### Phonorecords II (Rate period: 2013-2017)
- Physical/download rate: Rolled forward at **9.1 cents per track** — unchanged.
- Streaming mechanical royalty rate: Set at approximately **10.5%** of service revenue (with various formulas and minimums depending on service type).
- The CRB judges found that royalties were too low and that record companies were receiving a disproportionate share of combined mechanical and sound recording royalties, but did not dramatically alter rates.

#### Phonorecords III (Rate period: 2018-2022) — The Major Battle
- **January 2018 initial determination:** The CRB judges ruled for a phased increase in the streaming mechanical royalty rate from **10.5% to 15.1%** of service revenue by 2022 — a nearly **44% increase** over the rate period.
- **Rate schedule:**
  - 2018: 11.4%
  - 2019: 12.3%
  - 2020: 13.3%
  - 2021: 14.2%
  - 2022: 15.1%
- **Appeal by streaming services:** In 2019, Spotify, Amazon, Pandora, and Google filed appeals with the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals. Apple Music notably **did not** join the appeal.
- **D.C. Circuit ruling:** The court found that the CRB judges failed to give adequate notice to participants of their overhaul of the royalty rate structure combined with significantly increased and uncapped rates. The case was remanded.
- **Final resolution (August 2023):** After remand, the CRB affirmed the 15.1% rate, siding with songwriters and publishers. The rates for the entire 2018-2022 period were not finalized until this point — meaning songwriters faced years of delayed royalty adjustments.
- **Impact:** The prolonged legal battle became a rallying cry for songwriter advocacy groups. The "44% increase" was viewed as a hard-won victory for songwriters who had long argued they were undercompensated relative to sound recording royalties in streaming.

#### Phonorecords IV (Rate period: 2023-2027)
- **Settlement-based:** NMPA, NSAI, and DiMA reached a negotiated settlement to avoid a contested hearing.
- **Physical/download rates:** Increased to **12 cents per track** (or 2.31 cents per minute) for 2023, with annual inflation adjustments tied to Consumer Price Index.
- **Streaming rates (headline percentage of DSP revenue):**
  - 2023: 15.1%
  - 2024: 15.2%
  - 2025: 15.25%
  - 2026: 15.3%
  - 2027: 15.35%
- The settlement was formally adopted by the CRB.

- **Source:** [Federal Register — Phonorecords III](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/08/10/2023-14925/determination-of-royalty-rates-and-terms-for-making-and-distributing-phonorecords-phonorecords-iii); [Federal Register — Phonorecords IV](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/12/16/2022-27237/determination-of-royalty-rates-and-terms-for-making-and-distributing-phonorecords-phonorecords-iv); [Recording Academy coverage](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/copyright-royalty-board-determination-phonorecords-iii-songwriters-impact); [CRB rate proceedings page](https://www.crb.gov/rate/)

### 4.3 Webcasting Proceedings

#### Web IV (Rate period: 2016-2020)
- Following a six-week hearing with 47 witnesses and 660 exhibits (12,000+ pages), the CRB set webcasting royalty rates (per-performance rates for noninteractive streaming):
  - Ad-supported (free-to-consumer) services: **$0.0017 per performance**
  - Paid subscription services: **$0.0022 per performance** [the exact figure varies slightly between sources; some report $0.0023]
  - Minimum fee: **$500** per channel/station
- SoundExchange appealed, seeking higher rates. The D.C. Circuit upheld the CRB's determination.

#### Web V (Rate period: 2021-2025)
- Rates continued to be set through a combination of negotiated settlements and contested proceedings.

#### Web VI (Rate period: 2026-2030)
- CRB opened this proceeding in January 2024, with petitions to participate due in early February 2024.
- As of early 2025, the NAB and SoundExchange reached a settlement on webcasting royalty rates for broadcast simulcasts for 2026-2030, with rates increasing from prior levels. [single-source: Broadcast Law Blog]

- **Source:** [Federal Register — Web IV](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2016/05/02/2016-09707/determination-of-royalty-rates-and-terms-for-ephemeral-recording-and-webcasting-digital-performance); [SoundExchange commercial webcaster page](https://www.soundexchange.com/service-provider/commercial-webcaster/); [Broadcast Law Blog — Web VI](https://www.broadcastlawblog.com/2024/01/articles/copyright-royalty-board-starts-web-vi-proceeding-to-set-webcasting-royalties-paid-to-soundexchange-for-2026-2030-petitions-to-participate-due-february-5/)

## 5. FCC AND BROADCASTING REGULATIONS

### 5.1 Telecommunications Act of 1996 (Public Law 104-104)

- **Signed:** February 8, 1996, by President Bill Clinton
- **Key provision — radio ownership deregulation:** Lifted the national cap on radio station ownership (previously 40 stations nationally). Local ownership limits were relaxed based on market size.
- **Consolidation effects:**
  - **Clear Channel Communications** (now iHeartMedia) grew from **40 stations to 1,240 stations** — a 30x increase.
  - Within 5 years of the Act, the number of radio station owners in the United States dropped from approximately **5,100 to 3,800**.
  - iHeartMedia currently operates **850+ stations** across the country.
- **Impact on music industry:**
  - **Playlist homogenization:** Corporate ownership led to centralized programming decisions. Billboard Magazine reported that multiple large radio conglomerates played less new music after the Act. Local programming and content diversity was lost.
  - **Reduced market access for independent artists:** Fewer independent station owners meant fewer gatekeepers willing to take risks on unfamiliar music.
  - **Disproportionate impact on Black music:** Vice/Huck Magazine reporting documented that consolidation was particularly detrimental to urban radio formats, flattening regional characteristics of hip-hop and R&B programming across iHeartMedia's numerous Black radio stations.
  - **iHeartMedia bankruptcy:** The heavy debt from consolidation eventually led to iHeartMedia's Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing in March 2018 (emerged from bankruptcy in May 2019).

- **Source:** [NPR — radio ownership rules](https://www.npr.org/2019/06/07/730323196/one-more-scoop-of-vanilla-a-new-proposal-looks-to-loosen-radio-ownership-rules); [Telecommunications Act of 1996 (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications_Act_of_1996)

### 5.2 Terrestrial Radio Performance Right — The U.S. Exception

- **Current law:** Under U.S. copyright law, terrestrial (AM/FM) radio stations must pay royalties for the **musical composition** (to songwriters via ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, or GMR) but are **NOT** required to pay royalties for the **sound recording** (to performing artists and labels).
- **Historical origin:** When the Copyright Act of 1909 was enacted, there was no sound recording copyright. When sound recording copyright was created (1971 amendment, codified in 1976 Act), Congress deliberately excluded a public performance right, based on the theory that radio airplay promoted record sales and thus benefited artists indirectly.
- **International anomaly:** The United States is one of a very small number of developed countries that does not require broadcast radio to compensate performers for sound recordings. This has practical consequences: foreign PROs often withhold reciprocal payments to U.S. artists because the U.S. does not offer equivalent protections.
- **Legislative proposals (all failed to pass as of March 2026):**
  - **Performance Rights Act (2009):** S.379 / H.R.848 in the 111th Congress.
  - **Fair Play Fair Pay Act (2015):** Introduced by Rep. Jerrold Nadler and Rep. Marsha Blackburn.
  - **AM/FM Act (Ask Musicians for Music Act)**
  - **American Music Fairness Act (AMFA):**
    - First introduced June 2021 (bipartisan, Reps. Issa and Nadler, Sens. Padilla and Blackburn).
    - Approved by the House Judiciary Committee.
    - Reintroduced February 2, 2023 in both chambers.
    - Reintroduced January 2025 with Recording Academy and musicFIRST coalition support.
    - As of March 2026, the AMFA has not been enacted.
- **Opposition:** The National Association of Broadcasters (NAB) has consistently opposed a terrestrial performance right, arguing that radio airplay provides promotional value exceeding any royalty obligation, and that a new royalty would threaten the financial viability of small and rural stations.

- **Source:** [CRS report on public performance rights in sound recordings (congress.gov)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47642); [SoundExchange advocacy page](https://www.soundexchange.com/advocacy/closing-the-amfm-radio-royalty-loophole/); [Recording Academy AMFA page](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/issues-policy/american-music-fairness-act)
