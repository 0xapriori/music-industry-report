## 1. Artists Who Fought Their Labels in Court

### Prince vs. Warner Bros. Records (1993–2014)

**Contract:** In 1992, Prince signed what was reported as a $100 million deal with Warner Bros. to deliver six more albums. The contract gave Warner Bros. ownership and control of all master recordings.

**The Protest:** In 1993, Prince changed his stage name to an unpronounceable symbol (the "Love Symbol") and began appearing publicly with the word "SLAVE" written on his cheek. He stated he changed his name because he felt "his own name now belonged to the company."

**Resolution:** Prince fulfilled his contractual obligations and left Warner Bros. in 1996, releasing *Emancipation* via his own NPG Records. In 2014, Prince struck a landmark deal with Warner Bros. to regain complete ownership of all recordings he had made with the label, including a 30th-anniversary edition of *Purple Rain*.

### George Michael vs. Sony Music — *Panayiotou v Sony Music Entertainment (UK) Ltd.* (1992–1995)

**Case Filed:** October 30, 1992, Chancery Division (UK).
**Ruling:** June 21, 1994, by Justice Jonathan Parker.

**Michael's Arguments:** His recording contract constituted an unreasonable restraint of trade. He alleged Sony failed to promote *Listen Without Prejudice Vol. 1* as punishment for his decision to reduce his public profile.

**Court Ruling:** The court wholly rejected Michael's claims. Justice Parker held the contracts were reasonable, noting Michael had access to expert legal advice, had renegotiated multiple times, and had terms comparable to similarly successful artists.

**Contract Background:** Michael had renegotiated an eight-album deal as a solo artist with Sony in 1988, further renegotiated in 1990 to reflect his commercial success.

**Resolution:** Michael settled with Sony on July 13, 1995, and was released from his contract. Sony reportedly received between $30 million and $40 million. Michael subsequently signed with Virgin and DreamWorks.

### TLC Bankruptcy Despite Massive Sales (1995)

**Sales:** TLC sold an estimated 65 million records worldwide.

**Contract Terms:** Under their 1991 contract with LaFace Records, TLC received 56 cents per album sold, split three ways. After LaFace, Arista Records, and manager Pebbitone's entity recouped recording, manufacturing, and distribution costs, additional charges were applied for airline travel, hotels, promotion, music videos, food, clothing, and other expenses.

**Bankruptcy:** Members filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on July 3, 1995, declaring $3.5 million in debts. At the 1996 Grammy Awards, after winning Best R&B Album for *CrazySexyCool*, member Chilli stated: "We've sold 10 million albums worldwide. We're as broke as broke can be."

**Financial Reality:** Each member was reportedly taking home less than $50,000 per year. The group stated they received approximately 1% of an estimated $175 million in album sales.

**Resolution:** After two years of legal proceedings, TLC exited their Pebbitone contract and renegotiated directly with LaFace. They paid $3 million to own their own name.

## 3. Class Action and Collective Artist Actions

### F.B.T. Productions v. Aftermath Records (Eminem Case)
**Case:** *F.B.T. Productions, LLC v. Aftermath Records*, 621 F.3d 958 (9th Cir. 2010)

**Background:** In 1995, F.B.T. Productions signed Eminem and later transferred the contract to Aftermath Records. The contract provided 12%–20% royalties on "Records Sold" and 50% of net revenue on "Masters Licensed."

**Core Issue:** Whether digital downloads and ringtones constituted "sales" (lower royalty) or "licenses" (higher royalty).

**Ruling:** The Ninth Circuit ruled that permanent downloads and mastertones distributed through third parties (like iTunes) constituted licensing of copyrights, not sales, under the First Sale Doctrine. This entitled F.B.T. to the higher 50% licensing rate.

**Industry Impact:** The ruling changed industry practice primarily for legacy artists whose contracts predated digital distribution. Artists signed in the 2000s and later typically had contracts explicitly addressing digital royalty rates.

## 4. Impact of Specific Laws on Artist Earnings

### The 1976 Copyright Act — Work-for-Hire and Sound Recordings

**Key Provision:** Sound recordings were not included in the categories of "specially ordered or commissioned" works made for hire under Section 101. However, record companies routinely asserted that featured artists were "employees" creating recordings in the course of employment, satisfying the first prong of the work-for-hire definition.

**Termination Rights (Section 203):** Congress intended to give authors a "second shot" at reclaiming rights they assigned early in their careers. After 35 years, artists can theoretically reclaim copyrights — but the provision is complex and labels have contested its application to sound recordings.

**1999 Amendment (Secretly Added):** The Satellite Home Viewer Improvement Act of 1999 included a clause — inserted at RIAA's request without the knowledge of most congressional subcommittee members — adding "sound recordings" to the work-for-hire categories. This would have stripped artists of termination rights. The amendment had no connection to the bill's subject matter (satellite TV retransmissions) and was reportedly drafted by a legislative aide who subsequently accepted an RIAA lobbying position.

**Repeal (2000):** After public outcry and artist advocacy (led by the Recording Artists Coalition), Congress passed the **Work Made for Hire and Copyright Corrections Act of 2000**, repealing the amendment retroactively and directing courts to interpret the law as if neither the amendment nor its repeal had occurred.

### DMCA Safe Harbor — Impact on YouTube Earnings

**The Issue:** The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998) created "safe harbor" protections for online service providers hosting user-generated content, shielding platforms like YouTube from copyright infringement liability provided they process takedown notices.

**Earnings Gap:** Between 2014 and 2015, total YouTube views increased 132%, while rights payments increased only 11%. YouTube's per-stream rate decreased from $0.0020 to $0.0010 during that period. YouTube reportedly pays approximately one-sixth of what Spotify and Apple Music pay per stream.

**Artist Petition (2016):** A coalition including Paul McCartney, Lady Gaga, Beck, Gwen Stefani, Taylor Swift, and U2 signed a petition calling for DMCA reform to close safe harbor loopholes.

### Music Modernization Act — Blanket Mechanical License (2018)

**What Changed:** The MMA created the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC), which began operations in 2021 to collect and distribute mechanical royalties from digital music providers. Streaming services can now obtain a blanket license rather than negotiating individual licenses.

**Intended Benefit:** The MLC was designed to address the "black box" of unmatched royalties — mechanical royalties that went unpaid because digital services could not identify copyright owners. The MLC established a publicly available musical works database.

**Practical Challenges:** The system's effectiveness has been questioned due to persistent ownership dispute issues across the industry. Songwriters must register with the MLC to receive payments. Unmatched royalties can be distributed to known copyright owners based on market share starting in 2023.

### CRB Rate Decisions — Impact on Songwriter Per-Stream Payments

**Phonorecords III (2018–2022):** The Copyright Royalty Board raised mechanical royalty rates for streaming services by 44% over five years, increasing from 11.4% to 15.1% of service revenue.

**Phonorecords IV (2023–2027):** Settlement established a headline rate of 15.35% of interactive streaming service US revenue by 2027, phased in from 15.1% in 2023.

**Per-Stream Reality:** Streaming mechanical royalties amount to approximately one-half cent per stream, compared to 12 cents for a physical/downloaded song. Publishers initially proposed per-stream rate structures (rather than revenue percentages), arguing they would be fairer, but this was rejected.

### Terrestrial Radio Performance Right — The US Exception

**The Gap:** The United States is one of the only countries in the world that does not require terrestrial (AM/FM) radio stations to pay performance royalties to recording artists and sound recording copyright owners. The US is grouped with China, North Korea, and Iran in this regard.

**Financial Impact:** American artists and labels are denied an estimated **$200 million annually** in performance royalties. Additionally, foreign nations that pay their own artists for radio airplay currently withhold reciprocal royalties from American creators because the US does not reciprocate.

**Comparison:** Every OECD member country except the United States pays artists a performance fee for radio airplay. In the US, satellite radio, internet radio, and other digital services do pay performance royalties — only terrestrial AM/FM is exempt.

**Legislative Efforts — American Music Fairness Act:**
- Introduced in the 117th Congress (2021–2022) as H.R. 4130
- Reintroduced in the 118th Congress (2023–2024) as H.R. 791 and S. 253
- Reintroduced in the 119th Congress (2025–2026) as H.R. 861 and S. 326
- Has not yet been enacted as of March 2026

## 5. The Recoupment Trap — Documented Cases

### Courtney Love's "Piracy" Speech (May 16, 2000)

**Event:** Speech at Digital Hollywood Online Entertainment Conference, New York.

**Core Argument:** Love defined "piracy" not as Napster-style file sharing, but as major label recording contracts. She argued: "Record companies own copyrights forever, and the system is set up so almost nobody gets paid."

**Examples Cited:**
- TLC received less than 2% of $175 million in CD sales — roughly 40 times less than the profit split among management, production, and the label.
- Toni Braxton sold $188 million worth of CDs but was broke due to a contract paying less than 35 cents per album.

**On Napster:** Love stated "it's not piracy when kids swap music over the Internet" — the real piracy was the industry contracts.

**Sources:**
- [Salon — Courtney Love does the math](https://www.salon.com/2000/06/14/love_7/)
- [Deseret News — Love says musicians exploited by industry](https://www.deseret.com/2000/5/19/19507415/love-says-musicians-exploited-by-industry/)

### Other Documented Recoupment Cases

**Merle Haggard:** Never collected royalties from any of his 1960s and 1970s albums, which included 37 top-ten country singles and 21 number-ones. [single-source]

**Kreayshawn:** Signed a reported $1 million deal with Sony in 2011. Despite "Gucci Gucci" going platinum, she still owed Sony approximately $800,000 as of 2020 because overall sales did not recoup the advance. [single-source]

### Union of Musicians and Allied Workers (UMAW) — Justice at Spotify

**Launched:** October 2020.

**Key Demands:**
1. Pay artists one cent per stream (up from approximately one-quarter cent)
2. Provide a more transparent business model
3. Adopt user-centric streaming payments
4. Appropriately credit all contributors to recordings

**Actions:**
- 30,000+ musicians, workers, and fans signed the petition
- In-person delivery of demands at Spotify headquarters in 15 cities
- Protests at Spotify offices worldwide (31 cities, March 2021)
- Coalition of 180+ artists and advocacy organizations demanded Spotify abandon a controversial speech-recognition patent

**Outcomes:**
- Spotify launched its "Loud and Clear" transparency initiative
- Apple Music publicly claimed to pay one cent per stream, exceeding Spotify's rate and nominally matching UMAW demands
- Despite media attention, UMAW acknowledged that "the exploitation of musicians and allied workers unfortunately remains unchanged"

### #BrokenRecord Campaign (UK, 2020–present)

**Founded:** April 2020 by Tom Gray (musician, Gomez; PRS board member).

**Goals:** UK government regulation of the music industry, improved creator rights under UK Copyright Law, and a shift from pro-rata to user-centric payment models.

**Key Outcomes:**

1. **UK Parliamentary Inquiry (October 2020):** The Digital, Culture, Media and Sport Committee launched an inquiry into the "Economics of Streaming," receiving evidence from 300+ artists and oral testimony from Nile Rodgers, Ed O'Brien, Guy Garvey, and Nadine Shah, as well as representatives from Sony, Warner, Universal, Spotify, Amazon, Apple, and YouTube.

2. **Open Letter to Boris Johnson (April 2021):** Co-signed by Paul McCartney, Kate Bush, The Rolling Stones, Chris Martin, and 200+ notable figures in British music.

3. **Major Label Responses:**
   - **June 2021:** Sony Music announced clearing historic unrecouped debts for legacy artists (effective January 1, 2021)
   - **February 2022:** Warner Music Group announced a legacy unrecouped advances program (for artists signed before 2000 who didn't receive an advance after 2000; effective July 1, 2022). Warner reported 4,500 artists benefiting.
   - **April 2022:** Universal Music Group announced clearing unrecouped balances (for eligible creators who received no payments since January 1, 2000; effective January 1, 2022)

4. **Government Action (July 2021):** DCMS Committee published recommendations for alternative payment systems.

5. **Competition Review (January 2022):** Competition & Markets Authority announced a study into the music streaming market.
