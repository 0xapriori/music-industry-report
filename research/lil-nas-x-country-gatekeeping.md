# Lil Nas X, Country Music Gatekeeping, and BigXthaPlug — Research

## Key Academic Sources

1. **Schaap & Berkers, "Black Rap, White Rock: Non-Declarative Culture and the Racialization of Cultural Categories," *Sociological Inquiry*, 2022.** Genre associations operate at "non-declarative cognitive level" — rock's whiteness implicit, rap's Blackness explicit. Uses Lil Nas X removal and Tyler the Creator Grammy categorization as examples.

2. **Hansen, "A Different Country? Lil Nas X, the Sound of the Internet, and Queering the Cowboy," *Pop Masculinities*, Oxford University Press, 2022.** Country-trap fusion and queer cowboy iconography. Argues Lil Nas X serves as corrective to accounts bypassing Black contributions to country music.

3. **Doktor, "Racializing Sound in Early Jazz," *Open Access Musicology*.** Draws direct line from 1920s "race records" to Billboard's 2019 removal. Industry enforces "sonic color line."

4. **Orosz, "Straight Outta Nashville," *Popular Music and Society*, 2021.** Country producers borrow from Black genres while maintaining boundaries excluding Black artists.

5. **Orosz, "Lil Nas X, the 'Old Town Road,' and the (f)utility of Genre Labels," *Musicology Now*, 2019.** "Blue Eyed Soul" exists as euphemism for white soul but no comparable designation for "Black country singer." Had Jason Aldean recorded same song, it would never have been removed.

6. **Saporito, "#Canceled: Positionality and Authenticity in Country Music's Cancel Culture," WVU Master's Thesis, 2021.** Lil Nas X's cancellations boiled down to race. Contrasts with white artist John Rich escaping consequences.

## Billboard Controversy
- March 2019: "Old Town Road" removed from Hot Country Songs
- Billboard: song "does not currently merit inclusion"
- Spokesperson: "had absolutely nothing to do with the race of the artist"
- Song went on to 19 weeks at #1 on Hot 100 — longest-running #1 in history
- White artists (Sam Hunt, Florida Georgia Line, Jason Aldean) regularly use autotune, electronic beats, rapped vocals on country charts without challenge

## Historical Parallels
- DeFord Bailey (1920s-30s): Accepted on Grand Ole Opry because radio rendered him "racially anonymous"
- Charley Pride (1960s-70s): Acceptance contingent on racial erasure
- "Musical color line" institutionalized in early 1900s through "old-time" vs "race" records

## BigXthaPlug (2025)
- *I Hope You're Happy* reached #1 on Billboard country album chart
- #1 song on Hot Country Songs
- Invited to CMA Fest
- Saving Country Music: "an aggressive insult, and trespass to the intellect to try and sell it as 'country'"
- Admitted not knowing George Strait or Willie Nelson despite being Texas native
- No academic studies yet — too recent
