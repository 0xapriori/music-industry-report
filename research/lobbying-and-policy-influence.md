## 1. Major Lobbying Organizations

### 1.1 RIAA (Recording Industry Association of America)

**What it represents:** The RIAA is the trade organization representing the U.S. recorded music industry. Its members include the three major labels (Universal Music Group, Sony Music Entertainment, Warner Music Group) and hundreds of smaller labels. Critically, the RIAA represents *labels*, not individual artists.

**Leadership:** Mitch Glazier has served as Chairman and CEO since 2019. Glazier's career exemplifies the revolving door: he previously served as Chief Counsel for Intellectual Property to the U.S. House Judiciary Committee. While on congressional staff, Glazier inserted language into copyright legislation classifying music recordings as "works made for hire" -- shortly after which he was hired by the RIAA as Senior Vice President of Government Relations and Legislative Counsel.

**Lobbying expenditures (OpenSecrets, based on Senate Office of Public Records filings):**
- 2018: $5,642,155
- 2019-2023: Specific annual figures not retrieved; the RIAA spent between $2.4 million and $6.5 million annually on lobbying between 2001 and 2020 (per Wikipedia, citing OpenSecrets data)
- 2024: $6,910,041
- Q1 2025 alone: More than $2.5 million [single-source, Digital Music News]

**Lobbyist workforce:** In 2022, the MPA and RIAA together deployed 60+ lobbyists. In 2023, 28 out of 35 RIAA lobbyists had previously held government jobs. In 2024, 24 out of 32 had government backgrounds. (Source: OpenSecrets revolving door data, reported by TorrentFreak)

**Key policy positions:**
- Stronger copyright enforcement and anti-piracy measures
- DMCA Section 512 safe harbor reform (closing the "value gap")
- Terrestrial radio performance royalty (American Music Fairness Act)
- AI regulation protecting copyrighted works from unauthorized training use
- NO FAKES Act (protecting artist likenesses from AI deepfakes)

## 3. Key Policy Battles

### 3.1 SOPA/PIPA (2011-2012)

**What it was:** The Stop Online Piracy Act (House) and PROTECT IP Act (Senate) would have given copyright holders and the DOJ tools to combat online piracy by targeting foreign websites, including DNS blocking, search engine de-listing, and payment processor cutoffs.

**Who backed it:**
- RIAA, MPAA, and the broader content industry
- 350+ businesses and organizations signed a support letter (September 2011), including NBCUniversal, Pfizer, Ford Motor Company, Revlon, NBA, Macmillan US
- The film, music, and TV industry spent more than **$91 million** on lobbying for SOPA approval (Center for Responsive Politics)

**Who opposed it:**
- Google (spent ~$4 million opposing), eBay, Yahoo, Amazon, ICANN
- Internet companies hired **246 lobbyists** on SOPA/PIPA issues vs. **241** hired by TV/music/movie industry
- Combined lobbying on both sides: **$104.6 million** in Q4 2011 alone
- Wikipedia blackout (January 18, 2012) and broader internet protests

**What happened:** Both bills were shelved in January 2012 after unprecedented internet-coordinated protests. The Wikipedia blackout and Google's homepage protest generated millions of contacts to Congress. The episode demonstrated that tech companies' ability to mobilize users could overwhelm traditional lobbying spend.

**Sources:**

- [Sunlight Foundation: How SOPA Changed Lobbying](https://sunlightfoundation.com/2012/01/30/sopa-lobbyin/)
- [ProPublica: SOPA Opera](https://www.propublica.org/nerds/sopa-opera-which-legislators-support-sopa-and-pipa)

### 3.2 Music Modernization Act (2018)

**What it was:** Omnibus copyright reform that combined three bills: (1) blanket mechanical licensing for interactive streaming (creating the Mechanical Licensing Collective/MLC), (2) federal protection for pre-1972 sound recordings, and (3) a process for producers/engineers to receive royalties.

**Who pushed for it:**
- RIAA, NMPA, NSAI, ASCAP, Recording Academy, SESAC, Songwriters of North America (SONA)
- In January 2018, these groups united behind the Recording Academy's push
- Digital services (Spotify, Apple, Amazon, Google) also supported the final version
- Passed unanimously in both the House and Senate -- described by NPR as "a music industry peace treaty"

**Key compromises:**
- **Harry Fox Agency/Blackstone compromise:** SESAC and Harry Fox Agency (both Blackstone-owned) initially blocked a Senate vote. The compromise allowed the MLC to handle digital streaming licenses while private entities like Harry Fox continued negotiating licenses for restaurants, bars, and radio
- **SiriusXM compromise:** Satellite radio royalty rates would remain unchanged through 2027 (rather than 2022) in exchange for SiriusXM foregoing its right to appeal a recent CRB ruling. This cleared the final path to passage
- **Tech company participation:** Digital services agreed to fund the MLC in exchange for a blanket license that reduced their transaction costs and litigation risk

**Signed into law:** October 11, 2018, by President Trump

**Sources:**
- [U.S. Copyright Office: Creation of the MMA](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/creation.html)
- [NPR: A Music Industry Peace Treaty](https://www.npr.org/2018/09/19/649611777/a-music-industry-peace-treaty-passes-unanimously-through-congress)
- [IPWatchdog: Compromise Leads to Unconditional Support](https://ipwatchdog.com/2018/08/18/compromise-music-modernization-act-music-industry-support/)

### 3.3 DMCA Section 512 Safe Harbor Reform

**The "value gap" argument:** The music industry (led by RIAA and NMPA) argues that Section 512 creates a "value gap" where platforms like YouTube profit enormously from user-uploaded music while paying far less than they would under direct licenses, because the safe harbor shields them from liability as long as they respond to takedown notices.

**Notice-and-takedown vs. notice-and-stay-down:** The core reform demand is replacing the current system (where infringing content can be re-uploaded immediately after takedown) with "notice-and-stay-down" (requiring platforms to use filtering technology to prevent re-uploads).

**U.S. Copyright Office Section 512 Study (2020):** After a multi-year study, the Copyright Office concluded that "the operation of the section 512 safe harbor system today is unbalanced," highlighting areas where implementation diverged from Congress's original intent. Specific concerns included: eligibility qualifications, repeat infringer policies, knowledge requirements, takedown notice specificity, and subpoena processes.

**Industry positions:**
- **Content side (RIAA, NMPA, labels, publishers):** Called Section 512 a game of "whack-a-mole" and sought greater platform responsibility
- **Tech/platform side:** Praised safe harbors as "vital to the growth and success of an open and innovative internet"

**Status:** No comprehensive Section 512 reform legislation has passed as of early 2026. The EU addressed this through Article 17 of the Copyright Directive (see Section 5.1).

**Sources:**
- [U.S. Copyright Office Section 512 Study](https://www.copyright.gov/policy/section512/)
- [National Law Review: Section 512 is "Unbalanced"](https://natlawreview.com/article/copyright-office-issues-report-dmca-safe-harbors-section-512-unbalanced)
- [CRS: DMCA Safe Harbor Overview](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11478)

### 3.4 Copyright Royalty Board (CRB) Proceedings

**How CRB works:** A three-judge panel sets statutory royalty rates for compulsory licenses (mechanical royalties for streaming, digital performance royalties for internet/satellite radio). Proceedings are adversarial and expensive.

**Who participates (and the funding disparity):**
- **Seeking higher rates:** NMPA, NSAI (songwriters), SoundExchange, individual publishers
- **Seeking lower rates:** Spotify, Apple, Amazon, Google/YouTube, Pandora, SiriusXM
- **The asymmetry:** CRB proceedings are described as "costly and long legal battles pitting songwriters against some of the biggest companies in world history" (Recording Academy)

**Key 2015 CRB ruling (Webcasting IV):**
- SoundExchange wanted rates of 25-29 cents per 100 streams
- SoundExchange called the outcome "deeply disappointing"
- NAB said it was "pleased that streaming rates have begun to move in the right direction"
- Context spending in 2015: SoundExchange $960K lobbying, Pandora $1.3M lobbying, NAB $17.4M lobbying (CREW/OpenSecrets)

**Phonorecords III (2018):** CRB raised mechanical royalty rates for interactive streaming by 43.8% over five years (from 10.5% to 15.1% of revenue). Spotify, Google, Pandora, and Amazon appealed -- drawing intense criticism from songwriters and publishers.

**Phonorecords IV (2022-ongoing):** Negotiations for 2023-2027 rates. The CRB accepted a settlement increasing songwriter compensation, though specific rates are complex multi-factor formulas.

**Who benefits most:** Well-funded participants can afford years of litigation and expert witnesses. Independent songwriters and small publishers generally cannot participate directly and rely on NMPA/NSAI representation, whose interests may not perfectly align with those of the smallest creators.

**Sources:**
- [CRB Rate Proceedings](https://www.crb.gov/rate/)
- [Recording Academy: Songwriters Receive Monumental Victory](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/songwriters-receive-monumental-victory-copyright-royalty-board)
- [Music Business Worldwide: CRB New Rates](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/crb-officially-accepts-settlement-that-will-see-songwriters-paid-more-in-the-us-over-the-next-5-years/)

### 3.5 PRO Consent Decree Reform

**Background:** ASCAP and BMI have operated under DOJ antitrust consent decrees since 1941. The decrees require them to license their entire catalogs to any user who requests a license, with rates set by a federal "rate court" judge if negotiations fail. This prevents them from leveraging their market power but also constrains their negotiating ability.

**Who wants them modified/eliminated:**
- ASCAP and BMI (and their songwriter/publisher members)
- NMPA
- Argument: The decrees are outdated relics from the 1940s that hamper fair compensation in the digital age

**Who wants them maintained:**
- MIC Coalition (Music Innovation Consumers) -- represents digital services, broadcasters, restaurants, bars
- NAB
- Argument: Without consent decrees, ASCAP and BMI would have monopolistic pricing power over essential music licenses

**2014 partial withdrawal proposal:** ASCAP and BMI proposed letting publishers withdraw digital rights from the PROs (to negotiate directly with streaming services). DOJ rejected this.

**2016 DOJ review:** Concluded without modifications. DOJ found the consent decrees should remain.

**2019 DOJ review:** Antitrust Division reopened review. In January 2021, DOJ again decided to take no action to modify or terminate the decrees, but left open the possibility of future changes.

**NMPA reaction:** Called the DOJ's inaction "a massive missed opportunity."

**Sources:**
- [DOJ: ASCAP and BMI Consent Decree Review 2019](https://www.justice.gov/atr/antitrust-consent-decree-review-ascap-and-bmi-2019)
- [CRS: Music Licensing Consent Decrees](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11463)
- [BMI Advocacy Page](https://www.bmi.com/advocacy/doj_request_for_comment_consent_decree)

### 3.6 AM/FM Radio Performance Royalty Fight

**The issue:** The United States does not require terrestrial (AM/FM) radio stations to pay performance royalties to artists or sound recording copyright holders. Radio pays only the composition royalty (to songwriters via ASCAP/BMI). The U.S. joins Iran and North Korea as the only significant countries without this right.

**American Music Fairness Act (AMFA):**
- Introduced in both chambers of Congress (most recently relaunched in 2025)
- Would require AM/FM radio to pay performance royalties
- Endorsed by: AFL-CIO, A2IM, American Federation of Musicians, Recording Academy, RIAA, SAG-AFTRA, SoundExchange

**NAB opposition:**
- NAB spent $11.9 million on lobbying in 2024 (more than the RIAA's $6.9 million)
- NAB PAC raised $2.6 million in 2023-2024 cycle
- NAB president Curtis LeGeyt: the bill "disregards the value of radio and would undermine our critical public service to line the pockets of multinational billion-dollar record labels"
- Counter-legislation: Local Radio Freedom Act, which would maintain radio's royalty-free status
- Argument: Radio provides "free promotion" to artists; imposing royalties would create "economic hardship for locally owned radio stations"

**Financial impact of the gap:**
- American music played overseas generates ~$200 million annually in neighboring rights royalties
- These royalties are withheld from U.S. artists because the U.S. does not reciprocate -- foreign collecting societies hold back payments to Americans
- Every OECD country except the U.S. pays artists for terrestrial radio performance

**Status:** AMFA has not passed. The NAB's lobbying infrastructure significantly exceeds that of music industry advocates on this issue.

**Sources:**
- [SoundExchange: AM/FM Loophole](https://www.soundexchange.com/advocacy/closing-the-amfm-radio-royalty-loophole/)
- [Recording Academy: AMFA](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/issues-policy/american-music-fairness-act)
- [ITIF: Fixing Music Royalties Through AMFA](https://itif.org/publications/2025/12/19/fixing-music-royalties-through-the-american-music-fairness-act/)

### 3.7 State-Level Legislation and AI

**Tennessee ELVIS Act (2024):** Tennessee became the first state to modernize right-of-publicity laws for AI, preventing unauthorized AI deepfake vocal imitations. The music industry (centered in Nashville) was a key driver.

**Illinois HB 4875 (2024):** Modernized right of publicity law to address AI misuse, with music industry testimony supporting passage.

**Recording Academy state advocacy:** In 2024, the Recording Academy organized 7 State Capitol Advocacy Days (double previous years), reflecting increased focus on state-level protections.

**Federal AI bills:**
- **NO FAKES Act** (introduced 2024, revised 2025): Would create intellectual property protections for digital likenesses, allowing artists to license rights and sue over unauthorized deepfakes
- **TRAIN Act** (introduced 2024): Would require transparency from AI companies about whether copyrighted works were used in training data

**AI litigation:** Sony, UMG, and WMG filed lawsuits against AI music generators Suno and Udio in 2024 for alleged unauthorized use of copyrighted recordings in training data.

## 4. Who Benefits Most

### 4.1 The Power Structure

**Three major labels control ~80% of the U.S. recorded music market:**
- Universal Music Group: ~31% global market share
- Sony Music Entertainment: ~21%
- Warner Music Group: ~18.3%

(Market share figures based on 2018 IFPI data; percentages fluctuate year to year)

**The lobbying infrastructure primarily serves these entities.** The RIAA's board and funding come from its label members. The NMPA's major members are the publishing arms of the same conglomerates. When the RIAA wins a policy battle, the primary financial beneficiaries are the major labels.

### 4.2 The Artist Representation Gap

**"Artists don't really have a seat at any table"** -- this quote from a music manager (reported by BNN Bloomberg, 2019) summarizes the structural problem:

- The RIAA represents labels, not artists
- The NMPA represents publishers, not songwriters directly
- ASCAP/BMI represent songwriters for performance royalties but operate under consent decree constraints
- The Recording Academy lobbies on artist issues but also represents industry broadly
- Future of Music Coalition and Artist Rights Alliance are small organizations with limited budgets compared to industry trade groups

**The result:** Artist-focused organizations operate on tiny budgets compared to the entities they're trying to influence. The Future of Music Coalition is a nonprofit; the Artist Rights Alliance was only founded in 2019. Neither has lobbying expenditures remotely comparable to the RIAA or even the NMPA.

### 4.3 Cases Where Lobbying Outcomes Favored Corporations Over Artists

**CRB proceedings:** Tech companies (Spotify, Apple, Amazon, Google) with multi-billion-dollar valuations argue against rate increases for songwriters and performers. While the NMPA represents songwriter interests, the publishers (who take a percentage of royalties) also benefit. Individual artists cannot afford to participate in these proceedings.

**Music Modernization Act:** While hailed as a victory for songwriters, the MLC is governed by a board that includes publisher representatives. The blanket license system primarily benefits both digital services (reduced transaction costs) and large publishers (guaranteed payment streams). Whether small, independent songwriters benefit proportionally is debated.

**DMCA safe harbor:** The music industry's push for Section 512 reform is framed as protecting artists, but the primary beneficiaries of stronger enforcement would be major labels and publishers who own the most-streamed catalogs. Independent artists who use platforms like YouTube for discovery and promotion have mixed interests in this debate.

### 4.4 The "Value Gap"

**Industry argument:** Platforms like YouTube pay significantly less per stream than Spotify or Apple Music because they can rely on DMCA safe harbor rather than negotiating market-rate licenses. The RIAA and IFPI call this the "value gap."

**Counter-argument:** Tech platforms argue that user-generated content platforms provide promotional value and that the DMCA framework enables innovation.

**Who benefits from closing the value gap:** Primarily major labels and publishers, who would negotiate higher rates. Whether additional revenue would flow to artists depends on contractual terms -- many legacy recording contracts give artists small royalty percentages regardless of the per-stream rate.

## 5. International Comparisons

### 5.1 EU Copyright Directive -- Article 17 (formerly Article 13)

**What it does:** Article 17 effectively removes safe harbor protections for large user-upload platforms, requiring them to obtain licenses or implement measures to prevent copyrighted content from appearing. This is the "notice-and-stay-down" approach the U.S. music industry has sought domestically.

**Lobbying scale:**
- **Music industry:** Unified voice -- all sectors (labels, publishers, PROs, artist groups) supported Article 17
- **Google/tech opposition:** Google spent over EUR 31 million ($36 million) lobbying EU members against Article 13/17 (per UK Music's Michael Dugher). Google also lobbied through 24 other organizations that collectively spent EUR 25 million ($29 million)
- **MEPs reported unprecedented lobbying intensity** -- telephone calls, emails at a scale rarely seen on any EU legislative issue

**Outcome:** Directive adopted April 2019, with member states required to implement by June 2021. The music industry won its core demand, though implementation varies by country and some safeguards were included for user-generated content.

### 5.2 UK DCMS Committee Streaming Economics Inquiry (2020-2021)

**Scope:** Launched October 2020; received 300+ pieces of written evidence; report published July 2021.

**Key findings:**
- UK music streaming generates >GBP 1 billion annually with 114 billion streams
- Artists receive as little as 13% of streaming income
- Artists and songwriters testified that streaming earnings were insufficient to "keep the wolf from the door"
- The committee called for a **"complete reset"** of the streaming market

**Key recommendations:**
- Full Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) study into economic impact of major label dominance (UMG, Sony, WMG)
- Government support for independent labels to challenge majors
- Empowering creators to offset negotiating power disparity

**Government response (September 2021):**
- IPO tasked with setting up 3 music industry-led workshops on remuneration, transparency, and metadata
- CMA instructed to undertake a market study of music streaming

**Significance:** This was arguably the most thorough governmental examination of streaming economics and artist compensation ever conducted. Its findings -- particularly the 13% artist share figure and the "complete reset" language -- became widely cited in global policy discussions.

**Sources:**
- [UK Parliament: Economics of Music Streaming](https://committees.parliament.uk/work/646/economics-of-music-streaming/)

### 5.3 Terrestrial Radio Performance Rights -- International Comparison

**The U.S. is an outlier.** Every OECD member country pays artists a performance royalty for terrestrial radio play except the United States.

Countries that pay include: Austria, Australia, Belgium, Canada, Czech Republic, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, Japan, Luxembourg, Mexico, Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Slovakia, South Korea, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, Turkey, United Kingdom.

**Countries that do not pay (alongside the U.S.):** Iran and North Korea are frequently cited as the other significant holdouts.

**Consequence:** Foreign collecting societies withhold ~$200 million annually in royalties owed to American artists because the U.S. does not reciprocate. This is lost income directly attributable to NAB's successful lobbying to maintain the status quo.
