# Physical Music Sales Economics: CDs, Vinyl, and the Resurgence

*Research compiled March 2026. All figures US market unless otherwise noted.*

**Methodology note**: Every claim below is sourced from web research conducted in March 2026. Where data comes from a single source, it is marked `[single-source]`. Where I could not verify a figure from multiple sources, the gap is stated. The RIAA is the primary authority for US recorded music revenue data; Luminate (formerly Nielsen/SoundScan) is the primary authority for unit sales tracking.

## 1. Cost of Manufacturing CDs (2024-2026)

### Per-Unit Manufacturing Costs

CD manufacturing costs have plummeted compared to the 1990s:

| Run Size | Per-Unit Cost (2024-2025) | Packaging |
|----------|--------------------------|-----------|
| 100 units | $1.05-$1.79 | Eco-sleeve |
| 500 units | $1.40-$1.60 | Jewel case with insert |
| 1,000 units | $1.00-$1.30 | Standard packaging |
| 2,500 units | ~$0.80 | Standard |
| 5,000 units | ~$0.70 | Standard |

Sources: Accio supplier comparison, RushMediaPrint, Disc Makers, TrepStar

**For context**: In the 1990s, it cost up to $4.50 to manufacture a single CD. Today, costs are down to $0.90-$1.20 per disc thanks to automation and minimal packaging. (Source: Headphonesty, reporting on CD price-fixing concerns)

### Minimum Order Quantities

- North American suppliers: MOQs as low as 25-100 units
- Disc Makers: Replication starting at 500 units (notable for low MOQ)
- Asian suppliers: Typically 100-1,000+ units
- Print-on-demand services (e.g., Kunaki, TrepStar): No minimum

Sources: Accio, Disc Makers, TrepStar

### Wholesale vs. Retail Pricing

- **Retail price for new CDs**: Typically $12-$18 for standard editions; major label deluxe/reissues cluster at $19.98
- **Wholesale discount**: Retailers typically receive ~40% off retail (i.e., wholesale ~$9-$12 for a $15-$20 CD)
- **Retailer markup**: Up to 80% on wholesale cost reported, though actual margins are thinner in practice
- **Manufacturing cost vs. retail**: A CD costing $0.90-$1.20 to manufacture selling for $15-$20 represents an enormous spread, but the gap is consumed by distribution, marketing, overhead, and artist royalties

Sources: Headphonesty (CD price-fixing article), JPFolks forums, HowStuffWorks

### Margins Compared to the 1990s

The manufacturing cost has dropped from ~$4.50 (1990s) to under $1.50 (2024), yet retail prices for premium releases remain at $19.98 — roughly similar to 1990s pricing. This means the gross margin on physical CD production is substantially higher today than in the 1990s peak. However, vastly lower unit volumes (33 million CDs in 2024 vs. 943 million at peak in 2000) mean total profit dollars are far smaller. The three major labels pricing deluxe reissues at nearly identical $19.98 has drawn scrutiny. (Source: Headphonesty)

### Major CD Manufacturers Still Operating in the US

- **Disc Makers** (Philadelphia, PA) — also does vinyl pressing
- **Atomic Disc** (Portland, OR) — musician-run since 1993
- **Oasis CD Manufacturing**
- **Copycats Media** (Minneapolis, MN)
- **Bison Disc**
- **National Media Services**
- **Kunaki** — print-on-demand CDs, DVDs, vinyl, cassettes
- **Nova Disc** — plants in several US states
- **Groove House Records** — replication, duplication, and printing

Sources: Disc Makers, Atomic Disc, Accio

## 2. Cost of Manufacturing Vinyl Records (2024-2026)

### Per-Unit Manufacturing Costs

Vinyl pressing is significantly more expensive than CD manufacturing due to the mechanical nature of the process:

| Run Size | Weight/Type | Per-Unit Pressing Cost | Jackets/Packaging | All-In Per Unit |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| 100 units | Standard black | High — small runs costly | Basic | $10-$15+ |
| 200 units | 180g black | ~$1.80 pressing | ~$1.20 jackets | ~$8.80 all-in with freight |
| 300 units | Standard | Moderate | Basic | ~$7.60 all-in |
| 500 units | Black/smoky, gatefold | ~$2.10 pressing | ~$2.50 jackets | ~$6-$8 all-in |
| 1,000 units | 140g black | ~$1.20-$1.50 pressing | Standard | $5-$7 all-in |
| 1,000 units | 180g black | ~$1.50-$2.00 pressing | Standard | $7-$10 all-in |
| 1,000 units | 180-200g color, picture disc | ~$3.50 pressing | ~$4.00 jackets | ~$15+ all-in |

Sources: Vinyl Record Life (cost breakdown), Gotta Groove Records (pricing), Precision Record Pressing, Other Record Labels guide, Custom Record Factory

### Critical: Setup/Fixed Costs

Before any per-unit costs, vinyl pressing requires significant upfront investment:

- **Lacquer mastering**: $350-$700+ depending on complexity
- **Stamper creation**: $900-$1,600+
- **Total setup fees**: $1,000-$2,500 before a single record is pressed

This is why small runs are so expensive per unit — the setup costs are amortized across fewer copies.

Sources: Vinyl Record Life, Gotta Groove Records

### Pressing Plant Capacity and Wait Times

**The bottleneck situation (2021-2023)**: During the post-pandemic vinyl surge, pressing plants faced backlogs of up to 10-12 months. Limited pressing capacity, aging equipment, and surging demand created severe supply constraints, particularly for indie labels and smaller artists.

**Current status (2024-2026)**: The situation has improved substantially but is not fully resolved:

- **GZ Media** (world's largest) added 12 new pressing lines since 2023, cutting standard lead times to **14-18 weeks** for 12" LPs
- **GZ Media** is opening a third plant in Nashville capable of producing **85,000 records per day**
- **United Record Pressing** expanded Nashville capacity by **+20%** in 2025, with colored variant capability up **+26%**
- Current typical wait times: **4-6 months** for many plants, down from 10+ months at the worst point
- New boutique pressing startups have also added capacity

Sources: Grammy.com (vinyl shortage explainer), Mixmag, Billboard, Making Vinyl Conference 2024, Accio

### Colored/Specialty Vinyl vs. Standard Black

- **Cost premium**: Multi-color vinyl adds approximately **$1.00 per unit** in production costs
- **Why it costs more**: Standard black vinyl uses carbon black additive (which strengthens PVC); colored vinyl uses different dyes with unique melting properties, requiring more careful pressing
- **Real price difference at retail**: Much larger than $1 due to limited-run scarcity driving demand, not just manufacturing cost
- **Sound quality**: Colored vinyl may have marginally lower fidelity due to absence of carbon black reinforcement, though this is debated

Sources: Drocer Record, Gold Rush Vinyl, Discogs Digs, Unified Manufacturing, Audio MAV

### Minimum Order Quantities

- Most pressing plants: **100-300 unit minimum** for short runs
- Standard runs: 500-1,000+ for best pricing
- Some plants (e.g., Gotta Groove) offer runs as small as 100
- Setup costs make runs under 200 economically challenging

Sources: Gotta Groove Records, Precision Record Pressing, Custom Record Factory

### Wholesale vs. Retail Pricing for Vinyl

- **Standard new vinyl LP retail**: $25-$35
- **Deluxe/180g/gatefold**: $30-$45
- **Box sets**: $50-$150+
- **Record Store Day exclusives**: $25 (single LP) to $150+ (deluxe box sets)
- **Wholesale discount to retailers**: Approximately **40% off retail** (industry standard)
- **Retailer gross margin on new vinyl**: ~35% (tight by retail standards)
- **Used vinyl margin**: ~70% gross margin (much more profitable for stores)

Sources: ComebackVinyl (pricing methodology), Discogs forums, Financial Models Lab, Disc Makers blog

### Major Vinyl Pressing Plants in the US

- **United Record Pressing** (Nashville, TN) — one of the oldest, expanded 2025
- **GZ Media** (Nashville, TN) — Czech-based, world's largest, opening third Nashville plant
- **Gotta Groove Records** (Cleveland, OH) — indie-focused
- **Precision Record Pressing** (Burlington, Ontario/serves US market)
- **Quality Record Pressings** (Salina, KS) — audiophile-focused
- **Independent Record Pressing** (Bordentown, NJ)
- **Gold Rush Vinyl** (Austin, TX)
- **Capsule Labs**
- **Fidelity Record Pressing** — investing in automation
- **Disc Makers** (Philadelphia, PA) — also does CDs

The US has dozens of pressing plants; Hellbender Vinyl maintains an interactive map of all US vinyl plants. Gotta Groove Records also maintains a comprehensive directory.

Sources: Gotta Groove Records directory, Hellbender Vinyl map, vinyl-pressing-plants.com, Billboard

### Vinyl as Percentage of Total US Recorded Music Revenue

| Year | Vinyl Revenue | Total Revenue | Vinyl Share |
|------|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| 2015 | $416M | ~$7.0B | ~5.1% |

Vinyl represents nearly **three-quarters (75%)** of all physical format revenue, but only **~8% of total recorded music revenue** (2024). Streaming dominates at **84% of total revenue** ($14.9B). The physical-to-digital ratio has held steady at roughly 12% physical / 88% digital since 2018.

Sources: RIAA 2024 Year-End Report, Billboard, Variety

### Who Is Buying Vinyl? (Demographics)

**Age distribution** (2024 data):
- **Gen Z (under 25)**: Rapidly growing segment. 60% of Gen Z say they buy records (Futuresource Consulting). 76% of Gen-Z vinyl fans buy records at least once a month. 80% own a record player.
- **25-34 age group**: Largest buyer segment overall
- **35-44**: ~20% of indie record store buyers
- **18-24**: ~18% of indie record store buyers
- **84% of Gen-Z vinyl fans** shop for records in-store; 57% prefer in-store experience

**Key demographic shift**: Gen Z has become the "driving force" behind vinyl's popularity, according to the Vinyl Alliance. This is significant because the vinyl revival was initially driven by millennials and older collectors — the buyer base has gotten younger.

Sources: Luminate (Record Store Day 2025 report), Vinyl Alliance via Music Week, Futuresource Consulting, DJ Mag, CNN Business, Statista

## 4. CD Sales Trajectory

### Current US CD Revenue and Units

| Year | CD Revenue | CD Units Sold | Notes |
|------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 2000 (peak) | ~$13.2B | 943 million | Historical peak |
| 2022 | — | ~36M | — |
| 2023 | ~$536M | 36.8 million | +2.7% units YoY |
| 2024 | $541M | 33 million | +1% revenue YoY; units declined |

Sources: RIAA 2024 Year-End Report, Billboard, Accio

### Is the Decline Continuing or Stabilizing?

The picture is mixed:
- **Revenue**: Essentially flat — $541M in 2024, up just 1% YoY. This suggests stabilization at a low level rather than continued freefall.
- **Units**: Declining — from 36.8M (2023) to 33M (2024), a notable drop even as revenue held steady, suggesting price increases are compensating for volume loss.
- **Historical context**: CD units are down **~95% from the 2000 peak** of 943 million. The format has lost the mass market entirely.
- **Vinyl outselling CDs**: For the third consecutive year in 2024, vinyl albums outsold CDs in units (44M vs. 33M).

### Signs of a CD Resurgence?

There are genuine early signals, driven primarily by Gen Z:

- **43% of all CD buyers are under 35** — young consumers are driving the format
- Google Trends shows **40% increase** in interest in music CDs between 2024 and 2025
- Taylor Swift's *The Tortured Poets Department* sold **2.47 million CD copies** in H1 2024 alone
- Gen Z motivations: physical ownership, liner notes/artwork, affordability vs. vinyl ($12-$15 vs. $30-$40), "vintage" appeal, digital fatigue
- Major labels are releasing more CD variants and deluxe editions to capitalize

**However**: The CD "resurgence" is far smaller in scale than the vinyl revival. CDs lack vinyl's aesthetic/lifestyle appeal and audiophile cachet. It may stabilize as a niche collectible format rather than truly rebounding.

Sources: Axios, Washington Post, CNN Business, Fortune, Miami New Times, Hypebot, DJ Mag

### Japan's Continued CD Dominance — Context

Japan provides a striking contrast to the US market:

- **Physical formats = 65%** of Japan's total recorded music revenue (through September 2024). Streaming = 35%.
- This is essentially the inverse of the US (where streaming = 84%, physical = 12%)

**Why Japan is different**:
1. **Late streaming adoption**: Spotify and Apple Music only gained prominence in Japan in the late 2010s
2. **Sound quality culture**: Many Japanese consumers believe CDs suit human hearing better than compressed digital
3. **Idol/fan culture**: J-pop fans buy multiple copies of CDs for bundled perks — concert ticket vouchers, meet-and-greet invitations, collectible photo cards, bonus tracks
4. **"Jake-gai"**: Purchasing CDs for cover art quality and design — a culturally specific phenomenon
5. **Aging population**: 29% of Japan's population is over 65 and prefers physical formats
6. **Industry structure**: Japanese labels have been slower to pivot to digital, maintaining robust physical distribution infrastructure

Japan demonstrates that CD decline is not inevitable — it's driven by market structure, cultural preferences, and industry strategy, not just technology. However, even Japan is slowly shifting toward streaming.

Sources: Nippon.com, Believe, US Trade Gov (Japan Music Market report), Billboard, Statista, Equality-Empowerment

## 5. Physical Sales Economics for Different Players

### Major Label Artist: Vinyl Sale at $30-$40 Retail

**Revenue flow on a $35 vinyl LP (major label artist, sold through retail)**:

| Step | Amount | % of Retail |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Retail price | $35.00 | 100% |
| Retailer margin (~35-40%) | -$12.25 to -$14.00 | — |
| Wholesale price to label | ~$21.00-$22.75 | ~60-65% |
| Manufacturing + distribution | -$5.00 to -$8.00 | — |
| Marketing/overhead allocation | -$3.00 to -$5.00 | — |
| **Label gross profit** | **~$10-$14** | ~28-40% |
| Artist royalty (13-20% of retail, minus deductions) | — | See below |

**Artist royalty calculation**:
- Nominal royalty rate: 13-20% of suggested retail price (new artists: 13-16%; mid-level: 15-18%; superstars: 18-20%+)
- **Packaging deduction**: 20-25% is deducted before royalty calculation (industry standard for physical)
- So on a $35 LP at 15% royalty with 25% packaging deduction: 15% x ($35 x 0.75) = **$3.94 per unit** before recoupment
- **Recoupment**: Recording costs, video costs, tour support, and sometimes marketing are recoupable against royalties. An artist who hasn't recouped receives **$0** per vinyl sold until their advance is paid back.
- **Net result for unrecouped major label artist**: $0 per vinyl sale
- **Net result for recouped major label artist**: ~$3-$5 per vinyl sale

Sources: ASCAP, HowStuffWorks (recording contracts), Indie Music Academy, Mark Tavern Management, Record Label Mavericks, Sound on Sound

### Indie Artist Selling Vinyl Direct-to-Consumer

**Revenue flow on a $25 vinyl LP (indie artist, 300-unit pressing, sold direct at shows)**:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Manufacturing cost (300 units) | ~$7.60/unit all-in |
| Selling price at show | $25.00 |
| **Profit per unit** | **~$17.40** |
| **Profit margin** | **~70%** |
| Break-even point | ~47 units (on a $1,169 total pressing cost for 100 units) |

**Via Bandcamp** ($25 sale):
- Bandcamp commission: 10% = $2.50
- Payment processing: ~4-7% = $1.00-$1.75
- Net to artist: ~$20.75-$21.50
- Minus manufacturing: ~$7.60
- **Profit per unit via Bandcamp: ~$13-$14**
- **Profit margin: ~52-56%**

**Via consignment at record stores**:
- Store takes ~40% of retail
- Artist receives ~$15 on a $25 LP
- Minus manufacturing: ~$7.60
- **Profit per unit via consignment: ~$7.40**
- **Profit margin: ~30%**

**Key insight**: An indie artist selling 300 vinyl LPs direct at $25 each grosses $7,500 and nets ~$5,220 in profit. To earn equivalent revenue from Spotify at $0.004/stream, they would need approximately **1.3 million streams**. This is why vinyl is the single most important revenue format for indie artists selling physical music.

Sources: Disc Makers blog ("Is Vinyl Worth the Cost?"), Digital Music News, Hypebot, Bandzoogle, Atomic Disc

### Retailer Economics

**Independent record store**:
- New vinyl gross margin: ~35% (tight for retail)
- Used vinyl gross margin: ~70% (much more profitable)
- A $20 new record contributes ~$7 in gross profit
- A $20 used record contributes ~$14 in gross profit
- **Used vinyl is what keeps the lights on** for most independent record stores
- Average record store owner income: $45K-$150K (wide range) [single-source: Financial Models Lab]

**Amazon**:
- Referral fee for media products: ~15% of sale price
- If using FBA (Fulfillment by Amazon): additional 20-35% in fulfillment fees
- Total Amazon take on a vinyl sale: potentially 35-50%
- Amazon, Target, and Urban Outfitters leverage volume purchasing for lower wholesale costs than indie stores

Sources: ComebackVinyl, Discogs forums, Financial Models Lab, Repricer (Amazon fees), Zenventory

### Bandcamp's Model

- **Commission on physical sales**: 10% flat (vs. 15% for digital, dropping to 10% after $5,000)
- **Payment processing**: Additional 4-7% (separate from Bandcamp's cut)
- **Artist receives**: ~83-86% of sale price
- **Fulfillment**: Artist handles their own shipping/fulfillment; Bandcamp only processes the transaction
- **Bandcamp Fridays**: First Friday of each month, Bandcamp waives its revenue share. Generated $40M+ in sales in 2020 from 800,000+ fans.
- **Payouts**: Daily

Sources: Bandcamp Help Center (official fee page), Bandcamp Fair Trade Music Policy, Atomic Disc, Wikipedia (Bandcamp)

## 6. Economics of Limited Editions, Box Sets, and Special Releases

### How Labels Use Physical Scarcity to Drive Revenue

The modern physical music market is fundamentally a **scarcity-driven collectibles market**, not a mass-media distribution channel. Key strategies:

1. **Colored vinyl variants**: Release the same album in 4-8+ different colored vinyl editions, sometimes with retailer exclusives (Target, Walmart, Urban Outfitters, Amazon, indie stores each get a unique color)

2. **Limited-run pressings**: Marketing a vinyl as "limited edition" creates urgency. Consumers buy immediately rather than risk missing out. Secondary market prices for sold-out RSD releases can reach **200-400% of retail within hours**.

3. **Deluxe/super deluxe tiers**: Offer standard LP ($25-$30), deluxe LP with gatefold/bonus tracks ($35-$45), and super deluxe box set with art prints, posters, bonus discs ($75-$150+)

4. **Bundling with merchandise**: Box sets that combine vinyl with T-shirts, posters, coasters, art prints, or behind-the-scenes content. Deluxe box sets contribute roughly **33% of premium vinyl sales** [single-source: Taylor blog].

Sources: Billboard (multiple articles on variant strategy), Taylor (media packaging company), Variety

### Multiple Vinyl Variants and Chart Impact

This is one of the most significant and controversial dynamics in modern physical music:

**The scale of the strategy**:
- In early 2019, top 10 Billboard 200 albums averaged **3.3 physical variants per week**
- By end of 2023, that number jumped to **8.9 variants per week** — nearly tripled
- Eight of the year-end top 10-selling vinyl albums had sales boosted by multiple variant availability

**How it works for charts**:
- Each vinyl purchase counts as a full album sale on Billboard 200
- Multiple variants (different colors, covers, bonus tracks) encourage fans to buy 2, 3, or more copies
- Labels shifted to this strategy after ticket bundles were banned from chart counting

**Notable examples**:
- **Taylor Swift** — *Midnights*: 4 vinyl editions (Moonstone Blue, Jade Green, Mahogany, Blood Moon), each with different colored vinyl and cover art
- **Billie Eilish** — *Happier Than Ever*: 8 different colored vinyl versions including retailer exclusives for Target, Walmart, Urban Outfitters, Amazon, and indie stores
- **Billie Eilish's criticism**: Eilish publicly blasted artists who release multiple vinyl variants, calling it "so wasteful" (March 2024)

**Industry response**: Some independent record stores stopped reporting vinyl sales for Billboard charts, in part due to concerns about the variant strategy distorting chart positions. [single-source: Billboard]

Sources: Billboard (multiple: "Physical Album Variants Have Exploded," "Vinyl CD Album Variants Are Booming," "Limited-Edition Vinyl Records"), Variety (Taylor Swift/Olivia Rodrigo variants), Deadline (Billie Eilish criticism), SluttyVinyl

### Record Store Day Economics

**What it is**: An annual event (April, with a smaller Black Friday edition) featuring limited-edition vinyl releases available exclusively at independent record stores.

**Scale and impact** (2024-2025):
- RSD 2025 saw an **18% uplift** in sales compared to prior year
- RSD 2025 produced the **highest weekly vinyl sales in 30 years**
- For many stores, RSD is their **single biggest sales day** of the year — and what keeps them viable

**Mixed results in 2025**:
- Some stores reported sales **down ~33%** due to weather, economy, and tariff concerns
- Others (Rough Trade) reported sales **up 30%**, with RSD-specific titles up 20%
- The disparity suggests RSD success is increasingly dependent on store reputation and location

**Criticism and concerns**:
- RSD releases can be expensive ($30-$50+ for single LPs)
- Scalpers buy limited releases and flip them on secondary markets at massive markups
- Some argue RSD primarily benefits major labels and established artists, not the indie ecosystem it was designed to support
- Pressing plant capacity allocated to RSD releases can crowd out regular indie releases

Sources: Billboard (RSD 2025 report), Music Week, FieldStack (RSD recap), Wikipedia (Record Store Day)

## Summary: Key Takeaways

1. **Vinyl is a $1.4B market** in the US (2024), with 18 consecutive years of growth, but still only 8% of total recorded music revenue. It is a robust niche, not a mainstream format.

2. **The manufacturing bottleneck is easing** but not fully resolved. Lead times have dropped from 10+ months to 14-18 weeks at major plants, with significant capacity expansion underway (GZ Media, United Record Pressing).

3. **CDs are stable but shrinking**: $541M revenue in 2024, with units declining even as revenue holds due to price increases. Gen Z interest is real but far smaller than the vinyl revival.

4. **The vinyl market is a scarcity-driven collectibles market**. Colored variants, limited editions, and box sets are the primary revenue drivers, not mass distribution.

5. **Taylor Swift is the market**: Her vinyl sales are unprecedented — 1M+ units of a single album (1989 TV), 1 in 15 vinyl albums sold in 2023. No other artist is close.

6. **Indie artists benefit enormously from vinyl DTC sales**: $13-$17 profit per unit vs. fractions of a cent per stream. Vinyl is the most lucrative format for independent musicians.

7. **Record stores survive on used vinyl**: New vinyl margins (~35%) are thin; used vinyl margins (~70%) are what keep stores viable. Record Store Day provides a critical annual revenue spike.

8. **Japan shows CDs are not dead by nature** — cultural factors, fan economy structures, and industry strategy can sustain physical formats. The US decline is structural/strategic, not purely technological.
