## SECTION 1: HOW PERFORMING RIGHTS ORGANIZATIONS ACTUALLY WORK

### 1.1 ASCAP (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers)

**Structure & Governance:**
- Founded in 1914; operates as a **not-for-profit** membership association
- The only US PRO with a Board of Directors composed of songwriters, composers, and music publishers, elected by and from the membership every two years
- Board consists of 12 writer members and 12 publisher members
- Governed by Articles of Association and a Compendium of Rules and Regulations adopted by the Board
- CEO: Elizabeth Matthews (as of reporting period)
- President: Paul Williams (songwriter)

**How Collection and Distribution Works:**
- ASCAP licenses businesses, broadcasters, streaming services, and venues that publicly perform copyrighted music
- Issues blanket licenses granting access to its entire repertory
- Subject to a 1941 consent decree (amended most recently in 2001), which requires ASCAP to grant a license to any applicant and to accept any songwriter who meets minimum requirements
- Rate disputes go to a "rate court" in the Southern District of New York (SDNY), presided over by Judge Denise Cote
- ASCAP cannot deny a license; if it cannot agree on a rate with a licensee, the rate court sets the fee

**Membership Numbers:**
- Surpassed 1 million members in 2024
- Surpassed 1.1 million members in 2025, adding over 80,000 new members that year

**Total Collections (Recent Years):**

| 2024 | $1.835 billion | $1.696 billion | +5.7% |
| 2025 | $1.945 billion | $1.759 billion | +6.0% |

- 10-year compound annual growth rate: 6.7% for revenues, 7.3% for royalty distributions

### 1.2 BMI (Broadcast Music, Inc.)

**Structure & Governance:**
- Founded in 1939 (initially as a competitor to ASCAP, championing genres ASCAP underrepresented including country, R&B, and jazz)
- **Acquired by New Mountain Capital** in a deal that closed **February 8, 2024**, making BMI the **only for-profit PRO** in the US with an open-door membership policy
- New Mountain Capital is a growth-oriented investment firm with over $45 billion in assets under management
- Google/Alphabet fund acquired a minority stake as part of the transaction
- BMI affiliates received a one-time $100 million payout in connection with the sale
- CEO: Mike O'Neill (during transition period)

**For-Profit Margin Change:**
- Under the non-profit model, BMI retained approximately **10% of collections** for operating expenses
- Under the for-profit model, BMI raised its retained margin to **15% of collections**
- This means BMI now targets distributing **85% of licensing revenues** to songwriters, composers, and publishers (down from ~90%)
- BMI's stated rationale: retaining more cash enables reinvestment into better services and acquisitions that will ultimately grow songwriter revenues
- Risk identified by industry analysts: songwriters and publisher clients may notice a negative impact on earnings and leave the PRO

**Collection and Distribution:**
- Subject to a 1941 consent decree (amended most recently in 1994)
- Rate disputes go to SDNY rate court, presided over by Judge Louis Stanton
- Processes over 40 billion performances annually
- Digital streaming platforms contribute approximately 65% of total performance royalty collections as of 2024
- 1.4 million affiliates as of fiscal year 2023
- Licenses and collects on behalf of 22.4 million works

**Total Collections (Recent Years):**
| Fiscal Year (ending June 30) | Revenue | Distributions |
|-------------------------------|---------|--------------|
| FY2022 | $1.573 billion | $1.471 billion |
| FY2023 | Not publicly disclosed (first year of for-profit transition; annual report omitted headline revenue) |
| FY2024 | Not formally disclosed; international revenue reported at $245 million (~15% of total) [single-source] |
| FY2025 | Distributions hit "all-time high," up 10% over prior year (specific dollar figures not publicly released) |

**Note:** After the New Mountain Capital acquisition, BMI ceased publishing detailed revenue figures in its annual reports. This represents a significant transparency reduction compared to its prior practice and compared to ASCAP, which continues detailed financial reporting.

### 1.3 SESAC

**Structure:**
- Founded in 1930 (originally as the Society of European Stage Authors and Composers)
- **For-profit**, **privately held**, **invitation-only** PRO
- Acquired by **Blackstone** in January 2017 for approximately **$1.125 billion**
- Unlike ASCAP and BMI, SESAC is **not subject to a consent decree** and is not required to license to all comers at court-determined rates
- Does not accept unsolicited membership applications; prospective members typically need a representative (lawyer, manager, agent) to contact SESAC on their behalf

**How It Differs:**
- Selective representation model, emphasizing quality over quantity
- Because it lacks a consent decree, SESAC has more flexibility in rate negotiations
- Uses digital tracking, Nielsen Broadcast Data Systems monitoring, and watermarking for performance identification
- Administers public performance licenses for over 1.5 million musical works on behalf of more than 15,000 affiliated songwriters, composers, and music publishers

**Revenue (approximate):**
- ~$248 million in annual revenue circa 2016 (pre-Blackstone acquisition)
- ~$275 million by 2018 [single-source]
- Total operations (including subsidiary acquisitions) estimated at $400-$500 million [single-source]
- SESAC does not publicly report detailed financials

**Sources:** [Blackstone: SESAC Acquisition](https://www.blackstone.com/news/press/leading-music-rights-organization-sesac-to-be-acquired-by-blackstone/); [Billboard: SESAC Acquisition Price](https://www.billboard.com/music/music-news/blackstone-sesac-acquisition-price-7647846/); [SESAC About](https://www.sesac.com/about/); [SESAC Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SESAC)

### 1.4 GMR (Global Music Rights)

**Structure:**
- Founded in 2013 by music industry veteran **Irving Azoff** and long-term PRO executive **Randy Grimmett**
- For-profit, boutique PRO designed as a direct challenge to ASCAP and BMI's market dominance
- Not subject to consent decrees

**Roster:**
- Represents over 43,000 songs and approximately 83 songwriters
- High-profile roster includes: Pharrell Williams, Drake, Bruce Springsteen, Bruno Mars, the Eagles, Smokey Robinson, John Lennon (estate), U2, Tom Petty, and others
- Strategy: aggregate "must-have" songs that radio stations cannot operate without, then negotiate significantly higher rates than these artists received under ASCAP/BMI

**Legal Battles — GMR v. RMLC:**
- In 2016, the Radio Music License Committee (RMLC), representing ~10,000 radio stations, sued GMR alleging antitrust violations, claiming GMR packed several artists into a take-it-or-leave-it deal
- GMR countersued, alleging RMLC constitutes an "illegal buyer's cartel" controlling more than 90% of radio industry revenue
- In 2019, the Trump DOJ filed an amicus brief **supporting GMR**, suggesting RMLC was engaged in illegal price-fixing
- Case settled in 2022: GMR and RMLC filed a joint stipulation dismissing both antitrust lawsuits; a majority of commercial radio stations agreed to long-term licenses with GMR

**Significance:** GMR demonstrated that a PRO operating outside consent decree constraints could command higher rates for premium catalogs, but also triggered antitrust concerns from licensees who argued the "must-have" catalog approach was coercive.

**Sources:** [Variety: GMR-RMLC Settlement](https://variety.com/2022/music/news/irving-azoff-global-music-rights-rmlc-settlement-1235174154/); [MBW: GMR-RMLC Settlement](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/irving-azoffs-global-music-rights-and-commercial-radio-body-reach-final-settlement-agreement123/); [MBW: DOJ Sides with GMR](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/doj-sides-with-irving-azoffs-global-music-rights-in-antitrust-lawsuit-against-commercial-radio-body/)

### 1.5 SoundExchange

**Function:**
- Collects and distributes **digital performance royalties for sound recordings** (distinct from the composition-side PROs above)
- Designated by the Copyright Royalty Board to collect statutory royalties under Section 114 and Section 112 of the Copyright Act
- Covers non-interactive digital transmissions: satellite radio (SiriusXM), internet radio (Pandora non-interactive), cable TV music channels, etc.
- **Does not cover** interactive streaming (Spotify, Apple Music on-demand) — those royalties are negotiated directly between labels/distributors and platforms

**Distribution Split:**
- 50% to the sound recording copyright owner (typically the label)
- 45% to the featured artist
- 5% to non-featured musicians and vocalists (through AFM/AFTRA funds)

**Financial Performance:**
| Year | Gross Distributions |
|------|-------------------|
| 2022 | $959 million |
| 2023 | $1.003 billion (+4.3%) |
| 2024 | $1.052 billion (+4.9%) |

- Cumulative distributions since inception (2003): surpassed **$12 billion** milestone in February 2025
- Serves 700,000+ music creators

**Sources:** [SoundExchange: $1B in 2023](https://www.soundexchange.com/2024/01/22/soundexchange-distributes-1-billion-in-digital-royalties-to-creators-in-2023/); [SoundExchange: $12B Milestone](https://www.soundexchange.com/news/soundexchange-surpasses-12-billion-cumulative-distribution-milestone/); [InsideRadio: 2024 Payouts](https://www.insideradio.com/free/soundexchange-payouts-grew-5-in-2024-to-top-1-billion/article_713571b0-f343-11ef-97c5-1fc8b298070c.html)

### 1.6 The MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective)

**Structure:**
- Created by the **Music Modernization Act (MMA) of 2018**, signed into law by President Trump on October 11, 2018
- Began full operations on **January 1, 2021**
- Designated by the U.S. Copyright Office to administer blanket mechanical licenses for interactive streaming services (Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, YouTube Music, etc.)
- Non-profit organization; funded by digital service providers

**How It Works:**
- Digital service providers (DSPs) obtain a single blanket mechanical license from the MLC, rather than needing to identify and license each individual composition
- DSPs report their usage data and pay royalties to the MLC
- The MLC matches usage data to its database of musical works and distributes royalties to identified copyright owners
- Replaced the old song-by-song Notice of Intent (NOI) system under Section 115

**Financial Performance:**
- Total royalties processed: more than **$3.9 billion** to date
- Total royalties directly distributed: more than **$3.3 billion** in blanket royalties to rightsholders
- Has completed **54 monthly royalty distributions**, every one on time or early
- **68,000+ members** as of late 2025 (added 18,000+ in 2025 alone)
- Database contains data for more than **50 million songs** (6 million new works added in 2025)

**Historical (Unmatched) Royalties:**
- 20 DSPs transferred a combined **$424.4 million** in historical unmatched royalties to the MLC
- The MLC has distributed more than **$225 million** in historical royalties — more than 57% of the total amount of historical unmatched royalties transferred to it
- The Copyright Office recommended that distribution of remaining pre-2021 unmatched royalties not occur for 5 years (to allow more time for matching)

**Sources:** [The MLC Milestones](https://www.themlc.com/milestones); [MBW: MLC $3B Distributed](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/the-mechanical-licensing-collective-has-distributed-3bn-to-songwriters-and-publishers-since-2021/); [Billboard: MLC $424M Unmatched](https://blog.themlc.com/press-releases-beta/billboard-mlc-receives-over-424m-unmatched-black-box-streaming-royalties)

## SECTION 2: THE CONSENT DECREE PROBLEM

### 2.1 History of the Consent Decrees

**Origin (1941):**
- Beginning in the 1930s, the DOJ investigated ASCAP and BMI for anticompetitive business practices
- DOJ alleged that offering only blanket licenses constituted illegal "tying" (conditioning the sale of one good on the purchase of another) and price-fixing in violation of **Section 1 of the Sherman Antitrust Act**
- In 1941, both ASCAP and BMI settled, entering into consent decrees with the DOJ
- The decrees were designed to prevent monopolistic control over music licensing

**Key Amendments:**
- ASCAP consent decree: amended multiple times, most recently in **2001**
- BMI consent decree: amended most recently in **1994**
- Both amendments were approved by the rate court

### 2.2 What Consent Decrees Require

The decrees impose several fundamental constraints:

1. **Non-exclusive rights only:** ASCAP and BMI may obtain only the non-exclusive right to license musical performances (composers retain the right to license directly)
2. **Compulsory licensing:** Must grant a license to any applicant who requests one — cannot refuse
3. **Non-discrimination:** Must offer terms equal to similarly situated licensees
4. **Open membership:** Must accept as a member any songwriter who meets minimum requirements
5. **Rate court adjudication:** If a licensee and PRO cannot agree on a fee, either party may petition the SDNY rate court to set a "reasonable" rate
6. **DOJ oversight:** The Antitrust Division maintains ongoing oversight authority

### 2.3 The Rate Court Judges

Two SDNY judges have wielded enormous influence over music licensing rates:

- **Judge Denise Cote** — ASCAP rate court
  - Ruled in the Pandora case that ASCAP must offer Pandora a license including all works in its repertory; if ASCAP retains works to license to other users, it cannot exclude Pandora
  - Ruled against radio stations that refused to pay license fees, calling their behavior "unconscionable and unreasonable"

- **Judge Louis Stanton** — BMI rate court
  - Ruled that the BMI consent decree neither bars fractional licensing nor requires full-works licensing (contrasting with Judge Cote's interpretation)
  - His ruling created a split interpretation between the two rate courts on the critical question of fractional vs. full-works licensing

### 2.4 DOJ Reviews

**2014-2016 Review:**
- In June 2014, DOJ announced it would explore modifications to the consent decrees after ASCAP and BMI solicited a review
- On **August 4, 2016**, DOJ completed its review and decided **not to seek termination or modification**
- Controversially, DOJ also issued a "100% licensing" interpretation

**The "100% Licensing" Controversy (2016):**
- DOJ interpreted the consent decrees as requiring ASCAP and BMI to offer "full-work" licenses — meaning any single co-writer's PRO could license the entire work, not just that writer's fractional share
- This generated intense opposition:
  - NMPA President David Israelite called it "a massive blow to America's songwriters"
  - Sony/ATV CEO Martin Bandier called the decision "misguided and unprecedented"
  - The practical problem: if a song has co-writers in different PROs, 100% licensing could allow any one PRO to license the full work, creating legal uncertainty about who can authorize what
- Judge Stanton (BMI rate court) subsequently ruled against the DOJ's 100% licensing interpretation, finding the BMI consent decree does not require it

**2019-2021 Review:**
- On **June 5, 2019**, DOJ opened another review
- Review was disrupted by the COVID-19 pandemic
- In **January 2021**, DOJ closed the review **without proposing any modifications**
- Then-AAG Makan Delrahim urged DOJ to continue reviewing the decrees at least once every five years
- ASCAP and BMI expressed disappointment but noted the DOJ acknowledged that "songwriters are the backbone of the music marketplace" and that "greater competition rather than compulsory licensing is the answer"

### 2.5 Why Publishers Want Consent Decree Reform

Publishers and PROs argue that the consent decrees:
- Prevent them from negotiating market rates for digital performance rights
- Create a structural disadvantage vs. record labels, which negotiate freely
- Were designed for a 1940s broadcasting world and are ill-suited for the streaming era
- Force PROs to accept whatever rate the rate court determines, even if it is below what a free market would yield
- Constrain innovation in licensing models

## SECTION 3: HOW RIGHTS DEALS ARE CURRENTLY NEGOTIATED

### 3.1 Blanket Licensing vs. Direct Licensing

**Blanket Licensing:**
- A blanket license grants access to a PRO's entire repertory for a single fee
- Standard for ASCAP and BMI under the consent decrees
- Rates are set through negotiation or, failing that, rate court adjudication
- Efficient for licensees (one license covers millions of songs) but removes individual price discovery for specific works

**Direct Licensing:**
- Copyright holders negotiate individually with licensees
- Used by record labels for master recording rights with streaming services
- Some major publishers have attempted to withdraw digital rights from PROs to negotiate directly with streaming services (see Section 3.3)
- Also used for sync licensing (see Section 3.5)

### 3.2 How Streaming Services Negotiate

**Master Rights (Sound Recordings):**
- Labels negotiate directly with streaming platforms in a **free market** setting
- No consent decrees or rate courts constrain these negotiations
- Major labels (Universal, Sony, Warner) have significant leverage due to catalog concentration
- Rates are confidential but estimated at roughly 52-55% of streaming service revenue going to master rights holders [single-source]

**Composition Rights (Musical Works):**
- Performance royalties: collected by PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR) under blanket licenses, with rates constrained by consent decrees (for ASCAP/BMI) or negotiated freely (for SESAC/GMR)
- Mechanical royalties: set by the CRB in the Phonorecords proceedings (see Section 3.4)
- This creates a **fundamental asymmetry**: labels negotiate freely in a market, but composition rates are subject to government oversight and compulsory licensing

### 3.3 Publisher Partial Withdrawal from PROs

- In the early 2010s, some large publishers withdrew their catalogs' digital rights from ASCAP and BMI to negotiate directly with streaming services like Spotify and Pandora
- Rationale: publishers believed they could negotiate better rates in the free market than through rate-court-constrained PROs
- The **Second Circuit** determined that the consent decrees **prohibit selective (partial) withdrawal** — publishers must either use the PRO for all mediums or withdraw entirely
- NMPA petitioned the DOJ to amend the consent decrees to allow partial withdrawal
- **DOJ declined** to make this change in both the 2016 and 2021 reviews
- Result: publishers are locked into an all-or-nothing choice with their PRO, further constraining their negotiating flexibility

### 3.4 How the CRB Sets Mechanical Rates

**The Copyright Royalty Board:**
- Three Copyright Royalty Judges (CRJs) appointed by the Librarian of Congress
- Sets statutory royalty rates for mechanical licenses (reproduction/distribution of musical compositions)
- Proceedings known as "Phonorecords" cases

**Rate Standard Change:**
- **Old standard (801(b)):** Considered marketplace value but also weighed policy factors including industry stability — widely criticized by publishers as systematically depressing rates below market levels
- **New standard (Willing Buyer/Willing Seller):** Adopted via the **Music Modernization Act of 2018** — aims to approximate what rates would be in a free market negotiation
- The MMA's rate standard change applies to Phonorecords IV and beyond

**Key Phonorecords Proceedings:**

| Proceeding | Period | Headline Rate (Interactive Streaming) | Notes |
|-----------|--------|--------------------------------------|-------|
| Phonorecords III | 2018-2022 | 15.1% of service revenue | Increased from prior 10.5%; appealed by Spotify, Amazon, Google, Pandora in 2019; CRB upheld 15.1% in 2022; estimated $200M in back pay owed to songwriters |
| Phonorecords IV | 2023-2027 | 15.1% rising to 15.35% by 2027 | Settled (not litigated); first proceeding under willing buyer/willing seller standard; also improved alternate rate structures, per-subscriber penny rates, and Total Content Cost floor |

**Phonorecords IV Participants:**
- Affected services: Amazon Music, Apple Music, Google/YouTube Music, Pandora, Spotify
- Publisher/songwriter side: NMPA, Nashville Songwriters Association International (NSAI)
- Settlement was criticized by some songwriter advocacy groups for lack of transparency in negotiations

### 3.5 Sync Licensing — The True Market-Rate Right

**How It Works:**
- Required whenever music is synchronized with visual media (film, TV, advertising, video games)
- Requires **two separate licenses**: one for the composition (from the publisher) and one for the master recording (from the label)
- There is **no compulsory license** for sync rights — rights holders can refuse or set any price
- No consent decree governs sync rates
- Music supervisors initiate negotiations on behalf of productions

**Pricing:**
- No standard rate card; everything is negotiated case by case
- Factors: scope of use, term, territory, song prominence, project budget, artist profile
- US average commercial sync license: $15,000-$50,000 (2024, per Billboard) [single-source]
- Range: $500 (small indie film) to $500,000+ (major feature film or global advertising campaign)
- Sync is widely regarded as the one truly market-rate right in music because it operates on freely negotiated terms between willing counterparties

**Sources:** [Congress.gov: CRS Money for Something](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R43984); [Recording Academy: Phonorecords III](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/copyright-royalty-board-determination-phonorecords-iii-songwriters-impact); [Recording Academy: Phonorecords IV Settlement](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/songwriter-mechanical-royalty-rates-proposed-settlement-five-years-what-to-know); [MBW: Phonorecords IV](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/phonorecords-iv-music-publishers-react-to-us-streaming-royalty-rate-rise/); [Pepperdine JBEL: Consent Decrees in Streaming Era](https://digitalcommons.pepperdine.edu/jbel/vol10/iss2/8/); [Rolling Stone: Songwriters Fighting for Pay](https://www.rollingstone.com/pro/features/songwriters-spotify-amazon-crb-royalties-war-1015116/)

## SECTION 4: INEFFICIENCIES AND PROBLEMS WITH THE CURRENT SYSTEM

### 4.1 The "Black Box" — Unmatched Royalties

**The Problem:**
- When a song is streamed or broadcast but the rights holder cannot be identified from available metadata, the royalties go into an "unmatched" pool — colloquially called the "black box"
- The global value of black box royalties has been estimated at **$2.5 billion** (Digital Music News, 2017)
- Causes: poor or missing metadata, incomplete databases, fragmented rights ownership, failure to file proper Notices of Intent

**How Unmatched Royalties Are Distributed:**
- Historically, black box royalties were distributed to publishers based on **market share** — meaning major publishers received the largest share of unmatched funds
- This is structurally unfair because major publishers typically have the best systems to ensure their songs are properly matched, so the unmatched royalties disproportionately belong to indie songwriters and smaller publishers
- The MLC has partially addressed this: 20 DSPs transferred $424.4 million in historical unmatched royalties to the MLC; the MLC has matched and distributed over $225 million (57%+) of this amount

**Ongoing Issues:**
- "Fraud is rampant, detection is inconsistent, and billions are at stake — with no clear industry estimate of how much is being siphoned away" [single-source, Digital Music News 2025]

### 4.2 Delayed Payments

**Timeline from Stream to Payment:**
- Average wait: **2-6 months** for streaming royalties to reach artists
- Spotify, Apple Music, TIDAL, Amazon Music, Deezer: 2-3 month delay after streams occur
- DSPs report monthly sales to distributors on a **2-month delay**
- Performance royalties through PROs (ASCAP, BMI, PRS): paid **quarterly or semi-annually**, meaning 3-6 months or longer
- **International royalties: 12-18 months** due to reciprocal agreement processing times
- Each intermediary (DSP, distributor, PRO, sub-publisher) has its own reporting and reconciliation cycle, creating cascading delays

**Contrast with Other Industries:**
- Stock trades settle in T+1 (one business day)
- Credit card transactions settle in 1-3 business days
- Music royalties: months to years

### 4.3 Lack of Transparency

- Songwriters often cannot see how their specific royalty rates were determined
- BMI ceased publishing detailed revenue figures after going for-profit, reducing transparency further
- Rate court proceedings and CRB proceedings involve complex economic analysis that is difficult for individual songwriters to follow or influence
- Distribution formulas at PROs involve weighting factors (survey-based radio monitoring, digital tracking algorithms) that are not fully transparent to members

### 4.4 Multiple Rights Requiring Multiple Negotiations

A single song generates multiple distinct rights, each requiring separate licensing:

| Right | Who Administers | How Rate Is Set |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Public Performance (composition) | PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR) | Consent decree rate court / free negotiation |
| Mechanical (composition) | MLC / publishers directly | CRB Phonorecords proceedings |
| Sync (composition) | Publishers directly | Free market negotiation |
| Master Use (sound recording) | Labels/distributors | Free market negotiation |
| Digital Performance (sound recording) | SoundExchange | CRB statutory rate |

This fragmentation means a single streaming play of a song involves at least performance + mechanical rights for the composition, plus master rights for the recording — each flowing through different organizations with different payment timelines.

### 4.5 Fractional Ownership Complexity

- Modern songs frequently have **10+ co-writers**, each potentially represented by different publishers affiliated with different PROs
- A single song can have shares split across ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, and GMR simultaneously
- Each co-writer's share must be independently tracked, licensed, and paid
- The 100% licensing controversy (Section 2.4) was directly triggered by this fractional ownership reality
- Fractional ownership makes accurate metadata even more critical and errors even more consequential

### 4.6 International Collection Losses

**Reciprocal Agreement Problems:**
- PROs in different countries maintain reciprocal agreements to collect and remit royalties for each other's repertoires
- In practice, these agreements are "often slower, less transparent, and some money never finds its way home"
- Each society in the chain may deduct administrative fees, depleting the final payment
- Foreign societies don't always proactively register songs from reciprocal partners
- SoundExchange sends millions abroad in digital royalties annually, but foreign PMOs often cannot reciprocate because the US has no performance right for terrestrial AM/FM radio broadcasts — foreign societies keep US artists' broadcast royalties in their territories

**Structural Issues:**
- Local organizations in emerging/developing territories are less well-resourced
- Absence of full legal harmonization across jurisdictions leads to ambiguities
- No single global database of rights ownership exists (though efforts like the Global Repertoire Database have been attempted and failed)

### 4.7 The Metadata Problem

- Academic and trade reports estimate the global music industry loses **several hundred million dollars annually** to unclaimed or misdirected royalties caused by metadata errors
- One study found **46 million examples** of "author unknown" songs being streamed [single-source]
- A 2025 PRO report highlighted that up to **15% of indie music submissions** contained metadata mismatches that delayed or blocked royalty disbursement [single-source]
- Even small mismatches (e.g., "Jon Smith" vs. "Jonathan D. Smith") can block royalty payouts indefinitely
- There is no universal standard identifier linking a songwriter to all their works across all platforms and territories (ISNI, IPI, and ISRC exist but adoption is inconsistent)

### 4.8 Administrative Burden on Independents

- Major publishers have dedicated teams for royalty tracking, metadata management, and international collection
- Independent songwriters must navigate multiple PRO registrations, mechanical rights administration, sync licensing, and international collection — often without professional support
- The cost of proper administration can exceed the royalties earned for smaller catalogs
- This creates a structural advantage for major publishers, who can afford the infrastructure to minimize losses

## SECTION 5: THE CASE FOR A MARKET-BASED ALTERNATIVE

### 5.1 Why Music Rights Don't Currently Trade on Open Markets

**Structural Barriers:**
1. **Fragmented ownership:** A single song may have dozens of rights holders across multiple rights types
2. **Regulatory constraints:** Consent decrees and statutory licenses prevent free pricing for performance and mechanical rights
3. **Information asymmetry:** Major publishers and labels have superior data on rights valuations; independent creators do not
4. **Verification complexity:** Confirming who owns what share of which right is time-consuming and error-prone
5. **Lack of standardization:** No uniform "unit" of music rights exists for trading purposes
6. **High minimum investment thresholds** historically deterred smaller investors

### 5.2 What a Liquid Marketplace Could Look Like

**Core Features:**
- **Direct counterparty discovery:** Rights holders and licensees find each other without mandatory intermediaries
- **Transparent pricing:** Rates visible to all participants, enabling genuine price discovery
- **Real-time settlement:** Royalties flow from platform to rights holder in days, not months
- **Standardized rights units:** Fractional ownership stakes in compositions and recordings, tradeable like securities
- **Automated matching:** Play data matched to rights holders in real time, eliminating the black box
- **Global scope:** A single marketplace serving all territories, eliminating cross-border collection losses

### 5.3 Sync Licensing as a Market Model Proof of Concept

Sync licensing already operates closer to a true marketplace:
- No compulsory license — rights holders can say no
- Prices are freely negotiated based on supply and demand
- Music supervisors function like brokers, matching buyers and sellers
- Rates reflect genuine market value ($500 to $500,000+ depending on the work and use)
- The existence of a functioning sync market demonstrates that music rights can be priced by willing counterparties when regulatory constraints are removed

### 5.4 Existing Marketplace Experiments

**Royalty Exchange:**
- Online auction platform for buying and selling royalty assets
- Over **2,000 completed transactions** totaling **$170 million+** in volume
- Most transactions under $1 million each — accessible to smaller investors
- 30,000 registered investors
- Offers Private Syndicate investments via Special Purpose Vehicles for larger catalogs
- Demonstrates that price discovery is possible for music rights at scale, though liquidity remains limited

**Royal.io:**
- Founded by 3LAU; backed by Founders Fund
- Allowed artists to tokenize royalty streams and sell fractional ownership to fans
- Notable participants: Nas sold royalty tokens
- **Scaled back investment offerings in 2024** amid regulatory uncertainty about whether tokens constitute securities under US law
- Sunsetting its marketplace; continuing token trades and royalty distribution through OpenSea
- Key lesson: regulatory classification of tokenized rights as potential securities remains unresolved

**Audius:**
- Decentralized music streaming platform founded in 2018
- Claims 6+ million monthly users and 100,000+ artists (figures may be outdated)
- Artists retain 90% of revenue; payments in AUDIO cryptocurrency tokens
- **Problems:** limited mainstream catalog, crypto price volatility, user experience issues, governance slowness inherent to decentralized decision-making
- Has not achieved mainstream adoption comparable to Spotify or Apple Music

**ANote Music:**
- European platform offering a secondary market for music royalties since 2020
- Long-term goal: become "the stock exchange for music royalties"
- Smaller scale than Royalty Exchange

### 5.5 The Hipgnosis Model — Rights as Financial Assets

**Background:**
- Hipgnosis Songs Fund, founded by Merck Mercuriadis, went public on the London Stock Exchange in 2018
- Aggressively acquired song catalogs, spending approximately **$2.206 billion** across its portfolio
- Thesis: song catalogs are undervalued assets with predictable, growing cash flows — comparable to bonds or infrastructure assets

**What Happened:**
- Faced shareholder revolt over business plans and a planned catalog sell-off
- Third-party review accused management of overstating revenue (disputed by the company)
- **Blackstone acquired Hipgnosis Songs Fund for $1.6 billion** (effective July 29, 2024)
- Independent valuation firm subsequently valued the portfolio at **$2.36 billion** (August 2024) — approximately $150 million more than both the acquisition price and the total purchase cost
- Rebranded as **Recognition Music Group** in March 2025
- **Sony Music Publishing acquired subsidiary Hipgnosis Songs Group** on May 30, 2025

**What This Reveals:**
- Sophisticated financial buyers see song catalogs as investable assets with quantifiable value
- The Hipgnosis experience demonstrated both the opportunity (predictable cash flows) and the risk (valuation disputes, governance failures, limited liquidity for public shareholders)
- A more liquid marketplace could have provided better price discovery and exit options

### 5.6 Private Equity Flood into Music Rights

**Scale:** Total capital deployed into music rights since 2018 exceeds **$30 billion** [single-source, McKinsey]

**Major Players:**
| Firm/Entity | Notable Activity |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Blackstone | Acquired SESAC ($1.125B, 2017); acquired Hipgnosis Songs Fund ($1.6B, 2024) |
| New Mountain Capital | Acquired BMI majority stake (2024) |
| KKR | Partnered with BMG for catalog acquisitions; acquired Kobalt's KMR Music Royalties II portfolio ($1.1B, 62,000+ copyrights including The Weeknd, Lorde, Paul McCartney); $500M debt financing to HarbourView (2024); additional $500M financing |
| Apollo Global Management | Active in music rights acquisition vehicles |
| Primary Wave | Acquired Britney Spears' entire catalog (~$200M, December 2025) |
| Round Hill Music | Founded 2010; 100+ catalog deals worth $1B+; spent $200M+ across 40 acquisitions in 2022 alone |
| Concord Music Group | Major catalog acquisitions |

**What This Reveals:**
- Institutional investors have validated music rights as an asset class with attractive risk-adjusted returns
- These firms' willingness to pay significant multiples demonstrates that a transparent marketplace could unlock even more value through competitive bidding
- Currently, most deals are negotiated privately, with information asymmetry favoring well-resourced buyers
- A public marketplace would democratize access to these investments and potentially improve pricing for sellers (rights creators)

### 5.7 Why Incumbents Might Resist a Transparent Marketplace

**Information Asymmetry Benefits:**
- Major labels and publishers possess superior data on catalog valuations, streaming trends, and rights ownership
- This informational advantage allows them to acquire catalogs at favorable prices from less-informed sellers
- A transparent marketplace with public pricing would erode this advantage
- Black box distributions by market share benefit majors at the expense of independents

**Structural Advantages at Risk:**
- Major publishers serve as essential intermediaries — a direct marketplace would reduce their role
- PROs' blanket licensing model, while constrained by consent decrees, provides a reliable revenue base
- Labels' negotiating leverage with streaming services depends partly on the opacity of deal terms

### 5.8 How a Marketplace Could Solve Specific Problems

**The Black Box:**
- Real-time matching of plays to rights holders using blockchain-verified ownership records
- Transparent, auditable chain from play event to payment
- Eliminate the "distribute by market share" fallback that disadvantages independents

**Payment Delays:**
- Smart contracts enabling near-instant settlement upon verified play
- Eliminate cascading delay through multiple intermediaries
- Target: payment within days, not months

**Price Discovery:**
- Open bidding for rights reveals true market value
- Comparable to how stock exchanges replaced opaque OTC markets
- Would answer the fundamental question: what are specific music rights actually worth when freely traded?

**International Collection:**
- A single global platform eliminates the need for reciprocal agreements between national societies
- No fee erosion from multiple intermediary deductions
- Consistent rules and transparent processes across territories

### 5.9 Potential Downsides and Risks

- **Fragmentation:** A marketplace with thousands of individual rights holders could make blanket licensing more difficult for small businesses
- **Power imbalances:** Sophisticated institutional investors could price out small rights holders or exploit information advantages
- **Regulatory gaps:** Tokenized music rights may be classified as securities, triggering costly compliance requirements (as Royal.io discovered)
- **Quality control:** Open markets could be flooded with low-value rights, creating noise that obscures price signals
- **Loss of collective bargaining:** Individual creators negotiating alone may get worse terms than they would through collective representation
- **Technology barriers:** Not all rights holders have the technical sophistication to participate in a digital marketplace
- **Transition costs:** Migrating from the current system to a marketplace would be enormously complex and disruptive

## SECTION 6: INTERNATIONAL MODELS WORTH EXAMINING

### 6.1 UK: PRS for Music and PPL

**PRS for Music:**
- Collective management organization for musical works (compositions)
- Comprises two societies: **PRS** (Performing Right Society) for public performance and broadcast rights, and **MCPS** (Mechanical-Copyright Protection Society) for mechanical reproduction rights
- 175,000+ members
- Unlike the US, PRS handles both performance AND mechanical rights for compositions in a single organization
- Not subject to US-style consent decrees, though regulated under UK competition law

**PPL (Phonographic Performance Limited):**
- Collects royalties for **sound recordings** (master rights) when publicly performed or broadcast
- Functional equivalent of SoundExchange but broader: covers terrestrial radio, TV, and public venues (not just digital)
- **Key difference from US:** In the UK, terrestrial radio pays public performance royalties to both songwriters/publishers (through PRS) AND performers/recording owners (through PPL). In the US, terrestrial radio pays only the composition side (through PROs) — there is no federal performance right for sound recordings on AM/FM radio.

**Structural Advantage:** The UK system's two-organization model (PRS for compositions, PPL for recordings) is simpler than the US system's fragmentation across ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR, SoundExchange, and the MLC.

### 6.2 Nordic Countries — Collective Management Cooperation

**Key Organizations:**
- STIM (Sweden), Koda (Denmark), TONO (Norway), Teosto (Finland), Stef (Iceland)
- Together represent close to **250,000 members** plus international repertoire through reciprocal agreements
- Owned and governed by songwriters, composers, lyricists, and music publishers

**The Polaris Hub Model:**
- In 2013, Koda, Teosto, and TONO partnered to establish **Polaris**, a shared licensing hub for multi-territorial digital services
- Aimed to pool resources and create efficiencies across the Nordic region
- **However, the Polaris Hub was subsequently discontinued** because "the Nordic model is no longer sufficient in an increasingly competitive and global market"
- This suggests that even cooperative regional models face challenges in a global digital marketplace

**Current Collaboration:**
- The five Nordic CMOs recently launched **joint principles for licensing generative AI**, demonstrating continued cooperation on emerging issues
- They argue collective licensing is "effective, sustainable, and resource-efficient" for AI companies needing music rights access

**Assessment:** The Nordic model represents an intermediate approach — more cooperative than the fragmented US system but still territory-based. The discontinuation of Polaris suggests that multi-territorial licensing requires truly global solutions, not just regional coordination.

### 6.3 EU Collective Rights Management Directive (2014/26/EU)

**Adopted:** February 26, 2014

**Purpose:**
- Harmonize governance, financial management, and transparency rules for collective management organizations (CMOs) across the EU
- Enable **multi-territorial licensing** of musical works for online use — previously, online services needed separate licenses from CMOs in each EU country
- Facilitate development of cross-border online music services

**Key Provisions:**
- CMOs must meet transparency and reporting standards
- Rights holders have the right to choose which CMO represents them and to withdraw rights
- Multi-territorial licensing provisions (Title III) allow qualifying CMOs to license across EU borders

**Results:**
- Online music services can now obtain licenses from CMOs operating across EU borders (rather than country by country)
- Aggregation of repertoires has facilitated new online services and reduced transaction costs
- **However,** the European Commission has acknowledged that Title III "did not adequately facilitate licensing for online use of musical works on a multi-territorial level" — implementation has been uneven
- CMOs in smaller markets have faced challenges competing with larger organizations under the new framework
- The directive introduced competitive pressure that has altered CMOs' traditional monopoly positions in their home territories

### 6.4 Countries with More Market-Based Approaches

No country has fully replaced collective management with an open marketplace for performance or mechanical rights. However:

- **The US sync licensing market** is the closest existing model to a free market for music rights (no compulsory license, freely negotiated rates)
- **SESAC and GMR** in the US operate outside consent decrees, providing a partial example of market-based PRO negotiation
- **Australia (APRA AMCOS)** has experimented with more flexible licensing models for digital services [single-source]
- The EU Directive's multi-territorial licensing framework represents a partial move toward competition among CMOs, though it falls short of a true marketplace
