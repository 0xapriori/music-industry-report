# How Sample Clearance Actually Works: Mechanics, Costs, Players, and Economics

**Research compiled: March 2026**
**Purpose: Source material for music industry report**

---

## 1. The Clearance Process Step by Step

### What Happens When an Artist Wants to Sample Another Recording

Sample clearance is the legal process of obtaining permission to use a portion of an existing copyrighted recording in new music. Two separate clearances are required for every sample that uses an actual recording:

1. **Master Use License** -- permission from the owner of the sound recording (typically a record label, but sometimes the artist or producer)
2. **Publishing/Composition License** -- permission from the owner of the underlying musical composition (typically a music publisher, but sometimes the songwriter directly)

Both clearances are independently required. Obtaining one without the other does not grant the right to release the song. (Sources: Nolo, Spotify for Artists, Tracklib, Sound on Sound, multiple industry attorneys)

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1: Identify all rights holders.**
- Use performing rights organization databases (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) to identify publishers and songwriters
- Use label copy from the original recording to identify master owner
- Use PPL (master side) or PRS (publishing side) databases for additional information
- For older recordings, rights may have been sold multiple times, complicating identification

**Step 2: Submit a written clearance request to both the master owner and publisher(s).**
The request must include:
- Title and artist of the original work
- Title and artist of the new work
- Description of the sample (what portion, how long, how it's used)
- Duration of the sample in the new recording
- How the sample will be used (chorus, verse, intro, throughout)
- Intended release format and territory

**Step 3: Review and negotiation.**
- Rights holders review the request and decide whether to grant permission
- If willing, they propose commercial terms (fees, royalty splits, ownership percentages)
- Negotiation may go through multiple rounds
- Each rights holder negotiates independently

**Step 4: Execute license agreements.**
- Separate written agreements for master and publishing
- Terms specify territory, formats, duration, financial arrangements
- Clearance must be completed before the new recording is released

(Sources: Nolo "How to Obtain Sample Clearance," Spotify for Artists "Do's & Don'ts of Sample Clearances," multiple industry guides)

### The Role of Sample Clearance Specialists

Several companies specialize in navigating the clearance process:

- **DMG Clearances** -- Founded in 1996 by Deborah Mannis-Gardner, known as the "Queen of Sample Clearance" (Forbes). Clients include Kendrick Lamar, Beyonce, and Rihanna. DMG charges flat rates starting at $150/hour minimum for the first hour, with Stage One clearances (submission and negotiation) at $325 each for publishing and master rights. Mannis-Gardner has been clearing samples since 1990 and also handles all music clearance for Rockstar Games (Grand Theft Auto series since San Andreas, 2004). (Sources: Billboard, Synchtank, Okayplayer, Music Week)

- **Sample Clearance Ltd** -- UK-based clearance service

- **Tracklib** -- A newer model (marketplace) offering pre-cleared samples organized into three licensing tiers: Category A ($1,500), Category B ($500), Category C ($50), with over 90% of the catalog in Category C. In 2024, Tracklib added unlimited clearances to subscription tiers ($8.99-$19.99/month). (Sources: Tracklib, Music Ally)

### Typical Timelines

- Industry consensus: **2 to 6 months** is typical for a sample clearance
- Minimum recommended: at least 4-6 weeks, though most practitioners recommend starting months in advance
- Major publishers may take months to respond; smaller indie publishers sometimes respond in as little as two weeks [single-source: industry practitioner accounts]
- Deborah Mannis-Gardner advises against trying to clear samples at the last minute
- Finding rights holders alone can take weeks, especially for obscure recordings
- Financial negotiations can extend the process significantly

(Sources: Spotify for Artists, Feedtracks, multiple industry guides)

### What Happens When Clearance Is Denied

Rights holders have absolute discretion to deny clearance. There is no compulsory license for sampling (unlike mechanical licenses for cover songs). Notable consequences:

- The song cannot be legally released with that sample
- The artist must remove the sample, replace it, or abandon the track
- Some artists maintain informal **"Do Not Sample" lists** -- artists who categorically refuse to grant sample clearances. Tracy Chapman is a documented example; DMG Clearances confirmed she was on an unofficial "do not sample or interpolate" list. (Source: American University Business Law Review, NPR)

### What Happens When a Sample Is Used Without Clearance (Post-Release Disputes)

Using an uncleared sample constitutes copyright infringement. Consequences can include:

- **Statutory damages**: Up to $150,000 per sample, per copy distributed (Source: US Copyright Law)
- **Injunctions**: Courts can order the song removed from sale/streaming
- **Retroactive licensing demands**: The sampled party negotiates from maximum leverage, often demanding far more than pre-release clearance would have cost
- **Settlement payments**: Often confidential but documented cases range from hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars
- **Songwriting credit reassignment**: The original writers may be added to the credits of the new song

## 2. The Economics of Clearance

### Typical Cost Structure

Sample clearance deals typically involve two components:

1. **Upfront advance/fee** -- a one-time payment
2. **Ongoing royalty** -- either a percentage of ownership in the new composition (publishing side) or a per-unit/penny rate (master side), or both

### Documented Fee Ranges

**Publishing (Composition) Clearance:**
- Typical advance: $1,500 to $5,000 (Sources: Nolo, Music Industry How To, multiple industry guides)
- Typical ownership percentage demanded: 15% to 50% of the new song's publishing, depending on how prominently the sample features (Sources: Feedtracks, multiple industry sources)
- For a small vocal snippet, DMG Clearances targets around 5% of publishing [single-source: Synchtank interview with Mannis-Gardner]
- If the sample forms the "hook" of the new song, publishers may demand 50% to 100% of the publishing (Sources: Feedtracks, Audiobulb Music)

**Master (Sound Recording) Clearance:**
- Typical advance: $2,000 to $10,000 (Sources: Nolo, Music Industry How To, Sound on Sound)
- Typical royalty: 1% to 5% of retail price per unit sold, or an equivalent "penny rate" (e.g., $0.03 per unit) (Sources: Sound on Sound, Ask MetaFilter industry discussion, Art and Media Law)
- Some labels prefer a flat-fee "buyout" -- a higher one-time payment in exchange for no ongoing royalty
- A documented "standard" structure: $5,000 recoupable advance + 3% royalty rate (or ~$0.03 per unit) (Source: Ask MetaFilter, corroborated by Sound on Sound)

**Combined (Both Clearances):**
- Total minimum cost for both clearances: roughly $4,000 to $15,000+ in advances alone, plus ongoing royalties
- Publishers typically charge $1,500-$3,000 as a non-negotiable fee plus a piece of the new copyright, and labels charge several thousand dollars more (Source: Synchtank/Mannis-Gardner interview)

### Recoupable vs. Non-Recoupable Advances

- **Recoupable advance**: The upfront payment is deducted from future royalties owed to the sample owner. Once the advance is "recouped" through royalties, additional royalties begin flowing.
- **Non-recoupable fee (buyout)**: An upfront payment that is not offset against future royalties -- the sample owner keeps the fee AND receives ongoing royalties. This is increasingly common and is less favorable to the sampling artist. (Source: Sound on Sound, Nolo)

### The "Rollover" Advance Structure

In traditional sample clearance deals, advances were structured against "penny rates" (per-unit royalties). As units sold exceeded the advance, the rights holder would receive ongoing per-unit payments. Some deals included "rollover" provisions where additional advances were triggered at certain sales thresholds. [Note: Specific rollover thresholds are not well-documented in publicly available sources; this structure is referenced in industry discussions but detailed terms are typically confidential]

### Percentage of Song Ownership Typically Demanded

The percentage demanded by sample owners varies widely:

- Small, unrecognizable snippets: 2-10% of publishing
- Recognizable melodic or rhythmic elements: 15-25% of publishing
- Prominent hooks or signature elements: 25-50% of publishing
- Songs built primarily around a sample: 50-100% of publishing

Documented extreme cases:
- **Sting received 85% of Juice WRLD's "Lucid Dreams"** publishing for an interpolation of "Shape of My Heart" (Sources: Okayplayer, NME, The Fader, Nolazine -- multiple independent sources confirm this figure)
- **The Verve lost 100% of royalties** on "Bitter Sweet Symphony" to Mick Jagger/Keith Richards' representatives (Sources: NPR, Rolling Stone, Wikipedia -- extensively documented)

### How Clearance Costs Have Changed Over Time

**Pre-1991 (Before Grand Upright v. Warner):**
- Sampling was largely unregulated; many artists sampled freely without clearance
- The Bomb Squad (Public Enemy producers) layered 10-20+ samples per track with minimal or no clearance costs
- Albums like "It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back" (1988) and "Fear of a Black Planet" (1990) were created in this environment

**Post-1991 (After Grand Upright v. Warner):**
- The 1991 ruling established that all sampling requires clearance
- Clearance fees rose dramatically as rights holders recognized the value
- Law professors Peter DiCola and Kembrew McLeod estimated that clearing the samples on Public Enemy's "Fear of a Black Planet" at 2010 rates would result in a **loss of at least $5 per copy** -- a loss of $5 million on a platinum record (Source: Grand Upright Music Wikipedia article, citing academic research)
- Albums with dozens of samples became economically unviable
- Production shifted toward fewer samples per track, or replaying/interpolating to avoid master clearance

**Post-2005 (After Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films):**
- The Sixth Circuit ruled "Get a license or do not sample," eliminating the de minimis exception for sound recordings in that jurisdiction
- Even tiny, unrecognizable snippets required clearance (though the Ninth Circuit disagreed in 2016, creating a circuit split)
- Clearance costs remained high; leverage firmly with rights holders

**Streaming Era (2015-present):**
- Per-stream revenue is approximately $0.008 before splits among rights holders (Source: multiple streaming economics analyses)
- The math of sample clearance becomes more challenging: a $5,000 advance requires roughly 625,000 streams just to generate that amount in gross revenue (before the label, distributor, and other parties take their shares)
- DJ Shadow stated in 2017: "Music has never been worth less as a commodity, and yet sampling has never been more risky" (Source: Music Week)

### Cases Where Clearance Costs Exceeded or Consumed Revenue

- **Public Enemy**: Estimated $5/copy loss if "Fear of a Black Planet" samples were cleared at modern rates (Source: DiCola & McLeod academic study)
- **Puff Daddy (Sean Combs)**: Albums cost "a couple of hundred thousand dollars" in sample clearances during his peak sampling era; described as a period where "people were quoting astronomically high percentages" (Source: Synchtank/Mannis-Gardner interview)
- **Juice WRLD's "Lucid Dreams"**: 85% of publishing went to Sting, leaving minimal revenue for the creators of the new song despite it being a massive hit (Sources: multiple, see above)
- **The Verve's "Bitter Sweet Symphony"**: 100% of royalties went to Jagger/Richards representatives for over 20 years, despite the song being a global hit generating millions in revenue from sales, airplay, and sync placements. Rights were finally returned to Richard Ashcroft in 2019. (Sources: NPR, Rolling Stone, multiple)

### The "Price Out" Phenomenon

Rights holders can effectively block sampling by demanding prohibitive fees. There is no standardized regulation governing clearance fees, leaving room for subjective valuations:

- No obligation to offer reasonable terms
- No compulsory licensing mechanism (unlike cover songs)
- Rights holders can simply refuse or quote unaffordable prices
- The sampled party has all the leverage in negotiations
- This is functionally equivalent to a refusal for artists who cannot afford the quoted price

(Sources: Tracklib, Music Industry How To, Art and Media Law, Cordero Law)

## 3. Masters vs. Publishing in Sampling

### Why Both Clearances Are Required

A recorded song involves two separate copyrights:

1. **The musical composition** (melody, lyrics, harmony) -- owned by songwriters/publishers
2. **The sound recording** (the specific recorded performance) -- owned by the label or recording artist

When you sample an actual recording, you are using both the composition and the specific recording of it. Each copyright must be separately licensed. (Sources: Nolo, Spotify for Artists, U.S. Copyright Office)

### What Happens When Master Owner and Publisher Disagree

- Each rights holder acts independently; there is no requirement to coordinate
- If the publisher grants clearance but the master owner refuses (or vice versa), the sample cannot be used
- In the Car Seat Headrest case, the publisher initially appeared to approve the sample, but the songwriter (Ric Ocasek) ultimately refused, forcing a recall of physical copies (Source: Make It In Music, multiple)

### How the Split Works

- Publishing clearance typically involves a **percentage of ownership** in the new composition
- Master clearance typically involves an **advance plus a per-unit royalty** (penny rate or percentage of PPD/retail)
- The two deals are negotiated separately and have different structures
- Publishing deals affect ongoing songwriting royalties (performance, mechanical, sync)
- Master deals affect record sales/streaming revenue

### Re-Recording / Replaying a Sample to Avoid Master Clearance

Artists can avoid the master use license by **re-recording or replaying** the sampled element themselves (interpolation). This only requires clearance of the composition copyright:

**Economics of interpolation vs. direct sampling:**
- Interpolation requires only publishing clearance (one negotiation instead of two)
- Avoids the typically larger master clearance fee
- Publishing clearance for interpolation still involves royalty splits and advances
- Cost of re-recording/replaying must be factored in (studio time, musicians)
- Interpolation is generally cheaper and simpler than direct sampling

However, interpolation is not a guaranteed safe harbor:
- **Juice WRLD's "Lucid Dreams"** was an interpolation (not a direct sample) of Sting's "Shape of My Heart," yet Sting still received 85% of publishing (Sources: NME, Okayplayer, Nolazine)
- **Donna Summer's estate sued Kanye West** over an interpolation of "I Feel Love" on the Vultures album, even though he re-recorded the melody rather than sampling the original (Sources: Complex, Billboard, CBS News)

(Sources: By the Barricade, Medium/Eleven Eleven Music Consulting, MusicTech, U.S. Copyright Office educational materials)

## 4. Who Controls the Process

### How Major Labels Handle Incoming Sample Requests

- Major labels (UMG, Sony, Warner) have dedicated sample clearance departments
- They typically send a standard form to the requesting party requiring details of the intended use
- Response times vary: weeks to months
- Labels generally respond within "a couple of weeks" with an offer, but major publishers can take much longer
- Labels evaluate: prominence of the sample, commercial potential of the new recording, relationship with the requesting artist/label

(Source: Defected Records industry guide, Sound on Sound)

### The Power Dynamic

The sampled party holds virtually all leverage in clearance negotiations:

- No obligation to grant clearance at any price
- No compulsory licensing mechanism
- Can demand any percentage or fee
- Can simply refuse without explanation
- The requesting party has no recourse if refused (other than not using the sample)
- Post-release, leverage increases further -- the sampling artist faces statutory damages of up to $150,000 per infringement

### Estates and Catalog Owners: Often More Aggressive

Documented cases of aggressive enforcement by estates and catalog acquirers:

- **Donna Summer's estate** sued Kanye West over an interpolation on Vultures, despite explicit denial of permission having been communicated before release (Sources: Billboard, Complex, CBS News)
- **Allen Klein** (Rolling Stones' former manager who controlled their pre-1970 copyrights) demanded 100% of "Bitter Sweet Symphony" royalties after initially agreeing to 50% -- when the single became a hit, the terms changed (Sources: NPR, Rolling Stone, The Verve Live)
- **James Brown's estate** did not clear "Funky President" for use on Kanye's Vultures album (Source: Billboard)
- **John Lennon's estate** has rarely approved sample clearances since his death in 1980, blocking Chance the Rapper's "Juice" from streaming (Source: NME, Billboard)

### Impact of Catalog Acquisitions (Hipgnosis, Primary Wave, etc.)

The wave of catalog acquisitions since ~2018 has changed the landscape:

- **Hipgnosis Songs Fund** spent $2 billion+ acquiring catalogs from artists including Red Hot Chili Peppers, Lindsey Buckingham (Fleetwood Mac), Barry Manilow, Nikki Sixx (Motley Crue), and many more (Source: Variety)
- **Primary Wave** acquired catalogs including Britney Spears' (February 2026), and partnered with the Prince estate (Sources: Deadline, Primary Wave)
- Investment firms purchasing catalogs are motivated by maximizing return on investment, which can mean:
  - More aggressive monetization of sampling rights
  - Higher clearance fees to boost revenue from the acquired catalog
  - More active enforcement against uncleared samples
  - Less willingness to grant favorable terms to emerging artists

- However, an independent valuation of Hipgnosis's assets found the portfolio worth 26% less than the company's own figure ($1.95 billion vs. reported value), raising questions about the sustainability of high acquisition multiples (Source: Music Business Research)

[Note: Direct evidence linking catalog acquisitions to increased sample clearance fees is largely anecdotal and industry-reported rather than empirically documented. This is a widely discussed concern but specific before/after comparisons are not publicly available.]

### Streaming-Era Economics and Clearance Willingness

- With per-stream rates around $0.008, the revenue generated by a sampled song is much lower per-play than in the physical sales era
- This creates tension: clearance fees were established in an era of $10-$18 album sales, but most consumption now generates fractions of a cent per stream
- Some rights holders have become more willing to clear samples at lower upfront fees in exchange for higher ownership percentages, since the value is in long-term streaming accumulation
- Others have become less willing to bother with clearance at all, given the administrative cost relative to expected revenue [single-source: industry commentary]

## 5. The Independent Artist Problem

### Disproportionate Impact on Independent Artists

Sample clearance costs create a severe structural disadvantage for independent artists:

**Financial barriers:**
- Combined master + publishing clearance: typically $4,000-$15,000+ in advances alone
- Many indie artists cannot afford even the minimum clearance costs
- An indie artist may have a total recording budget of $2,000-$5,000 -- less than the cost of clearing a single sample
- Major label artists have clearance costs covered by the label (recoupable against the artist's royalties, but not an out-of-pocket expense)

**Leverage disparity:**
- Major label artists benefit from pre-existing relationships between labels
- A request from Universal Music for a Warner Music sample carries more weight than a request from an unknown indie artist
- Major labels can offer "most favored nations"-style reciprocal arrangements that indie artists cannot
- Rights holders are more likely to grant clearance when the new song has commercial backing and marketing support

**Legal risk:**
- Statutory damages of up to $150,000 per infringement make uncleared sampling an existential financial risk for indie artists
- Lord Finesse sued Mac Miller for $10 million over an uncleared sample on a free mixtape ("Kool Aid and Frozen Pizza" sampling "Hip 2 Da Game"); case settled for undisclosed terms (Sources: Billboard, Rolling Stone, MTV)

### The Creative Impact

The economics of sample clearance have measurably changed what music sounds like:

- **The Bomb Squad's dense, multi-sample production style** (Public Enemy) is essentially extinct in commercial music due to clearance costs (Source: Grand Upright Music Wikipedia, multiple music journalism sources)
- DJ Shadow stated: "If you're clearing one sample out of 18, and they want 50% and everybody down the line wants 50%, then it's kind of like, 'Hang on guys, this isn't going to work!' It literally makes the art form illegal and dangerous." (Source: Music Week, 2017)
- Production has shifted toward: fewer samples per track, interpolation/replay instead of direct sampling, original composition, royalty-free sample libraries
- Sample-based genres most affected: hip-hop, electronic, dance music, trip-hop

### De La Soul: A Case Study in Sample Clearance and Catalog Availability

De La Soul's classic albums (including "3 Feet High and Rising," 1989) were **unavailable on streaming platforms for 34 years** until March 3, 2023, primarily due to unresolved sample clearances:

- The delay was rooted in a dispute between De La Soul and their former label Tommy Boy Records over who would pay for uncleared samples
- Major samples (Hall & Oates, Steely Dan) had been cleared, but smaller samples in interstitial "skits" had not
- After Tommy Boy was acquired by Reservoir Media, DMG Clearances restarted negotiations in January 2022
- It took approximately one year to license most samples
- Samples that could not be cleared were interpolated or replayed for the streaming versions
- Some tracks were altered (e.g., "Cool Breeze on the Rocks" was replaced with "Cool Breeze on the Rocks (The Melted Version)")

(Sources: Okayplayer, Slate, Wikipedia, MiDIA Research)

## 6. Notable Clearance Disputes and Failures

### Landmark Legal Cases

**Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc. (1991)**
- Biz Markie sampled Gilbert O'Sullivan's "Alone Again (Naturally)" without clearance
- Judge Kevin Duffy opened his opinion with "Thou shalt not steal"
- Court found copyright infringement; album pulled from sale
- **Impact**: Established that all sampling requires permission; fundamentally changed hip-hop production economics
- (Sources: Wikipedia, GW Law MCIR, Justia, multiple legal analyses)

**Bridgeport Music, Inc. v. Dimension Films (2005)**
- N.W.A.'s "100 Miles and Runnin'" used a two-second sample of Funkadelic's "Get Off Your Ass and Jam"
- Sixth Circuit ruled: "Get a license or do not sample" -- eliminated the de minimis exception for sound recordings
- In 2016, the Ninth Circuit reached the opposite conclusion, creating a circuit split that remains unresolved
- (Sources: Wikipedia, Indiana Law Journal, Harvard Cyber Law)

### Major Clearance Failures and Disputes

**The Verve -- "Bitter Sweet Symphony" (1997)**
- Sampled the Andrew Oldham Orchestra's version of the Rolling Stones' "The Last Time"
- Initially negotiated 50% royalty split with Allen Klein (Stones' former manager)
- After the song became a hit, Klein demanded 100% of royalties and all songwriting credit for Jagger/Richards
- The Verve received zero royalties for over 20 years
- Also sued separately by Andrew Loog Oldham (master rights holder)
- In 2019, Jagger, Richards, and Klein's son finally returned rights to Richard Ashcroft
- (Sources: NPR, Rolling Stone, BBC, extensively documented across dozens of sources)

**Nicki Minaj -- "Sorry" / Tracy Chapman (2018)**
- Minaj wanted to sample Chapman's "Baby Can I Hold You"
- Chapman refused clearance (she is on the "do not sample" list)
- Minaj delayed her album Queen but the track leaked to radio
- Chapman sued; settled for $450,000 in January 2021
- (Sources: NPR, Variety, Rolling Stone, NBC News)

**Car Seat Headrest -- "Teens of Denial" (2016)**
- Sampled The Cars' "Just What I Needed"
- Matador Records believed they had clearance from the publisher
- Publisher did not actually have authority to approve the use in the US
- Ric Ocasek refused to approve the sample
- Physical copies had to be recalled and destroyed at enormous cost
- The band had to write a new song to replace the track
- (Sources: Make It In Music, Sound on Sound)

**Vanilla Ice -- "Ice Ice Baby" / Queen & David Bowie (1990)**
- Sampled the bassline of "Under Pressure" without credit or payment
- Queen and Bowie threatened litigation
- Settled privately; Vanilla Ice later disclosed he paid $4 million to acquire the publishing rights to "Under Pressure" (Source: American Songwriter, multiple)

**Juice WRLD -- "Lucid Dreams" / Sting (2018)**
- Interpolated Sting's "Shape of My Heart"
- Sting received 85% of publishing rights
- Producer Nick Mira publicly criticized Sting on social media
- Sting also faced a $15 million lawsuit from Yellowcard over the same song [single-source: Teal Cheese]
- (Sources: Okayplayer, NME, The Fader, Billboard)

**Mac Miller -- "Kool Aid and Frozen Pizza" / Lord Finesse (2012)**
- Sampled Lord Finesse's "Hip 2 Da Game" on a free mixtape
- Lord Finesse sued for $10 million
- The video had received 20+ million YouTube views before takedown
- Settled for undisclosed terms in December 2012
- (Sources: Billboard, Rolling Stone, MTV)

### Kanye West: Serial Sampling Controversies

Kanye West has faced at least 14 sampling lawsuits throughout his career (Source: Billboard). Notable incidents:

- **Yeezus (2013)**: Received a $12 million budget, with ~$4 million allocated for recording costs including sample clearance (Source: Complex, citing contract documents)
- **Vultures 1 & 2 (2024)**: Multiple uncleared samples led to lawsuits:
  - Donna Summer's estate sued over interpolation of "I Feel Love" (clearance was explicitly denied before release)
  - Ozzy Osbourne/Black Sabbath objected to uncleared "Iron Man" sample
  - James Brown's estate confirmed "Funky President" was not cleared
  - Marshall Jefferson sued over use of "Move Your Body"
  - (Sources: Billboard, Complex, CBS News, Rolling Stone, Vibe)

### Chance the Rapper: Mixtape-Era Clearance Problems

- "Juice" from Acid Rap (2013) could not be released on streaming because it samples Donny Hathaway's "Jealous Guy" (written by John Lennon); the Lennon estate has rarely approved sample clearances since 1980 (Sources: Billboard, NME)
- Sued by jazz musician Abdul Wali Muhammad over "Windows" from 10 Day mixtape, which sampled Lonnie Liston Smith's "Bridge Through Time" (Source: Complex)

## 7. Key Takeaways for the Report

### Verified Facts (Multiple Sources)

- Two separate clearances (master + publishing) are always required for direct sampling
- Typical combined advance costs: $4,000-$15,000+ (range documented across multiple sources)
- Publishing ownership demands: 15-50% typical, up to 100% in extreme cases
- Master royalty rates: typically 1-5% per unit or equivalent penny rate
- Timeline: 2-6 months typical
- The sampled party has essentially all leverage
- No compulsory licensing mechanism exists for sampling
- Grand Upright (1991) and Bridgeport (2005) are the two landmark cases
- De La Soul's catalog was unavailable for streaming for 34 years due to clearance issues
- The Verve lost all royalties on "Bitter Sweet Symphony" for 20+ years
- Sting received 85% of "Lucid Dreams" publishing
- Nicki Minaj paid $450,000 to settle with Tracy Chapman
- Vanilla Ice paid $4 million for "Under Pressure" publishing
- Public Enemy-style multi-sample production became economically unviable post-1991

### Items Requiring Flagging

- [single-source] DMG Clearances targets ~5% publishing for small vocal snippets (Synchtank interview only)
- [single-source] Specific Tracklib licensing tier prices may have changed since reporting
- [confidential] Most settlement amounts are undisclosed; reported figures may be approximations
- [circuit split] Whether the de minimis exception applies to sound recording sampling depends on jurisdiction (Sixth Circuit says no; Ninth Circuit says yes)
- [anecdotal] Claims about catalog acquisitions increasing clearance fees are industry commentary, not empirically documented

### Areas Where Real Data Is Unavailable

- Aggregate statistics on how many sample clearance requests are denied vs. approved do not appear to exist publicly
- Total industry spending on sample clearance fees is not tracked or reported
- Systematic comparison of clearance costs for the same sample when requested by major label vs. indie artist is not documented (though the disparity is widely acknowledged)
- Detailed financial terms of most clearance deals are confidential
