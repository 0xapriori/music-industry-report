### 1. Isaac Hayes Estate — Catalog Control, Posthumous Rights, and the Trump Lawsuit

**Background:**
Isaac Hayes died on August 10, 2008. His catalog spans his work as a songwriter, performer, and producer — most prominently through Stax Records and its subsidiary Enterprise Records.

**Who Controls the Masters:**
- Hayes' Stax/Enterprise-era master recordings are owned by **Concord Music Group**. Concord acquired the Stax catalog through its 2004 purchase of Fantasy Records, which had bought the Stax catalog in 1977 after Stax's bankruptcy. Concord reactivated the Stax label in December 2006 and signed Hayes as one of the first acts on the revived imprint.
- The Hayes estate does **not** control the original Stax-era masters. These remain with Concord as part of the broader Stax catalog acquisition chain (Stax -> Fantasy -> Concord).

**Who Controls the Publishing:**
- In October 2023, **Primary Wave Music** announced a publishing administration and marketing deal with the Estate of Isaac Hayes. Primary Wave administers Hayes' publishing interests and has rights to market his name, image, and likeness.
- Specific composition rights are split among multiple publishers. For example, "Hold On, I'm Coming" (co-written by Hayes and David Porter) has rights divided between **Universal Music Publishing Group (UMPG)** and **Warner Chappell Music Publishing**.
- Isaac Hayes III has been the primary family representative managing the estate's business affairs.

**The Trump Campaign Lawsuit (2024-2026):**
- In August 2024, the Hayes estate sued Donald Trump and his 2024 presidential campaign for unauthorized use of "Hold On, I'm Comin'" at rallies.
- The Trump campaign argued that the Hayes estate did not own the copyright to the song, and suggested Hayes had been personally favorable toward the GOP.
- A federal judge denied Trump's motion to dismiss the case.
- In February 2026, the lawsuit was mutually resolved. The Hayes estate characterized the resolution as "reaffirming artist rights."

**Key Takeaway:** The Hayes estate illustrates the common separation between masters ownership (held by labels/corporate successors) and publishing administration (managed by estate-appointed partners). The family controls publishing income and NIL rights but not the original recordings.

### 2. Clipping — "Visions of Bodies Being Burned" and Experimental Sampling

**Background:**
Clipping (stylized as clipping.) is an experimental hip-hop group consisting of rapper Daveed Diggs and producers William Hutson and Jonathan Snipes. Their approach to sampling is fundamentally different from mainstream hip-hop.

**Sampling Philosophy:**
- Clipping primarily builds beats from **found sounds and field recordings** rather than traditional music samples: beer bottles, alarm clocks, bricks breaking, thermoses (the thermos sampled for "Work Work" from 2014's CLPPNG was an opening night gift from a play Jonathan Snipes sound-designed).
- The group has stated explicitly that they **avoid traditional music samples in part because they are difficult to clear**. This creative constraint shaped their sonic identity.
- Their approach aligns with hip-hop's foundational ethos of "digging up sounds" but substitutes crate-digging for environmental sound capture.

**"Say the Name" — The Geto Boys Clearance Challenge:**
- The album's title quotes a line from Geto Boys' "Mind Playing Tricks on Me."
- For the single "Say the Name," the chorus repeats this quote. However, the isolated vocal track of "Mind Playing Tricks on Me" was never available separately.
- **Workaround:** Rather than sampling the original recording, the band recorded Daveed Diggs' voice and **pitch-shifted it down** to approximate the original's character.
- Even this approach required clearance negotiations. Obtaining permission proved difficult — the track was originally intended for the predecessor album *There Existed an Addiction to Blood* but was held back due to clearance delays.
- The song was described as already "old to them" by the time it appeared on *Visions of Bodies Being Burned*, indicating a clearance timeline of potentially over a year. [single-source — based on band interview context]

**Creative Process:**
- Every song begins with a roughly 2-hour conversation/argument about what the song should be.
- The group contextualizes sounds extensively — looking up YouTube videos, discussing aesthetic goals — before beginning production.

**Key Takeaway:** Clipping demonstrates how clearance difficulty can become a creative driver. Their avoidance of traditional samples is partly pragmatic (clearance costs and complexity) and partly aesthetic, resulting in a distinctive sonic palette built from non-musical sources.

### 3. Biz Markie / "Alone Again (Naturally)" — Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc.

**The Case:**
- **Case citation:** Grand Upright Music, Ltd v. Warner Bros. Records Inc., 780 F. Supp. 182 (S.D.N.Y. 1991)
- **Date:** December 17, 1991
- **Judge:** Kevin Thomas Duffy, U.S. District Court, Southern District of New York

**What Was Sampled:**
- Biz Markie sampled Gilbert O'Sullivan's 1972 hit "Alone Again (Naturally)" for the track "Alone Again" on his 1991 album *I Need a Haircut*, released on Cold Chillin' Records (distributed by Warner Bros.).
- Biz Markie's team had written to O'Sullivan's representatives **requesting permission** before release. Permission was denied. **They used the sample anyway.**

**The Ruling:**
- Judge Duffy's opinion famously opened with the biblical commandment: **"Thou shalt not steal."**
- The court granted an injunction against the defendants and ruled that sampling without permission constitutes copyright infringement.
- Critically, Judge Duffy **referred the matter to the U.S. Attorney for the SDNY** for potential criminal prosecution under 17 USC 506(a) and 18 USC 2319 — an extraordinary step that sent shockwaves through the industry.
- Warner Bros. was ordered to pull *I Need a Haircut* from circulation.

**Career Impact on Biz Markie:**
- The album was recalled from stores. The track "Alone Again" was pulled.
- Biz Markie's recording career effectively stalled. His next album, pointedly titled ***All Samples Cleared!*** (1993), reflected the new legal reality but did not achieve commercial success comparable to his earlier work.
- He pivoted toward **DJing, live performance, and less sample-dependent production**, eventually becoming known more as a personality/DJ (including appearances on children's television) than as a recording artist.
- Biz Markie died on July 16, 2021, at age 57.

**Industry-Wide Impact:**
- The ruling established the legal precedent that **all samples must be cleared before release**.
- Sample clearance fees made records like those produced by the Bomb Squad for Public Enemy — which used dozens of samples — **prohibitively expensive to produce**.
- The decision fundamentally shifted hip-hop production away from dense sample collages toward fewer, more prominently featured samples or original instrumentation.

### 4. Kanye West — Sample-Heavy Production Economics

**Scale of Sampling:**
Kanye West is one of the most prolific samplers in modern music. Key albums and their sampling profiles:

**My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy (2010):**
- The album contains approximately **29+ distinct samples** across 13 tracks (per WhoSampled/Spotify sample playlists).
- Sample clearance was handled by Eric Weissman of Music Licensing, Inc.
- Known clearance cost: **$62,500** was paid to clear the sample of Eddie Bo's "Hook & Sling" — but this led to a lawsuit from TufAmerica (the rights owner), which alleged that Roc-a-Fella/Universal used the song in ways beyond the written license agreement.
- Aphex Twin (Richard D. James) allegedly attempted to contact West to perform a polished version of his sample himself but was shut down by West's team, who reportedly also tried to avoid paying clearance. [single-source]

**The Life of Pablo (2016):**
- Contains approximately **39+ samples** (based on Spotify sample playlist).
- The album's fluid, evolving nature (West updated tracks after initial release) complicated clearance timelines.

**Vultures 1 & 2 (2024) — The Uncleared Samples Crisis:**
This represents a dramatic departure from industry norms:
- **Donna Summer's estate**: Publicly stated West asked to use "I Feel Love," was denied, but used it anyway (potentially with re-sung vocals or AI). The estate sued; the track "Good (Don't Die)" was removed from streaming.
- **Black Sabbath/Ozzy Osbourne**: The sample of "Iron Man" on "CARNIVAL" was pulled from the album version after threats of legal action from the Osbournes.
- **James Brown's estate**: Told Billboard that "Funky President (People It's Bad)" was not cleared for use in "FUK SUMN."
- **Portishead**: Co-founder Geoff Barrow publicly stated that their song "Machine Gun" was sampled on "Field Trip" (Vultures 2) without proper clearance.
- **Brand Nubian**: Reservoir Media was still negotiating clearance for the "Slow Down" sample in "KEYS TO MY LIFE" after release.
- Rell Lafague, president/COO of Reservoir Media, stated: **"On the level of Kanye in 2024 to put out an entire album with samples that haven't even been requested to be cleared, I don't think I've ever really seen that today."**

**Economics of Clearing Kanye Samples:**
- According to Pusha T, sample holders sometimes request **"97 percent of the record"** when clearing samples for Kanye, exploiting his fame and perceived willingness to pay.
- **Cost reduction strategy:** Kanye frequently has samples **replayed** (re-performed by session musicians), which requires only a publishing license (cheaper) rather than a master recording license.
- **In-house advantage:** Many songs he samples are owned by the label he's signed to (Universal), allowing internal deals at reduced rates.
- Industry-standard sample clearance involves two separate licenses: one for the **master recording** (from the label) and one for the **composition/publishing** (from the publisher/songwriter).

### 5. The Verve / "Bitter Sweet Symphony" — ABKCO, Allen Klein, and the 2019 Resolution

**The Sample:**
- The Verve sampled a 1966 **orchestral recording** of the Rolling Stones' "The Last Time" by the **Andrew Oldham Orchestra** (Andrew Loog Oldham was the Stones' former manager/producer).
- The composition rights to "The Last Time" were controlled by **ABKCO Records**, headed by Allen Klein (the Stones' former business manager).

**The Initial Deal:**
- The Verve's label negotiated a license from **Decca Records** (which held the master recording rights) to use a **five-note segment** of the orchestral recording.
- The agreed split: **50% of royalties** to the Jagger/Richards composition rights holders.

**What Went Wrong:**
- Allen Klein claimed the Verve used **more of the sample than agreed** — a larger portion than the licensed five-note segment.
- Klein demanded the Verve either relinquish **100% of songwriting royalties** or pull the single from sale.
- The Verve settled: **all songwriting credits were changed to Jagger-Richards**, and **100% of publishing royalties** went to ABKCO.
- Richard Ashcroft reportedly received a total of **$1,000** from what became one of the decade's biggest songs.
- **Estimated lost revenue:** $5 million in publishing revenue (Billboard estimate) to potentially $7-12 million over 22 years. [single-source for the $7-12M figure]

**The 2019 Resolution:**
- Allen Klein died in 2009. In early 2019, Ashcroft's managers approached Klein's son Jody Klein.
- Jody Klein connected them to the Rolling Stones' manager Joyce Smyth, who spoke to Mick Jagger and Keith Richards.
- In April 2019, **ABKCO, Jagger, and Richards agreed to return the songwriting credits and royalties to Ashcroft**.
- The resolution was voluntary — not court-ordered. It took 22 years.

### 6. Juice WRLD / "Lucid Dreams" — Sting's "Shape of My Heart"

**What Was Used:**
- "Lucid Dreams" (2018) **interpolates** (re-performs, not directly samples) the melody from Sting and Dominic Miller's 1993 song "Shape of My Heart."
- Producer Nick Mira built the beat around the interpolated guitar melody.

**Royalty Arrangement:**
- Juice WRLD's producer Nick Mira stated that **Sting receives 85% of the royalties** from "Lucid Dreams."
- Sting and co-writer Dominic Miller received co-writing credit and 85% of the copyright.

**Was There a Lawsuit?**
- Contrary to widespread reporting, **Sting denied suing Juice WRLD**. A Sting spokesperson told Billboard the claims of legal action were "simply untrue."
- The royalty arrangement appears to have been a **negotiated settlement/license**, not a court-imposed judgment.
- Sting described the song as a "beautiful interpretation that is faithful to the original song's form" and joked that the royalties would "put my grandkids through college."

**Additional Legal Complication:**
- Pop-punk band **Yellowcard** separately sued Juice WRLD for $15 million, claiming "Lucid Dreams" copied melodic elements from their song "Holly Wood Died." This case was complicated by Juice WRLD's death in December 2019.

**Key Takeaway:** Even interpolations (not direct samples) can result in the original rights holders receiving the vast majority of revenue. The 85% figure demonstrates how interpolation can be more expensive than many producers expect.

### 7. Mac Miller / "Kool Aid and Frozen Pizza" — Lord Finesse Lawsuit

**What Was Sampled:**
- Mac Miller used the beat from Lord Finesse's 1995 track **"Hip 2 Da Game"** for his song "Kool Aid and Frozen Pizza," which appeared on his 2010 mixtape *K.I.D.S.*
- The mixtape was released as a **free download** on DatPiff.com.

**The Lawsuit:**
- Lord Finesse filed a **$10 million lawsuit** against Mac Miller, his label Rostrum Records, and mixtape website DatPiff.com.
- At the time of the suit, *K.I.D.S.* had been downloaded more than **500,000 times**, streamed over **450,000 times**, and the "Kool Aid & Frozen Pizza" video had nearly **24 million YouTube views**.
- The case highlighted a legal gray area: mixtapes were traditionally understood as free promotional material where uncleared samples were tolerated, but Miller's mixtape had generated significant commercial value through YouTube ad revenue and career momentum.

**Resolution:**
- A confidential settlement was reached on **December 6, 2012**, following a settlement conference before U.S. Magistrate Judge Henry B. Pitman (Southern District of New York).
- The terms were **not disclosed** to anyone other than the presiding judge.

**Key Takeaway:** This case demonstrated that the "free mixtape" defense does not immunize artists from sample infringement claims, particularly when the work generates significant commercial attention and revenue through indirect channels (YouTube, streaming, career advancement).

### 8. DJ Shadow / "Endtroducing....." — Album Built Entirely from Samples

**The Album:**
- Released in 1996 on Mo' Wax Records, *Endtroducing.....* is recognized as one of the first albums composed **entirely from sampled material**.
- It entered the **Guinness Book of Records** as the first album made completely from samples.
- DJ Shadow (Josh Davis) used approximately **500 samples** across the album, sourced primarily from obscure vinyl records found through crate-digging.

**Clearance Economics:**
- By 1996, the post-Grand Upright legal landscape meant sample clearance was mandatory. Mo' Wax Records managed to clear the necessary samples without spending a fortune — though exact figures have not been publicly disclosed.
- DJ Shadow has spoken publicly about the economic impossibility of dense sampling: **"If you're clearing one sample out of 18, and they want 50% and everybody down the line wants 50%, then it's kind of like, 'Hang on guys, this isn't going to work!' It literally makes the art form illegal and dangerous."**
- Industry context for clearance costs: a deal for an under-ten-second sample by a major artist can run **over $50,000 for the master recording alone**, with additional publishing fees on top.

**How It Was Possible:**
- Many of the samples were drawn from **extremely obscure sources** — records found in dollar bins at flea markets and thrift stores — where rights holders were often difficult to locate or had minimal commercial leverage.
- The album was released on **Mo' Wax**, a UK-based independent label, which may have faced different legal and economic dynamics than a US major label release.
- The exact clearance strategy (which samples were cleared, which were deemed too obscure to pursue, etc.) has not been fully documented publicly.

### 9. Madlib / MF DOOM — "Madvillainy" and Crate-Digging Economics

**The Album:**
- *Madvillainy* (2004), released on Stones Throw Records, is a collaboration between producer Madlib and rapper MF DOOM under the name Madvillain.

**Production and Sampling Approach:**
- Madlib produced the album using minimal equipment: a **Boss SP-303 sampler**, a turntable, and a tape deck.
- Samples span an extraordinary range: from a 1942 film (*The Ghost of Frankenstein*) to 1960s-70s instrumentals from around the world, with a notable emphasis on **Bollywood film soundtracks**.
- **Crate-digging in Brazil:** Madlib traveled to Brazil and purchased multiple crates of vinyl records. He recorded **hundreds of beats in his hotel room**, many of which ended up on *Madvillainy*. He reportedly lost two of the crates he acquired. [single-source]

**Sample Clearance:**
- Madlib was asked to change certain instrumentals to address clearance concerns, but **in defiance, he lied about forgetting the samples in order to keep them** on the album. [single-source]
- This anecdote reflects the tension between artistic vision and clearance requirements on independent labels with limited budgets.
- Stones Throw Records, as an independent label, operated with far smaller clearance budgets than major labels, which likely influenced which samples were formally cleared and which were deemed low-risk due to obscurity.

### 10. De La Soul — Catalog Streaming Disputes with Tommy Boy Records

**The Dispute:**
- De La Soul's back catalog was **absent from streaming platforms for years** due to a dispute with Tommy Boy Records, their original label.
- In early 2019, Tommy Boy announced plans to bring the catalog to streaming, but De La Soul criticized the terms as **"unbalanced, unfair"** — the split would be roughly **90% Tommy Boy, 10% De La Soul**.
- De La Soul launched a **"Tommy Boycott" campaign** and received public support from artists like Nas and Questlove.

**Resolution:**
- After seven months of stalled negotiations, De La Soul cut ties with Tommy Boy in August 2019.
- By 2021, the group announced they had acquired ownership of their masters.
- On **March 3, 2023**, De La Soul's catalog finally debuted on streaming services.

**Why It Matters for Sampling:**
- De La Soul's *3 Feet High and Rising* (1989) is one of the most sample-dense albums in hip-hop history. The complexity and cost of clearing those samples for streaming was a significant factor in the catalog's long absence from digital platforms — a clearance burden compounded by the unfavorable revenue split.

### 11. Girl Talk (Gregg Gillis) — The Fair Use Argument

**The Artist:**
- Girl Talk (Gregg Gillis) creates densely layered mashup albums using **hundreds of unlicensed samples per album** — none of which have been formally cleared.

**The Fair Use Argument:**
- Gillis relies on copyright law's **"fair use" doctrine**, arguing that his meticulous sampling, pitch-shifting, and editing create works that "surpass the original elements" and constitute **transformative use**.
- Fair use is determined by four factors: (1) purpose and character of use, (2) nature of the copyrighted work, (3) amount used, and (4) effect on the market for the original.

**Why He's Never Been Sued:**
- Despite using hundreds of unlicensed samples, **no rights holder has ever sued Girl Talk**.
- Multiple theories explain this:
  1. **Fear of adverse precedent:** A court ruling in Gillis's favor could establish broad fair use protections for sampling, which rights holders want to avoid.
  2. **No market harm:** Girl Talk's music arguably drives listeners to discover the original sampled works rather than substituting for them.
  3. **Public/critical support:** The positive reception makes a lawsuit a PR risk for any plaintiff.
- Gillis stated: **"We believed in what we were doing"** — but acknowledged that fair use can only be determined by a court.

### 12. Three 6 Mafia / UGK — Southern Sample Chains

**"Sippin' on Some Syrup" as Sample Chain Example:**
- Three 6 Mafia's "Sippin' on Some Syrup" (2000, featuring UGK and Project Pat) samples the instrumental from **Marvin Gaye's 1978 "Is That Enough"** from the album *Here, My Dear*.
- The track itself has been **extensively re-sampled by subsequent artists**, creating a downstream sample chain where the Three 6 Mafia track becomes source material for the next generation.

**Broader Southern Sampling Culture:**
- Three 6 Mafia (DJ Paul and Juicy J) drew heavily from **soul, funk, and horror film soundtracks**, with Willie Hutch being a frequently sampled source.
- Their production approach — looping soul/funk samples over drum machine patterns — became foundational to Memphis and Southern hip-hop production.
- The downstream sampling of their work demonstrates how a single cleared (or uncleared) sample can generate chains of derivative works, each requiring their own clearance negotiations.

**Sources:**

### 13. J Dilla — Legacy, "Donuts," and Sample Provenance

**The Album:**
- *Donuts* (2006) is an instrumental hip-hop album containing **31 tracks built from approximately 34 primary samples** drawn from rock, jazz, and soul.
- Released on **February 7, 2006** (J Dilla's 32nd birthday) on Stones Throw Records — just **three days before his death** on February 10, 2006.
- Almost every track uses a single song (usually soul/funk/disco) as its structural sample, with additional lyric samples layered on top. Only two tracks contain no lyric samples.

**Estate and Posthumous Rights:**
- J Dilla's estate has faced legal challenges. In September 2020, **Music Sales Corporation** (which had purchased 10cc's intellectual property in 2019) sued the Dilla estate over samples. This has been characterized by some commentators as potential "sample trolling" — acquiring catalogs specifically to pursue infringement claims. [single-source for "sample trolling" characterization]
- The estate's management has been described as eventually resolving these issues, with Dilla's family now receiving fair compensation for his work.

**Legacy and Influence:**
- *Donuts* is widely regarded as one of the most influential hip-hop albums ever made, with the **"Sampling Donuts" project** (samplingdonuts.com) providing exhaustive documentation of every sample used.
- The album demonstrates how sample-based production at its highest level creates entirely new works from existing material — raising fundamental questions about the nature of musical authorship that sampling law continues to grapple with.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### Sample Clearance Economics (General)
- Two licenses are required: **master recording license** (from the label owning the recording) and **composition/publishing license** (from the songwriter/publisher).
- Costs are negotiated case-by-case. Range: from a few thousand dollars to six figures per sample, plus ongoing royalty percentages.
- Re-playing (interpolating) a sample eliminates the master recording license requirement but still requires a publishing license.
- Artists with leverage (Kanye, major label acts) face inflated rates; obscure samples from unknown artists can sometimes be cleared cheaply or go unchallenged.

### Key Legal Precedents Referenced
1. **Grand Upright Music v. Warner Bros. (1991)** — Established that sampling without clearance = infringement
2. **Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films (2005)** — Sixth Circuit ruled that even minimal sampling of a sound recording requires a license ("Get a license or do not sample")
3. **VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone (2016)** — Ninth Circuit disagreed with Bridgeport, holding that de minimis sampling of sound recordings may be permissible

### The Clearance Paradox
- Dense, creative sampling (Bomb Squad, DJ Shadow, De La Soul) became economically nonviable after 1991.
- Artists responded by: (a) using fewer samples, (b) replaying samples, (c) sampling obscure sources, (d) operating on independent labels with higher risk tolerance, or (e) ignoring clearance requirements and accepting legal risk (Kanye's Vultures approach).
