## 1. LANDMARK SAMPLING COURT CASES

### 1.1 Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc. (1991)

- **Citation:** 780 F. Supp. 182 (S.D.N.Y. 1991)
- **Court:** United States District Court, Southern District of New York
- **Judge:** Kevin Thomas Duffy
- **Decided:** December 17, 1991
- **Parties:** Gilbert O'Sullivan (through Grand Upright Music, Ltd.) v. Biz Markie (Marcel Hall) and Warner Bros. Records Inc.
- **Facts:** Rapper Biz Markie sampled a portion of Gilbert O'Sullivan's 1972 hit "Alone Again (Naturally)" for his track "Alone Again" on his third album, *I Need a Haircut*. Warner Bros. had contacted O'Sullivan's representatives to obtain a license for the sample but were denied permission. Warner Bros. released the album containing the sample anyway.
- **Ruling:** Judge Duffy granted a preliminary injunction against the defendants, halting further distribution of the album. The opinion opened with the biblical admonition: **"Thou shalt not steal."** — the only legal authority cited being the Seventh Commandment.
- **Criminal Referral:** Judge Duffy took the extraordinary step of referring the matter to the United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York for potential criminal prosecution under 17 U.S.C. § 506(a) (criminal copyright infringement) and 18 U.S.C. § 2319 (criminal penalties for infringement). No criminal charges were ultimately filed.
- **The court stated:** "Defendants' . . . callous disregard for the law and for the rights of others requires not only the preliminary injunction sought by the plaintiff but also sterner measures."
- **Impact:** This was the first federal court ruling to directly establish that unauthorized digital sampling constitutes copyright infringement. It fundamentally transformed the hip-hop industry by making sample clearance a mandatory step in music production. Warner Bros. pulled *I Need a Haircut* from stores.
- **Legal criticism:** The opinion has been widely criticized by legal scholars for its brevity, lack of legal analysis, and failure to apply established copyright doctrines such as fair use or de minimis use. Despite this, it created a powerful chilling effect on sampling.
- **Sources:** [Justia — 780 F. Supp. 182](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/FSupp/780/182/1445286/); [GWU Music Copyright Infringement Resource](https://blogs.law.gwu.edu/mcir/case/grand-upright-v-warner/); [Hastings Law Journal, Vol. 45, Issue 2](https://repository.uclawsf.edu/hastings_law_journal/vol45/iss2/4/)

### 1.2 Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc. (1994)

- **Citation:** 510 U.S. 569 (1994)
- **Court:** Supreme Court of the United States
- **Docket No.:** 92-1292
- **Opinion Author:** Justice David Souter (unanimous on core holding; Kennedy concurred separately)
- **Decided:** March 7, 1994
- **Parties:** Luther Campbell (a/k/a Luke Skyywalker) et al. (2 Live Crew) v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc.
- **Facts:** 2 Live Crew's 1989 song "Pretty Woman" was a parody of Roy Orbison's 1964 classic "Oh, Pretty Woman" (written by Orbison and William Dees). 2 Live Crew's manager had contacted Acuff-Rose Music, the copyright holder, to request a license for the parody. Acuff-Rose refused, but 2 Live Crew released the song anyway, crediting Orbison and Dees as songwriters.
- **Ruling:** The Supreme Court reversed the Sixth Circuit (which had held that the commercial nature of the parody barred fair use) and remanded for further proceedings. The Court held that:
  - A commercial parody can qualify as fair use under 17 U.S.C. § 107.
  - The commercial nature of a work is only one factor in the four-factor fair use analysis, not a presumption against fair use.
  - **Transformative use** is central to the first fair use factor: "the more transformative the new work, the less will be the significance of other factors, like commercialism."
  - Parody has "an obvious claim to transformative value" because it can provide social benefit by commenting on the original.
  - The amount of the original used must be evaluated in light of the purpose of the use — a parody must be able to "conjure up" the original.
- **Impact on sampling law:** While this case involved parody rather than sampling per se, it established the transformative use framework that has been central to fair use analysis in subsequent music copyright cases. It remains the Supreme Court's most significant statement on fair use in the music context.
- **Sources:** [510 U.S. 569 (Justia)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/510/569/); [Cornell LII](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/html/92-1292.ZS.html); [Oyez](https://www.oyez.org/cases/1993/92-1292)

### 1.3 Newton v. Diamond (2004)

- **Citation:** 388 F.3d 1189 (9th Cir. 2004)
- **Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Ninth Circuit
- **Prior Opinion:** 349 F.3d 591 (9th Cir. 2003), amended on rehearing
- **Parties:** James W. Newton Jr. v. Michael Diamond, Adam Horovitz, Adam Yauch (Beastie Boys), Capitol Records, Inc.
- **Facts:** The Beastie Boys digitally sampled the opening six seconds of James Newton's performance of his composition "Choir" (1978) — a piece for flute and voice incorporating elements of African-American gospel, Japanese ceremonial music, traditional African music, and classical music. The six-second sample consisted of three notes (C, D-flat, C) with a held C note underneath. The Beastie Boys obtained a license from the sound recording copyright holder (ECM Records) to use the sample in their track "Pass the Mic" (1992), where the sample was looped over 40 times throughout the song. However, they did not obtain a license from Newton for the underlying musical composition.
- **Ruling:** The Ninth Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the Beastie Boys, holding that the use of the three-note sequence from Newton's composition was **de minimis** and therefore not actionable as copyright infringement.
- **Key Legal Distinction:** The case drew a critical line between **sound recording copyright** and **musical composition copyright.** The Beastie Boys had properly cleared the sound recording (master) copyright. The only question was whether the underlying musical composition — the notes themselves — was infringed. The court found the three-note sequence, taken in isolation from Newton's performance technique, was too trivial to constitute infringement of the composition.
- **Significance:** This case established that clearing the master recording does not automatically clear the composition, and vice versa. It also applied the de minimis doctrine to musical compositions in the sampling context — a doctrine that was later rejected for sound recordings (but not compositions) in *Bridgeport*.
- **Sources:** [388 F.3d 1189 (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/388/1189/569849/); [GWU Music Copyright Infringement Resource](https://blogs.law.gwu.edu/mcir/case/newton-v-diamond/)

### 1.4 Bridgeport Music, Inc. v. Dimension Films (2005)

- **Citation:** 410 F.3d 792 (6th Cir. 2005)
- **Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Sixth Circuit
- **Prior opinions:** 383 F.3d 390 (6th Cir. 2004) (original); 401 F.3d 647 (6th Cir. 2004) (amended)
- **Opinion Author:** Circuit Judge Ralph B. Guy Jr.
- **Panel:** Guy and Gilman, Circuit Judges; Barzilay, Judge
- **District Court:** Middle District of Tennessee, 230 F. Supp. 2d 830 (M.D. Tenn. 2002), Judge William J. Haynes Jr.
- **Parties:** Bridgeport Music, Inc., Westbound Records, Inc., and Southfield Music, Inc. v. Dimension Films et al.
- **Facts:** N.W.A.'s 1990 track "100 Miles and Runnin'" sampled a two-second guitar chord from Funkadelic's 1975 song "Get Off Your Ass and Jam." The sample was lowered in pitch, and the resulting sound was looped five times in the song. The track appeared on the soundtrack to the 1998 film *I Got the Hook Up*, distributed by Dimension Films. The district court had granted summary judgment to the defendants, finding the sample was de minimis.
- **Ruling:** The Sixth Circuit **reversed**, holding that **the de minimis defense does not apply to sound recordings**. The court created a bright-line rule: **"Get a license or do not sample."**
- **Statutory Interpretation:** The court relied on 17 U.S.C. § 114(b), which provides that the copyright in a sound recording "is not infringed by the duplication of another sound recording that consists entirely of an independent fixation of other sounds." The court interpreted this to mean that Congress intended to give sound recording owners an exclusive right over any unauthorized copying — no matter how small the portion taken.
- **Key Reasoning:** The court argued that: (1) a sound recording owner has the exclusive right to "sample" from the original recording, (2) even short samples take something of value, (3) the market for licensing samples is well-established, and (4) a bright-line rule provides clarity and avoids the difficult, subjective analysis of what constitutes de minimis use.
- **Important limitation:** The ruling applies only to **sound recordings**, not to **musical compositions**. The de minimis defense remains available for claims involving only the underlying composition.
- **Impact:** This ruling made the Sixth Circuit (covering Michigan, Ohio, Kentucky, and Tennessee — including Nashville, a major music industry center) the strictest jurisdiction for sampling cases. Any unauthorized use of even a fraction of a second of a copyrighted sound recording constitutes infringement.
- **Criticism:** The opinion was widely criticized by legal scholars, musicians, and First Amendment advocates for being overly rigid, discouraging creativity, and misinterpreting the Copyright Act.
- **Sources:** [410 F.3d 792 (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/410/792/574458/); [Harvard Cyber Law (abridged opinion)](https://cyber.harvard.edu/people/tfisher/IP/2005%20Bridgeport%20Abridged.pdf); [GWU MCIR](https://blogs.law.gwu.edu/mcir/case/bridgeport-music-v-dimension-films-et-al/)

### 1.5 VMG Salsoul, LLC v. Ciccone (2016)

- **Citation:** 824 F.3d 871 (9th Cir. 2016)
- **Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Ninth Circuit
- **Case No.:** 13-57104
- **Decided:** June 2, 2016
- **Panel:** Circuit Judges Susan P. Graber and Barry G. Silverman; District Judge David A. Ezra (sitting by designation)
- **Dissent:** Judge Silverman dissented
- **Parties:** VMG Salsoul, LLC v. Madonna Louise Ciccone, Shep Pettibone, WB Music Corporation, Webo Girl Publishing Inc., Lexor Music Inc., Warner Music Group, Warner Bros. Records Inc.
- **Facts:** VMG Salsoul alleged that producer Shep Pettibone copied a **0.23-second** segment of horns from the Salsoul Orchestra's 1983 track "Ooh I Love It (Love Break)" and used a modified version of that snippet when recording Madonna's 1990 hit "Vogue." The horn stab was allegedly altered and placed into the final mix.
- **Ruling:** The Ninth Circuit **affirmed** the district court's grant of summary judgment for the defendants, holding that even if the sample was copied, the use was **de minimis** — a reasonable listener would not recognize the brief snippet in "Vogue" as originating from "Love Break."
- **The Circuit Split:** The court **expressly declined to follow** the Sixth Circuit's bright-line rule from *Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films*. The majority held:
  - The de minimis exception applies to sound recordings just as it applies to all other copyright infringement actions.
  - Congress did not eliminate the de minimis doctrine for sound recordings when it enacted 17 U.S.C. § 114(b).
  - *Bridgeport*'s reasoning was "not persuasive" and its interpretation of § 114(b) was incorrect.
- **Dissent:** Judge Silverman would have followed *Bridgeport*, arguing that the majority's approach created uncertainty and that Congress intended to give sound recording owners broader protection.
- **Attorney's Fees:** The court vacated the district court's award of attorney's fees to the defendants, finding that the case was not so unreasonable as to warrant fee-shifting.
- **Supreme Court:** No petition for certiorari was filed in this case [single-source — based on absence from Supreme Court docket records found in research].
- **Impact:** This decision formalized a **circuit split** on sampling law. Artists in the Ninth Circuit (covering California, including Los Angeles — the other major music industry center) can assert a de minimis defense for sound recording samples. Artists in the Sixth Circuit cannot.
- **Sources:** [824 F.3d 871 (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/13-57104/13-57104-2016-06-02.html); [UCLA Journal of Law & Technology](https://uclajolt.com/circuit-split-de-minimis-sampling-from-copyrighted-recordings-after-vmg-salsoul-llc-v-ciccone/); [Venable LLP analysis](https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2016/06/emvmg-salsoul-llc-v-madonna-louise-ciccone-et-alem)

### 1.6 TufAmerica, Inc. v. Diamond (Beastie Boys) (2013)

- **Citation:** 968 F. Supp. 2d 588 (S.D.N.Y. 2013)
- **Court:** United States District Court, Southern District of New York
- **Case No.:** 12 Civ. 3529 (AJN)
- **Parties:** TufAmerica, Inc. v. Michael Diamond, Adam Horovitz (Beastie Boys), and various publishing companies
- **Facts:** TufAmerica, a licensee of the Washington, D.C. funk band Trouble Funk's copyrighted songs, sued the Beastie Boys for copyright infringement, claiming five Beastie Boys songs contained unauthorized samples from four Trouble Funk recordings.
- **Ruling:** The district court granted summary judgment to the Beastie Boys, holding that **TufAmerica lacked standing** to sue. Only two of Trouble Funk's three co-owners had signed agreements purporting to grant TufAmerica exclusive licensing rights. Because all co-owners did not consent, TufAmerica held only a non-exclusive license, which does not confer standing to sue for infringement.
- **Separate TufAmerica v. Jay-Z Litigation:** TufAmerica also sued Jay-Z (Shawn Carter) over the track "Run This Town," alleging it sampled Trouble Funk's music. Jay-Z moved to dismiss.
- **Significance:** The case illustrates the importance of proper chain-of-title documentation in sampling disputes, and demonstrates how procedural issues (standing, licensing structure) can be dispositive before the court ever reaches the merits of the sampling claim.
- **Sources:** [Loeb & Loeb LLP analysis](https://www.loeb.com/en/insights/publications/2015/04/tufamerica-v-diamond); [GWU MCIR](https://blogs.law.gwu.edu/mcir/case/tufamerica-v-diamond-et-al-beastie-boys/); [Harvard JOLT](https://jolt.law.harvard.edu/digest/so-called-sample-troll-sues-jay-z-alleging-run-this-town-infringes-copyright)

### 1.7 Williams v. Gaye (Blurred Lines) (2015/2018)

- **Trial Verdict:** March 10, 2015 (C.D. Cal.)
- **Appeal:** Williams v. Gaye, 895 F.3d 1106 (9th Cir. 2018)
- **Parties:** Pharrell Williams, Robin Thicke, and Clifford Harris Jr. (T.I.) v. Bridgeport Music Inc. and the Estate of Marvin Gaye (Frankie Christian Gaye and Nona Marvisa Gaye)
- **Facts:** The Gaye estate alleged that the 2013 hit "Blurred Lines" (written by Pharrell Williams, Robin Thicke, and T.I.) infringed the copyright in Marvin Gaye's 1977 song "Got to Give It Up." The claim was based on the **musical composition** copyright (the sheet music deposit copy), not a direct sample of the sound recording.
- **Ruling:** The jury found infringement and awarded **$7.4 million** in damages. The trial judge later reduced this to **$5.3 million** and added a running royalty of 50% of future songwriting royalties. The Ninth Circuit **affirmed** in 2018.
- **Final damages:** In December 2019, Judge John A. Kronstadt ordered total damages of **$4.98 million** plus interest and 50% of future royalties from "Blurred Lines."
- **Note on sampling:** This was **not** a sampling case — no audio was directly taken from Gaye's recording. The case involved alleged copying of compositional elements (groove, feel, keyboard and bass patterns). However, it profoundly affected the sampling landscape because it expanded the scope of what courts might consider protectable musical expression, creating a chilling effect beyond traditional sampling.
- **Sources:** [895 F.3d 1106 (9th Circuit opinion)](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2018/03/21/15-56880.pdf); [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/pop-culture/music/robin-thicke-pharrell-williams-pay-5-million-marvin-gaye-estate-n947666); [Harvard JSEL](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/jsel/wp-content/uploads/sites/78/2019/02/HLS105.pdf)

## 2. THE PRE-GRAND UPRIGHT ERA: HIP-HOP'S "WILD WEST" OF SAMPLING

### 2.1 The Uncleared Sampling Era (circa 1979–1991)

- From the late 1970s through approximately 1991, hip-hop producers sampled freely from existing recordings with little or no concern for copyright clearance. Copyright law existed and applied in theory, but **no court had directly addressed digital sampling** in a published opinion.
- The practice was foundational to hip-hop. Early sampling was often done with rudimentary equipment — turntables, then drum machines (E-mu SP-1200, Akai MPC60), and early digital samplers. Producers built entire tracks from collages of existing recordings.
- **Key examples of prolific sampling in this era:**
  - James Brown's recordings — particularly the drum break from "Funky Drummer" (1970) — became among the most sampled sources in hip-hop history. By 1986, tracks like "South Bronx" (Boogie Down Productions), "Eric B. is President" (Eric B. & Rakim), and others had popularized sampling Brown's catalog.
  - Public Enemy's production team, the Bomb Squad (Hank Shocklee, Keith Shocklee, Eric "Vietnam" Sadler), pioneered a dense, layered approach using dozens of samples per track on albums like *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* (1988) and *Fear of a Black Planet* (1990).
  - The Beastie Boys' *Paul's Boutique* (1989) and De La Soul's *3 Feet High and Rising* (1989) represent the pinnacle of the uncleared sampling era.

### 2.2 De La Soul — *3 Feet High and Rising* and the Turtles Lawsuit (1989/1991)

- **Album:** *3 Feet High and Rising* (Tommy Boy Records, 1989) — De La Soul's debut album, produced primarily by Prince Paul. The album contained samples from dozens of sources.
- **The Lawsuit:** Former Turtles members **Howard Kaylan and Mark Volman** (who also performed as Flo & Eddie) sued De La Soul in **1991** over an unauthorized 12-second sample of the Turtles' 1969 song **"You Showed Me."** The sample appeared in an interstitial skit called "Transmitting Live from Mars."
- **Key Detail:** Many of the album's major, recognizable samples (from artists like Hall & Oates and Steely Dan) had been cleared. The smaller samples used in interstitial "skits" — including the Turtles sample — had not been.
- **Settlement:** The case was settled out of court. Kaylan and Volman reportedly received damages of **up to $1.7 million** from an initial claim of **$2.5 million**.
- **Volman's public statement:** "Sampling is just a longer term for theft."
- **Impact:** This settlement, along with the *Grand Upright* ruling the same year, sent a clear message to the hip-hop industry that uncleared sampling carried severe financial risk. The combination of these two events in 1991 is widely regarded as the end of hip-hop's uncleared sampling era.
- **Sources:** [Rolling Stone — "Songs on Trial: 12 Landmark Music Copyright Cases"](https://www.rollingstone.com/politics/politics-lists/songs-on-trial-12-landmark-music-copyright-cases-166396/de-la-soul-vs-the-turtles-1991-61686/); [Briffa Legal](https://www.briffa.com/blog/classic-copyright-cases-de-la-soul/); [Library of Congress National Recording Preservation Board](https://www.loc.gov/static/programs/national-recording-preservation-board/documents/3-Feet-High-and-Rising.pdf)

### 2.3 Beastie Boys — *Paul's Boutique* (1989)

- **Album:** *Paul's Boutique* (Capitol Records, 1989), produced by the Dust Brothers (Michael Simpson and John King).
- **Sample Count:** The album contains approximately **105 identifiable samples** (some estimates range from 100 to 300 depending on methodology). The final track alone, "B-Boy Bouillabaisse," contains approximately **24 individual samples**.
- **Clearance Costs at the Time:** The Beastie Boys reportedly spent over **$250,000** on sample clearances for the album — an extraordinary sum for the late 1980s. However, many samples were cleared "easily and affordably," according to Sound on Sound, because the market for sample licensing was not yet established and rights holders had not yet recognized the value of their catalogs in this context.
- **Could it be made today?** This is one of the most frequently asked questions in music law. The consensus among music attorneys and industry commentators is **no** — or at least not at any reasonable cost. Estimates for clearing all samples under modern licensing conditions range from **$300,000 to $500,000** or more, and many rights holders would likely refuse to license at any price. The album's dense, collage-like approach to sampling is economically infeasible under the post-*Grand Upright*, post-*Bridgeport* legal regime.
- **Sources:** [Paul's Boutique Samples & References](https://paulsboutique.info/); [Wikipedia — Paul's Boutique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique); [The Vinyl Factory](https://www.thevinylfactory.com/features/beastie-boys-pauls-boutique-samples)

### 2.4 The Shift from Uncleared to Cleared Sampling

- **Production cost impact:** After *Grand Upright* (1991) and the Turtles settlement (1991), the cost of hip-hop production that relied on sampling increased dramatically. Sample clearance became a mandatory budget item, requiring negotiations with both the **master recording** owner (typically a label) and the **composition** owner (typically a publisher).
- **Creative impact:** Producers adapted in several ways:
  - **Reduced sampling:** Many producers shifted to using fewer, more carefully chosen samples that could be cleared at reasonable cost.
  - **Original production:** Producers increasingly created original beats using synthesizers, drum machines, and live instrumentation. The rise of West Coast G-funk (Dr. Dre's *The Chronic*, 1992) and Southern hip-hop styles relied more on original composition and less on multi-layered sampling.
  - **Interpolation:** Rather than sampling the original recording, producers began re-recording recognizable musical elements, requiring clearance only from the composition copyright holder (not the master recording owner). See Section 5.
  - **Chopping and manipulation:** Producers developed techniques to alter samples so heavily (pitch-shifting, time-stretching, reversing, layering) that they became unrecognizable, hoping to avoid detection or to argue de minimis use.
- **Industry-wide chilling effect:** The WNYC Studios *On the Media* series documented how "free sampling was banned, and all samples now had to be cleared by the labels of the music getting sampled, and in many cases royalties had to be paid." The primary upshot was that "the practice's creative potential" was constrained.
- **Sources:** [WNYC Studios — On the Media](https://www.wnycstudios.org/podcasts/otm/episodes/hip-hop-sampling-on-the-media); [GRAMMY.com](https://www.grammy.com/news/10-most-controversial-hip-hop-samples-history-lawsuits-videos)

## 3. THE CIRCUIT SPLIT: BRIDGEPORT vs. VMG SALSOUL

### 3.1 The Two Competing Standards

The United States currently operates under **two contradictory legal standards** for digital sampling of sound recordings, depending on which federal circuit the case is heard in:

| | **Sixth Circuit (Bridgeport, 2005)** | **Ninth Circuit (VMG Salsoul, 2016)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Rule** | No de minimis defense for sound recordings. Any unauthorized sampling — no matter how brief or unrecognizable — constitutes infringement. | De minimis defense applies to sound recordings just as to all other copyrighted works. |
| **Standard** | "Get a license or do not sample." | Would a reasonable audience recognize the appropriation? If not, no infringement. |
| **Statutory basis** | 17 U.S.C. § 114(b) — interpreted as granting sound recording owners exclusive rights over any reproduction. | § 114(b) interpreted as addressing only situations where an entire recording is re-recorded, not as eliminating general copyright defenses. |
| **Geographic coverage** | Michigan, Ohio, Kentucky, Tennessee (includes Nashville) | California, Oregon, Washington, Arizona, Nevada, Hawaii, Alaska, Idaho, Montana (includes Los Angeles) |
| **Practical effect** | Absolute liability for any unauthorized sample of a sound recording. | Liability only if the sample is recognizable to the average listener. |

### 3.2 Why the Supreme Court Has Not Resolved It

- As of March 2026, the Supreme Court has **not granted certiorari** in any case presenting the circuit split on sampling and the de minimis doctrine for sound recordings.
- No certiorari petition appears to have been filed in *VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone* itself [single-source — based on absence from Supreme Court docket records].
- **Possible reasons the Court has not acted:**
  - The split is relatively narrow — it affects a specific subset of copyright cases (sound recording sampling) rather than a broad doctrinal question.
  - The music industry has largely adapted by either clearing samples or avoiding sampling in jurisdictions with strict rules.
  - The Court may be waiting for a case with a more compelling factual record or one where the circuit split directly determined the outcome.
  - The issue has not generated the volume of litigation that typically draws Supreme Court attention, in part because the chilling effect of *Bridgeport* has discouraged uncleared sampling in the Sixth Circuit.

### 3.3 Practical Impact on Artists

- **Forum shopping:** The circuit split creates incentives for strategic forum selection. A plaintiff seeking strict liability for sampling may prefer to file in the Sixth Circuit; a defendant may seek the Ninth Circuit's more permissive standard.
- **Risk calculus:** Artists and labels based in Los Angeles (Ninth Circuit) face a lower legal risk when using brief, unrecognizable samples than those based in Nashville (Sixth Circuit).
- **Industry practice:** Despite the Ninth Circuit's more permissive rule, the music industry's standard practice remains to clear all samples before release. The risk of litigation — and the uncertainty created by the circuit split — makes this the safest approach regardless of jurisdiction.
- **Sources:** [UCLA Journal of Law & Technology](https://uclajolt.com/circuit-split-de-minimis-sampling-from-copyrighted-recordings-after-vmg-salsoul-llc-v-ciccone/); [Washington University Law Review, Vol. 95, Issue 1](https://openscholarship.wustl.edu/law_lawreview/vol95/iss1/9/); [Drexel Law Review, Vol. 10, No. 2](https://drexel.edu/~/media/Files/law/law%20review/v10-2/Burns%2010%20Drexel%20L%20Rev%20445.ashx)

## 4. INTERNATIONAL SAMPLING LAW

### 4.1 Kraftwerk v. Pelham — "Metall auf Metall" (Germany / EU, 1999–ongoing)

This is the world's longest-running sampling lawsuit, spanning over two decades and involving multiple national and supranational courts.

- **Parties:** Ralf Hütter and Florian Schneider-Esleben (of Kraftwerk) v. Moses Pelham and Martin Haas (hip-hop producers)
- **Facts:** Pelham and Haas sampled a **two-second rhythmic sequence** from Kraftwerk's 1977 track "Metall auf Metall" and looped it as the beat for rapper Sabrina Setlur's 1997 song "Nur Mir." No license was obtained.
- **Litigation timeline:**
  - **1999:** Kraftwerk filed suit in Germany.
  - **2008:** German Federal Court of Justice (BGH) ruled in favor of Kraftwerk (I ZR 112/06).
  - **2012:** German Federal Constitutional Court (BVerfG) ruled that the BGH had insufficiently considered Pelham's artistic freedom under the German Basic Law, and remanded the case.
  - **2017:** BGH referred questions to the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU), Case C-476/17.
  - **July 29, 2019:** The CJEU ruled in favor of Kraftwerk, holding that phonogram producers have the **"exclusive right to authorize or prohibit reproduction in whole or in part"** of their recordings. However, the CJEU held that sampling a sound recording in a **modified and unrecognizable form** does not constitute infringement.
  - **April 30, 2020:** The BGH delivered its ruling in "Metall auf Metall IV" (I ZR 115/16), finding that post-InfoSoc Directive, the two-second sample constituted infringement and did not fall within the scope of an exception.
  - **Post-2020:** The case was remanded again to the Higher Regional Court of Hamburg (OLG Hamburg), which examined whether the **"pastiche" exception** (Section 51a of the German Copyright Act, UrhG — implementing the InfoSoc and DSM Directives) could apply. As of 2023, the pastiche question was referred back to the CJEU for further clarification.
  - **As of March 2026,** the case has produced approximately **ten separate judicial opinions** over 27 years and is **not yet finally resolved** [single-source — based on available reporting].
- **Key CJEU Principles:**
  - Sampling that produces an **unrecognizable** result does not infringe the reproduction right (similar to the Ninth Circuit's de minimis approach).
  - Sampling that produces a **recognizable** copy without authorization infringes the reproduction right.
  - The "free use" exception in German copyright law was found to be incompatible with broader EU law as a standalone defense.
  - The "pastiche" and "quotation" exceptions may provide limited defenses but remain subject to further interpretation.
- **Sources:** [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/2019/07/31/kraftwerk-sampling-case-eu-top-court-rules-on-20-year-dispute-over-two-second-clip); [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/articles/business/8524267/kraftwerk-european-court-justice-ruling-metall-auf-metall); [Kluwer Copyright Blog](https://copyrightblog.kluweriplaw.com/2023/09/21/the-next-chapter-in-the-metall-auf-metall-saga-pastiche-to-be-clarified-by-the-cjeu/); [Lexology](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=cf43a4c3-0b7d-43b2-ae6a-1128ece3db88)

### 4.2 International Legal Framework

- **Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (1886, revised):** Establishes minimum standards for copyright protection among signatory nations. Applies the principle of "national treatment" — works are protected in each signatory country under that country's own copyright laws.
- **WIPO Copyright Treaty (1996):** Modernizes the Berne Convention for the digital age.
- **TRIPS Agreement (1994):** Sets minimum intellectual property standards for World Trade Organization member states.
- **Practical effect:** There is no uniform international standard for sampling. Each country applies its own copyright framework. The Berne Convention guarantees that foreign works receive the same protection as domestic works, but the scope of that protection (including exceptions like de minimis or fair dealing) varies by jurisdiction.

### 4.3 United Kingdom

- The UK has **no statutory "fair use" doctrine** equivalent to 17 U.S.C. § 107. Instead, it has **"fair dealing"** exceptions, which are narrower and limited to specific purposes (criticism, review, quotation, reporting current events, parody, pastiche, or caricature, research or private study).
- **No settled UK case law on sampling:** As of the date of this research, **no UK court has delivered a final ruling in a music sampling copyright case.** The specific boundaries of fair dealing as applied to sampling remain untested in UK courts [single-source — based on academic analysis by Julie Ewald and Paul G. Oliver].
- **Potential defenses under UK law:**
  - **Quotation (Section 30(1ZA) CDPA 1988):** Use of a quotation is permitted if fair dealing, the work has been made available to the public, the quotation is no more than required, and sufficient acknowledgement is given.
  - **Pastiche (Section 30A CDPA 1988):** Fair dealing for the purpose of caricature, parody, or pastiche does not infringe. This defense was introduced by the Copyright and Rights in Performances (Quotation and Parody) Regulations 2014 (SI 2014/2356).
- **Sources:** [Lawdit Music](https://www.lawditmusic.co.uk/music-sampling-and-fair-use-when-does-inspiration-become-infringement/); [University of Warwick Law School](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/soc/law/aboutus/wulj/article_10.pdf); [SSRN — Ewald & Oliver](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2898820)

### 4.4 Cross-Border Sampling Disputes

- Cross-border sampling disputes are governed by the jurisdiction where the allegedly infringing work is distributed. Under the Berne Convention's national treatment principle, a sampling dispute involving (for example) an American recording sampled in a French production would be adjudicated under French copyright law if the suit were brought in France.
- The rise of global digital distribution (streaming platforms) has complicated jurisdictional questions, since a song containing a sample is simultaneously distributed in every jurisdiction where the streaming platform operates.
- **Sources:** [Inter-American Law Review — University of Miami](https://inter-american-law-review.law.miami.edu/international-copyright-law-sampling-united-states-refusal-enforce-countrys-copyright-laws/); [Anthology: Inside Intellectual Property Rights (UPH)](https://ojs.uph.edu/index.php/Anthology/article/view/9329)

## 5. THE "INTERPOLATION" ALTERNATIVE

### 5.1 Sampling vs. Interpolation: Legal Definitions

- **Sampling:** Taking a portion of the **actual sound recording** (the master) and incorporating it into a new work. Requires clearance from **both** the sound recording copyright holder (typically the record label) **and** the musical composition copyright holder (typically the publisher/songwriter).
- **Interpolation:** **Re-recording** or re-performing a recognizable element of an existing musical composition — the melody, chord progression, lyric, or arrangement — using new musicians and new recording equipment. The original sound recording is not used. Requires clearance **only** from the **composition copyright holder** (publisher/songwriter), **not** the sound recording owner (label).
- **Cover songs (compulsory license):** Under 17 U.S.C. § 115, anyone may record and distribute a cover of a previously released song by obtaining a compulsory mechanical license at the statutory rate (currently **12 cents per copy** for songs over 5 minutes, **9.1 cents per copy** for songs 5 minutes or under, as of January 1, 2024). This license does **not** require the copyright holder's consent. However, the compulsory license applies only to faithful reproductions — not to substantially altered versions. An interpolation that significantly transforms the original composition falls outside the compulsory license and requires direct negotiation.

### 5.2 Why Artists Increasingly Interpolate Instead of Sampling

- **Reduced clearance complexity:** Interpolation eliminates the need to negotiate with the sound recording owner (the label), which often demands higher fees and a larger share of royalties.
- **Cost:** Sample clearance for master recordings can involve substantial upfront fees (often **$5,000–$100,000+** depending on the prominence of the source material) plus ongoing royalties (often 15–50% of the new song's publishing or income). Interpolation requires only a publishing license, which is typically negotiated at a lower cost.
- **Control:** Labels that own master recordings may refuse to license a sample at any price, or may demand co-ownership of the new recording. Publishers are generally more willing to negotiate interpolation licenses because they earn income from the use of their compositions.
- **Legal certainty:** After *Bridgeport*, using even a fraction of a second of a sound recording in the Sixth Circuit carries strict liability for infringement. Interpolation avoids this risk entirely because no portion of the original sound recording is used.
- **Dr. Dre as pioneer:** The rise of interpolation as a production technique is closely associated with Dr. Dre, who — rather than sampling — frequently hired musicians to replay recognizable musical elements for his productions (notably on *The Chronic*, 1992, and *2001*, 1999). This approach allowed him to capture the sonic flavor of the original while paying only composition royalties.

### 5.3 The Two Copyrights in Music

Under U.S. law, a single song embodies **two distinct copyrights:**

1. **The Musical Composition (publishing):** The underlying song — melody, harmony, lyrics, arrangement. Owned by the songwriter(s) and/or their publisher. Registered with the U.S. Copyright Office (typically as a PA registration).
2. **The Sound Recording (master):** The specific recorded performance of the composition. Owned by the performer(s) and/or their record label. Registered with the U.S. Copyright Office (typically as an SR registration).

These two copyrights have **different owners, different licensing requirements, and different legal standards:**

| | **Composition** | **Sound Recording** |
|---|---|---|
| **Typical owner** | Songwriter / publisher | Artist / record label |
| **Copyright symbol** | © | ℗ |
| **Sampling requires clearance from** | Yes (both sampling and interpolation) | Yes (sampling only; not interpolation) |
| **De minimis defense available?** | Yes (all circuits) | Yes (9th Cir.) / No (6th Cir.) |
| **Compulsory license available?** | Yes, for cover recordings (§ 115) | No |

### 5.4 Cases Where Interpolation Was Disputed

- **Williams v. Gaye (Blurred Lines, 2015/2018):** Though not technically an interpolation dispute, the case centered on whether "Blurred Lines" impermissibly copied compositional elements (groove, feel, keyboard patterns) from "Got to Give It Up" — precisely the kind of elements that would be at issue in an interpolation dispute. The $5.3 million verdict demonstrated that **interpolation does not provide a safe harbor** if the resulting composition is substantially similar to the original.
- **Olivia Rodrigo / Taylor Swift (2021):** Olivia Rodrigo retroactively added Taylor Swift as a co-writer on "Deja Vu" and "1 Step Forward, 3 Steps Back" from her debut album *SOUR* (2021) after observers noted melodic similarities to Swift songs. While not a formal lawsuit, this illustrates how interpolation-like disputes are often resolved through credit and royalty agreements rather than litigation [single-source].
- **The general principle:** An interpolation can still constitute copyright infringement of the underlying composition if the re-recorded elements are **substantially similar** to the original. The fact that no master recording was sampled does not protect against a claim of compositional infringement.
- **Sources:** [U.S. Copyright Office — Sampling, Interpolations, Beat Stores and More](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/educational-materials/Sampling-Interpolations-Beat-Stores-and-More-An-Introduction-for-Musicians-Using-Preexisting.pdf); [Harvard JSEL — "When Gravy Meets Roll"](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/jsel/2023/04/when-gravy-meets-roll-the-future-of-interpolation-in-music-recording/); [NYU JIPEL](https://jipel.law.nyu.edu/the-art-of-sampling-and-copyright-infringement-how-do-we-strike-a-balance-between-the-two/)

## 6. KEY STATUTORY PROVISIONS

For reference, the primary U.S. statutory provisions governing music sampling:

- **17 U.S.C. § 106:** Exclusive rights of copyright owners (reproduction, distribution, public performance, etc.)
- **17 U.S.C. § 107:** Fair use defense (four-factor test)
- **17 U.S.C. § 114(b):** Scope of exclusive rights in sound recordings — the provision at the center of the Bridgeport/VMG Salsoul circuit split. Provides that the copyright in a sound recording "is not infringed by the duplication of another sound recording that consists entirely of an independent fixation of other sounds, even though such sounds imitate or simulate those in the copyrighted sound recording."
- **17 U.S.C. § 115:** Compulsory license for making and distributing phonorecords of nondramatic musical compositions
- **17 U.S.C. § 506(a):** Criminal copyright infringement
- **18 U.S.C. § 2319:** Criminal penalties for copyright infringement

## 7. SUMMARY TIMELINE

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1970s–1980s | Hip-hop producers sample freely; no court has addressed digital sampling |
| 1989 | De La Soul's *3 Feet High and Rising* and Beastie Boys' *Paul's Boutique* released — peak of the uncleared sampling era |
| 1991 | **Grand Upright Music v. Warner Bros.** — Judge Duffy rules sampling requires permission; "Thou shalt not steal." Turtles members sue De La Soul; settled for reportedly up to $1.7M |
| 1992 | Dr. Dre's *The Chronic* pioneers interpolation-heavy production as an alternative to sampling |
| 1994 | **Campbell v. Acuff-Rose** — Supreme Court rules commercial parody can be fair use; establishes transformative use framework |
| 1999 | Kraftwerk files suit against Pelham in Germany — beginning of 27-year (and counting) "Metall auf Metall" litigation |
| 2004 | **Newton v. Diamond** — 9th Circuit holds three-note sample from composition is de minimis |
| 2005 | **Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films** — 6th Circuit eliminates de minimis defense for sound recordings: "Get a license or do not sample" |
| 2013 | **TufAmerica v. Diamond** — Court dismisses sampling claim against Beastie Boys on standing grounds |
| 2015 | **Williams v. Gaye** jury verdict — $7.4M for "Blurred Lines" compositional similarities to Marvin Gaye |
| 2016 | **VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone** — 9th Circuit preserves de minimis defense for sound recordings, creating circuit split with Bridgeport |
| 2019 | CJEU rules in favor of Kraftwerk: recognizable samples infringe, unrecognizable ones do not |
| 2024 | Sony v. Trefuego ($803K damages); Kanye West faces multiple sampling suits over *Vultures 2* |
| 2026 | Circuit split remains unresolved; Supreme Court has not granted certiorari on the issue |
