## 1. The Mechanical Licensing Gap (Pre-MMA)

### 1.1 How Streaming Services Operated Without Proper Mechanical Licenses

Before the Music Modernization Act (Pub. L. 115-264, signed October 11, 2018), streaming services were required to obtain mechanical licenses on a song-by-song basis under Section 115 of the Copyright Act. When they could not identify a song's copyright owner, they were supposed to file a Notice of Intent (NOI) with the U.S. Copyright Office and pay royalties into escrow.

In practice, streaming services filed over one million "address unknown" NOIs with the Copyright Office, which released them from the obligation to pay mechanical royalties for those works. This created a systemic gap: services streamed compositions at scale without compensating songwriters or publishers.

- Source: [U.S. Copyright Office, Music Modernization FAQ](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/faq.html)
- Source: [Songtrust, "What is a Notice of Intent?"](https://blog.songtrust.com/what-is-a-notice-of-intent)
- Source: [Mayer Brown, "The Music Modernization Act: What Licensee Services Need to Know"](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/perspectives-events/publications/2018/10/the-music-modernization-act-what-licensee-services)

### 1.4 The "Black Box" of Unmatched Royalties

Under the MMA, 20 digital music providers (DMPs) transferred a combined $424.4 million in historical unmatched royalties to the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC). (This figure was subsequently revised to approximately $397 million after the CRB finalized rates applicable to unmatched usages during the Phonorecords III rate period.)

- Source: [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/pro/mechanical-licensing-collective-receives-over-424-million-unmatched-royalties-streaming-services/)
- Source: [The MLC, Historical Unmatched Royalties](https://www.themlc.com/historical-unmatched-royalties)

### 1.5 Has the MLC Fixed the Problem?

**Progress:**
- By early 2024, the MLC had matched and distributed more than $25 million in historical royalties from the Phonorecords I and II periods (more than 85% of Phono I royalties and more than 45% of Phono II royalties).
- The MLC began distributing matched historical royalties for Phonorecords III-period usages in April 2024.

**Ongoing concerns:**
- Hundreds of millions of dollars in unmatched royalties remain. Under the MMA, unmatched royalties that remain unclaimed for three years can be distributed to music publishers on a market-share basis.
- Independent songwriters and small publishers have criticized this provision, arguing that major publishers -- which have the best metadata systems and therefore the lowest unmatched rates -- would be the primary beneficiaries of market-share distribution at the expense of indie creators.
- The U.S. Copyright Office issued regulations recommending that the initial market-share distribution of unmatched mechanical royalties not occur for at least five years, and that the five-year period should not start until the MLC's claim portal is fully functioning.
- The MMA shifted the burden of registration onto songwriters, publishers, and producers, and constrained the ability of copyright holders to sue digital services for infringement for lawsuits filed after January 1, 2018.

- Source: [The MLC Blog](https://blog.themlc.com/press-releases-beta/billboard-mlc-receives-over-424m-unmatched-black-box-streaming-royalties)

## 2. Safe Harbor Abuse

### 2.1 Section 512 DMCA Safe Harbor: Original Intent vs. Current Use

The DMCA safe harbor provisions (17 U.S.C. Section 512) were enacted in 1998 to protect online service providers (OSPs) from liability for user-uploaded infringing content, provided they meet certain conditions: adopting a repeat infringer policy, accommodating standard technical measures, and responding to takedown notices.

**U.S. Copyright Office Section 512 Study (May 21, 2020):**
- Title: "Section 512 of Title 17"
- Key finding: The safe harbor system is "unbalanced" -- the balance has "shifted almost uniformly in favor of online service providers to the detriment of rightsholders."
- The study noted a "value gap" between the value some user-upload services extract from creative content and the revenue they return to creators.
- Areas identified as out of sync with Congress's original intent: eligibility qualifications, repeat infringer policies, knowledge standards, notice specificity, subpoenas, and injunctions.
- The Office recommended targeted fine-tuning rather than wholesale changes.
- Source: [U.S. Copyright Office, Section 512 Study](https://www.copyright.gov/policy/section512/)
- Source: [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/copyright-office-issues-report-dmca-safe-harbors-section-512-unbalanced)
- Source: [Venable LLP](https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2020/07/dmca-512-report-key-findings)

### 2.2 The "Value Gap"

YouTube pays approximately $0.00069 per stream, while Apple Music pays approximately $0.01 and TIDAL approximately $0.01284 per stream. YouTube's rate is roughly one-seventh of what other interactive streaming services pay, according to industry figures.

YouTube's safe harbor classification as a user-upload platform allows it to negotiate lower rates than services that directly license content, even though YouTube functions as a de facto music streaming service.

- Source: [Royalty Exchange](https://royaltyexchange.com/blog/how-music-streaming-platforms-calculate-payouts-per-stream-2025)
- Source: [Digital Music News](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2025/10/23/youtube-music-struggle-against-spotify-apple-music-amazon-music/)

### 2.3 YouTube Content ID: Voluntary, Not a Legal Obligation

Content ID is YouTube's proprietary, voluntary content identification system. It is not required by the DMCA. Key facts:

- Google only approves Content ID for accounts with a "substantial body of original material that's frequently uploaded by the YouTube user community" -- currently approximately 9,000 accounts.
- Google touts that 98% of copyright issues are caught via Content ID, but Content ID matches do not trigger copyright strikes under YouTube's repeat infringer policy. This means that virtually all copyright-infringing material identified through Content ID is "entirely insulated" from the three-strikes termination policy required by the DMCA.
- A class action lawsuit challenged YouTube's failure to issue copyright strikes when Content ID identifies infringing uploads.

- Source: [EFF, "Unfiltered: How YouTube's Content ID Discourages Fair Use"](https://www.eff.org/wp/unfiltered-how-youtubes-content-id-discourages-fair-use-and-dictates-what-we-see-online)
- Source: [ClassAction.org](https://www.classaction.org/blog/youtube-hit-with-class-action-over-rampant-copyright-infringement-lack-of-protections-for-ordinary-content-creators)
- Source: [Duke Law & Technology Review](https://scholarship.law.duke.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1271&context=dltr)

### 2.4 Repeat Infringer Policy Enforcement

**BMG Rights Management v. Cox Communications (4th Cir. 2018):**
- **Case:** No. 16-1972
- **Holding:** The Fourth Circuit ruled Cox was not entitled to DMCA safe harbor because it failed to reasonably implement its repeat infringer policy.
- **Key evidence:** Internal Cox emails showed employees determined they would terminate infringing accounts but did not do so because of the revenue those accounts generated. Cox had "blacklisted" Rightscorp in 2011, auto-deleting millions of infringement complaints sent on BMG's behalf.
- **Legal standard established:** "At a minimum, an ISP has not 'reasonably implemented' a repeat infringer policy if the ISP fails to enforce the terms of its policy in any meaningful fashion."
- The court rejected Cox's argument that "repeat infringers" meant only those adjudicated in court.

**Sony Music Entertainment v. Cox Communications (4th Cir. 2024):**
- On February 20, 2024, the Fourth Circuit vacated an unprecedented $1 billion judgment against Cox, reversing the vicarious liability verdict while preserving the contributory infringement framework.

- Source: [Harvard JOLT](https://jolt.law.harvard.edu/digest/bmg-v-cox-court-of-appeals-denies-dmca-safe-harbor-in-landmark-copyright-case)
- Source: [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca4/16-1972/16-1972-2018-02-01.html)
- Source: [IPWatchdog](https://ipwatchdog.com/2018/02/21/no-dmca-safe-harbor-coxs-13-strike-policy-terminating-repeat-infringers/)
- Source: [Wiley Law](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Fourth-Circuit-Opens-Door-to-Future-ISP-Defenses-in-Vacating-1-Billion-Copyright-Judgment-Against-Cox-Communications)

## 3. Pro-Rata vs. User-Centric Payment Models

### 3.1 How the Pro-Rata Model Works

Under the pro-rata (or "big pool") model used by all major streaming services, all subscriber revenue is pooled, and each track's share of total streams determines its share of royalties. A subscriber's individual payment does not go directly to the artists they listen to.

**Example:** If a subscriber listens only to independent artists, but the global stream share is dominated by major-label superstars, the subscriber's fee primarily funds superstar payouts.

### 3.2 Research on Pro-Rata vs. User-Centric

**France -- Centre National de la Musique (CNM) / Deloitte study:**
- Found that a user-centric move would see streaming revenues for classical music rise by an estimated 24%, while rap/hip-hop would see reductions of 21% and 19% respectively.

**Academic research (ScienceDirect, 2024):**
- A study using data from a French music streaming platform (150,000+ users, 890 million streams) analyzed consequences of user-centric payment. Found that the pro-rata rule may outperform user-centric in efficiency and fairness when superstar marginal cost is lowest, and that transitioning could enlarge the existing royalty gap.

**UK context:**
- The UK has discussed an "Artist Growth Model" that would redistribute a share of top earners' revenue to mid-tier artists.

- Source: [ScienceDirect, "Alternative payment models in the music streaming market"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167624524000258)
- Source: [ScienceDirect, "Pro-rata vs User-centric in the music streaming industry"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0165176523001362)
- Source: [Music Business Research](https://musicbusinessresearch.wordpress.com/2024/09/23/the-music-streaming-economy-part-15-pro-rata-versus-user-centric/)

### 3.3 Streaming Fraud and Playlist Manipulation

**United States v. Michael Smith (S.D.N.Y., 2024):**
- First criminal prosecution of AI-generated streaming fraud in the U.S.
- Smith, 52, of Cornelius, North Carolina, used AI to generate thousands of fake songs (titles like "Zygotic Washstands" by fake bands like "Callous Post") and bots to stream them millions of times.
- Scheme ran 2017-2024; over $10 million in fraudulent royalties obtained.
- Charges: wire fraud conspiracy, wire fraud, and money laundering conspiracy (each carrying up to 20 years).
- Smith pled not guilty before U.S. District Judge John Koeltl in Manhattan (September 2024). Case outcome pending as of this writing.

### 3.4 Spotify Discovery Mode -- Payola Parallels

- **Launched:** 2020 (initially as a test)
- **Mechanism:** Artists/labels flag tracks for algorithmic priority in exchange for accepting a royalty reduction of approximately 30%.
- **Congressional scrutiny:** In 2021, the House Judiciary Committee requested information on Discovery Mode over its similarities to payola (the practice of secretly paying for radio airplay, prohibited under 47 U.S.C. Section 508).

**Class action lawsuit (November 2025):**
- Filed by subscriber Genevieve Capolongo in the U.S. District Court, S.D.N.Y.
- Alleges Spotify's Discovery Mode constitutes "modern payola" -- selling visibility through undisclosed promotional deals while presenting recommendations as algorithmically neutral.
- Seeks restitution, punitive damages, and a court order requiring disclosure of financial arrangements behind Discovery Mode recommendations.
- Spotify called the lawsuit "nonsense."

**FTC implications:** Under the FTC's Endorsement Guides, accepting a royalty discount in exchange for promotion arguably constitutes a material connection that must be disclosed to consumers. Failure to do so could transform Discovery Mode from a personalization feature into a deceptive advertisement.

- Source: [Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/class-action-lawsuit-spotify-payola-discovery-mode-1235460448/)
- Source: [Recording Academy](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/does-spotifys-new-discovery-mode-resemble-anti-creator-payola)

### 3.5 Spotify Audiobook Bundling -- Impact on Per-Stream Rates

- **March 2024:** Spotify reclassified its Premium subscription tiers as "bundles" after adding 15 hours of monthly audiobook access.
- **Legal basis:** Under the Phonorecords IV settlement, bundled multimedia services may pay lower mechanical royalty rates than standalone music subscriptions.
- **Rate impact:** Spotify values music at 52% of the bundle -- approximately $5.70 per subscriber. The mechanical royalty rate dropped from the headline rate (e.g., 15.2% for 2024) to approximately 24.5% of Total Content Cost for the relevant accounting period. **[single-source on specific 24.5% figure]**
- **Financial impact:** NMPA projected a loss of approximately $150 million in mechanical royalties in the first year, with cumulative losses projected at over $3.1 billion through 2032.
- **Litigation:** The MLC filed suit challenging the bundling classification. In January 2025, Judge Analisa Torres (S.D.N.Y.) ruled that Spotify could legally classify its Premium service as a bundle, finding that audiobook streaming constitutes a distinct product with more than "token value." The case was dismissed with prejudice.

- Source: [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/business/streaming/spotify-songwriters-less-mechanical-royalties-audiobooks-bundle-1235673829/)

## 4. Rate-Setting Inconsistencies

### 4.1 CRB Phonorecords III: The 44% Rate Increase and Its Appeal

**The determination:**
- In 2018, the Copyright Royalty Board (CRB) ruled to increase mechanical streaming royalty rates from 10.5% to 15.1% over the period 2018-2022 -- a 44% increase.
- This was hailed as a "monumental victory" for songwriters by the Recording Academy and NSAI.

**The appeal:**
- In 2019, Spotify, Amazon, Google, and Pandora appealed the rate increase. Apple Music notably did not join the appeal.
- The appeal delayed implementation and withheld increased royalties from songwriters for years.
- **Irony noted by industry commentators:** The three major label groups (Universal, Sony, Warner) -- which also operate the world's three largest music publishing operations -- benefited enormously from streaming's growth on the recording side while the streaming services they partnered with on recordings fought to suppress the mechanical (publishing) rates that their own publishing subsidiaries would have received.

**Resolution:**
- In July 2022, the CRB reaffirmed the 15.1% headline rate on remand.
- Final determination published in the Federal Register on August 10, 2023.
- Federal Register citation: 88 FR 54336 (August 10, 2023)
- Source: [Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/08/10/2023-14925/determination-of-royalty-rates-and-terms-for-making-and-distributing-phonorecords-phonorecords-iii)
- Source: [NMPA](https://www.nmpa.org/phonorecords-iii-remand-upholds-15-1-rate-increase-reduces-some-protections/)
- Source: [Recording Academy](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/songwriters-receive-monumental-victory-copyright-royalty-board)
- Source: [Billboard](https://www.billboard.com/articles/business/8527519/digital-services-appeal-copyright-royalty-board-rate-determination-nmpa)

### 4.2 Phonorecords IV (2023-2027)

- **CRB Case:** 21-CRB-0001-PR (2023-2027)
- **Settlement (not litigated):** Agreed between NMPA, NSAI, and DiMA; accepted by CRB on December 30, 2022.
- **Federal Register:** 87 FR 77414 (December 16, 2022)
- **Rates:** Headline mechanical rate phases from 15.1% (2023) to 15.35% (2027). Physical phonorecord/permanent download rate increased to 12 cents per track or 2.31 cents per minute.
- **Services bound:** Amazon Music, Apple Music, Google/YouTube Music, Pandora, Spotify.
- Source: [Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/12/16/2022-27237/determination-of-royalty-rates-and-terms-for-making-and-distributing-phonorecords-phonorecords-iv)
- Source: [Recording Academy](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/songwriter-mechanical-royalty-rates-proposed-settlement-five-years-what-to-know)

### 4.3 Controlled Composition Clauses

- Most recording contracts set a mechanical royalty at 75% of the statutory rate (approximately 6.82 cents against the 9.1 cent statutory rate).
- The statutory rate remained frozen at 9.1 cents for over 12 years.
- The Digital Performance Right in Sound Recordings Act of 1995 prohibits controlled composition clauses for digital phonorecord deliveries (permanent downloads) in most cases. For streaming, services pay publishers directly, so controlled composition clauses primarily affect physical CD/vinyl sales.
- BMG publicly eliminated controlled composition clauses from its U.S. record contracts, calling them "poisonous."

- Source: [ASCAP](https://www.ascap.com/help/music-business-101/controlled-composition-clauses)
- Source: [Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/bmg-exterminates-poisonous-controlled-composition-clauses-from-its-us-record-contracts/)
- Source: [Bost Legal Group](https://bostlegalgroup.com/what-is-a-controlled-composition-clause-in-a-recording-contract/)

### 4.4 Interactive vs. Non-Interactive Streaming: Legal Distinctions

- **Interactive services** (e.g., Spotify, Apple Music): Defined as services enabling a member of the public to receive a transmission "on request." Must negotiate direct licenses with copyright holders; the Section 114 statutory license does not apply.
- **Non-interactive services** (e.g., Pandora's radio mode): Eligible for the statutory license under Section 114. Rates set by the CRB.
- **Rate-setting standards:**
  - Non-interactive (Section 114): Uses the "willing buyer/willing seller" standard (as amended by the MMA, which replaced the prior 801(b)(1) standard for all Section 114 proceedings).
  - The 801(b)(1) standard required consideration of four factors: maximizing availability of creative works, affording a fair return, reflecting relative roles of owners and users, and minimizing disruptive impact on existing industry structures.
  - Interactive mechanical rates (Section 115): Set by CRB under a separate process (the Phonorecords proceedings described above).

- Source: [Copyright Office, Music Modernization FAQ](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/faq.html)
- Source: [Fordham IP Law Journal](https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1774&context=iplj)
- Source: [SoundExchange, Licensing 101](https://www.soundexchange.com/service-provider/licensing-101/)

## 5. Artist Contract Issues vs. Legal Protections

### 5.1 California Labor Code Section 2855 (The "Seven-Year Rule")

- **Enacted:** 1937
- **Provision:** Personal service contracts of a "special, unique, unusual, extraordinary, or intellectual character" cannot be enforced against the employee beyond seven years from the commencement of service.

**The 1987 Amendment (Music Industry Carve-Out):**
- Section 2855(b) added a provision specific to phonorecord production: artists may invoke the seven-year rule but must first give written notice, and the label retains the right to recover damages for undelivered material under the contract.
- This effectively weakens the seven-year rule for recording artists by creating a financial penalty for exercising it.

**Legislative reform attempts:**
- The California FAIR Act (A.B. 983 / A.B. 2926) has been introduced multiple times to restore full seven-year rule protection for recording artists by repealing the 1987 amendment. As of this writing, it has not been enacted.

- Source: [California Legislative Information, Lab. Code Section 2855](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=2855.)
- Source: [Fordham IP Law Journal, "Restoring the Seven Year Rule in the Music Industry"](https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/iplj/vol26/iss1/6/)
- Source: [Anand Law](https://www.anandlaw.com/the-seven-year-rule-california-labor-code-%C2%A72855/)
- Source: [The California FAIR Act](https://cafairact.com/7yearrule)

### 5.2 Work-for-Hire Classification of Sound Recordings

**The core legal question:** Whether sound recordings qualify as "works made for hire" under Section 101 of the Copyright Act, which would vest copyright ownership in the label rather than the artist.

**The 1999 controversy:**
- In 1999, RIAA lobbyists inserted "sound recordings" into the definition of works made for hire via the Satellite Home Viewer Improvement Act -- without public debate.
- In 2000, Congress repealed the change after artist backlash, restoring the prior status.

**Current legal ambiguity:**
- For a sound recording to be work-for-hire for an independent contractor (which most artists technically are), it must (1) be specially ordered/commissioned, (2) be subject to a written agreement designating it as work-for-hire, and (3) fall into one of nine statutory categories in Section 101.
- Sound recordings are not explicitly listed among the nine statutory categories (absent the repealed 1999 amendment), making their work-for-hire status legally contestable.
- Legal precedent remains split. No Supreme Court ruling has resolved the question.

- Source: [Arizona State Law Journal](https://arizonastatelawjournal.org/2021/05/11/you-belong-with-me-retaining-authorship-and-ownership-of-sound-recordings/)
- Source: [Matt Stahl, Columbia Law School](https://slc.law.columbia.edu/sites/default/files/content/docs/Stahl-%20%20Work%20for%20Hire.pdf)
- Source: [Harvard Journal of Sports & Entertainment Law](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/jsel/wp-content/uploads/sites/78/2019/02/HLS104.pdf)

### 5.3 35-Year Termination Right (17 U.S.C. Section 203)

- **Provision:** Authors (or their heirs) may terminate grants of copyright beginning 35 years from the date of execution (or 35 years from publication, whichever is earlier, for grants covering publication).
- **Scope:** Applies to all grants executed on or after January 1, 1978 (except works made for hire and grants by will).
- **Inalienable right:** Section 203(a)(5) states: "Termination of the grant may be effected notwithstanding any agreement to the contrary." Labels cannot contractually prevent exercise of this right.

**How labels discourage use:**
- Classifying recordings as "works made for hire" (which are exempt from termination rights) in standard contracts.
- Using confidential settlement agreements/NDAs when termination disputes arise, preventing public legal precedent from forming.
- The practical complexity of the notice requirements (specific windows, Copyright Office filing requirements) creates barriers for unrepresented artists.

**Notable example:** In June 2017, Paul McCartney reached a confidential settlement with Sony regarding recapture of Beatles copyrights under termination provisions. Terms were not disclosed. **[single-source on confidentiality detail]**

- Source: [17 U.S.C. Section 203](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/203)
- Source: [U.S. Copyright Office, Termination of Transfers](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/203.html)
- Source: [NYU Law Review](https://nyulawreview.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/10/NYULawReview-93-4-Brennick.pdf)

### 5.4 NDAs in Settlement Deals

Settlement agreements in music copyright disputes routinely include non-disclosure provisions that prevent the terms from becoming public. This has the effect of suppressing legal precedent: when potentially landmark cases settle confidentially, courts never issue rulings that could guide future disputes. Key termination right negotiations between artists and labels are kept out of public view, leaving other artists without guidance on what outcomes are achievable.

- Source: [Public Knowledge](https://publicknowledge.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/Making-Sense-of-the-Termination-Right-1.pdf)
- Source: [International Musician](https://internationalmusician.org/the-truth-about-non-disclosure-agreements/)

### 5.5 360 Deal Provisions

- **Structure:** Labels receive a percentage of the artist's revenue across multiple streams: record sales, touring/live performance, publishing, merchandise, and brand deals.
- **Cross-collateralization:** Advances from all revenue streams can be recouped by the label before the artist receives payment. Artists may owe royalties from touring income even if the tour itself was unprofitable.
- **Active vs. passive interest:** "Active" 360 deals give the label control rights over ancillary revenue streams (requiring the artist to sign with the label's publishing or merchandise company), while "passive" deals simply take a percentage without control.
- **Labor law concerns:** The extent to which 360 deals effectively make an artist an employee across all revenue streams while classifying them as an independent contractor for copyright purposes creates tension with labor law principles, though no definitive case law exists on this point. **[single-source -- legal commentary, not adjudicated]**

- Source: [Stone & Sallus Law](https://www.stonesalluslaw.com/the-art-of-a-360-deal-understanding-an-entertainment-contract/)

## 6. Recent Developments (2023-2026)

### 6.1 AI-Generated Music Copyright Issues

**Copyright Office Part 1: Digital Replicas (July 31, 2024):**
- Addressed the use of AI to create digital replicas of performers.

**Copyright Office Part 2: Copyrightability (January 29, 2025):**
- Affirmed that copyright does not extend to purely AI-generated material.
- Prompts alone provide insufficient human "control" for authorship.
- Three types of qualifying human contribution: (1) human-provided prompts (insufficient alone), (2) human-created expressive inputs perceivable in the output, (3) human modifications/arrangements of AI output.
- AI-generated work can be registered "when and if it embodies meaningful human authorship."

**Court of Appeals ruling (March 21, 2025):**
- The U.S. Court of Appeals issued a ruling denying copyright protection for purely AI-generated works, reinforcing the human authorship requirement.

- Source: [Copyright Office, Part 2 Report (PDF)](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/Copyright-and-Artificial-Intelligence-Part-2-Copyrightability-Report.pdf)
- Source: [Copyright Office, AI Policy Guidance (PDF)](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/ai_policy_guidance.pdf)
- Source: [Perkins Coie](https://perkinscoie.com/insights/update/copyright-office-solidifies-stance-copyrightability-ai-generated-works)

### 6.2 Spotify's New Royalty Model (April 2024)

- Tracks must reach a minimum of 1,000 streams in the previous 12 months to be eligible for royalty payments.
- Streams must come from a minimum number of unique listeners (threshold not publicly disclosed).
- Revenue from sub-1,000-stream tracks is redirected to the general payout pool.
- Spotify projected this would move $40 million from sub-threshold tracks to qualifying tracks in 2024.
- Stated rationale: combating artificial streaming, reducing processing costs for micro-payments, and deterring noise/ambient uploads gaming the system.

- Source: [Spotify for Artists Blog](https://artists.spotify.com/en/blog/modernizing-our-royalty-system)
- Source: [Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/confirmed-next-year-tracks-on-spotify-1000-plays/)
- Source: [iMusician](https://imusician.pro/en/resources/blog/spotify-royalty-model-changes-in-effect-since-april-2024)

### 6.3 Tennessee ELVIS Act

- **Full name:** Ensuring Likeness Voice and Image Security Act
- **Signed:** March 21, 2024, by Governor Bill Lee
- **Effective:** July 1, 2024
- **Significance:** First U.S. state law specifically addressing AI voice cloning of artists.
- **Key provision:** Adds "voice" to existing publicity-rights protections; prohibits use of AI to clone an artist's voice without consent.
- **Enforcement:** Criminal enforcement as a Class A misdemeanor.
- **Legislative vote:** Unanimous -- 93-0 in the House, 30-0 in the Senate.

- Source: [Tennessee Governor's Office](https://www.tn.gov/governor/news/2024/1/10/tennessee-first-in-the-nation-to-address-ai-impact-on-music-industry.html)
- Source: [ASCAP](https://www.ascap.com/news-events/articles/2024/03/elvis-act-tn)
- Source: [Vanderbilt Law School](https://law.vanderbilt.edu/why-tennessees-elvis-act-is-the-king-of-artificial-intelligence-protections/)

### 6.5 Federal AI Legislation: NO FAKES Act

- **Full name:** Nurture Originals, Foster Art and Keep Entertainment Safe Act
- **Congressional bill:** H.R. 2794, 119th Congress (2025-2026)
- **Status:** Reintroduced April 2025 in both Senate and House; referred to Judiciary Committees in both chambers.
- **Key provisions:** Grants individuals exclusive right to authorize digital replicas; creates civil liability for unauthorized use; includes safe harbors for OSPs acting in good faith; exceptions for news, commentary, parody.
- **Support:** Recording Academy, RIAA, UMG, WMG, Sony; over 400 artists signed a letter of support (including 21 Savage, Missy Elliott, Deadmau5, Scarlett Johansson).
- **Opposition:** EFF, Center for Democracy and Technology, ALA, CCIA -- arguing the bill's DMCA-style notice-and-takedown provisions risk incentivizing removal of lawful speech.
- **Not yet enacted** as of March 2026.

- Source: [Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/2794/text)
- Source: [Recording Academy](https://www.recordingacademy.com/advocacy/news/no-fakes-act-introduced-in-the-senate)
